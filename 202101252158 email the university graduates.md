## Methods
### Procedure
This study examined the experiences of the graduates of the fifteen public universities in Michigan. In collaboration with these universities, about 41,000 ==emails were sent out to all May 2012 graduates from their home institution== ^[A good example for [[2021 TIES]]?]. The survey was distributed between January and March 2013, 8–10 months after graduation. Respondents were asked to click on a link, which led them to an online survey, including questions about employment, location of residence, income, and reasons for leaving or staying in Michigan. The survey was confidential, took about 10 minutes to complete, and respondents were entered into a drawing for a $100 Visa gift card. There was a 17% response rate, with 7054 participants completing the survey.

### Measures and analytic strategy
The present study analyzes an open-ended survey question that asked: ‘what have been the biggest challenges you have faced since graduating from college?' ^[[[2021 TIES]][[202101252208 2021雜學國發會國際論壇研究構想]]可參考] Nearly 80% (N = 5624) of the respondents answered this question. Each of these responses was coded thematically by an undergraduate research assistant ^[[[202101252208 2021雜學國發會國際論壇研究構想]] 還是需要實習生？], who had been trained in qualitative data analysis and theories of career development during the recession. The research assistant worked closely with one of faculty members on the study team to develop a coding system. Reliability was established by the faculty member also coding data and comparing the codes. As recommended by Charmaz's (1995) suggestions for inductive qualitative analysis, we began with inductive open coding for major themes, going line by line through the data. This approach involves staying close to the data in order to look for patterns within it (Charmaz 1995). We used a qualitative data analysis program (Atlas.ti) ^[[[202101252208 2021雜學國發會國際論壇研究構想]]須考慮] to enter codes, highlight passages of text, and compare co-occurrences of codes. Because we made the codes as specific as possible (Charmaz 1995), we ended with over 100 codes. In order to accurately reflect the data, many of the responses were categorized into multiple codes, as they revealed multiple challenges faced after college.

As suggested by Miles and Huberman (1994), the coding scheme and analytical
framework were continually refined throughout the coding and analytic process, as the research assistant and professor reviewed the data and the emerging codes. The theme categories were modified when new themes emerged or previous themes appeared less relevant. After the coding was complete, the faculty member grouped related codes together into larger overarching categories (i.e. difficulty finding a job, financial difficulties, and difficulties making the transition to adulthood more generally). This strategy sought to bring together potentially relevant codes on a given theme. Next, we analyzed how each theme was distributed according to gender and parents' education.

To contextualize and add breadth to the qualitative analysis, we report key results
from the closed-ended survey. As Onwuegbuzie, Johnson, and Collins (2009, 133)
suggest, qualitative and quantitative research can complement each other well, especially when combining strong ‘paradigmatic mixing.' We also used chi square tests to compare men and women and those with different class backgrounds (measured by the question ‘what is the highest level of education completed by your parent(s) or guardian(s)?', with response options including ‘high school or less,' ‘associate's or technical degree,' ‘some college,' and ‘bachelor's degree or higher'; for the purposes of our comparison we grouped the first three options together) in terms of work status (measured by the question ‘you are currently employed', with response options including ‘full-time (35+ h/week),' ‘part-time (less than 35 h/week),' ‘full-time active military duty,' and ‘not currently employed'), debt levels (measured by the question: ‘how much student loan debt do you currently owe?' with response options of ‘none,' ‘up to $5000,' followed by a listing of debt levels broken down in ascending order) and income (measured by the question ‘what is your current annual income?', with response options that increase by $15,000 increments, until $75,000, when they increase by $25,000 increments). By moving back and forth between the qualitative and quantitative data, we hope to provide a fuller and more nuanced picture than has been previously examined about the experiences and challenges facing recent college graduates.
