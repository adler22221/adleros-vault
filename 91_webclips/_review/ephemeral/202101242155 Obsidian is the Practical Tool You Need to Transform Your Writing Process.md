# Obsidian is the Practical Tool You Need to Transform Your Writing Process
## How to research, outline, and draft articles in Obsidian

I've come across a new text editor, and it has completely transformed how I research, outline, and draft my articles.

I'm always looking for ways to improve my writing process. While my _growth_ is about becoming a better and more proficient writer, my process is what supports that goal.

**In my case, I like to:**

-   Keep everything in one place
-   Easily “search” for files
-   Connect ideas and build a knowledge base

[I shared my article-writing process in a previous article; the key for me is to take time every now and then to reflect on what's working and what isn't.](https://medium.com/honest-creative/why-your-creative-process-should-change-often-1c3367607522) I mentioned that I used Google Docs to plan and draft my articles… but I've recently come across a new tool.

While there is much to love about Google Docs (easy accessibility, sharing docs), I couldn't organize my docs the way I wanted to. Even with a naming schema for my files, it was difficult to find what I was looking for in the _infinite scroll._ My drafts were often buried beneath daily work-related files.

Not to mention, there's the fear that I'll run out of space on Google Drive, or wake up one morning to find that everything's been deleted (unlikely, but stranger things have happened). That's the problem with a platform you don't control.

**Long story short, I came across** [**Obsidian**](https://obsidian.md/) **in my search for a note-taking app patterned after the** [**Zettelkasten method**](https://zettelkasten.de/posts/overview/) **(check it out, let it blow your mind).** I've previously used nvAlt and [The Archive](https://zettelkasten.de/the-archive/), both of which are fantastically simplistic, but didn't meet my needs. You may have heard of popular app [Roam Research](https://roamresearch.com/)… while it has rave reviews, it's pretty expensive.

Obsidian is an up-and-coming program that I think will give Roam Research a run for its money. Obsidian is only in its beta stage, so it will only get better.

Now, I'm planning and drafting my Medium articles exclusively in Obsidian.

Let's take a look.

When you download Obsidian, you're prompted to create your first “Vault” for your notes. I've set up my Obsidian vault to save all my notes in Dropbox.

Obsidian uses plain text markdown files. Your notes are saved as text files (.txt), and “your data sits in a local folder.”

Text files (.txt) have longevity in the sense that the file format is not proprietary to any specific software; therefore, we'll have a way to open plain text files for many years to come.

Obsidian allows you to create folders within your vault. It's much easier to categorize and organize your notes by nesting within folders.

It's always bothered me that other text editors did not have this capability. This was one of the shortcomings of Google Docs, as well.

When I first started using Obsidian, I created just one parent folder called “Medium Writing.” But I quickly realized I was limiting myself; I could create multiple folders to organize my in-progress articles.

![Image for post](https://miro.medium.com/max/1518/1*oeq3CWNGSDWYYe61JXCAAg.png)

Screenshot by the Author

I created 3 folders, one for each stage of my writing process:

1\. Research

2\. Outlines

3\. Drafts

That's how I move through my writing process. I start with a brain dump or research, and then I create a new document for my content outline. I create a third doc for my first draft. All subsequent drafts are given a separate doc, as well.

I like to preserve each step in the process in case I need to go back and reference later. The progression of these steps is important to me. It's also really helpful if I've gone off track, and need to reference an earlier draft.

It's really not as complicated as it sounds. It's what works for me. I'm not one of those writers who can bang out a “finished” draft in one go (not yet, anyway!). So I break things down, moving from (1) brainstorming to (2) outlining to (3) drafting. My first drafts are stronger as a result (not so unfocused or meandering!).

As I'm working (moving from one step to the next), I like to pull up my docs side-by-side. So, for example, I'll have my outline on the left, and a blank doc on the right. I'll reference my outline as I'm drafting.

In Google Docs, I'd create a new file and then drag the docs side-by-side (I'd open up two browser tabs). But I can do this even more seamlessly in Obsidian. There's a feature within the text editor that allows you to “split vertically” or “split horizontally.”

I can easily select the notes I'd like to display side-by-side.

Obsidian also allows users to “preview” their markdown files as a formatted doc. I like to “preview” the notes I'm referencing (see the note on the left in the screenshot above) because it's easier to read.

By the end of my writing process, I've typically got 3 files for an article: (1) Research, (2) Outline, (3) Draft. In Obsidian, I can use internal hyperlinks to connect all 3 files and easily navigate between them.

Obsidian is advertised as your “second brain”:

> “The human brain is non-linear: we jump from idea to idea, all the time. Your second brain should work the same.”

Hence the internal hyperlinks to “connect” related notes. Using one of Obsidian's “plugins,” you can view your backlinks for a particular note in a sidebar to the right.

The best part is, you can click into the “graph” view for a birds-eye-view of your web of notes and how they're connected.

I also use “tags,” or hashtags, for easier searchability. A few of the tags I use for my articles include, “medium,” “medium article,” and “research,” “outline,” or “draft.”

I've finally found the text editor I've been looking for! As Obsidian intended, the program will function as my “second brain.”

I plan to write another article about how I'm using Obsidian for note-taking and idea capture.

I'm excited that Obsidian gives me a place to organize my in-progress articles. (My articles are no longer cluttering up Google Docs!) I hope this gives you a better idea of what you can accomplish using Obsidian. Give it a shot and let me know what you think!
