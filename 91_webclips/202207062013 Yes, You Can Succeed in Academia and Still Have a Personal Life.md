# Yes, You Can Succeed in Academia and Still Have a Personal Life
A highly accomplished professor in my field, someone for whom I have enormous respect, recently boasted during a conference that he scheduled PhD seminars late on weeknights so that doctoral students wouldn’t get the “false notion” that academia is a nine-to-five gig. Over the years, I’ve heard many professors sardonically tell a variant of the joke that our profession provides unparalleled flexibility to work any 80 hours you want each week. And I constantly note that graduate students and new professors express anxiety about having to give up their personal lives to “make it.”

Whether out of boasting or out of fear, we seem to tell ourselves that work-life balance is incompatible with success as a professor. But this is a false tradeoff. It’s absolutely possible to have a fulfilling and successful academic career while working reasonable hours and enjoying a rich personal life. I’ve observed many impressive colleagues achieve this balance in their own way, both at the Wharton School where I teach and elsewhere. While there are endless methods and technologies for [time management and productivity](https://hbsp.harvard.edu/inspiring-minds/my-fixation-on-time-management-almost-broke-me), they are not the answer to overwork and burnout. Instead, we need to recognize a deeper principle that teaches us why work-life balance isn’t just possible, but essential for academic success.

> “Let’s develop a norm of sharing how to find sanity and joy in this wonderful career path, instead of giving the impression that one must make insane sacrifices to be an academic.”

### Maximize Insight, Not Time. The Rest Will Follow.

When I started my PhD studies, I was married and had a young child. I noticed that many of my fellow students spent all day and night at their offices. I knew that I couldn’t keep those hours and preserve the kind of personal or family life I wanted, which caused me no shortage of fear. What if I ran out of time to study for classes? What if I didn’t have as much time to do research as others? Would my professors think I wasn’t committed?

After struggling with these questions for weeks, I decided there had to be a better way. In consultation with my wife, I made a hard but fateful decision: I would work a reasonable but fixed number of hours on weekdays and almost never on weekends, unless an unusually tight deadline forced me to work. I’ve stayed true to that commitment for over 15 years.

Along the way, I discovered the single most important principle that has guided me in finding work-life balance:

> The scarcest resource in academia is insight, _not_ time.

This is true for any kind of knowledge work, in which you are motivated by and rewarded for your ideas. If you conduct research, insight is what produces groundbreaking questions, what inspires novel arguments, and what solves thorny methodological issues. If you teach, insight is what helps you know how to pique your students’ interest, how to convey complex ideas clearly, and how to evaluate their learning effectively. Once you have insight, you don’t have to spin your wheels wondering what to do.

As simple as the principle seems, it has a profound implication: We must maximize insight instead of maximizing time. Dedicating inordinate amounts of time to our academic tasks—to teaching, to research, to service—is counterproductive because we end up exhausting our brains, the very machines that produce insight.

I’ve found four ways to maximize insight, while keeping time under control, that I believe can help you be both productive and personally satisfied.

#### 1\. Take Time to Rest and Renew Your Mind and Body

Work-life balance is a complement, not a substitute, to academic productivity. Our brains need rest and rejuvenation to produce insight. That includes giving our minds time off, our bodies exercise and good nutrition, and our emotions a healthy outlet. Our minds keep on processing and solving ideas while we take time off to sleep, socialize, and exercise. When we come back to our work after doing those things, we’re more creative and focused, and our brains are as sharp and insightful as possible.

There’s good research to back this up, particularly on the cognitive benefits of [sleep](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature02223) and [exercise](https://www.health.harvard.edu/mind-and-mood/exercise-can-boost-your-memory-and-thinking-skills). Some of the most valuable ideas I’ve had are associated with sleep. Just to give one example, the core insight that led to my dissertation came to me during the transition between sleep and wakefulness. Based on that experience, I try to use sleep strategically. If I am thinking through a complex issue, I consider it before bed and then go to sleep. As soon as I wake up, I write down the first impressions that come to mind. These end up being some of the most usable insights to solve the issue.

I could make similar statements about the benefits of exercise or time with loved ones. I don’t do those things just because they help my academic performance. I do them because they bring me happiness. But an inevitable side effect is that they also help maximize insight when I get back to work, saving me time when I sit down to focus because I spin my wheels less than I do when my brain is exhausted.

#### 2\. Divide and Conquer Tasks That Don’t Require Your Insight

One way to prevent mental exhaustion is to reserve your time and focus for tasks that really require it. To do this, seek help for time-consuming tasks that don’t directly require your insight—teaching tasks such as grading or keeping class participation records, or research tasks such as gathering data, coding, and copyediting.

The load can be delegated to a teaching assistant, shared with a coauthor, or outsourced for a modest fee. Many times, sharing these tasks creates win-win opportunities to mentor students. For example, undergraduate and MBA students who are eager to learn more about a company or industry happily help me write new teaching materials. And a dataset that needs cleaning up is a chance to teach a new PhD student data skills. Additionally, there are many resources—often sitting unused—that make hiring external help very affordable. These may include work-study programs offered by your university or online services such as Upwork.

> “Work-life balance is a complement, not a substitute, to academic productivity. Our brains need rest and rejuvenation to produce insight. That includes giving our minds time off, our bodies exercise and good nutrition, and our emotions a healthy outlet.”

Even if your institution doesn’t have resources that allow you to spend money on outsourcing, it’s important to learn to delegate and work in collaboration with others. This is less about money than about being willing to give up control, learning to use whatever resources your institution offers, and budgeting time to manage collaborators and students.

This is a hard lesson for newly minted PhDs to learn (it was for me, at least) because graduate school trains us to do all the grunt work ourselves. But that’s not always necessary, even as a doctoral student, and particularly after becoming a professor—and it’s an unproductive way to maximize insight. I’ve seen colleagues at well-endowed institutions be extremely unproductive because they aren’t willing or able to work with others; as a result, they fail to spend their research or teaching budgets. I’ve also seen colleagues at institutions with limited budgets be extremely productive because they know the difference between what they must do and what they can rely on others to do.

#### 3\. When You Work, Work—and Budget Your Time Accordingly

Because our brains can only be at maximum capacity for limited periods of time, when we do turn them on to work, we must use that time as effectively as possible. That means truly focusing and working productively when we work so that we can compress the amount of time we need to get things done. Because the techniques and technologies of everyone’s workflows are idiosyncratic, I’ll suggest two concepts that are broadly applicable.

-   **It’s better to finish a small number of things all the way than to inch forward on many things simultaneously.** Finish the lesson plan for one class instead of working a little bit on 10 different classes. Submit one paper to a journal this month instead of making marginal progress on three papers during the month. Getting a few things completely done is more efficient and effective than getting many things partially done. It also creates more frequent psychological wins.
    
-   **Be serious about eliminating distractions.** Stick a do-not-disturb note to your office door, put your phone in a drawer, ask friends not to call during certain hours, or do whatever it takes to avoid getting sidetracked. I’m not implying you must be a robot. I simply mean that if you work steadily during designated work times, you’ll have more time for leisure (without feeling anxious or guilty) when you aren’t working.
    

In trying to apply these concepts, I’ve realized that most tasks take just about as long as I decide they will. If I tell myself it’ll take me four hours to write part of a paper or to prepare for a class, it does. If I tell myself it’ll take me eight hours instead, it does. This is true if I focus while working, which I can do if I’ve let my mind and body rest properly beforehand. Of course, this assumes I have a sense of my efficient work rate. No matter how much I’d want it to take only a day to perform all the data analysis for a project, that’ll never happen. So I never budget just one day for that kind of work, and I usually add extra buffer time to account for unexpected situations. I don’t always estimate correctly, but over time I’ve developed a reasonable intuition about what kinds of things take me what amount of time.

> “It’s often more important to decide what projects not to pursue than which ones you will pursue. The opportunity cost of working on something that moderately excites you is not working on something that really excites you.”

Being productive during work time and budgeting time accordingly has produced three benefits. First, it has lowered the second guessing and stress involved in performing a task, which wastes energy and kills focus. Second, it has prevented overwork and delayed projects, which result from trying to fit too much into a day or week or month. Third, it has allowed me to manage collaborators’ expectations and keep my commitments to them—they don’t sit waiting for my part, and our joint projects progress more smoothly.

#### 4\. Be Deliberate About What You Will Not Do

It’s often more important to decide what projects _not_ to pursue than which ones you will pursue. The opportunity cost of working on something that moderately excites you is not working on something that really excites you. That means being highly selective about which research projects to pursue, whom to work with, what hours of the day to work, etc. Beware of the supposedly “quick-and-easy” project. Nothing in academia fits that description. Thinly dividing your time among too many projects at once is seriously damaging to your insightfulness. This is another reason why the few-and-all-done approach is better than the many-partially-done approach. You can attend to many things, just not all at the same time.

And if you find yourself too stretched, consider dropping a project. It may be the best gift to yourself and to your collaborators. When I was a year into being a professor, desperate to publish so as not to perish, I received a revision request from a top journal in my field. I was excited at first, but it quickly became clear that doing what the editor and reviewers wanted would not just require a lot of work (which I was willing to do), it would also take the paper in a direction with which I completely disagreed. Additionally, I had a couple of other papers that were much more promising and central to my identity as a scholar; working on the revision would significantly delay submitting and publishing the other two papers.

After an agonizing back and forth, I decided not to resubmit the paper to the journal that had requested a revision. Several colleagues told me I was crazy. I wasn’t sure that I wasn’t crazy. But I soon realized that the time I dedicated to the more promising papers paid off. Eventually, both were published and had a more powerful effect on my intellectual development and on my field than the other paper would have had. My main point is this: If you don’t see a path to get excited about a project that requires a lot your time, and if you have an alternative project of higher potential that you are truly excited about, think seriously about the cost of dedicating your time to one over the other. Choosing what _not_ to spend time on is the more important decision, because it frees up your mind for deeper insight about what you _do_ spend time on.

### You _Can_ Be a Happy and Successful Academic

I hope you find this core principle—that your scarcest resource is insight—and these four insight-maximizing strategies useful. I recognize that my personal lifestyle and my approach to work are tailored to my needs, strengths, and weaknesses, and they may not apply to everyone. People with different work styles and personal circumstances (e.g., single, no children, approaching retirement) will define balance differently.

More than advocating for a specific way of working, I hope we change the message we are broadcasting about what academic life is about. This is especially important for senior professors, whose boasts about inordinately long work hours send the wrong message to younger colleagues. Let’s develop a norm of sharing how to find sanity and joy in this wonderful career path, instead of giving the impression that one must make insane sacrifices to be an academic.
