## A. Section Arc

### Part III Preface (book p.137–141) [VERIFY-PAGE: book p.137]

The preface announces the methodological turn that governs the whole of Part III. Whereas Part II examined human existence from the "outside" — the environmental-philosophy perspective treating the human as a node within natural and social ecologies — Part III approaches the same human being from the "inside": from the standpoint of the full-sized living person (等身大の〈生活者〉). Uegaki names this complementary approach "the analysis of life" (〈生〉の分析).

The preface provides a compressed preview of Ch. 5's argument: human life, in any era and any culture, is structured around three inescapable tasks — securing survival (〈生存〉の実現), realising one's existence-in-society (〈現実存在〉の実現), and handing on to the next generation (〈継承〉の実現). These are the "three moments of life" (〈生〉の三契機). The preface then notes the contemporary paradox: all three are being realised, but the people living them have no felt sense of doing so. The reason is that in modern society the three moments are accomplished entirely through "entrustment" (委託) to the social apparatus (〈社会的装置〉). The lifeworld (〈生活世界〉) that used to be the concrete arena in which people lived out these moments has consequently become "hollowed out" (空洞化). The modern human's relationship to the social apparatus is structurally analogous to a user (〈ユーザー〉) logging in to a system — hence "life-as-a-user" (〈ユーザー〉としての生). [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp.138–141]

The preface closes by anticipating Ch. 6's question: why are there exactly three moments, and what does the concept of society-as-stage-set tell us about the social apparatus?

### Ch. 5, §1: "Human Life" and the Lifeworld (book p.143) [VERIFY-PAGE: book p.143]

Uegaki opens with the "geocentric analogy" (天動説の比喩): the heliocentric model is correct from outside, but for a full-sized living human the sun rises and sets. Environmental philosophy gives the structurally correct external picture; "life analysis" gives the experientially true internal picture. Neither cancels the other.

The internal picture discloses the human as 〈生活者〉 — literally a "lifer," someone who lives a life — whose medium is the 〈生活世界〉 (lifeworld). Uegaki notes that Western philosophy has consistently privileged "life as spirit" (精神としての生活) over "life as livelihood" (暮らしとしての生活): Aristotle's contemplative life ranked highest and the material household lowest; Locke and Rousseau imagined a rights-bearing subject who neither hungers nor dies; Arendt's highest faculty was action (speech, politics), not labour; even Marx's ideal labour was self-expressive rather than subsistence-oriented [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp.144–146]. Uegaki's claim is that a genuine anthropology must re-embed "life as spirit" within "life as livelihood" — the whole picture must start from the material ground, not transcend it.

### Ch. 5, §2: The Three Moments — 〈生存〉, 〈現実存在〉, 〈継承〉 (book p.146) [VERIFY-PAGE: book p.146]

This is the definitional core of the chapter.

**〈生存〉 / Survival**
Definition (Table 2 + body): As the destiny of the human being who has a biological foundation and is a living organism, survival means: within the lifeworld as its arena, securing necessary goods, processing materials, making tools, and accumulating knowledge [VERIFY-PAGE: book p.147]. Its orienting dimension is the *life-character* (生命性) of human existence. It is the ground of "life as livelihood." Uegaki insists that any account of human life that removes this moment is one-sided, because the biological substrate is irreducible. [VERIFY-PAGE: book p.147–148]

**〈現実存在〉 / Existence-in-society**
Definition: As the destiny of the human being whose essential characteristic is to live together with others, existence-in-society means: within the lifeworld as its arena, forming oneself as a member of the collective, sharing information and building trust among members, and carrying out collective decision-making and division of roles. It encompasses both "passive socialisation" (受動的社会化 — acquiring one's personhood through social relations, i.e. becoming "human" through the community) and "active socialisation" (能動的社会化 — taking on and resolving the problems that arise within collective life). Its orienting dimension is the *social character* (社会性) of human existence. Uegaki notes that while 〈生存〉 is the ground of "livelihood," 〈現実存在〉 connects upward to "life as spirit" — it is where the spiritual-social dimension of life operates, but always resting on the livelihood base. [VERIFY-PAGE: book p.147]

**〈継承〉 / Succession**
Definition: As the destiny of the human being who as a living organism will inevitably meet death, succession means: through the lifeworld, receiving what has been inherited from the previous generation, improving it, and passing it on again to the next generation. Its orienting dimension is the *historical character* (歴史性) of human existence. This moment is irreducible to both "livelihood" and "spirit": it introduces the temporal dimension — the chain of generations that constitutes the lifeworld across time. Without it, the lifeworld collapses with the current generation, condemning the next to extreme difficulty. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp.147–148]

Uegaki summarises the three in Table 2 (book p.148). Each moment expresses a specific existential vulnerability: survival expresses that "I" cannot exist cut off from the material world and other living things; existence-in-society expresses that "I" can only be "I" through others; succession expresses that those others extend not only spatially but along the temporal axes of past and future — "the chain of being" (存在の連なり). Together the three moments allow the human being to take hold of life as something felt as real, and to root one's existence in the world. [VERIFY-PAGE: book p.148–149]

### Ch. 5, §3: "Human Life" Made Invisible (book p.149) [VERIFY-PAGE: book p.149]

The paradox: in contemporary society all three moments are being realised — yet people feel no sense of realising them. How?

Survival in modern society means: consuming goods and services supplied by the market economy system and the state administration system; and securing a stable supply of money ("the key") to access those systems. This Uegaki calls "economic activity" (経済活動). Existence-in-society in modern society takes two forms: (a) "active socialisation" — in modernity largely taken over by the social apparatus (specialists, bureaucracies), so that the ordinary individual's contribution to solving collective problems is near zero; (b) "passive socialisation" — reduced in modern conditions to "self-realisation" (自己実現), meaning the pursuit of individually desired outcomes, statuses, and evaluations. Succession in modern society is realised chiefly through "school education" (学校教育), which the social apparatus delivers to the next generation as a structured transmission of the accumulated knowledge-system [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp.149–152].

The shared structure: in each case, the root of the three moments is carried by the social apparatus acting as intermediary — an immense black box — and what remains visible to the individual user is only the fragmented end-products: economic activity, self-realisation, school education. [VERIFY-PAGE: book p.152]

### Ch. 5, §4: "Life-as-a-User" and the Hollowing-out of the Lifeworld (book p.153) [VERIFY-PAGE: book p.153]

This section introduces the user analogy explicitly. Modern humans are like users of a web service: they create an account (nationality), acquire a key (money), log in (connect to the social apparatus), and receive all services on offer — but they cannot modify the system itself, cannot set its rules, and remain at the periphery of a vast black box. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp.151–152]

The historically new element: in pre-modern and early modern communities (hunting-gathering, "traditional community," "urban community," and even the period before high economic growth), the three moments were fused in every act of livelihood — rice planting, roof thatching, community meetings, festival preparation. Each act simultaneously realised survival, existence-in-society, and succession as a unified whole. People had to front the lifeworld directly; the question "am I really living?" would have been unintelligible. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp.153–154]

In modern society this fusion has dissolved. All that remains as experientially real are economic activity, self-realisation, and school education. The lifeworld — the concrete, full-sized arena in which the three moments were integrated — has become hollowed out (空洞化). The transition can be stated symbolically: "the life of the 〈生活者〉" (livelihood-person) has given way to "life-as-a-user." [VERIFY-PAGE: book p.155]

Uegaki is careful not to romanticise the pre-modern: the trajectory is irreversible (the accumulation of "the social" cannot be wound back), and most modern people have already lost the capacities needed for the earlier mode of life. The point is diagnostic, not nostalgic. [VERIFY-PAGE: book p.156]

### Ch. 5, §5: "Collective Survival," Fundamental Conflict, and Society as Stage-set of Life (book p.155/157) [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp.155–159]

This section provides the deep-evolutionary grounding for why there are three moments and why society exists.

**Collective survival (集団的〈生存〉):** All mammals achieve survival partly through group behaviour, but human collective survival is extraordinarily developed — both in quality (density of coordination) and in scale. Humans are, among mammals, uniquely "collective" organisms. [VERIFY-PAGE: book p.157]

**Fundamental conflict (〈根源的葛藤〉):** Collective survival generates an inherent contradiction. Because the genetic unit in mammals is the individual, "my survival" and "our survival" are strongly correlated but never fully identical. Social insects and colonial invertebrates resolve this because they have minimal or zero individuality; their individual and collective survival are biologically unified. Humans and other mammals cannot do this: they remain individual genetic units who must cooperate. The tension is irreducible. Uegaki names this the fundamental conflict (〈根源的葛藤〉) inherent in human existence — the irresolvable friction between "my survival" (点の〈生存〉, individual) and "our survival" (金の〈生存〉, collective). [VERIFY-PAGE: book p.158]

As humans raised collective survival to ever higher levels, this contradiction deepened in parallel. Some mechanism was needed to manage it.

**Society as stage-set of life (「〈生〉の舞台装置」としての〈社会〉):** Uegaki's hypothesis: what we have been calling 〈社会〉 (society) originally developed as an auxiliary apparatus (補助装置) for mitigating the fundamental conflict and enabling collective survival to proceed smoothly. Society-as-stage-set consists of the three components already introduced: "social structures" (社会的構造物), "social institutions/norms" (社会的制度), and "meaning-system = world-image" (意味体系=世界像). These three components are inseparable — they together constitute the skeleton of the lifeworld. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp.158–159]

It is in this context that the three moments gain their internal coherence (developed further in §6 below): existence-in-society is required because survival is collective; succession is required because the lifeworld sustaining collective survival is an artefact that must be maintained across generations.

### Ch. 5, §6: Inner Connection of the Three Moments (book p.159) [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp.159–162]

The section answers: why exactly three moments?

**Why 〈現実存在〉 is required for 〈生存〉:** Because human survival is collective survival, realising survival requires realising existence-in-society (forming collective selfhood, solving collective problems). Once society-as-stage-set was created and collective survival became dependent on it, existence-in-society acquired a new layer of meaning beyond merely living together: it means rooting oneself in the lifeworld constituted by the stage-set, and nurturing it together with others. Without the stage-set, humans can no longer bear the contradiction internal to their fundamental conflict. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp.159–160]

**Why 〈継承〉 is required for 〈生存〉:** The lifeworld sustaining collective survival is humanly constructed (人為的). It can be destroyed. But reaching sufficient robustness to withstand the fundamental conflict required the accumulated effort of many generations. Its loss would devastate the survival prospects of descendants. Succession therefore means not merely biological reproduction but: receiving the "social" (社会的なもの) inherited from past generations, improving it, and handing it on — exactly as defined above. [VERIFY-PAGE: book p.160]

**Inner connection of 〈継承〉 and 〈現実存在〉:** In possessing a transgenerational society, humans became "bearers of the times" (時の担い手). Living as such a bearer means: receiving something entrusted from past generations, and entrusting something to future generations — connecting the life of the past to the life of the future. This redefines even death: because human life is embedded in the "chain of being," biological death does not complete one's life. One's life reaches beyond the biological terminus. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp.160–161]

**The Western-philosophy critique re-read through the three moments:** Western philosophy's consistent neglect of survival (〈生存〉) and focus on existence-in-society (〈現実存在〉) — "existence-in-society without survival" — is now explicable historically: its authors were members of a class released from survival anxiety within a robust lifeworld, for whom survival and succession were so self-evidently secured that they became invisible. The irony of modernity: we have now achieved the inverse condition — the lifeworld has become hollowed out, survival is invisible for different reasons (black-box entrustment), and it is precisely this loss of self-evidence that forces us, for the first time, to interrogate the anthropological meaning of 〈生存〉. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp.161–162]

**Closing note on hollowed-out lifeworld and 〈ユーザー〉:** In the hollowed-out lifeworld, where life-as-a-user is normalised, the originary meaning of all three moments, and the weight of the fundamental conflict, are both rendered invisible and unfelt. [VERIFY-PAGE: book p.162]

## D. Relevance to Thesis Ch IV

### §B: User → Cocoon Arc

Uegaki's Chapter 5 is the most direct source for the Ch IV §B argument. The structural sequence is:

1. The three moments are the anthropological definition of what it means for a human to be alive.
2. Modern society realises all three through entrustment to the social apparatus — a transition from the livelihood-person to the user.
3. Life-as-a-user is characterised by: disconnection from the material world and the chain of being; reduction of others to single-role service relationships; pursuit of self-realisation that requires neither resistant others nor past/future generations.
4. This user-life is the condition in which the lifeworld has become hollowed out — the concrete, meaning-laden arena is gone.

The cocoon figure in Ch IV §B should be grounded in Uegaki's account of "hollowing-out": the user exists within the social apparatus as a service-consumer but without the full-thickness lifeworld. The cocoon is precisely the user interface — protective, comfortable, but severed from the chain of being. The key linkage is: entrustment → invisibilisation of the three moments → loss of roots (存在の連なりからの浮遊) → cocoon condition.

### §A: Squeeze Hypothesis / Universe–World–Metaverse

Uegaki's stage-set of life concept provides the conceptual grounding for "world" in the thesis sense. The stage-set = the humanly constructed arena (social structures + institutions + meaning-system) that enables collective survival and mitigates fundamental conflict. This maps onto "world" as the idealist, meaning-laden, human-constructed space nested within the universe. The social apparatus corresponds to the stage-set *minus* meaning-system — a shell of structural and institutional machinery operating without the lifeworld. This distinction (stage-set with meaning vs. apparatus without meaning) is directly relevant to the thesis's argument that the metaverse accelerates the displacement of world by neo-artificiality.

Additionally: fundamental conflict is a candidate for grounding the thesis's claim that the squeeze is not merely a technological phenomenon but an expression of tensions inherent in human existence. The squeeze between universe and metaverse can be read as an intensification of the original fundamental conflict — now the individual is squeezed not only between self and collective but between their biological substrate (universe) and the neo-artificial apparatus (metaverse), with the meaning-bearing world (stage-set) being hollowed out in between.

## E. Brief Final Report (8 bullets)

- **〈生存〉 (survival):** The destiny of a biologically grounded organism to secure necessities, process materials, make tools, accumulate knowledge in the lifeworld. Grounds the life-character of existence; foundational base of all three moments. [VERIFY-PAGE: book p.147]
- **〈現実存在〉 (existence-in-society):** The destiny of an essentially social being to form collective selfhood, share information, build trust, and carry out collective decision-making — encompassing both passive socialisation (becoming human through others) and active socialisation (solving collective problems). Connects to the social character of existence. [VERIFY-PAGE: book p.147]
- **〈継承〉 (succession):** The destiny of a mortal organism to receive from prior generations, improve, and pass on to the next. Introduces the historical/temporal dimension; cannot be reduced to livelihood or spirit. Its failure threatens the survival of future generations. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp.147–148]
- **Inner connection:** The three moments are not parallel but structurally entailed: 〈現実存在〉 is required *for* 〈生存〉 because human survival is irreducibly collective; 〈継承〉 is required *for* 〈生存〉 because the lifeworld sustaining collective survival is a fragile human artefact that must be maintained transgenerationally. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp.159–161]
- **Fundamental conflict:** The irreducible friction in mammals between individual and collective survival (genetic unit = individual, but strategy = collective). Unique to humans in its extremity. Society-as-stage-set arose as an auxiliary apparatus to mitigate this conflict. [VERIFY-PAGE: book p.158]
- **Stage-set of life vs. social apparatus:** Society originally = stage-set (structures + institutions + meaning-system, together); the social apparatus = stage-set *minus* meaning-system. This distinction is crucial for the thesis: the metaverse/neo-artificiality belongs on the apparatus side, not the world side. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp.140–141, 158–159]
- **Life-as-a-user / hollowing-out:** Modern entrustment of all three moments to the social apparatus produces the user condition: the three moments are realised via a black box; the lifeworld loses its full-thickness integration and becomes hollow. Life-as-a-user is structurally rootless — floating free of the chain of being. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp.152–155]
- **Thesis Ch IV relevance:** The user → cocoon arc in §B maps directly onto Uegaki's sequence: entrustment → invisibilisation → hollowing-out → rootlessness. The stage-set/apparatus distinction maps onto world/metaverse in the squeeze hypothesis. Fundamental conflict is a candidate deep-anthropological ground for the squeeze's urgency.
