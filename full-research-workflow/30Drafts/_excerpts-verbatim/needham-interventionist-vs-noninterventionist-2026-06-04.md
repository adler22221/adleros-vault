## §1 — Geo-ecology (the root; climate ruled out)

> "The comparison between China and Europe is particularly instructive, almost a bench-test experiment one might say, because the complicating factor of climatic conditions does not enter in… the range of climate in the Chinese culture-area is not unlike that in Europe." — **p.1**

> "Europe, as I have often said, is like an archipelago. There is the Baltic, the North Sea, the Irish Channel, the Mediterranean, the Aegean, the Black Sea and then the great Atlantic outside the Pillars of Hercules — all inviting maritime commerce and the activities of sea-captains. In contrast to all this, China is a vast land-mass, well suited for the activities of peasant-farmers in their millions." — **pp.1–2**

> hydraulic imperative: "a vast series of water-works directed to (a) the conservation of the great rivers… (b) the use of water for irrigation, especially for wet rice cultivation, and (c) the development of a far-flung canal system" → the bureaucratic "economic high command." — **p.5**

## §2 — Occupational psychology (command vs patience)

> "the Chinese were always primarily peasant-farmers, and not engaged in either animal husbandry or sea-faring. These two latter occupations encourage excessive command and obedience; the cowboy or shepherd drives his animals about, the sea-captain gives orders to his crew which are neglected at the peril of everyone's life on board, but the peasant-farmer, once he has done all that is necessary for the crops, must wait for them to come up." — **pp.11–12**

## §3 — *wei* / *wu wei* and "interventionism" (the crux)

> "This difference is expressed epigrammatically in the Chinese terms *wei* 為 and *wu wei* 無為. *Wei* meant application of the force of will-power, the determination that things, animals, or even other men, should do what they were ordered to do, but *wu wei* was the opposite of this, leaving things alone, letting Nature take her course, profiting by going with the grain of things instead of going against it, and knowing how not to interfere. *Wu wei* was the great Taoist watchword…" — **p.16** (Needham derives the pair explicitly from "above, pp.1–2," i.e. the peasant/shepherd-seaman difference)

> "the best magistrate was he who intervened least in society's affairs." — **p.16**

> "if the non-interventionist character of the 'villages–prince' relationship engendered a certain conception of the world which was propitious to the progress of science, it had certain natural limitations. It was not congruent with characteristically **occidental 'interventionism', so natural to a people of shepherds and sea-farers**… The suggestion is that experiment demanded too much active intervention." — **p.17** ← Needham's own interventionist/non-interventionist terms

> the state too: "the good functioning of the entire society, even if this responsibility was carried out with the **minimum intervention**. One remembers that the ancient definition of the Ideal Ruler was that he should sit simply facing the south and exert his virtue… so that the Ten Thousand Things would automatically be well governed." — **p.18**

## §4 — Attitudes to Nature ("(10)", pp.90–93)

> organic vs mechanical: phenomena "acted on everything else, not by mechanical impulsions but by co-operation… the natural world was not something hostile or evil, which had to be perpetually subdued by will-power and brute force, but something much more like the greatest of all living organisms… Call it an organic naturalism." — **p.91**

> "agricultural rather than pastoral; hence perhaps a more patient, less dominating, more feminine attitude to natural resources. The 'man of Sung', who pulled up his sprouts in order to make the crop grow faster, was a standard laughing-stock for the peasant-farmers of China through two millennia, accustomed as they were to a much more patient attitude towards Nature." — **p.92**

> the Baconian hinge where the two stances meet: "to use Nature it was necessary to go along with her. The Taoists would have applauded Francis Bacon's saying, *Natura enim non imperatur nisi parendo* (**Nature can only be commanded by obeying her**)." — **p.92**

> the Western pole named: "How different was all this from the **feudal or imperialist domination of Nature** arising from the Hebrew tradition. The People of the Book were never guided towards any restraint in the utilisation of those natural resources…" — **p.92**; and the Baconian-alienation account, **p.75**: "Since Francis Bacon's time the essence of the scientific method has been alienation… Nothing inhibits the ability to understand, and after understanding to **manipulate and exploit to the full**."

## §5 — The single-science teleology — and the thesis's refusal (Hui)

Needham's own conclusion (which the thesis does **not** adopt):
> Chinese science and technology, before "like all other ethnic cultural rivers, they flowed into the **ocean of modern science**." — **p.2**

**Hui's reframing of the Needham Question** (what the thesis keeps instead):
> "answering Needham's question is not to show who is more advanced than the other, but rather to elaborate on the **different systems of technological thought**." — Yuk Hui, "Machine and Ecology" (2020) [`huiMACHINEECOLOGY2020`]; `[VERIFY page — NLM-routed]`. Cf. *QCT in China* (2016), §17 "Needham's Question," **p.165**: the question indexes "the collapse of Qi-Dao in its confrontation with modernisation."

→ Keep the **co-emergence** (mode-of-production ↔ worldview ↔ technoscience); discard the **single ocean / one finish line**. There is a plurality of cosmotechnics; the interventionist Western mode is one, not the telos of the rest. (Ties to the technodiversity material in [[eastern-dominance-as-enlightenment-mode-verbatim-2026-06-04]].)

## §6 — Consequence for science (why it matters)

> non-interventionism was "propitious" for *observational* science — "action at a distance," wave-theory, tides, **magnetism** ("a totally non-European contribution") — and for state-organised early science (astronomy, surveys); **but** "so long as 'bureaucratic feudalism' remained unchanged, mathematics could not come together with empirical Nature-observation and experiment to produce something fundamentally new" — "experiment demanded too much active intervention." — **pp.17–18**
