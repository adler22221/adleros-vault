## 4. The translation divergence (side by side)

| | **"widest sense" clause** | **final clause** | **"men/humans"** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **German (GS 3)** | im umfassendsten Sinn fortschreitenden Denkens | sie als **Herren** einzusetzen | den Menschen |
| **Jephcott 2002** | in the **widest sense** as the advance of thought | installing them as **masters** | human beings |
| **Cumming 1972** | in the most general sense of progressive thought | establishing their **sovereignty** | men |

**What is at stake.** The German "sie als **Herren** einzusetzen" literally = "to install them as **lords/masters**." Jephcott's "installing them as masters" is the **literal** rendering of *Herren*. Cumming's "establishing their sovereignty" is a freer rendering that drops the *master/lord* noun and substitutes an abstract noun (sovereignty) — closer in sense to the Baconian "the sovereignty of man lieth hid in knowledge" that A&H quote a few lines later, but one step removed from the German *Herr*. The prompt's other guessed Cumming variant ("making men masters") is **not** the wording on disk — the disk text is "establishing their sovereignty."

Two further wording differences that may matter to the Ch III stipulation:
- The exact phrase **"in the widest sense"** (which the mode-definition quotes) is **Jephcott's**. Cumming has "in the **most general sense**." If the chapter quotes the snippet "Enlightenment … in the widest sense as the advance of thought," that is verbatim Jephcott, not Cumming.
- "the **advance of thought**" (Jephcott) vs "**progressive thought**" (Cumming) for *fortschreitenden Denkens*.

## 5. Which to cite

**Recommendation for the Ch III mode-definition (stipulating "Enlightenment" = the metaphysical revolution, A&H's "widest sense," not the historical period):**

- **Quote JEPHCOTT (Stanford 2002, p.1)** for the in-text quotation. Reasons: (a) it is the phrase the chapter already leans on — "understood in the **widest sense** as the advance of thought" — which directly licenses the "not-the-period, but-the-metaphysical-revolution" stipulation; (b) it is the current scholarly standard / critical edition (ed. Schmid Noerr) and the more literal rendering of *Herren* ("masters"), which keeps the **disposition-to-domination / mastery** resonance the thesis trades on; (c) the Jephcott PDF is the same source already used in the companion self-preservation dossier, so citations stay internally consistent.
- **Optionally gloss the German** "sie als Herren einzusetzen" in a footnote to foreground the *Herrschaft/Herr* (mastery/domination) sense — this strengthens the bridge to the thesis's "disposition to domination."
- **Cite Cumming only** if the author specifically wants the older/Verso pagination or the "establishing their sovereignty" reading (e.g. to echo the Baconian "sovereignty of man"). If so, the wording is "**In the most general sense of progressive thought, the Enlightenment has always aimed at liberating men from fear and establishing their sovereignty**" (p.3) — do **not** mix it with the "widest sense" phrasing, which is Jephcott's.

> One-line rule: if the chapter prints the words **"widest sense,"** the citation must be **Jephcott p.1**; "establishing their sovereignty" must be **Cumming p.3**. Don't blend.
