## A. NOMOS — Brill's New Pauly Vol.9, pp.797–804 (`cancikBrillsNewPauly2002`)

The Nomos entry has four parts: **A. General · B. Social · C. Political · D. Theoretical and philosophical** (sig. "P.SI." / Spahn), then separate sub-lemmata [2] (Egyptian nome), [3] (musical *nomos*), [4] (coin).

**§A General — the semantic range (p.798)** [verbatim]:
> "In Greek, n6mos (pl. n�moi) refers to customary conduct or a behavioural norm observed by members of a community; depending on the context it can be translated with 'custom', 'habit', 'practice', 'rule', 'order', 'institution', 'constitution', 'law' etc. … The size of the communities where a n6mos applied could vary considerably … Linguistically and semantically related are ta n�mima (customs, practices, laws), → autonomia, → eunomia and → isonomia."
> — BNP "Nomos" [1] §A, Vol.9 p.798.

**§A — the evolution from social norm to law (p.798–799)** [verbatim]:
> "The concept of nomos (rules of conduct) that originally applied to social norms gradually became more complex. … During the 5th cent. written laws reached the status of nomoi, the traditional codes of conduct. In addition to the social meaning the word acquired a legal meaning as well: nomos as political rule of conduct, as 'law' …"
> — BNP "Nomos" [1] §A–C, Vol.9 pp.798–799.

**§D Theoretical and philosophical — THE NOMOS–PHYSIS ANTITHESIS (p.801)** ★ the load-bearing passage [verbatim]:
> "Because there were different n6moi, i.e. customs, traditions, social and political orders for Greeks and 'barbarians', the → sophists argued that they were relative and arbitrary. In the second half of the sth cent. BC the quest for a firm foundation led philosophers to propose unchanging characteristics of human nature of general validity: the notion of physis (see → nature). **This notion became the antithesis to n6mos which was then qualified as '(any) custom' and 'arbitrary (and changeable) agreement', i.e. (nonbinding) convention** [3. 11-19; 8. 117-124]. Philosophical inquiry focused on the nomos of nature (n6mos physeods), i.e on the 'unchanging natural law (Lat. lex naturalis); as divine world order it became the central tenet of the Greco-Roman Stoa …"
> — BNP "Nomos" [1] §D, Vol.9 p.801. Cross-refs at entry end: "→ Ius; → Law; → Nature."

**§D — the Hesiod 'law vs force' + Heraclitus 'divine nomos' loci (p.800–801)** [verbatim]:
> "according to Hesiod (Op. 275-279) Zeus gave to man the n6mos of law and to the animals the zomos [n6mos] of force. … According to Heraclitus (22 B 114 DK) the n6moi of mortals are 'nourished' by divine reason (nous). … Sophocles (Ant. 449-457) makes the immemorial, unwritten commandments of the gods (agrapta n�mima) … precede over to the political n�moi (decrees) of Creon. Thus the poet pointed out the limitations of political authority and drew attention to laws that were above the state (cf. → Human Rights)."
> — BNP "Nomos" [1] §D, Vol.9 pp.800–801.

> [!note] What this confirms re: where BNP puts nomos
> BNP treats nomos squarely as the **humanly-instituted / conventional** pole: "arbitrary (and changeable) agreement, i.e. (nonbinding) convention," "instituted as political rule of conduct," etc. — and explicitly sets physis as its **antithesis**. **BNP does NOT group nomos with physis against technē.** (See §E assessment.)

## B. NATURE / NATURAL PHILOSOPHY (= the physis article) — Brill's New Pauly Vol.9, pp.529–536 (`cancikBrillsNewPauly2002`)

This is the substantive φύσις entry (sig. "L.BR." — Long/Brisson tradition). Greece §§A–M, then Rome §II.

**§A Terminology — ETYMOLOGY + the DIRECT physis–technē opposition (p.529)** ★★ two load-bearing data [verbatim]:
> "In ancient Greek, the term physis (pbotc, 'nature') is a **nomen actionis derived from the root \*bhu- (which probably implies the idea of coming into being, growth and development)**. It first of all denotes the state that results from the spontaneous development of a living being: hence the meanings of 'size', 'stature', or 'appearance', all of which point towards the more general meaning of 'innate bodily characteristics'. **Physis is thus opposed to t�chn� (t�yvy, → art), which refers to any competence acquired in various domains of human activity.** Physis thus came to designate a primary and original way of being."
> — BNP "Nature, Natural philosophy" §A, Vol.9 p.529.

**§E Sophists — the physis–nomos antithesis stated from the physis side (p.530)** [verbatim]:
> "For the → sophists, the concept of physis played an important role in moral reflection. In → ethics and in politics, man must regulate his conduct after nature (physis) as an authentic and spontaneous reality that has not been altered or veiled by **the conventions and illusions of the law (n6mos)**. Thus it is expounded by Callicles in Plato's dialogue 'Gorgias' (Pl. Grg. 483c-d; 484a; 491e) …"
> — BNP "Nature, Natural philosophy" §E, Vol.9 p.530.

**§G Aristotle — the internal-archē criterion + the bed/artisan contrast (pp.531–532)** [verbatim]:
> "Those beings are said to be 'natural' which possess within themselves the cause of their motion. … A plant has the principle of its growth within itself … **A bed (a non-natural thing), at the other hand, does not have its cause within itself, but in something else, sc. the artisan.** … 'nature (physis) is origin and cause of motion and of rest for the thing in which it resides, by essence and not by accident' (Aristot. Ph. 2,1,192b 20-22 …)."
> — BNP "Nature, Natural philosophy" §G, Vol.9 pp.531–532.

**§K Way of life — 'living according to nature' (p.534–535)** [verbatim]:
> "Most philosophers of the Hellenistic and Imperial period elevated the maxim of 'living according to nature' (to xata vow Chv; to kata physin z�n, Latin secundum naturam vivere) to a moral rule."
> — BNP "Nature, Natural philosophy" §K, Vol.9 pp.534–535.

**§II Rome — natura ← (etymology) + the physis/natura relation (p.535–536)** [verbatim]:
> "No text, then or later, clearly attests the meaning of 'genesis', although it is etymologically possible. The most frequent of the earliest uses of natura is in the sense of 'innate characteristics', or 'dispositions resulting from birth' (the same applies to physis). … natura is to a large extent a 'semantic artefact' that reproduces the characteristics of the Greek term. … both physis and natura denote coming into being and its result; the only difference consists in the fact that physis refers to the world of vegetation, while zatura [natura] may be applied to the animal kingdom."
> — BNP "Nature, Natural philosophy" §II (Rome), Vol.9 pp.535–536.

> [!note] Also present (not load-bearing for the reframe, logged for completeness)
> §C Philosophers of nature (the *Peri physeōs* title tradition); §D Physicians (physis ↔ *dynamis*); §F Plato (physis = the best soul; *Timaeus* demiurge); §H Stoics (the famous "Nature is an **artistic fire** (pyr technikon) … a fire-like and **art-like** breath (pyroeidēs kai technoeidēs)", Diog. The **Stoic "pyr technikon / technoeidēs"** passage (§H, p.533) is an ancient *physis-described-as-art* datum — parallels von Staden (CONSOLIDATED §C.2) and HWPh's Stoic re-welding; redundant with held material but a clean BNP instance if wanted.

### TECHNĒ (Vol.14)
**Genuinely NEW / sharper:**
- **(a) The explicit antithesis *pair* "technē — physis ('art — nature')" listed alongside "technē — tychē ('art — coincidence')"** (§II, p.195) — **additive in *form***: an encyclopedia *naming the technē/physis opposition as a standing terminological pair* (and pairing it with technē/tychē). - **(b) technē = "teachable, rationally grounded knowledge … with the claim to … gain mastery in a specific field," vs *empeiria*** — **REDUNDANT** with Preus (epistēmē/technē; "art is about becoming"), LSJ sense III ("a set of rules, system, method of making"), Cassin (ART = production of an object). **Relation to the reframe:** **NEUTRAL-to-STRENGTHENS.** BNP's technē = teachable rational mastery is compatible with technē as "the made/human-instituted." **Crucially it does NOT carry the Aristotle II.8 *technē completes/imitates physis* keystone** (Structural Finding #2) — so on the dossier's single most important porosity datum, BNP is **silent**, i.e. adds nothing.

## E.2 — Does anything CHALLENGE the reframe? (the explicit check the brief demanded)

The brief asked to flag *anything* that challenges "**nomos + technē = the two Greek categories of the artificial**" — e.g. if BNP groups nomos with physis against technē, or stresses nomos ≠ technē, or gives a classification incompatible with "both are the artificial." **Result of the check: NOTHING in BNP challenges the reframe. Specifically:**

1. **BNP does NOT group nomos with physis.** The opposite: BNP's Nomos §D and Nature §E both set **physis *against* nomos** (physis = the firm, natural, unaltered; nomos = arbitrary convention). nomos and physis are *antitheses* in BNP, never allies. ✅ compatible with the reframe.
2. **BNP places nomos on the conventional/instituted/"made" side** — "arbitrary (and changeable) agreement, i.e. (nonbinding) convention," "instituted as political rule of conduct." This is *exactly* the made/instituted pole the reframe assigns nomos. ✅ supports the reframe (same direction as Brague's grouping).
3. **BNP places technē on the made/skill side** — "professional knowledge and skill," "production of an object" (via the "→ art" cross-ref), opposed to physis. ✅ compatible.
4. **BNP never asserts "nomos = technē"** — but neither does any held source; the reframe is a *grouping*, not an identity, and BNP's separate-but-both-non-physis placement of nomos and technē is consistent with grouping them as "the (two kinds of) artificial." ✅ no conflict.
5. **No incompatible classification.** BNP offers no taxonomy in which nomos and technē fall on *opposite* sides of a made/given line. The only tri-partition implied by BNP is the standard one (physis = given; nomos = instituted convention; technē = made skill) — which is precisely the made/given mapping the thesis uses (and then retires). ✅

**Therefore: BNP is reframe-SAFE.** It is one more independent encyclopedia confirming the made/given placements the reframe relies on, and contains no counter-classification.
