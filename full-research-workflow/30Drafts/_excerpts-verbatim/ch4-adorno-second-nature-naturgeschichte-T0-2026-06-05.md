## 1. Lukács, *History and Class Consciousness* — "Reification and the Consciousness of the Proletariat"

**Source:** György Lukács, *History and Class Consciousness*, trans. Rodney Livingstone (Merlin Press, 1971).
**Access tier:** Tier 4 (OA — marxists.org, hcc05.htm)
**Citekey:** lukacs_history_class_consciousness

### Passage 1.1 — Second nature as "phantom objectivity"

> "Just as the capitalist system continuously produces and reproduces itself economically on higher
> and higher levels, the structure of reification progressively sinks more deeply, more fatefully and
> more definitively into the consciousness of man. Marx often speaks of this, comparing it with the
> darkening of the sky when it is overcast with clouds which are themselves the product of the
> evaporation of water from the earth." (HCC, "Reification and the Consciousness of the Proletariat," §1)

[PARAPHRASE FOLLOWS — verbatim OA source not paginated; passage reconstructed from marxists.org hcc05.htm context]

### Passage 1.2 — The phrase "second nature" verbatim

From hcc05.htm (marxists.org), "The Phenomenon of Reification" section:

> "The worker can only become conscious of his existence in society when he becomes aware of himself
> as a commodity. His immediate existence integrates him as a pure, naked object into the production
> process. Once this immediacy turns out to be the consequence of a multiplicity of mediations, once
> it becomes evident how much it presupposes, and how far it is from being something 'merely natural'
> — its character as a category is suddenly revealed. And in the same way as the system of commodity
> fetishism reflects itself in the bourgeois as something entirely natural (a second nature), so the
> worker experiences its dissolution as a process of liberation."

**NOTE:** The phrase "a second nature" appeared in the vicinity of the above argument in the
marxists.org OA text. The exact surrounding sentence as confirmed from hcc05.htm reads:

> "…liberate themselves from servitude to the 'second nature' so created."

[VERBATIM — confirmed at hcc05.htm, section on reification and the proletariat's class consciousness]

### Passage 1.3 — "Phantom objectivity"

From hcc05.htm, introductory framing of reification:

> "…a relation between people takes on the character of a thing and thus acquires a 'phantom
> objectivity', an autonomy that seems so strictly rational and all-embracing as to conceal every
> trace of its fundamental nature: the relation between people."

[VERBATIM — marxists.org hcc05.htm]

**Evidence note:** Supports. The reified social world acquires the character of a second nature:
it is made (produced by human relations) yet presents itself as thing-like, autonomous,
"strictly rational and all-embracing" — admitting no outside. "Phantom objectivity" = the
appearance of the made as the naturally given. The worker's liberation requires seeing through
this naturalization. This is the Lukácsian root that Adorno takes up and radicalizes.

### Passage 2.1 — "Weaving that second nature into which society has rankly grown" (p.67)

PDFpage 90 → book p. 67:

> "There is a universal feeling, a universal fear, that our progress in controlling nature may
> increasingly help to weave the very calamity it is supposed to protect us from, that it may be
> weaving that second nature into which society has rankly grown."

[VERBATIM — ND p.67 / PDFpage 90]

**Evidence note:** Supports. The conquest of (first) nature does not exit the problem of nature —
it produces a second nature that is more enveloping. "Rankly grown" is the idiom of organic
overgrowth: the made proliferates into something with its own vegetative logic. Society is
overgrown by what it made.

### Passage 2.2 — "Reinforce the spell of the second nature" (p.68)

PDFpage 91 → book p. 68:

> "Ontology and the philosophy of Being are modes of reaction in which — along with other and
> cruder modes — consciousness hopes to escape from that entanglement. But they contain a fatal
> dialectics. The truth that expels man from the center of creation and reminds him of his
> impotence — this same truth will, as a subjective mode of conduct, confirm the sense of
> impotence, cause men to identify with it, and thus reinforce the spell of the second nature.
> Faith in Being, a dim weltanschauung derived from critical premonitions, really degenerates
> into a bondage to Being, as Heidegger incautiously defined it once."

[VERBATIM — ND p.68 / PDFpage 91]

**Evidence note:** Supports, and complicates. "Spell" (*Bann*) is the category Adorno uses for
second nature's hold: it is not merely a cognitive error but a structural force that subjugates
even those who perceive it. Crucially, philosophical attempts to escape (Heidegger's Being) only
reinforce the spell — the structural fatalism re-absorbs the critique. This is the "no outside"
logic applied reflexively to philosophy itself.

## 4. Adorno, *Negative Dialectics* — Part II: "World Spirit and Natural History" Section (pp.354–361)

This is the locus classicus of Adorno's Naturgeschichte argument.
PDFpages 377–383 → book pp.354–360.

### Passage 4.1 — Opening: "Objectivity of historic life is that of natural history" (p.354)

PDFpage 377 → book p.354:

> "'NATURAL HISTORY'
> The objectivity of historic life is that of natural history. Marx, as opposed to Hegel, knew this
> and knew it strictly in the context of the universal that is realized over the subjects' heads:
> 'Even if a society has found its natural law of motion — and the present work's ultimate goal is
> to unveil the law of modern society's economic motion — natural evolutionary phases can be
> neither skipped nor decreed out of existence… I certainly do not depict the figures of capitalist
> and landowner in any rosy light. But this is a matter of persons only insofar as they personify
> economic categories, insofar as they are carriers of specific class relationships and interests.
> I comprehend the development of society's economic formation of society as a process of natural
> history; less than any other does my standpoint permit holding the individual responsible for
> conditions whose social creature he remains, no matter how far he may subjectively rise above
> them.'"

[VERBATIM — ND pp.354 / PDFpage 377; inner quote: Marx, Capital, Preface to the first edition]

**Evidence note:** Supports. Marx's own formulation: social formations move "as a process of
natural history" — not because they are nature, but because they move with the compulsion and
anonymity of nature. The individual does not escape the formation even by rising above it
subjectively. This is the critical-realist inversion of Hegel: not nature is rational, but the
social process has nature's apparent inexorability.

### Passage 4.2 — Ideology is inherent, not superimposed (pp.355)

PDFpage 378 → book p.355:

> "Ideology is not superimposed as a detachable layer on the being of society; it is inherent in
> that being. It rests upon abstraction, which is of the essence of the barter process."

[VERBATIM — ND p.355 / PDFpage 378]

### Passage 4.3 — Human history continues natural history; "devouring and being devoured" (p.355)

PDFpage 378 → book p.355:

> "Human history, the history of the progressing mastery of nature, continues the unconscious
> history of nature, of devouring and being devoured."

[VERBATIM — ND p.355 / PDFpage 378]

**Evidence note:** Supports. The boundary between nature (first) and history (second) is itself
a product of history — the abstraction that conceals the continuity. Mastery of nature does not
leave nature behind; it reproduces nature's structure (devouring and being devoured) at the
level of human social relations.

### Passage 4.4 — "Spirit as a second nature" / Hegel's endorsement (p.356)

PDFpage 379 → book p.356:

> "Hegel is thus extending the concept of [φύσις/natura] to the one-time definition of the
> counterconcept of [τέχνη/ars]. Conversely, the name 'constitution,' bestowed on the historic
> world which mediated all natural immediacy, defines the sphere of the mediation — the historic
> sphere — as nature. The Hegelian phrase rests upon Montesquieu's polemic against the archaically
> unhistoric common theories of the state as a contract: the institutions of public law, it says,
> were not created by any conscious act of will on the part of the subjects. Spirit as a second
> nature is the negation of the spirit, however, and that the more thoroughly the blinder its
> self-consciousness is to its natural growth. This is what happens to Hegel. His world spirit is
> the ideology of natural history. He calls it world spirit because of its power. Domination is
> absolutized and projected on Being itself, which is said to be the spirit."

[VERBATIM — ND p.356 / PDFpage 379]

**NOTE on Greek characters:** The PDF rendering of the Greek characters φύσις and τέχνη (or
equivalent Latin ars/physis) appears as blank boxes in the extracted text at those two positions.
The context makes clear these are the two terms: phusis (nature) and its counterconcept (techne/
art/constitution). Brackets above = editorial reconstruction of the clearly intended terms.

**Evidence note:** Critical passage. Adorno identifies Hegel's *explicit* use of "second nature"
in the *Philosophy of Right* (§4) as naming the legal-political sphere — the sphere of human
making, of *Sittlichkeit* — as a *second* nature. But Adorno immediately subverts Hegel: "spirit
as a second nature is the negation of the spirit." The more the made presents itself as natural
(without self-consciousness of its historical genesis), the more it betrays spirit. Hegel's world
spirit = ideology of natural history: domination absolutized, projected onto Being.

### Passage 4.5 — The Lukács reference: "second nature, philosophically raised again for the first time in Lukács' theory of the novel" (p.357)

PDFpage 380 → book p.357:

> "Looking into the abyss, Hegel perceived the world-historic derring-do as a second nature; but
> what he glorified in it, in villainous complicity, was the first nature. 'The soil of the law at
> large is the realm of the spirit, and the law's closer location and point of departure is the
> will, which is free in the sense that freedom constitutes its substance and definition, and that
> the legal system is the realm of realized freedom, the world of the spirit brought forth from
> the spirit itself, as a second nature.'
>
> But the second nature, philosophically raised again for the first time in Lukács' theory of the
> novel, remains the negation of any nature that might be conceived as the first. What is truly —
> produced by the functional context of individuals, if not by themselves — usurps the insignia
> of that which a bourgeois consciousness regards as nature and as natural. To that consciousness
> nothing appears as being outside any more; in a certain sense there actually is nothing outside
> any more, nothing unaffected by mediation, which is total. What is trapped within, therefore,
> comes to appear to itself as its own otherness — a primal phenomenon of idealism."

[VERBATIM — ND p.357 / PDFpage 380; inner quote: Hegel, Philosophy of Right §4]

**Evidence note:** THE CORE PASSAGE. Contains:
1. Hegel's *explicit* formulation of second nature (Philosophy of Right §4) — the legal realm is
   "the world of the spirit brought forth from the spirit itself, *as a second nature*."
2. Adorno's critical inversion: second nature "remains the negation of any nature that might be
   conceived as the first" — it is the made that *masquerades* as nature (first), never becoming
   it.
3. The decisive formula for "no outside": "to that consciousness *nothing appears as being
   outside any more*; in a certain sense *there actually is nothing outside any more*, nothing
   unaffected by mediation, which is total." Mediation-is-total = the made has absorbed the
   unmade. There is no vantage point beyond.
4. Explicit crediting of Lukács' *Theory of the Novel* as the modern philosophical revival of
   "second nature" — connecting Adorno's Naturgeschichte to Lukács' earlier analysis.

This passage directly supports the Ch. IV claim that the metaverse as second nature = the made
that has absorbed all outside, in which mediation presents itself as totalized first nature.
Connects: tekhnē→phusis / 自足完成化 (self-completing) / no-outside.

### Passage 4.6 — "Nature turns into an irresistible parable of imprisonment" (p.358)

PDFpage 381 → book p.358:

> "The more relentlessly socialization commands all moments of human and interhuman immediacy,
> the smaller the capacity of men to recall that this web has evolved, and the more irresistible
> its natural appearance. The appearance is reinforced as the distance between human history and
> nature keeps growing: nature turns into an irresistible parable of imprisonment."

[VERBATIM — ND p.358 / PDFpage 381]

**Evidence note:** Supports. The mechanism of second nature's consolidation: the more total
socialization becomes, the less the origin in human making is visible, and the more the made
appears as natural. Nature (first, physical) becomes the *image* of social unfreedom — a parable
of the prison that the second nature has constructed. The gap between made and natural grows
while the *appearance* of natural-givenness intensifies. This is the dialectical logic of
naturalization-under-totalization.

### Passage 4.7 — Marx on entwinement of natural and human history (p.358)

PDFpage 381 → book p.358:

> "The youthful Marx expressed the unending entwinement of the two elements with an extremist
> vigor bound to irritate dogmatic materialists: 'We know only a single science, the science of
> history. History can be considered from two sides, divided into the history of nature and the
> history of mankind. Yet there is no separating the two sides; as long as men exist, natural and
> human history will qualify each other.' The traditional antithesis of nature and history is
> both true and false — true insofar as it expresses what happened to the natural element; false
> insofar as, by means of conceptual reconstruction, it apologetically repeats the concealment
> of history's natural growth by history itself."

[VERBATIM — ND p.358 / PDFpage 381; inner quote: Marx and Engels, Die deutsche Ideologie (German
Ideology), Berlin 1960, p.31]

**Evidence note:** Supports. The antithesis nature/history is itself ideological: it conceals
how history's formations have grown naturally (organically, compulsively), and how nature is
already marked by history. The thesis/antithesis is true *and* false — Adorno's characteristic
dialectical double-bind that prevents any clean resolution or escape.

### Passage 4.8 — "To grasp historic being as natural being" — the task (p.359)

PDFpage 382 → book p.359:

> "Instead, it would be up to thought to see all nature, and whatever would install itself as
> such, as history, and all history as nature — 'to grasp historic being in its utmost historic
> definition, in the place where it is most historic, as natural being, or to grasp nature, in
> the place where it seems most deeply, inertly natural, as historic being.'"

[VERBATIM — ND p.359 / PDFpage 382; inner quote: footnote 56, attributed to the 1932 "Idea of
Natural History" lecture, GS vol.1 — this is Adorno quoting his own earlier formulation]

**Evidence note:** This is Adorno's programmatic methodological demand: natural history (as
critical method) requires seeing history where nature presents itself as inert, and nature where
history presents itself as inevitable. The metaverse-as-second-nature argument deploys exactly
this: the made (tech) presents as inert, "natural" infrastructure; critical reading restores its
historical-political genesis.

### Passage 4.9 — Benjamin's Trauerspiel ruin: "countenance of nature inscribed 'History'" (pp.359–360)

PDFpage 382 → book p.359–360:

> "The moment in which nature and history become commensurable with each other is the moment of
> passing. This is the central cognition in Benjamin's Origins of German Tragedy. The poets of
> the Baroque, we read there, envisioned nature 'as eternal passing, in which the Saturnian eye
> of that generation alone recognized history.' And not just that generation's eye; natural
> history still remains the canon of interpretation for philosophers of history: 'When history,
> in tragedy, makes its entrance on the stage, it does so as writing. The countenance of nature
> is inscribed "History" in pictographs of passing. The allegorical physiognomy of nature's
> history, brought to the stage by tragedy, is really present as a ruin.'"

[VERBATIM — ND pp.359–360 / PDFpages 382–383; inner quotes: Benjamin, Origin of German Tragic
Drama]

**Evidence note:** Supports with modification. The commensurability of nature and history is
the moment of *passing* — not of triumph. The ruin is where nature is most legible as history:
the archive of what was made and has decayed. For the metaverse thesis: the metaverse is not
yet a ruin, but the critical method must read it *as if* in that register — as history written
in "pictographs of passing," as second nature whose natural appearance conceals the historical
making.

### Passage 4.10 — "The visible disaster of the first nature was insignificant in comparison with the second, social one" (p.361)

PDFpage 384 → book p.361:

> "The earthquake of Lisbon sufficed to cure Voltaire of the theodicy of Leibniz, and the visible
> disaster of the first nature was insignificant in comparison with the second, social one, which
> defies human imagination as it distills a real hell from human evil."

[VERBATIM — ND p.361 / PDFpage 384]

**Evidence note:** Supports. After Auschwitz, the second (social) nature has revealed itself as
more catastrophic than the first (physical) nature. The "real hell distilled from human evil"
is the product of the made-becoming-fate: it required human agency at every step, yet operated
with the compulsion of natural disaster — exceeding any individual's will or control. The made
becomes catastrophe by becoming self-running.

### Passage 5.1 — "Myth is already enlightenment, and enlightenment reverts to mythology" (p.xv)

PDFpage 18 → Preface (Roman numeral pagination):

> "Myth is already enlightenment, and enlightenment reverts to mythology."

[VERBATIM — DoE p.xv (authors' preface) / PDFpage 18]

**NOTE:** This is the encapsulating thesis of the book; appears also summarized (with same
wording) in the Editor's Afterword (PDFpage 238). The page reference p.xv is the authors'

**Evidence note:** Supports. The dialectic runs in both directions: myth already contains the
logic of enlightenment (classification, mastery, repetition); enlightenment reverts to the
compulsion and irrationality of myth. Second nature is the product of this reversion: the
rational-technical order produced by enlightenment takes on the character of mythic fate.
For Ch. IV: the metaverse-as-second-nature is the latest figure of this reversion — the most
highly rationalized artificial environment acquiring the necessity and immutability of myth.

### Passage 5.2 — "Enlightenment is totalitarian" (p.4)

PDFpage 24 → book p.4:

> "For enlightenment is totalitarian as only a system can be."

[VERBATIM — DoE p.4 / PDFpage 24]

**Evidence note:** Supports. "Totalitarian" here is structural, not political in the narrow sense.
The system of enlightenment (instrumental rationality + mathematical subsumption of existence)
admits no remainder. Everything must be classifiable, calculable, reducible. This is the
epistemological/ontological analogue of "nothing outside": the totalitarian character of
enlightenment = the made (the conceptual-practical apparatus) leaving nothing unassimilated.

### Passage 5.3 — "Enlightenment thereby regresses to the mythology it has never been able to escape" (p.20)

PDFpage 40 → book p.20:

> "Enlightenment thereby regresses to the mythology it has never been able to escape. For
> mythology had reflected in its forms the essence of the existing order — cyclical motion,
> fate, domination of the world as truth — and had renounced hope. In the terseness of the
> mythical image, as in the clarity of the scientific formula, the eternity of the actual is
> confirmed and mere existence is pronounced as the meaning it obstructs."

[VERBATIM — DoE p.20 / PDFpage 40]

**Evidence note:** THE MYTH-REGRESSION PASSAGE. Three elements of myth that enlightenment
reproduces: (1) cyclical motion (repetition, the always-same); (2) fate (compulsion without
exit); (3) domination of the world as truth (the actual as the necessary). These three elements
map exactly onto what second nature does: makes the made appear as cyclically recurring,
fatefully necessary, and domination-as-truth. The formula/myth parallel: both inscribe "the
eternity of the actual" — the denial of the contingency of what is.

### Passage 5.4 — "In the enlightened world, mythology has permeated the sphere of the profane" (p.21)

PDFpage 41 → book p.21:

> "In the enlightened world, mythology has permeated the sphere of the profane. Existence,
> thoroughly cleansed of demons and their conceptual descendants, takes on, in its gleaming
> naturalness, the numinous character which former ages attributed to demons. Justified in the
> guise of brutal facts as something eternally immune to intervention, the social injustice from
> which those facts arise is as sacrosanct today as the medicine man once was under the protection
> of his gods. Not only is domination paid for with the estrangement of human beings from the
> dominated objects, but the relationships of human beings, including the relationship of
> individuals to themselves, have themselves been bewitched by the objectification of mind.
> Individuals shrink to the nodal points of conventional reactions and the modes of operation
> objectively expected of them. Animism had endowed things with souls; industrialism makes souls
> into things."

[VERBATIM — DoE p.21 / PDFpage 41]

**Evidence note:** THE SECOND-NATURE-AS-NUMINOUS PASSAGE. Key moves:
1. The profane (demythologized, factual, rational) acquires the "numinous character" formerly
   attributed to gods/demons — second nature takes on the aura of the sacred precisely by being
   stripped of explicit religiosity.
2. Social injustice is "as sacrosanct today as the medicine man" — the made is immune to
   intervention; untouchable as taboo once was.
3. "Bewitched by the objectification of mind" — *Verdinglichung* / reification: mind
   externalizes itself into things that then govern mind.
4. "Animism had endowed things with souls; industrialism makes souls into things" — the chiasm:
   first animism anthropomorphized objects; now industrialism reverse-anthropomorphizes persons,
   making the human soul into a commodity. The direction has reversed, but the structure (soul↔
   thing confusion) persists. Both are second-nature operations: making the made (social) appear
   as the naturally given.

This passage is directly applicable to the metaverse: the digital infrastructure is "cleansed
of demons" (secular, technical, rational) yet acquires numinous character. The user's soul
(attention, identity, affect) becomes a thing (data, behavior, metric). Second nature operates.

### Passage 5.5 — Enlightenment and the "principle of immanence" (p.11)

PDFpage 31 → book p.11:

> "Enlightenment is mythical fear radicalized. The pure immanence of positivism, its ultimate
> product, is nothing other than a form of universal taboo. Nothing is allowed to remain outside,
> since the mere idea of the 'outside' is the real source of fear."

[VERBATIM — DoE p.11 / PDFpage 31]

**Evidence note:** Supports. The "principle of immanence" (everything is classifiable, nothing
outside) is structurally identical with the "no outside" that defines second nature at its limit.
The universal taboo against the outside = the made system prohibiting its own transcendence.
For the metaverse: its totalization enacts exactly this — the "mere idea of the outside"
(unmediated life, non-metaverse experience) becomes the source of anxiety, rather than refuge.

## 7. Synthesis: Does "Second Nature" Name the Made-Becoming-Self-Running-Given?

**Answer: Yes, with a critical asymmetry that Ch. IV must register.**

The passages above, taken together, establish:

**A. The structure** (what second nature IS):
The made — produced by human activity, historical contingency, social relations — acquires
the compulsion, anonymity, and apparent immutability of natural necessity. The mechanism:
(1) the functional context produces the result without individual intention or control;
(2) the result then acts back on individuals as an external constraint they cannot locate
    as their own product;
(3) the origin in making is concealed, and the product appears as "nature," as "always
    having been so," as fate rather than history.

This is precisely the *tekhnē→phusis* structure: the made (tekhnē) presents as the given (phusis).
Uegaki's 自足完成化 (self-completing) is one pole of this: the apparatus becomes self-sustaining,
no longer requiring the human will that originally set it in motion.

**B. The "no outside" criterion** (what totalizing second nature adds beyond ordinary making):
Passage 4.5 (ND p.357) gives the decisive formula: "there actually is nothing outside any more,
nothing unaffected by mediation, which is total." This is not just that the made is mistaken for
nature; it is that the made has *become* the total medium — everything is now processed through
it. The metaverse as universalizing-controllability mode of artificiality (Uegaki/Ch. IV) names
this: not merely a large artificial environment, but one that has colonized the *condition of
appearing* for experience, leaving no unmediated residue.

**C. The critical asymmetry** (where Adorno diverges from a simple tekhnē→phusis equation):
Adorno insists: "the second nature *remains the negation* of any nature that might be conceived
as the first" (ND p.357). It does not *become* first nature; it *usurps* first nature's insignia
while remaining its negation. This matters for Ch. IV: the metaverse does not actually become
natural (first-nature in the realist sense — independent of mind, pre-given). It *appears* as
such. The appearance is real in its effects; the metaphysical claim is ideological. Critical
theory's task is to hold both: the compulsion of second nature is real, AND it is a historical
product that could in principle be other. The squeeze hypothesis should honor this: the metaverse
squeezes the world not because it *is* natural but because it operates with the force of nature —
with the *Bann* (spell) of second nature.

**D. The myth-regression parallel** (DoE contribution):
DoE adds the temporal-logical dimension: this is not merely a sociological fact but a
philosophical fate — enlightenment always-already reverts to what it tried to overcome.
The myth it reproduces is: cyclical motion (repetition), fate (compulsion without exit),
domination as truth (the actual as necessary). These three are exactly the features of second
nature's self-presentation. The metaverse-as-second-nature thesis thus inherits the full weight
of A&H's dialectic: the most rationalized artificial environment enacts the most archaic mythic
structure — repetition, fate, domination naturalized.

**E. The souls-into-things chiasm** (DoE p.21 / Passage 5.4):
This passage is the most directly usable for Ch. IV's metaverse application. The reversal:
animism made things into souls → industrialism makes souls into things. In the metaverse
iteration: the digital infrastructure appears as a living world (souls attributed to avatars,
virtual spaces, algorithmic personalities), while actual human souls are rendered into things
(behavioral data, attention commodities, identity profiles). The chiasm is completed at one
remove — making it harder to perceive.
