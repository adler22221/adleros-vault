# Verbatim source-dossier PART C: the primary ancient canon (φύσις / τέχνη / νόμος)

**Compiled:** 2026-06-01 · **For:** [[master-thesis]] (Between the Universe and the Metaverse) · **Cluster C** (primary ancient canon + scholarly monographs)

**Relation to the existing dossier.** This file is the deep canonical backbone for the 2,500-year *phusis (given/self-arising) / technē (made/craft) / nomos (convention/law)* distinction. It is the companion to and EXTENSION of [[nomos-phusis-tekhne-verbatim-2026-05-30]] (which already holds Heidegger *QCT*, LSJ φύσις/τέχνη/νόμος, Preus, Brague, Bensaude-Vincent & von Staden, and the Floridi sub-dossier, plus Aristotle *Physics* II.1). PART C adds what that file did not have: **Aristotle *Physics* II.8 and II.2, *NE* VI.3–4, *Metaphysics* Z.7; Plato *Laws* X, *Gorgias*, *Protagoras*; Antiphon's *On Truth* primary fragment; Heidegger's dedicated *Physis* essay; and the Naddaf / Hadot / Collingwood / Lloyd monograph layer.** Aristotle *Physics* II.1 is re-quoted here in fuller form because the canon section needs it intact, and because the MIT II.1 text carries the **Antiphon "planted-bed" fragment** that anchors §A1 and §F.

> [!warning] Attribution guard (load-bearing — keep explicit)
> The author's two synthesis-claims — "**nomos has always been tekhne**" and "**tekhne is becoming phusis through the metaverse**" — are the **AUTHOR'S OWN**. No source below *asserts* either. What the ancient canon DOES supply, and what makes this dossier load-bearing, is that the phusis/technē boundary is **already porous in the canon itself, and porous in BOTH directions**:
> - **Aristotle *Physics* II.8** — technē *completes what phusis cannot finish* and *imitates* phusis (technē continuous with / modelled on phusis).
> - **Plato *Laws* X** — technē / nous / soul is *PRIOR to* phusis; "the great and primitive works… will be works of art… and after them will come nature." (This is the closest ancient anticipation of "technē grounding/becoming phusis.")
> These are not the author's claim, but they are the ancient hinges the author's claim swings on. Flagged in §A8 and §P1.

## PART A — ARISTOTLE

### A1. *Physics* II.1 (Bekker 192b8–193b) — the locus classicus of the phusis/technē boundary
**Source:** Aristotle, *Physics* II.1, trans. R. P. Hardie & R. K. Gaye. **Route:** MIT Internet Classics Archive, `https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/physics.2.ii.html` (HTTPS; full text), retrieved 2026-06-01.

> "Of things that exist, some exist by nature, some from other causes. 'By nature' the animals and their parts exist, and the plants and the simple bodies (earth, fire, air, water)—for we say that these and the like exist 'by nature'. All the things mentioned present a feature in which they differ from things which are not constituted by nature. Each of them has within itself a principle of motion and of stationariness (in respect of place, or of growth and decrease, or by way of alteration). On the other hand, a bed and a coat and anything else of that sort, qua receiving these designations i.e. in so far as they are products of art—have no innate impulse to change."
> — *Physics* II.1, 192b8–193a (Hardie & Gaye)

> "So it is with all other artificial products. None of them has in itself the source of its own production. But while in some cases (for instance houses and the other products of manual labour) that principle is in something else external to the thing, in others… it lies in the things themselves (but not in virtue of what they are). 'Nature' then is what has been stated. Things 'have a nature' which have a principle of this kind."
> — *Physics* II.1, 192b–193a (Hardie & Gaye)

**The Antiphon "planted bed" fragment, embedded in II.1 (193a12):**
> "Some identify the nature or substance of a natural object with that immediate constituent of it which taken by itself is without arrangement, e.g. the wood is the 'nature' of the bed, and the bronze the 'nature' of the statue. As an indication of this Antiphon points out that if you planted a bed and the rotting wood acquired the power of sending up a shoot, it would not be a bed that would come up, but wood—which shows that the arrangement in accordance with the rules of the art is merely an incidental attribute, whereas the real nature is the other, which, further, persists continuously through the process of making."
> — *Physics* II.1, 193a (Hardie & Gaye)

**Load-bearing:** the canonical statement of the wall — things *by nature* (φύσει) hold the principle of motion/rest **within themselves**; products of *art* (ἀπὸ τέχνης) have **no innate impulse to change**, their source lying **external**, in the maker. Antiphon's bed makes the point materially: the *technē-arrangement* (bed-form) is "merely an incidental attribute"; the *phusis* (wood) is what "persists continuously."

### A2. *Physics* II.2 (194a21) — "art imitates nature"
**Route:** same MIT HTTPS file.

> "But if on the other hand art imitates nature, and it is the part of the same discipline to know the form and the matter up to a point (e.g. the doctor has a knowledge of health and also of bile and phlegm, in which health is realized, and the builder both of the form of the house and of the matter, namely that it is bricks and [beams])…"
> — *Physics* II.2, 194a21–22 (Hardie & Gaye)

**Note:** the bare slogan **"art imitates nature"** (ἡ τέχνη μιμεῖται τὴν φύσιν) is here a premise about method, set up for the fuller treatment at II.8.

### A8. *Physics* II.8 (198b10–199b33) — ★ TECHNĒ COMPLETES AND IMITATES PHUSIS ★ (the keystone)
**Route:** same MIT HTTPS file, retrieved 2026-06-01. This is the single most load-bearing ancient passage for the porosity question.

The teleological thesis and the house-analogy:
> "Further, where a series has a completion, all the preceding steps are for the sake of that. Now surely as in intelligent action, so in nature; and as in nature, so it is in each action, if nothing interferes. Now intelligent action is for the sake of an end; therefore the nature of things also is so. Thus if a house, e.g. had been a thing made by nature, it would have been made in the same way as it is now by art; and if things made by nature were made also by art, they would come to be in the same way as by nature."
> — *Physics* II.8, 199a8–15 (Hardie & Gaye)

★ The keystone sentence:
> "Each step then in the series is for the sake of the next; and generally art partly completes what nature cannot bring to a finish, and partly imitates her. If, therefore, artificial products are for the sake of an end, so clearly also are natural products."
> — *Physics* II.8, 199a15–18 (Hardie & Gaye)

The ship-building art "in the wood":
> "It is absurd to suppose that purpose is not present because we do not observe the agent deliberating. Art does not deliberate. If the ship-building art were in the wood, it would produce the same results by nature. If, therefore, purpose is present in art, it is present also in nature. The best illustration is a doctor doctoring himself: nature is like that."
> — *Physics* II.8, 199b26–31 (Hardie & Gaye)

> [!important] Why A8 is the closest ancient hinge to the author's "technē becoming phusis"
> 1. **"art partly completes what nature cannot bring to a finish"** — technē is not merely *outside* phusis (as II.1's wall implies); it *finishes phusis's own unfinished work*. The made is a continuation of the given. This is the canonical warrant for treating the technē/phusis boundary as **functionally continuous**, not sealed.
> 2. **"and partly imitates her"** — technē takes phusis as its *model*; the two share the same teleological form ("for the sake of an end").
> 3. **"If the ship-building art were in the wood, it would produce the same results by nature."** — Aristotle's own counterfactual: were technē *internal to its matter* (the very mark that II.1 reserved for phusis), it would simply BE a natural process. The ONLY thing separating technē from phusis, on Aristotle's own statement, is the *externality of the principle of motion*. The author's claim that the metaverse-made is acquiring an internal, self-running principle is exactly the claim that this last barrier is being crossed. ⚠ Aristotle does NOT say the barrier is crossed; he uses the counterfactual to argue the *reverse* (that nature, like art, acts for an end). But the conditional itself is the hinge.

### A-NE. *Nicomachean Ethics* VI.3–4 (1139b–1140a) — technē vs epistēmē vs phronēsis
**Source:** Aristotle, *NE* VI, trans. W. D. Ross. **Route:** MIT Classics, `https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.6.vi.html` (HTTPS), retrieved 2026-06-01.

VI.3 — *epistēmē* (scientific knowledge) is of the necessary/eternal:
> "We all suppose that what we know is not even capable of being otherwise… Therefore the object of scientific knowledge is of necessity. Therefore it is eternal; for things that are of necessity in the unqualified sense are all eternal; and things that are eternal are ungenerated and imperishable."
> — *NE* VI.3, 1139b19–24 (Ross)

★ VI.4 — *technē* (art) is of the contingent, its origin in the maker, NOT in nature:
> "All art is concerned with coming into being, i.e. with contriving and considering how something may come into being which is capable of either being or not being, and whose origin is in the maker and not in the thing made; for art is concerned neither with things that are, or come into being, by necessity, nor with things that do so in accordance with nature (since these have their origin in themselves). Making and acting being different, art must be a matter of making, not of acting."
> — *NE* VI.4, 1140a10–17 (Ross)

> "Art, then… is a state concerned with making, involving a true course of reasoning."
> — *NE* VI.4, 1140a20–21 (Ross)

**Load-bearing:** the Ethics gives the cleanest verbatim of the technē/phusis contrast as an *origin* contrast — technē's products have their **"origin in the maker and not in the thing made,"** explicitly opposed to "things that… [come to be] in accordance with nature (since these have their origin in themselves)." This is the same en heautō / en allō distinction that Heidegger fastens on (§H). The author's claim targets precisely this "origin" criterion.

### A-Z. *Metaphysics* Z.7 (1032a12–b14) — by nature / by art / by chance; "the form is in the soul"
**Source:** Aristotle, *Metaphysics* VII (Z).7, trans. W. D. Ross. **Route:** MIT Classics, `https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/metaphysics.7.vii.html` (HTTPS), retrieved 2026-06-01.

The trichotomy of coming-to-be:
> "…all things produced either by nature or by art have matter; for each of them is capable both of being and of not being, and this capacity is the matter in each… Thus, then, are natural products produced; all other productions are called 'makings'. And all makings proceed either from art or from a faculty or from thought."
> — *Metaphysics* Z.7, 1032a20–28 (Ross)

★ "from art proceed the things of which the form is in the soul":
> "Some of them happen also spontaneously or by luck just as natural products sometimes do… but from art proceed the things of which the form is in the soul of the artist. (By form I mean the essence of each thing and its primary substance.)"
> — *Metaphysics* Z.7, 1032a32–b2 (Ross)

> "…the medical art and the building art are the form of health and of the house, and when I speak of substance without matter I mean the essence."
> — *Metaphysics* Z.7, 1032b13–14 (Ross)

**Load-bearing:** Z.7 sorts all genesis into *by nature* (internal source) vs *by art / faculty / thought* (the form pre-existing **"in the soul of the artist"**) vs *by chance*. The made's form is **prior, in a mind**; the natural's form is **in the thing itself**. Cf. Plato *Laws* X (§P1), which takes exactly this "form-in-soul priority" and runs it cosmically: if soul/art's form is prior, then art is prior to nature.

## PART P — PLATO

### P1. *Laws* X (888e–892c) — ★ TECHNĒ / NOUS / SOUL PRIOR TO PHUSIS ★ (the keystone)
**Source:** Plato, *Laws* Book X, trans. Benjamin Jowett. **Route:** MIT Classics, `https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/laws.10.x.html` (HTTPS, full text), retrieved 2026-06-01. Stephanus ≈ 888e–892c.

**(a) The materialist thesis the Athenian reports (888e–889e): the greatest things by phusis/tychē, the least by technē; and the nomos–physis antithesis stated.**
> "They say that fire and water, and earth and air, all exist by nature and chance, and none of them by art… After this fashion and in this manner the whole heaven has been created, and all that is in the heaven, as well as animals and all plants, and all the seasons come from these elements, not by the action of mind, as they say, or of any God, or from art, but as I was saying, by nature and chance only. Art sprang up afterwards and out of these, mortal and of mortal birth, and produced in play certain images and very partial imitations of the truth…"
> — *Laws* X, 889b–c (Jowett)

> "…also that legislation is entirely a work of art, and is based on assumptions which are not true."
> — *Laws* X, 889d (Jowett)

> "In the first place, my dear friend, these people would say that the Gods exist not by nature, but by art, and by the laws of states, which are different in different places, according to the agreement of those who make them; and that the honourable is one thing by nature and another thing by law, and that the principles of justice have no existence at all in nature…"
> — *Laws* X, 889e (Jowett)

**(b) ★ Plato's rebuttal (891e–892c): soul → mind → art → law are PRIOR to body and nature; "the great and primitive works… will be works of art."**
> "[They] do not know that she [the soul] is among the first of things, and before all bodies, and is the chief author of their changes and transpositions. And if this is true, and if the soul is older than the body, must not the things which are of the soul's kindred be of necessity prior to those which appertain to the body?"
> — *Laws* X, 892a (Jowett)

> "Then thought and attention and mind and art and law will be prior to that which is hard and soft and heavy and light; and the great and primitive works and actions will be works of art; they will be the first, and after them will come nature and works of nature, which however is a wrong term for men to apply to them; these will follow, and will be under the government of art and mind."
> — *Laws* X, 892b (Jowett)

> "Ath. Because those who use the term mean to say that nature is the first creative power; but if the soul turn out to be the primeval element, and not fire or air, then in the truest sense and beyond other things the soul may be said to exist by nature; and this would be true if you proved that the soul is older than the body, but not otherwise."
> — *Laws* X, 892c (Jowett)

> [!important] Why P1 is the closest ancient anticipation of "technē grounding / becoming phusis"
> Plato does NOT merely blur the line — he **inverts the priority**. Against the materialists (for whom phusis is "the first creative power" and technē a late, derivative imitation), Plato argues that **soul / mind / art / law are *prior*, and "nature" properly so-called *follows* them and is *governed by art and mind.*** Two moves matter for the thesis:
> 1. **"the great and primitive works and actions will be works of art; they will be the first, and after them will come nature"** — the order made→given is *reversed* into made-grounds-given. This is the ancient text that most nearly states "technē grounds phusis."
> 2. **"the soul may be said to exist *by nature*"** — that which is most properly *technē/nous*-like (soul) is here said to *be phusis* in the truest sense. The made-principle is re-described as the given-principle. ⚠ Plato's "art/soul" is divine cosmic nous, NOT human technē or a metaverse; and his "nature" is *demoted*, not *displaced by a new artificial layer*. The author's directional, metaverse-conditioned claim is his own. But the *form of the inversion* — made is prior to and grounds the given — is Platonic.

### P2. *Gorgias* (482e–484c) — Callicles: nomos vs physis (convention "at variance" with nature)
**Source:** Plato, *Gorgias*, trans. Benjamin Jowett. **Route:** Project Gutenberg eBook #1672, `https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/1672/pg1672.txt`, retrieved 2026-06-01. *(The MIT Classics Gorgias file is truncated mid-dialogue, before Callicles' speech; Gutenberg carries the complete Jowett.)*

The antithesis stated (482e):
> "…you are appealing now to the popular and vulgar notions of right, which are not natural, but only conventional. Convention and nature are generally at variance with one another…"
> — *Gorgias* 482e (Jowett)

Callicles' argument (483b–d): nomos is the weak many's instrument; physis says the stronger should rule:
> "The reason, as I conceive, is that the makers of laws are the majority who are weak; and they make laws and distribute praises and censures with a view to themselves and to their own interests; and they terrify the stronger sort of men, and those who are able to get the better of them, in order that they may not get the better of them…"
> — *Gorgias* 483b–c (Jowett)

> "…whereas nature herself intimates that it is just for the better to have more than the worse, the more powerful than the weaker; and in many ways she shows, among men as well as among animals, and indeed among whole cities and races, that justice consists in the superior ruling over and having more than the inferior."
> — *Gorgias* 483c–d (Jowett)

> "Nay, but these are the men who act according to nature; yes, by Heaven, and according to the law of nature: not, perhaps, according to that artificial law, which we invent and impose upon our fellows, of whom we take the best and strongest from their youth upwards, and tame them like young lions…"
> — *Gorgias* 483d–484a (Jowett)

**Load-bearing:** the *Gorgias* gives the antithesis in its sharpest moral form — **nomos = "that artificial law, which we invent and impose"** (the made / posited) vs **physis = "the law of nature"** (the given). Callicles weaponises it; for the thesis what matters is only that the canon explicitly frames nomos as the *humanly-invented-and-imposed*, i.e. on the *technē / made* side of the line. (This grounds the author's separate "nomos has always been tekhne" synthesis — but again, the *identity* claim is the author's; the canon supplies only the *made-side placement*.)

### P3. *Protagoras* (320c–322d) — the Prometheus myth: technē as compensation for natural lack
**Source:** Plato, *Protagoras*, trans. Benjamin Jowett. **Route:** MIT Classics, `https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/protagoras.html` (HTTPS, full text), retrieved 2026-06-01.

Man left "naked and shoeless" by deficient phusis:
> "Now while he was in this perplexity, Prometheus came to inspect the distribution, and he found that the other animals were suitably furnished, but that man alone was naked and shoeless, and had neither bed nor arms of defence."
> — *Protagoras* 321c (Jowett)

★ Technē (the mechanical arts + fire) stolen to remedy the lack:
> "…and Prometheus, not knowing how he could devise his salvation, stole the mechanical arts of Hephaestus and Athene, and fire with them (they could neither have been acquired nor used without fire), and gave them to man. Thus man had the wisdom necessary to the support of life, but political wisdom he had not…"
> — *Protagoras* 321d (Jowett)

> "Now man, having a share of the divine attributes… He was not long in inventing articulate speech and names; and he also constructed houses and clothes and shoes and beds, and drew sustenance from the earth."
> — *Protagoras* 322a (Jowett)

**Load-bearing:** the *Protagoras* myth makes technē **constitutive of the human** — humans are by *phusis* deficient (naked, unarmed) and survive *only* by *technē* (the stolen arts). Technē is not a luxury added to a self-sufficient nature; it is the *prosthesis* that completes a nature that is, on its own, not viable. This is the mythic correlate of *Physics* II.8's "art completes what nature cannot bring to a finish" (§A8), and the locus classicus behind the modern "technics as the human's originary supplement" reading (Stiegler — cf. the thesis's [[thesis_stiegler_obara_triangulation]] memory).

## PART ANT — ANTIPHON, *ON TRUTH* (the primary nomos/physis fragment)

### ANT1. *On Truth* (POxy 1364 = DK 87 B44), col. 1–2 — VERBATIM (Ostwald translation)
**Source:** Martin Ostwald, "Nomos and Phusis in Antiphon's *Peri Alêtheias*," in *Cabinet of the Muses: Rosenmeyer Festschrift* (Berkeley: Dept. of Classics, UCB, 1990), pp. 293–306. **Route:** OA PDF, UC eScholarship `https://escholarship.org/uc/item/7kg1w5zm`, pymupdf-extracted read-only, 2026-06-01. (Permalink stable; peer-reviewed.) The fragment is Ostwald's translation of POxy 1364, conventionally DK 87 B44 / fr. A DK.

★ The core nomos/physis statement (Col. 1):
> "So justice consists in not transgressing the regulations (nómima) of the city of which one is a citizen. Thus a human being is likely to use justice to his own best advantage, if he stresses the importance of the laws in the presence of witnesses, but when he is alone and there are no witnesses (follows) the dictates of nature (tà tês phúseōs). For the dictates of the laws (tà tôn nómōn) are adventitious, whereas the dictates of nature are inescapable; dictates of the laws, based on agreement as they are, are not natural growths, whereas the dictates of nature (tà tês phúseōs), being natural growths, are not based on agreement."
> — Antiphon, *On Truth*, POxy 1364 col. 1 (Ostwald 1990, p. ~294)

The escapable/inescapable contrast (Col. 2):
> "So he who transgresses the regulations… is free from shame and penalty, if he remains undetected by those who are a party to the agreement; but this is not the case if he is detected. However, if someone should try the impossible, namely to violate what is connate with nature, even if he remains undetected by all men, the adverse effect will be no less, and if all see him, it will be no greater. For he is not harmed through opinion but through truth."
> — Antiphon, *On Truth*, POxy 1364 col. 2 (Ostwald 1990)

Nomos as "inimical to nature" (Col. 2 close):
> "The reason for our inquiry into these matters is that the majority of things sanctioned as just in terms of the law are enactments inimical (polemíōs) to nature."
> — Antiphon, *On Truth*, POxy 1364 col. 2 (Ostwald 1990)

The "all equal by nature" fragment (Frag. B = POxy 1364 fr. B; IEP corroboration):
> "Those born of illustrious fathers we respect and honour, whereas those who come from an undistinguished house we neither respect nor honour. In this we behave like barbarians towards one another. For by nature we all equally, both barbarians and Greeks, have an entirely similar origin…"
> — Antiphon, *On Truth* (quoted in IEP, "The Sophists," §3.a; cf. Ostwald 1990 col. 2–3: "for by nature we are all equally equipped in every respect to be barbarians and Greeks")

**Load-bearing — the canonical "made vs given" definition:** Antiphon states the antithesis in its purest form: **nomos = "adventitious… based on agreement… not natural growths"** (humanly posited, escapable, by convention) vs **phusis = "inescapable… natural growths… not based on agreement"** (given, necessary, self-arising). This is THE primary-source warrant that *nomos is the made / agreed* and *phusis is the given / grown* — the foundation under the author's "nomos has always been tekhne" synthesis (the *made-side placement* is Antiphon's; the *identity with technē* is the author's).

### ANT2. Antiphon on technē *defeating* phusis (via Yuk Hui) — porosity in the made→given direction
**Source:** Yuk Hui, *The Question Concerning Technology in China* (Urbanos/Falmouth: Urbanomic, 2016), quoting Antiphon fr. 65 (Pendrick numbering). **Route:** NLM "A-Sources" notebook (`c68ca15e-5a07-4598-a5e0-dd68b9fa73b9`), `cited_text`, 2026-06-01.

> "Each *techne* is a remedy for overcoming chance occurrences (*tyche*) and errors that crop up in the process of making, as Antiphon says: 'we conquer by techne things that defeat us by physis'."
> — Yuk Hui, *The Question Concerning Technology in China*, quoting Antiphon

**Load-bearing:** a *second* Antiphon fragment, running the relation the *other* way — **technē as that which "conquers" what phusis "defeats us by."** Where ANT1 fixes the made/given line, ANT2 (in the same author) already has technē *overcoming* phusis. The porosity is intra-Antiphontine.

## PART H — HEIDEGGER (the dedicated *Physis* essay + *QCT* hinge)

> [!info] Route + honesty for §H
> Heidegger's **"On the Essence and Concept of Φύσις in Aristotle's *Physics* B, 1" (1939; in *Pathmarks*, ed. McNeill, Cambridge UP 1998; = *Wegmarken* / GA 9)** is **NOT on disk** and is **not a standalone source** in the NLM Heidegger notebook. > "Physis also, the arising of something from out of itself, is a bringing-forth, poiesis. Physis is indeed poiesis in the highest sense. > — Heidegger, *Introduction to Metaphysics*, quoted in Casey Rentmeester, *Heidegger and the Environment*, and in *Daoist Resonances in Heidegger* (NLM `cited_text`)

## PART F — FINDINGS: how close does the ancient canon itself come to porosity?

> [!important] The headline result for the thesis
> The phusis/technē boundary is **already porous in the canon, and the canon runs the relation in BOTH directions.** Three of the four "porosity" vectors the thesis needs are *ancient*; only the specific *metaverse-conditioned, asymmetric made→given substrate-formation* is the author's own.

| Vector | Ancient warrant (verbatim above) | Direction | Closeness to author's claim |
|---|---|---|---|
| **technē *completes* phusis** | Aristotle *Phys.* II.8 199a15–18: "art partly completes what nature cannot bring to a finish" | technē continues/finishes phusis | HIGH — the made as continuation of the given |
| **technē *imitates* phusis** | *Phys.* II.2 194a21; II.8 199a17: "and partly imitates her" | technē modelled on phusis | MED — shared teleological form |
| **technē *prior to / grounds* phusis** | ★ Plato *Laws* X 892b: "the great and primitive works… will be works of art… and after them will come nature… under the government of art and mind"; "the soul may be said to exist by nature" | technē/nous → phusis (inversion) | **HIGHEST** — the made grounds the given; the made re-described as the given |
| **technē *conquers/overcomes* phusis** | Antiphon (via Hui): "we conquer by techne things that defeat us by physis"; Hadot's "Promethean" forcing of nature | technē over phusis | MED — domination motif, not substrate-formation |
| **the only barrier is the *internal arche*** | Aristotle *NE* VI.4 1140a13 ("origin in the maker… not… in accordance with nature"); *Phys.* II.8 199b28 ("If the ship-building art were *in the wood*, it would produce the same results by nature"); Heidegger: artifact's "origin… 'outside' the thing made" | — | **the hinge**: the author's claim = the made acquiring an *internal* arche |

**What the canon does NOT give (stays the author's synthesis):**
1. **"Nomos has always been tekhne."** The canon (Antiphon §ANT1, *Gorgias* §P2, *Laws* X §P1a) firmly places **nomos on the made/agreed/posited side** ("adventitious… based on agreement… not natural growths"; "that artificial law, which we invent and impose"). That is the *made-side placement*. The **identity** of nomos with technē is NOT stated by any source — it is the author's reading of the shared made-side membership. ⚠ Keep this guard (esp. for 米/Mi on logical derivation).
2. **"Tekhne is becoming phusis through the metaverse."** The canon's closest move is **Plato *Laws* X's *inversion*** (technē/soul *prior to and grounding* phusis) — but Plato's "art" is divine cosmic *nous*, his "nature" is *demoted* (not displaced by a new artificial layer), and there is no temporal/technological *becoming*. Aristotle II.8 makes technē *continuous with* phusis but explicitly *denies* the made has the internal arche (199b28 is a counterfactual used to argue the *reverse*). So the *directional, metaverse-conditioned, substrate-forming* claim is the author's own — but it is now scaffolded by (a) the *continuity* warrant (II.8), (b) the *inversion-of-priority* warrant (*Laws* X), and (c) the precise statement of *the one remaining barrier* (the internal arche — NE VI.4, Heidegger).

**Dialectical framing for the committee (cf. 劉/Liu on scope):** present the thesis as completing a figure the canon already half-draws. The ancients ran **phusis→technē** in three ways (nature completed-by / imitated-by / conquered-by art) AND **technē→phusis** once, radically, in *Laws* X (art *grounds* nature). The thesis runs **technē→phusis** in a *new, fourth way*: the metaverse-made acquiring the *internal, self-running arche* that Aristotle and Heidegger reserved for the given. That is *argued* (directional, metaverse-conditioned); the long history of phusis/technē traffic is *mentioned* (the pedigree).
