## Part II Preface — 第二部のための序 (book pp. 81–84)

### Argumentative function

The preface bridges Part I (which diagnosed 〈生の自己完結化〉 and 〈生の脱身体化〉 and the "ghost" of autonomous selfhood) and Part II's constructive programme. Uegaki argues that resolving those diagnoses requires going back to the most radical question: "What kind of being is the human?" and attempting a "re-definition of the human" (「人の再定義」). The methodological answer is to explain human existence from its *outside* — through the relationship with the 〈環境〉 that surrounds it (「環境哲学」 approach).

The preface also frames the two chapters of Part II:

- **Ch. 3** — Establishes the basic framework: what is 〈環境〉 ontologically? How does human existence create the 人為的生態系 (artificial ecosystem) as 〈社会〉 on top of the natural ecosystem, living in that dual-layer 〈環境〉?
- **Ch. 4** — Takes the same framework and applies it to 7 million years of human history, arguing for a constant deep structure (the 二重構造) punctuated by transformative "singularities" (特異点).

The preface names three singularities in the history of that 人為的生態系: (1) the establishment of agriculture (~10,000 BP — 「〈人間〉と〈自然〉を間接化」); (2) the emergence of modern social forms (nation-state / market economy / fossil fuels — 「〈社会〉と〈自然〉の切断」); (3) the tentative current moment of 〈自己完結社会〉 as 「〈社会〉と〈人間〉の切断」 — the possible "third singularity." This third singularity will be the pivot to Ch. IV in the thesis.

## 第三章「人間存在と〈環境〉」 (book pp. 85–104) — Section-by-Section Arc

### §1 — なぜ人間学において〈環境〉が問題となるのか (book p. 85)

**Core move**: The common conception of "environment" (as pristine nature / pollution / ecology) is historically recent — a product of the 1960s–70s environmentalism that redefined 環境 as "nature to be protected." Uegaki proposes returning to the original meaning: 「めぐりとりまく外界」 — the outer world *as it surrounds a subject*. This original meaning carries no value-loading. It is this pre-ecological, subject-oriented sense that the 環境哲学 approach exploits.

The question therefore becomes: why explain human existence through this relationship to the surrounding 〈環境〉? The answer will unfold across the chapter.

[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 85]

### §2 — 主体によって定義され、存在を成立させる基盤となる〈環境〉の概念 (book pp. 86–89)

**Argument**: From the basic meaning ("outer world surrounding a subject"), Uegaki derives an *ontological* 〈環境〉 concept: an environment defined by a particular subject in a particular way, and simultaneously the *ground* on which that subject's existence is constituted. The philosophical method called 環境哲学 is precisely this.

The key contrast is with what he calls the 「素朴実在論的環境概念」 — naive-realist environmentalism that posits a single objective environment equally present to all subjects. Uegaki rejects this.

**Evidence via thought experiment** (book p. 87–88): Suppose a substance X toxic to all current life is massively released and wipes out most organisms. After a billion years, new life evolves that requires X. Would X's spread have been "environmental destruction" for *them*? Answer: no. The environment's content is entirely determined by *what subject is assumed*. Environment is always "environment for something" (「何ものかにとっての環境」). A universally shared environment is, strictly speaking, inconceivable.

**Uexküll's Umwelt** (book pp. 88–89): To give this precision, Uegaki turns to J. von Uexküll's 「環世界」 (Umwelt) concept. Each organism lives in its own entirely distinct 環世界. The tick (マダニ) example: only butyric acid, warmth, and impact force — no vision, no hearing. This *is* the tick's world. The lesson: an organism's 〈環境〉 is the outer world *reconstructed* through the subject's perceptual capacities and the meanings it finds there.

[VERIFY-PAGE: book pp. 86–89]

**Additional dimension — habitat (ハビタット)** (book pp. 89–90): Uegaki adds a second dimension from zoologist Kohara Hideo's 「ハビタット」 concept — the organism's "broad dwelling-place," equivalent to ecological niche. Kohara argues the ハビタット expresses the organism's *mode of existence*: it is the product of millions of years of natural selection in which the organism's body evolved to fit a specific position in nature. If a Japanese flying squirrel (ムササビ) is raised in a bottle with no height to glide from, it cannot express its ムササビ-nature — it exists as a biological body but fails to actualize its existence. Conclusion: 〈環境〉 is not only *defined by* the subject but simultaneously the *ground required for the subject's existence to be fully actualized*. Without the appropriate 〈環境〉, the organism's being remains incomplete.

[VERIFY-PAGE: book pp. 89–90]

### §3 — 「人間的〈環境〉」における〈環境〉の「二重構造」 (book pp. 89–95)

**Transition problem**: If humans are also biological beings, they too must have a proper 〈環境〉 that defines and grounds them. But the Western philosophical tradition (especially Aristotle's rational soul; Scheler's "world-openness" [weltoffen]) typically finds human essence in the *transcendence* of the environmental determination that other organisms live within. Scheler: other organisms are immersed in their Umwelt, but humans alone have *Geist* (精神 / 用神 in OCR) that can inhibit reflex drives, objectify and analyze rationally, and thereby achieve freedom from the Umwelt.

**Uegaki's counter-move** (book pp. 91–92): Neuroscience and evolutionary psychology show that cognition and consciousness have a clear genetic/biological basis — our world too is a reconstruction from highly limited perceptual input, and even our "Geist" is a finite bodily mechanism acquired through evolution. This does *not* mean humans lack specificity; but that specificity must be found *within* human 環世界, not as escape from it. Human uniqueness is an *immanent structure* within the 「人間的〈環境〉」.

**The key claim via Kohara** (book pp. 91–93): Kohara observes that the human's ecological position is qualitatively different from all other animals: humans have created an "artificial world, a socialized special position 'within nature' of their own making." [VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 91-92, Kohara quote]. Humans have built a second "habitat" — an accumulation of artificial things (「モノ」の集積体), which Uegaki calls the 「人為的生態系」 (artificial ecosystem). So the human's 〈環境〉 has a **dual structure** (二重構造): (1) the natural ecosystem in which human bodies are embedded as organisms; (2) the 人為的生態系 superimposed on top.

**Concretization of the dual structure** (book pp. 92–93): The example of cultivated farmland (耕地): a paddy or field is genuinely both natural ecosystem (the soil's native biota) and artificial ecosystem (a special, human-maintained food web). Even a "modern city" — seemingly all artificial — still has mammals, insects, underground biota. Even beyond the city, the traditional 里山 and 奥山 show gradations of human intervention. The 二重構造 is not a binary ("natural" vs. "social" environment) but a continuum of artificial-ecosystem *density* (濃淡) diffused throughout the entire domain of human existence.

**Why "ecosystem"**: The 人為的生態系 is not merely a pile of artefacts. For the human subject, those artefacts stand in relational order: a tool (道具) makes sense only within the ordered whole of a 集落's layout. This ordered totality constitutes its own kind of 環世界 for humans — a second Umwelt that non-human animals cannot access (for them, the same artefacts are just raw material). [VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 93]

**Social construction and intergenerational transmission** (book pp. 93–94): The 社会的構造物 (the material side of the 人為的生態系) is created by thousands of generations. For any specific subject, it presents itself *as a pre-existing 〈環境〉* — an environment that precedes the subject's existence while simultaneously being the target of that subject's modifications. Each generation's modifications become the next generation's constitutive 〈環境〉. The 社会的構造物 thus acquires a quasi-independent reality beyond individual biological bodies.

[VERIFY-PAGE: book pp. 89–95]

### §4 — 「人為的生態系」の"物質的側面"と"非物質的側面" (book pp. 91–95, plate image p. 95)

**Problem**: So far the 人為的生態系 has been analyzed from its **material side** only — physical constructions, tools, farmland, architecture. But there are non-material things that also function as 〈環境〉 in exactly the same structural way: created by humans, transmitted across generations, preceding and constituting individuals.

Uegaki identifies **two non-material dimensions**:

1. **社会的制度** (Social institutions / social apparatus) [book pp. 93–94]: Mechanisms that organically coordinate the consequences of individuals' actions into collective functional outcomes. The market economy: each actor maximizes private interest; the aggregate achieves efficient resource distribution. The bureaucratic apparatus (官僚機構): each person executes assigned duties; the aggregate achieves organizational goals. Also: caste systems, pre-modern organized division of labor. Theorized by Durkheim (division of labor) and Weber (bureaucracy). These are what is commonly called "social systems," representing this dimension of the 人間的〈環境〉.

   [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp. 93–94]

2. **意味体系=世界像** (Meaning system = world-image / Weltbild) [book pp. 94–95]: The conceptual and semantic bedrock through which we understand and recognize things — the "framework of interpretation." Every object is grasped in terms of meaning: a cup is for drinking, a Buddha image expresses Buddhist faith, a meeting is for collective decision-making, a promise is for binding future action. These meanings interconnect and present the world as *one image* (世界像). Uegaki associates this with Heidegger's 「世界内存在」, Husserl/Schutz's 「生活世界」 (Lebenswelt). We can grasp anything as "meaningful" only because we have already *internalized* such a 意味体系=世界像.

   [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp. 94–95]

**Figure 2** (book p. 95): The structure of 「人間的〈環境〉」 is diagrammed as:
- 自然生態系
  - 「人為的生態系」 containing: 社会的構造物 / 社会的制度 / 意味体系=世界像
    - → these are also labeled as 「自然的環世界」 (natural Umwelt) and 「人為的環世界」 (artificial Umwelt)

[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 95 — figure]

### §5 — 「人間的〈環境〉」における特異な構造としての〈社会〉 (book pp. 95–104)

**Terminological consolidation** (book pp. 95–96): Uegaki now relabels: the two 環世界 layers are called 「自然的環世界」 (natural Umwelt) and 「人為的環世界」 (artificial Umwelt). The 人為的環世界 consists of the material 社会的構造物 plus the non-material 社会的制度 and 意味体系=世界像. These three are deeply interconnected: 社会的制度 and 意味体系=世界像 give physical 社会的構造物 its orderly/meaningful form; conversely, 社会的構造物 gives non-material institutions and meaning-systems their material embodiment. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp. 95–96]

**Naming move**: What had been called 人為的生態系 / 人為的環世界 is now renamed 〈**社会**〉 (Society with chevrons). This is an unusual usage: the word 社会 comes from "fellows" (仲間), and common usage refers merely to a group or community. But Uegaki recovers the sociological tradition (Durkheim, Weber, Marx) that saw "the social" as a structure independent of individual actors, with quasi-substantive reality, shaping individuals' modes of existence. The thesis: what that tradition studied as "the social" *is* the 人為的生態系 = 人為的環世界. Thus: **humans are the beings who create 〈社会〉 as an 人為的生態系 on the surface of the natural ecosystem and live within that dual 〈環境〉**. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp. 96–97]

**Comparative zoology** (book pp. 97–99): Is this 〈環境〉-character truly unique to humans? Uegaki surveys five groups of "social animals": colonial invertebrates (e.g. jellyfish, coral — genetically identical, quasi-single-organism); eusocial insects (ants, bees — queen + sterile workers, biologically "non-individuated"); birds and non-human mammals (genuine individuality + communication-based collective action — shared with humans); and humans. The first two are "de-individuated" — their sociality is biologically determined at the collective-body level, not a separately constructed environment. Birds and mammals show genuine individuality plus coordinated action, and their tools and nests have structural resemblances to 社会的構造物; some birdsong transmitted across generations resembles 意味体系=世界像. But these are limited: tools and nests do not accumulate into a multigenerational 環世界, and birdsong lacks a material substratum. The *root difference* is qualitative: humans alone create a 人為的生態系 that (a) encompasses both material and non-material dimensions, (b) functions as a genuinely quasi-independent entity (実体), and (c) is transmitted without interruption across generations. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp. 97–100]

**Final definition** (book p. 100): The human is: "the living being that creates 〈社会〉 as an 人為的生態系 on the surface of the natural ecosystem and lives within that dual 〈環境〉, and that — unlike other social animals — transmits this endlessly to successive generations." [VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 100 — paraphrase, not verbatim]

### Key tension / divergence to flag for Ch. IV

The thesis's Umwelt-scheme (from Ch. I) maps 環世界 = world = human meaning-space *nested within* the universe (Umgebung). Uegaki uses 環世界 in a *richer* sense — he distinguishes:

- 自然的環世界 (the natural Umwelt, corresponding to the thesis's implicit biophysical layer)
- 人為的環世界 (the artificial Umwelt, corresponding to the thesis's world / meaning-space)

For the thesis, this creates both a resource and a consistency challenge:

**Resource**: Uegaki's 二重構造 directly supports the squeeze thesis's claim that human existence is constituted between a natural substrate (universe) and an artificial layer. The 人為的生態系 → 〈社会〉 progression provides precise vocabulary for what the thesis calls the "human-constructed meaning-space" (world).

**Consistency challenge**: In the thesis, the "world" is consistently equated with the Umwelt (環世界) as human meaning-space — but Uegaki's framework identifies *two* 環世界 layers, one natural and one artificial. The thesis's Ch. I–III usage aligns most closely with Uegaki's 人為的環世界 only. The thesis does not explicitly assign a separate "natural Umwelt" designation, instead treating the universe as Umgebung (the outer surroundings). This is compatible but not identical.

**For Ch. IV**: When deploying Uegaki's 人為的生態系 → 〈社会〉 in the metaverse argument, the thesis should note: the metaverse represents a *third layer* on top of Uegaki's dual structure — a 〈社会〉 that progressively de-grounds itself from both the natural ecosystem *and* the biological substrate. Uegaki's Ch. 4 (preface, singularities 1–3) already anticipates this: the "third singularity" is the moment 〈社会〉 loses coherence with human existence itself, not just with nature. This maps directly onto the thesis's 自己完結化 argument.

**On 環境 vs. 環世界 in Uegaki**: Uegaki uses 〈環境〉 as the master ontological concept (outer world as constitutive ground), and 環世界 as the species-specific instantiation of that 〈環境〉. The thesis should be careful not to conflate Uegaki's 環世界 with the thesis's "world" directly, since Uegaki's 環世界 encompasses both natural and artificial dimensions while the thesis's "world" (世界) is restricted to the meaning/idealist layer.

## Relevance to Ch. IV §C (Metaverse and 自己完結化)

1. **〈社会〉 as 人為的環世界**: Uegaki provides the most precise theoretical grounding for why the thesis's artificial layer (the 人為的生態系) is not merely a social construct but a genuine *constitutive environment* — the ground without which humans cannot fully actualize their existence. This strengthens the claim that the metaverse's self-completion is not merely a cultural shift but an existential displacement.

2. **三成分 (social structures / institutions / meaning-systems)**: The thesis's Ch. IV can use this triple to specify *which* dimension of the 人為的環世界 the metaverse targets: primarily the 意味体系=世界像 and 社会的制度, with the material 社会的構造物 increasingly becoming digital infrastructure. This maps onto the thesis's distinction between material and non-material artificiality.

3. **Intergenerational transmission and quasi-independence**: Uegaki's insistence that 〈社会〉 acquires quasi-independent reality because it is transmitted across generations is directly relevant to the thesis's claim that the metaverse constitutes a self-completing system — it is the first 人為的生態系 designed to fully close the feedback loop (self-finish/自己完結化) rather than remain open to intergenerational modification.

4. **The third singularity (Preface)**: The preface names 〈社会〉 losing coherence with human existence as a "third singularity" — this is the most direct citation point for the thesis's Ch. IV claim about the metaverse as a qualitative break from the prior history of the 人為的生態系.

5. **Scheler counter-argument as foil**: The Scheler discussion (humans as "weltoffen," transcending Umwelt) is a useful foil for Ch. IV — the metaverse appears to be the technological instantiation of the Schelerian fantasy: an environment so complete that it seems to liberate humans from biophysical limitation. Uegaki's critique (Scheler is wrong; humans remain within their 環世界) suggests the metaverse's "freedom" is structurally illusory.

## Brief Summary (5–8 bullets)

- Uegaki opens with an etymological-conceptual recovery: 環境 is not "nature to protect" but the subject-constituted outer world — an ontological 〈環境〉 defined by a specific subject that simultaneously grounds that subject's existence. The Uexküll 環世界 gives this precision; Kohara's ハビタット adds that without the appropriate 〈環境〉 an organism cannot actualize its existence.
- Human specificity lies not in transcending the Umwelt (contra Scheler / Aristotle) but in having a *dual* 〈環境〉: the natural ecosystem (自然的環世界) plus the 人為的生態系 they create on top of it (人為的環世界). The 二重構造 is the definitive structural feature of human existence.
- The 人為的生態系 comprises three components: 社会的構造物 (material), 社会的制度 (institutional), 意味体系=世界像 (semantic/conceptual). These are mutually constitutive: material forms require meaning to be "orderly"; non-material institutions require material embodiment.
- The 人為的生態系 acquires quasi-independent status because it is built across thousands of generations: for any individual, it is a pre-given 〈環境〉; yet that individual modifies it, and the modification becomes the next generation's ground. This produces an entity with its own momentum beyond individual biology.
- Uegaki relabels this whole structure 〈社会〉 — not in the everyday sense of "group" but in the sociological sense of "the social" as an independent quasi-entity structuring human existence (Durkheim / Weber / Marx). Humans are defined as: creators of multigenerational 人為的生態系 = 〈社会〉, living within the dual 〈環境〉 this creates.
- Cross-thesis: Uegaki's 自然的環世界 maps onto the thesis's universe/Umgebung layer; his 人為的環世界 / 意味体系=世界像 maps onto the thesis's world/Umwelt layer. The thesis needs to be precise: it uses 環世界 to name the human meaning-world (= Uegaki's 人為的環世界 only), not Uegaki's full dual structure.
- Most direct Ch. IV use: the preface's "third singularity" (〈社会〉 losing coherence with human existence itself) is the theoretical anchor for the metaverse-as-自己完結化 argument. The 人為的生態系's three components also provide vocabulary to specify which layer the metaverse colonizes.
- One terminological risk: Uegaki's 人為的生態系 covers human social reality broadly (agriculture, cities, meaning-systems). The thesis's "metaverse" is a historically specific phase of that ecosystem. Ch. IV §C should be explicit that the metaverse is Uegaki's 人為的生態系 undergone 自己完結化 — not a replacement of it.
