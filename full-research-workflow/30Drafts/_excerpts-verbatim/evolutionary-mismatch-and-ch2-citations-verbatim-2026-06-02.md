# TARGET A — Canonical evolutionary-mismatch sources

> **KEY METHODOLOGICAL FINDING for the thesis.** Across *every* canonical definition retrieved, evolutionary mismatch is pinned to **(a) TRAITS** (phenotypes / adaptations) and **(b) a change of ENVIRONMENT** that is explicitly read as **both temporal (diachronic) AND spatial (migration)** — never as a synchronic, triadic, or principle-layer relation. This is exactly the boundary the "squeeze" aims to generalise. The single cleanest anchor is **Lloyd et al. 2011, p. 4** (A4 below): *"a negative consequence that results from a trait that evolved in one environment being placed in another environment."* The clearest temporal-vs-spatial statement is **Bourrat & Griffiths 2021, p. 1** (A1).

## A4 — Lloyd, Wilson & Sober (2011), "Evolutionary Mismatch and What to Do About It: A Basic Tutorial"
Elisabeth A. Lloyd, David Sloan Wilson & Elliott Sober (2011), Evolution Institute. (An updated version exists: **Lloyd, Rao, Wilson & Sober (2024), "Evolutionary Mismatch and How to Evaluate It: A Basic Tutorial"**, prosocial.world.)
**Route:** (definition) verbatim *as quoted in* Bourrat & Griffiths 2021, p. 16; (typology) WebFetch of the 2024 updated tutorial at prosocial.world.

> ⚠ Full text of the **2011** PDF is not freely hosted (Semantic Scholar / Scribd only; Evolution Institute migrated to prosocial.world). The definition below is the authoritative *verbatim with page number* as reproduced in Bourrat & Griffiths; the typology is from the **2024** successor tutorial (note the year when citing the typology).

**THE definition — explicitly trait-based, environment-to-environment (the cleanest anchor for the thesis):**
> "An evolutionary mismatch can be defined as a negative consequence that results from a trait that evolved in one environment being placed in another environment."
— Lloyd et al. [2011], p. 4, **emphasis in original**, *as quoted in* Bourrat & Griffiths 2021, p. 16.

**Typology of mismatch (from the 2024 successor tutorial; verbatim def + the spatial-mismatch example):**
> "a state of disequilibrium whereby an organism that evolved in one environment develops a phenotype that is harmful to its fitness or well-being in another environment."
— Lloyd, Rao, Wilson & Sober (2024).

> [Spatial mismatch, verbatim example]: "a species of aquatic insect ... sometimes the insects live in ponds that have fish in them (which is disastrous for the insects) while at other times they live in ponds that lack fish."
— Lloyd et al. (2024).

Types named (2024): **temporal mismatch** (traits adaptive in ancestral E1 become maladaptive as conditions change over time); **spatial mismatch** (individual-level misfit without population-level temporal change — the aquatic-insect/fish case); **developmental mismatch** (Romanian-orphanage exemplar); **phenotypic-plasticity mismatch** (Dutch-famine fetal-programming exemplar).

## A5 — Cofnas (2016), "A teleofunctional account of evolutionary mismatch"
Nathan Cofnas (2016), *Biology & Philosophy* 31:507–525.
**Route:** verbatim *as quoted in* Bourrat & Griffiths 2021, pp. 16–17. (Primary article not separately fetched; B&G quote it with page numbers.)

**Cofnas's definition (note: explicitly about TRAITS / their selected effects):**
> "[Cofnas] 'defines mismatch as deviations in the environment that render biological traits unable, or impaired in their ability, to produce their selected effects' (Cofnas [2016], p. 507). By 'selected effects' Cofnas means the effects for which the trait is an adaptation."
— Bourrat & Griffiths 2021, p. 16.

**Cofnas's mismatch = "novelty"; the mouse/cat thought-experiment (mismatch need not be harmful):**
> "Evolutionary novelty is defined as deviations in the environment that prevent biological traits from performing, or impair their ability to perform, such functions. Such impairment is not necessarily bad for organisms from the standpoint of either fitness or welfare. Mice may possess mechanisms the proper function of which is to escape from cats. These mechanisms do not perform these functions in cat-free environments, but cat-free environments are good for mice."
— Cofnas [2016], pp. 510–11, *as quoted in* Bourrat & Griffiths 2021, p. 16.

**Cofnas's complaint (which B&G reject):**
> "scientists have generally been working without a clear definition of mismatch."
— Cofnas [2016], pp. 507–8, *as quoted in* Bourrat & Griffiths 2021, pp. 5, 16.

## A7 — Other canonical definitions (all trait + environment-change), verbatim via Bourrat & Griffiths 2021

**Textbook definition (Stearns & Medzhitov 2015):**
> "[m]ismatch occurs when organisms that were well adapted to one set of environmental circumstances cannot evolve rapidly enough to adapt to a new set of circumstances."
— Stearns & Medzhitov [2015], p. 13, *as quoted in* Bourrat & Griffiths 2021, p. 2.

**Nutritional-ecology definition (Raubenheimer, Simpson et al. 2012) — the one B&G endorse:**
> "Mismatch occurs when the timescale and/or magnitude of environmental change exceeds the combined capacity of adaptation owing to homeostatic mechanisms, phenotypic plasticity and transgenerational adaptation."
— Raubenheimer et al. [2012], p. 1641, *as quoted in* Bourrat & Griffiths 2021, pp. 11, 16.

**Behavioural ecology — mismatch ≡ adaptive lag (Laland & Brown 2006):**
> "it is a truism that all organisms must experience some adaptive lag, here meaning a mismatch between current selection pressures and behavior."
— Laland & Brown [2006], p. 98, *as quoted in* Bourrat & Griffiths 2021, pp. 1–2.

**Morris (2018) — optimal-environment definition (for completeness):**
> "CC: evolutionary mismatch obtains when an organism O is in an actual environment Ea such that O's fitness is lower than it would be in an optimal environment Emf."
— Morris [2018], *as quoted in* Bourrat & Griffiths 2021, p. 15.

### A3-RESOLVED — Gluckman, Peter & Mark Hanson, *Mismatch: Why Our World No Longer Fits Our Bodies* (Oxford University Press, 2006)

**THE headline definition — the "mismatch paradigm" (book's thesis), trait/"design" vs environment:**
> "So a species thrives if it lives in an environment for which its 'design' matches it. The greater the degree of mismatch between environment and design, the greater the cost. In this book we call this the mismatch paradigm, and the cost of such mismatch is often disease, just as the cost to the Sherpa of inhabiting the high Himalayan valleys was goitre and cretinism."
— Gluckman & Hanson 2006, **p. 7** (phys p.20). [route: libgen.li PDF → pymupdf]

**The paradigm restated, with degrees of match/mismatch (section "The mismatch paradigm"):**
> "A match does not have to be 'all or nothing'—there are degrees of match and degrees of mismatch ... The greater the degree of match between an organism's constitution and its environment, the more likely the organism is to thrive; the greater the degree of mismatch the more the organism has to adapt or cope. This incurs costs and these rise as the degree of mismatch increases. If the organism cannot cope at all, then the consequences will be a greater risk of disease or death."
— Gluckman & Hanson 2006, **pp. 11–12** (phys p.24–25). [route: libgen.li PDF → pymupdf]

**The TEMPORAL-vs-SPATIAL / organism-vs-environment distinction (thesis-critical — mismatch arises from change on either side):**
> "Mismatch can be created by changes in either the organism or its environment. It might arise for example as a result of a mutation in a gene essential for the organism's healthy life in that environment. The disease of lactose intolerance results from a mutation in the gene responsible for synthesizing the enzyme lactase. ... Alternatively mismatch can arise from the environment changing rapidly or drastically. Sailors in the eighteenth century had a high chance of dying on long voyages from scurvy ... [because] the sailors' nutritional environment had changed from one where there was some fresh food to one in which all food had to be dried or salted."
— Gluckman & Hanson 2006, **pp. 11–12** (phys p.25). [route: libgen.li PDF → pymupdf]

**Mismatch as the modern human condition (book's payoff claim):**
> "Many humans now live in environments to which they are not well matched and for many the degree of mismatch is increasing. Mismatch occurs in everyday existence in seemingly ordinary environments. And mismatch has a cost—it turns out to be a major determinant of our social structure, health, and quality of life. Understanding the mismatch paradigm gives us a new perspective on what we are."
— Gluckman & Hanson 2006, **pp. 12–13** (phys p.25–26). [route: libgen.li PDF → pymupdf]
