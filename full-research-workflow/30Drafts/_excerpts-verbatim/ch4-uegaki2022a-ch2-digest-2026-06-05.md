## A. Section-by-Section Argument Arc

### (1) 〈自立した個人〉というイデオロギー (pp. 67–69)
**Framing move.** Chapter 2 takes up where Chapter 1 left off: having described the current states of 〈生の自己完結化〉 (self-finishing of life) and 〈生の脱身体化〉 (disembodiment of life), Uegaki now asks what human ideal made those states possible — and what it means that this ideal has collapsed into a "ghost" (亡霊).

The ideal in question is the 〈自立した個人〉 — defined as: the anthropological position that locates the essence of the human being in the individual, and holds as the ultimate ideal a state in which each person achieves 意志の自律 (autonomy of will) through complete self-judgment (自己判断) and self-determination (自己決定), unconstrained by any external authority.

[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 67–68] Definition passage: 〈自立した個人〉とは人間の本質を個人に見いだし、それぞれの個人が何ものにもとらわれることなく、十全な自己判断/自己決定を通じて意志の自律を達成しているという人間の状態…ひとつの人間学でありイデオロギーとして定義される。

Uegaki traces the genealogy of this ideology in Japan across three historical moments:

1. **Meiji (Fukuzawa Yukichi):** 一身独立して一国独立する — independence of the individual as the precondition for national independence against foreign powers. [VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 68 footnote 2 cites Fukuzawa 1942:33]

2. **Post-war (Maruyama Masao):** After Japan's defeat, Maruyama diagnoses the catastrophe as resulting from a "system of irresponsibility" (無責任の体系), rooted in the absence of ethical subjectivity. His prescription: the Japanese must reconstitute themselves as individuals — as subjects of free cognition, ethical responsibility, and order-formation. This move hardens the binary: "pre-modern" communal world (克服されるべき前近代的人間世界) vs. the "new human world of self-reliant individuals." [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp. 68–69, citing Maruyama 1961]

3. **High-growth era resonance:** The ideology gained extraordinary persuasive power because it aligned perfectly with the lived experience of the high-growth generation — who actually did move from an "old world" (故郷/parents/community) to a "new world" (urban, free, youthful). The material expansion of the 20th century made the promise of the self-reliant individual seem credible.

**Key claim at close of §1:** By today, the 〈自立した個人〉 has become another "ghost" (亡霊) of the "era without ideals" — surviving after the 20th-century world that sustained it has dissolved, perpetuated through channels such as opposition to totalitarianism, the rhetoric of "the irreplaceable individual," and critical theory's project of liberation from ontological oppression.

### (2) 根源的な矛盾① ——約束されたシナリオと「20世紀」(pp. 70–71)
**The three conditions and the promised scenario.**

The ideology of 〈自立した個人〉 presupposed three necessary conditions for individual 自立 (self-reliance/independence):

- **Condition 1:** Social constraints and coercive forces that impede self-judgment/self-determination must be removed.
- **Condition 2:** The conditions for self-judgment/self-determination must be distributed equally.
- **Condition 3:** The individual must mature such that their self-judgment/self-determination embodies harmony with the freedom of others and with the public good — 「各人のため」と「万人のため」が一致する、より高級な自由 (the higher freedom in which "for each person" and "for all persons" coincide), expressed as 積極的自由 (positive freedom) or true 意志の自律.

[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 70] Three conditions passage: 個人の「自立」のためには、①自己判断/自己決定の障害となる社会的制約および強制力は取り除かれなければならないこと、②自己判断/自己決定を行う条件は平等に与えられなくてはならないこと、③…個人は成熟しなければならない.

**The promised scenario:** Conditions 1 and 2 would be achieved first (through market expansion, welfare provision, and repeated social messaging to "stand on your own"), and once achieved, Condition 3 would follow naturally through individual cultivation.

**The 20th-century matrix:** This scenario worked — appeared to work — only because it was embedded in the assumption of limitless progress and development. As long as people believed themselves to be "on the way" toward the great project, the gap between the ideal and reality could be deferred as a mere "waystation" (通過点). E. Fromm's warning against "escape from freedom" is cited as structurally parallel: just as Fromm argued we must not retreat to an idealized past despite freedom's loneliness, so the ideology perpetuated itself by treating all disconfirming evidence as temporary insufficiency.

**The contradiction:** Conditions 1 and 2 were substantially achieved — primarily through the market economy and the expansion of administrative services. But Condition 3 never arrived. The more the space for self-determination expanded, the less it produced the higher synthesis of individual and collective freedom. This gap was routinely explained away as "incomplete implementation" (条件整備の不徹底), leading to an "infinite regress" (無限の循環構造) — ever-more-refined versions of Conditions 1 and 2, and perpetual deferral of Condition 3.

When 20th-century growth stopped and administrative expansion became impossible, this regress inevitably hit a wall.

### (3) 根源的な矛盾② ——〈生の自己完結化〉および〈生の脱身体化〉の"写像"としての個人の「自立」(pp. 71–75)

**The deeper, structural contradiction: the 写像 (mapping/mirror-image) argument.**

This is the chapter's most original and theoretically productive section for the thesis.

The true problem, Uegaki argues, is not the one commonly recognized (the "paradox of freedom" — liberated individuals turning into self-interested private actors). That paradox was already well-known and was incorporated back into the "scenario" as evidence of the continued need to perfect Condition 1 and 2. The deeper problem is a structural coincidence:

> **The refinement of conditions for individual 自立 (self-reliance) and the process of 〈生の自己完結化〉 and 〈生の脱身体化〉 are, in reality, two sides of the same coin — different angles on the same phenomenon.**

[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 72] 写像 argument passage: 「自立」のための条件となる人間存在の"解放"とは、見方を変えれば、そのまま生きることに直接的な他者との関係性を必要としなくなる〈生の自己完結化〉、そして生の前提から身体を持つことの意味が欠落していく〈生の脱身体化〉と地続きの関係にある。

In other words: the "liberation" that enables individual 自立 is, when viewed from another angle, precisely the condition in which one no longer needs direct relations with embodied others (= self-finishing), and in which the necessity of having a body progressively drops out of the premises of life (= disembodiment). The individual's 「自立」 is the *mirror image* (写像) — the individual-level projection — of these two social-structural processes.

**The true paradox:** The more single-mindedly we pursue the ideal of 〈自立した個人〉, the more our lives are driven into the "infinite circulation structure" of 〈関係性の病理〉 (pathology of relations) and 〈生の混乱〉 (life-disorder), not out of them.

**Why the ideology cannot see this:** 〈自立した個人〉's anthropology is fundamentally committed to the "unlimited liberation" (無限の解放) of the human being. This commitment makes it impossible to accurately perceive the human reality we are facing, and causes it to systematically misinterpret the meaning of contemporary people's confusion about relationships and their anxiety about living.

**The "alienation" detour (疎外論):** A major variant of the ideology tried to solve the "freedom paradox" by attributing the failure of Condition 3 to the existence of the 〈社会的装置〉 — specifically the market economy — as a new form of bondage replacing traditional oppression. The prescription: liberation from the 〈社会的装置〉. But this leads to a structural impossibility — "double liberation" (二重の解放). Every liberation from something requires a new artificial scaffolding (人為的な舞台装置) to maintain the liberated state. Liberation from shared communal labor requires state administrative services; liberation from hard labor requires robots. One cannot be liberated from the 〈社会的装置〉 without specifying what replaces it — and this question is consistently evaded.

[VERIFY-PAGE: book pp. 73–74] Double-liberation passage: 人間が何ものかから解放されるためには、常にそうした状態を作りだす"人為的な舞台装置"が必要となるからである。

**The "two-world" transition period:** There was a historical phase (high-growth era) in which "double liberation" seemed plausible — the transitional phase where local community and 〈社会的装置〉 coexisted. People could attack the 〈社会的装置〉 while still relying on local community's human foundations, and vice versa. But once local community's human substrate dissolved and the 〈社会的装置〉 became the total infrastructure of the life-world, this double-position became unavailable. The 〈社会的装置〉 is now imagined not as an oppressive power but as the sole guarantor of freedom and equality — the indispensable foundation of life itself.

**The real face of the "liberated individual":** Contemporary people are not simply selfish egoists (a common misdiagnosis). They act with a degree of moral consideration, freely choose and self-determine, and voluntarily head toward 〈自己完結〉 (self-finishing). What operates among them is the 「不介入の倫理」 (ethics of non-intervention): a tacit moral compact in which one does not intervene in others' lives and does not permit others to intervene in one's own, and in exchange accepts full personal responsibility for the outcomes of one's own life.

[VERIFY-PAGE: book pp. 74–75] 不介入の倫理 passage: 他者の生に介入しないと同時に、自らの生に他者が介入することをも許さない、そしてその代わりに、自らの生の帰結については各自が一切の責任を負うという暗黙の道徳的配慮、いわば「不介入の倫理」というものだからである。

**The bombshell conclusion:** We are not still "on the way" to the ideal. We have arrived. The person we see today — self-finishing, user of the 〈社会的装置〉, ethically non-intrusive — is the "moral subject" that the project of 〈自立した個人〉 produced. The world we see is the actual realization of that ideal's promise.

[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 75] われわれはすでに〈自立した個人〉に到達しているのであり、われわれが今日見ている人間こそ、「自立」のプロジェクトがもたらした「道徳的主体」の正体であるということ…

### (4) 新たな"人間学"の必要性 (p. 75)
**Programmatic close.** The task is not to identify yet again why 自立 has not been achieved and to refine Conditions 1 and 2 further. The task is to diagnose the essential error in the anthropological presuppositions of the ideology itself, and from there to reconstruct an understanding of the human being at the most fundamental level — the question of what the essence of human existence and of life is. This "re-definition of the human" (人間の再定義) does not mean constructing a new positive ideal to replace 〈自立した個人〉; it means constructing a new Weltanschauung/anthropological frame capable of understanding and explaining the era.

## C. 自立 / 自律 Distinction — Verdict

**Finding:** Uegaki does not stage a formal terminological contrast between 自立 and 自律 as two explicitly opposed concepts in this chapter. Rather, the distinction is structurally embedded in the argument:

- **自立** (self-reliance/independence) = the structural/political condition sought by the ideology — absence of external constraint, freedom of judgment and decision. It is what Conditions 1 and 2 aim to produce.
- **自律** (意志の自律, autonomy of will) = the normative ethical endpoint — Condition 3, the Rousseauan synthesis, positive freedom. This is what the scenario *promised* would follow from 自立, but never delivered.

The chapter's core argument is precisely that the ideology **confused or conflated** 自立 (a structural enabling condition) with 自律 (a normative telos requiring more than structural liberation). Achieving 自立 does not automatically produce 自律; indeed, the unlimited pursuit of 自立 structurally produces 〈自己完結化〉 instead.

**Quote-lead for [VERIFY-PAGE]:** The most quotable sentences on this distinction occur at p. 67–68 (definition of 〈自立した個人〉 as aiming at 意志の自律 via 自己判断/自己決定) and p. 70–71 (Condition 3 as the unachieved telos). A clean-source pull of both passages is recommended.

There is no single sentence in Ch.2 where both terms appear in direct contrast in a clear syntactic structure — the distinction is argumentatively staged, not terminologically proclaimed. For the thesis, the clearest expression is the structure: the project claimed to achieve 自律 via 自立, but what it actually produced was 〈自己完結化〉.

Also relevant (from the summary chapter at lines 1086–1091, which provides a clean restatement): 〈自立した個人〉の思想、すなわち人間の本質を個人に見いだし、それぞれの個人が何ものにもとらわれることなく、十全な自己判断/自己決定を通じて、意志の自律を達成することを理想とする。[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. ~ch2 summary — locate clean source]

## D. Relevance Tags to Thesis Ch IV

### §A (Anthropology-of-freedom / paradox)
**HIGH.** This chapter is the direct source for the argument that the Enlightenment/positive-liberty synthesis is structurally unrealizable. Uegaki's three-conditions framework and the promised scenario directly support the thesis's claim that the demand for Condition 3 (each's freedom through all's) cannot be achieved in real 共同. The "freedom paradox" is named and anatomized here. The 自立/自律 gap is the Japanese-language articulation of the paradox that is central to Ch IV §A.

### §B (User / cocoon / apparatus)
**HIGH.** The 不介入の倫理 and the portrait of contemporary people as 〈ユーザー〉 of the 〈社会的装置〉 who self-finish and mutually non-intervene maps directly onto the thesis's apparatus-cocoon dynamic. The argument that 〈社会的装置〉 is no longer perceived as power but as the sole guarantee of freedom and equality is a key warrant for why dependence on the apparatus is not felt as dependence. The "double liberation" impossibility argument supports the thesis claim that outsourcing to the apparatus is structurally propelled, not chosen.

### §C (自己完結社会 typology)
**MEDIUM-HIGH.** The chapter reinforces the descriptive typology of the 自己完結社会 from Ch.1 by now explaining its ideological driver. The 写像 argument is critical: 自己完結化 is not a side-effect or deviation; it is the individual-level expression of what the dominant anthropology was systematically producing. This upgrades 自己完結化 from symptom to logical outcome.

### §F (Immanent reframe)
**MEDIUM.** Section (4) opens the question of whether a new anthropology is possible — not a new positive ideal, but a new Weltanschauung. This is the space into which the thesis's own immanent reframe (finite life / 有限の生 vs. limitless life / 無限の生) enters. Uegaki is clearing the ground here rather than filling it. The "re-definition of the human" task named at p. 75 is structurally parallel to what the thesis's Ch VI undertakes.

## E. Complications and Enrichments to the Thesis Spine

**Complication 1 — The "arrival" argument.** Uegaki's most provocative claim is that 〈自立した個人〉 has *already been realized* — the self-finishing, non-intervening person is the project's actual product, not a failure state. This is stronger than the thesis spine's current framing, which treats 自己完結化 as a propelled *outcome*. Uegaki claims it is the *telos*. This distinction matters for Ch IV: does the apparatus propel a process that was not intended, or does it deliver exactly what was promised? Uegaki's answer: the latter. This could sharpen the thesis's causal claim.

**Complication 2 — Double liberation impossibility.** The argument that every liberation requires a new "artificial stage-set" is structurally relevant to the thesis's artificiality argument in Ch II. The 〈社会的装置〉 is itself a form of artificiality; Uegaki's point that you cannot be liberated from it without specifying a replacement anticipates the thesis claim that neo-artificiality (the metaverse) does not free us from the apparatus but deepens it. Could be cited in Ch II or Ch V.

**Complication 3 — Post-war Japan specificity.** Uegaki grounds the argument heavily in Japanese post-war intellectual history (Maruyama, Ōtsuka, wartime → 高度経済成長 transition). This is valuable contextual grounding for a thesis examined at a Japanese university, but the universalizing claims need to be tracked carefully. The Rousseau/Kant/Marx/Berlin footnotes (fn. 12) indicate Uegaki considers this a Western modern phenomenon mapped onto Japan, not a Japan-specific pathology.

**Enrichment — Fromm resonance.** Uegaki cites Fromm's *Escape from Freedom* (1941) as structurally analogous to the 20th-century deferral mechanism. This is a useful secondary triangulation: the same logic that kept people from "escaping to freedom's past" also kept them from questioning the project's premises. The thesis could use this to show that the self-finishing outcome was not visible as such from within the project.

**Enrichment — 不介入の倫理 as a named concept.** This is precisely the relational structure of the cocoon-user that the thesis needs to name. Uegaki provides a ready Japanese academic term. Ch IV §B should cite this explicitly; it describes the ethical micro-structure that makes mutual non-intervention feel like respect rather than abandonment.

## F. Final Report — Highest-Value Findings (5–8 bullets)

1. **The 写像 argument is the chapter's load-bearing contribution.** 〈自立した個人〉's pursuit of 自立 and the emergence of 〈自己完結化〉 / 〈脱身体化〉 are not in tension — they are the same process seen from individual vs. social angles. This directly supports the thesis spine's claim that 自己完結化 is the *propelled* outcome of the dominant anthropology, not an exception. [VERIFY-PAGE: p. 72]

2. **The 自立/自律 distinction is structural, not terminological.** Uegaki does not define them against each other in a single sentence; the distinction is staged across the three-conditions framework. 自立 = structural condition (Conditions 1+2); 自律 = normative telos (Condition 3). The ideology conflated them. No clean single-sentence contrast exists in Ch.2 — the thesis must construct the formulation, citing Uegaki's framework as the source.

3. **The "promised scenario" framework** (three conditions + one sequence) gives the thesis a precise description of the Enlightenment-mode demand that it can cite by name. The scenario promised that Conditions 1+2 → Condition 3 follows. It never did. [VERIFY-PAGE: p. 70]

4. **The "arrival" bombshell.** We have already arrived at 〈自立した個人〉. The ethics of non-intervention IS the moral structure the project produced. This is a stronger claim than the thesis currently makes and may need to be engaged with explicitly in Ch IV. If accepted, it shifts the thesis's causal argument from "apparatus propels a deviation" to "apparatus delivers the promise."

5. **不介入の倫理 as a technical term** for the relational micro-structure of the self-finishing user. This is a quotable, academically established term in the Japanese literature that names exactly what Ch IV §B describes. Pull the clean definition. [VERIFY-PAGE: pp. 74–75]

6. **Double-liberation impossibility** (pp. 73–74) provides a structural argument for why the 〈社会的装置〉 cannot be critiqued from within the 〈自立した個人〉 framework — and why every attempt to do so (alienation theory, associationism) simply deepens dependence on a substitute apparatus. Useful for Ch II (artificiality) and Ch V (metaverse as apparatus).

7. **The 亡霊 framing** is important for the thesis's intellectual-historical positioning. By calling 〈自立した個人〉 a "ghost," Uegaki signals that the ideology's continued operation is unacknowledged, zombie-like — the same rhetorical move the thesis needs to make about the Enlightenment-mode demand for 共同. Worth citing the passage where 亡霊 is first applied (p. 69). [VERIFY-PAGE: p. 69]

8. **Section (4)'s programmatic call** for a new anthropology that does not construct a new positive ideal but re-describes human existence from its foundations is structurally homologous with the thesis's own Ch VI move. Citing this as the intellectual task Uegaki sets, which the thesis's squeeze ontology answers, would strengthen the thesis's claim to be doing genuine constructive anthropology rather than mere critique.
