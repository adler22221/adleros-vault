## 1. EPISTEMIC FALLACY — *primary-confirmed*

The canonical definition. Originates in *A Realist Theory of Science* (1st ed. 1975; 2nd ed. 1978; the locus classicus cite in the CR literature is **RTS p.36**, referring to the 1978 Harvester/Humanities ed.).

[VERBATIM: Bhaskar, *A Realist Theory of Science* (2008 Routledge ed.) | p.26 (passage runs p.25→26; = canonical "p.36" in 1978 ed.) | "The metaphysical mistake the argument of the previous section allows us to pinpoint may be called the 'epistemic fallacy'. This consists in the view that statements about being can be reduced to or analysed in terms of statements about knowledge; i.e. that ontological questions can always be transposed into epistemological terms."]

Continuation (same passage, p.26) — establishes the **squeeze-relevant** consequence (foreclosure of the intransitive world):

[VERBATIM: Bhaskar, *A Realist Theory of Science* (2008 Routledge ed.) | p.26 | "The idea that being can always be analysed in terms of our knowledge of being, that it is sufficient for philosophy to 'treat only of the network, and not what the network describes', results in the systematic dissolution of the idea of a world ... independent of but investigated by science."]

> **Folio note:** Confirmed in the local PDF: the standalone printed page number "26" sits on the PDF page carrying this text (`Roy Bhaskar - A Realist Theory of Science (2008).pdf`, PDF index 58). NLM independently returned "pages 25 and 26" for the 2008 ed. **Use RTS p.36 if citing the standard 1975/1978 edition** (Bhaskar himself back-references "RTS pp. 36ff" in later prefaces — verbatim confirmation below). Page label per edition is the only variable; the wording is identical across editions.

Bhaskar's own later back-reference fixing the canonical folio + naming the epistemic fallacy "the definition of being in terms of knowledge":

[VERBATIM: Bhaskar, *Reclaiming Reality* (intro, in NLM corpus) | n.p. | "This mistake is the epistemic fallacy, the definition of being in terms of knowledge (see RTS pp. 36ff) ... It was a small step from here to unearth the meaning of the epistemic fallacy in the ontic fallacy (SR pp. 23, 66 and chapter 3)."]

## 2. ONTIC FALLACY — *primary-confirmed*

Bhaskar's complement. Defined most explicitly in *Scientific Realism and Human Emancipation* (1986; "SR"), and restated in *Dialectic* (1993) and *Plato Etc.* (1994). Core formula: **the compulsive determination of knowledge by being** — the naturalisation / eternalisation / reification of knowledge as a direct reflection of being.

### 2a. The paired formula (cleanest definition) — *Scientific Realism and Human Emancipation*

[VERBATIM: Bhaskar, *Scientific Realism and Human Emancipation* (2009 Routledge ed.; orig. 1986) | pp.15–16 (1986 Verso ed.: p.16) | "On the epistemic fallacy, being either just is (constituted by) knowledge or is analysable in terms of the knowledge (of objects). On the ontic fallacy, knowledge either just is being simpliciter or is analysable in terms of the being of its objects."]

The "anthroporealist exchange" — both fallacies as the joint engine (directly squeeze-relevant: humanised nature for naturalised science):

[VERBATIM: Bhaskar, *Scientific Realism and Human Emancipation* (2009 ed.; orig. 1986) | p.16 | "Thus in what I will call the great 'anthroporealist exchange' a naturalised (and so incorrigibly eternalised) science is purchased at the price of a humanised nature. In this way is revealed the tacit complicity of the epistemic and ontic fallacies ... The twinned fallacies are by no means mutually exclusive; rather they characteristically presuppose each other."]

The two-category-mistakes statement (SR 2009 p.171; 1986 Verso p.253) — ties each fallacy to a directional collapse:

[VERBATIM: Bhaskar, *Scientific Realism and Human Emancipation* (2009 ed.) | p.171 (1986 Verso ed.: p.253) | "Positivist philosophy is thus founded on two category mistakes: the derealisation of reality, i.e. the collapse of the ID, entraining the epistemologisation of being, covered and cloaked by the epistemic fallacy; and the desocialisation of science, i.e. the collapse of the TD, resulting in the ontologisation of knowledge, the point of the exercise, manifesting and realising the ontic fallacy."]
> (ID = intransitive dimension; TD = transitive dimension.)

### 2b. *Dialectic: The Pulse of Freedom* (1993)

[VERBATIM: Bhaskar, *Dialectic: The Pulse of Freedom* (2008 Routledge ed.; orig. 1993) | p.4 | "Operating hand-in-hand with this overt collapse, engendered or masked by the epistemic fallacy, is its practical counterpart, the ideology of the compulsive determination of knowledge by being—for instance, in the guise of reified facts or hypostatized ideas—in what I have characterized as the 'ontic fallacy'."]
> **Folio confirmed in local PDF:** this is the only occurrence of the phrase "compulsive determination of knowledge by being" in `Roy Bhaskar - Dialectic ... (2008, Routledge) - libgen.li.pdf` (PDF index 40); NLM labels the printed folio **p.4** (the §3 "Negation" passage of the General Introduction). The "reified facts or hypostatized ideas" formulation is here, verbatim.

[VERBATIM: Bhaskar, *Dialectic: The Pulse of Freedom* (2008 ed.; orig. 1993) | p.191 | "Third, and closely connected to this, it co-exists symbiotically with an esoteric naturalization of knowledge—e.g., in the Humean case, with the reification of facts and fetishism of their conjunctions; that is to say, with the compulsive determination of knowledge of being by being. This is the reciprocal, equilibrating ontic fallacy."]

The analysis or definition of statements about being in terms of statements about our knowledge (of being). In subject—object identity or equivalent theory it entails the converse ontic fallacy, viz. the definition or assumption of the compulsive determination of knowledge by being."]

## 3. THESIS GLOSS — ontic/epistemic ↔ Platonic/Aristotelian mapping

> **This mapping is the *thesis's* interpretive gloss for §IV.3.2 / §IV.3.5 — NOT Bhaskar's own wording.** Bhaskar does not label the ontic fallacy "Platonic/idealist" or the epistemic fallacy "Aristotelian/empiricist." Flag accordingly in prose; do not attribute the mapping to Bhaskar.

- **Thesis claim:** *ontic fallacy = the Platonic / idealist engine* (knowledge naturalised as a reflection of being → hypostatised ideas, reified facts; being dictates knowledge). **Epistemic fallacy = the Aristotelian / empiricist engine** (being dissolved into knowledge / experience; knowledge dictates being). Both **foreclose the intransitive real** → hence the squeeze.
- **Warrant Bhaskar *does* supply (use these, verbatim, to anchor the gloss without overclaiming):**
  - The epistemic fallacy "results in the systematic dissolution of the idea of a world ... independent of but investigated by science" (RTS p.26) — i.e. it forecloses the intransitive. This is the direct textual hook for "squeeze from the epistemic/empiricist side."
  - The ontic fallacy = "the ontologisation of knowledge" / "compulsive determination of knowledge by being" via "reified facts or hypostatized ideas" (Dialectic p.4; SR p.171). The phrase **"hypostatized ideas"** is Bhaskar's own and is the closest he comes to the Platonic register — usable as a bridge, but the *Platonic* label remains the thesis's.
  - Bhaskar grounds the **complicity** of the two ("they characteristically presuppose each other," SR p.16) — supports the thesis's "two engines, one squeeze" structure.
  - Bhaskar traces the epistemic fallacy "back to Parmenides" (Dialectic p.4) and the quest for certain foundations "from Plato on" (SR p.16) — i.e. Bhaskar *does* route both fallacies through the ancient Greek lineage, which licenses (but does not itself assert) the thesis's Plato/Aristotle sorting.

> Suggested in-prose framing: present the Platonic↔ontic / Aristotelian↔epistemic mapping as *"In the terms of this thesis, ..."* or *"We may align Bhaskar's pair with ..."*, then cite Bhaskar verbatim only for what he actually says (dissolution of the world; ontologisation of knowledge; complicity; the Parmenides/Plato lineage).
