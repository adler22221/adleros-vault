## 1. Hartmut Rosa — *The Uncontrollability of the World* (Polity 2020) — THE HEAD-WORD

### 1a. Author's Note "Beyond Control: A Note from the Author on the Key Term of This Book" — the translation history and the FINAL choice

Rosa narrates the English-term search and states the translator's final settlement explicitly:

> "For me, *Unverfügbarkeit* is one of the key elements of every experience of being in resonance with someone or something. Ever since I first used it in an article in German, I have been wondering about how to translate it into English. My first attempt was 'elusiveness.' [...] But I ultimately decided against this term, because resonance is not always elusive. [...] So I tried 'unavailability.' But I never liked this one. [...] So I turned to 'unpredictability.' [...] But *Unverfügbarkeit* actually goes deeper than this. It is not just about non-predictability, but about non-engineerability."
[VERBATIM: Rosa, *Uncontrollability of the World*, Polity 2020 | Author's Note, pp. vii–viii | "My first attempt was 'elusiveness' ... So I tried 'unavailability' ... I turned to 'unpredictability' ... non-engineerability"]

The definition of the *verfügbar* drive (the positive pole — note the cluster of English glosses, with the German given in parenthesis):

> "This is exactly what this book is about: modernity's incessant desire to make the world engineerable, predictable, available, accessible, disposable (i.e. *verfügbar*) in all its aspects."
[VERBATIM: Rosa, *Uncontrollability of the World*, Polity 2020 | Author's Note, p. viii | "modernity's incessant desire to make the world engineerable, predictable, available, accessible, disposable (i.e. verfügbar) in all its aspects"]

**The decisive sentence — the authorized English of *Unverfügbarkeit*:**

> "Second, this desire for control produces, behind our backs, a world that in the end is utterly uncontrollable in all the relevant aspects. [...] The drive and desire toward controllability ultimately creates monstrous, frightening forms of uncontrollability. **Uncontrollability: This is the term that Jim, the masterful translator of this book, found to capture all the aspects of *Unverfügbarkeit* discussed here.** And, even if it is not exactly equivalent to *Unverfügbarkeit* in every nuance, it surely is the closest we can get. And Jim, who also translated my big book on *Resonance*, was sensitive enough in his translation to capture all those nuances in the text itself."
[VERBATIM: Rosa, *Uncontrollability of the World*, Polity 2020 | Author's Note, p. ix | "Uncontrollability: This is the term that Jim, the masterful translator of this book, found to capture all the aspects of Unverfügbarkeit discussed here"]

> **Reading:** the title-noun *Unverfügbarkeit* → **"uncontrollability"** (authorized). The positive pole *verfügbar* is rendered as a CLUSTER — "engineerable, predictable, available, accessible, disposable" — with **"controllable/controllability"** used as the running English term throughout the body for the drive (see 1b–1c). "Available/availability" and "disposable" are present but as *components* of the cluster, NOT the chosen head-word. So: head-noun = controllability/uncontrollability; "availability" and "disposability" are secondary facets Rosa lists.

### 1b. The drive defined: "the world as a point of aggression" (constitutive interplay of control/uncontrollability)

> "The interplay between control and uncontrollability is constitutive not only of many varieties of sports, but of games in general [...] Our encounter with the uncontrollable and our desire or struggle to bring it under control form a red thread that runs through all areas of our lives."
[VERBATIM: Rosa, *Uncontrollability of the World*, Polity 2020 | Introduction "On Snow", p. 3 | "The interplay between control and uncontrollability is constitutive ... a red thread that runs through all areas of our lives"]

> "From games to love, from snow to death, human life and human experience are defined by uncontrollability."
[VERBATIM: Rosa, *Uncontrollability of the World*, Polity 2020 | Introduction "On Snow", pp. 3–4 | "human life and human experience are defined by uncontrollability"]

> "My hypothesis is this: because we, as late modern human beings, aim to make the world controllable at every level — individual, cultural, institutional, and structural — we invariably encounter the world as a 'point of aggression' [...] a series of objects that we have to know, attain, conquer, master, or exploit. And precisely because of this, 'life,' the experience of feeling alive and of truly encountering [the] world — that which makes resonance possible — seems to elude us."
[VERBATIM: Rosa, *Uncontrollability of the World*, Polity 2020 | Introduction "On Snow", p. 4 | "aim to make the world controllable at every level ... seems to elude us"]

### 1c. THE FOUR DIMENSIONS of making the world controllable (Rosa's actual enumeration)

Chapter 2 is titled **"Four Dimensions of Controllability."** Rosa's own wording of the four (do not paraphrase — this is his list):

> "Upon closer inspection, it quickly becomes apparent that making the world controllable is not a homogeneous process, but one that consists of at least four different elements or can be divided along four dimensions.
> (1) Making the world controllable means, first, making it **visible**, that is, making it **knowable**, expanding our knowledge of what is there. [...]
> (2) Making the world controllable means, furthermore, making it physically **reachable or accessible**. [...]
> (3) Inextricably linked with this is the third dimension of bringing the world under control, namely by making it **manageable**. [...]
> (4) Distinct from this mode [...] is a fourth dimension of making the world controllable, namely by making it **useful**, pressing it into service. [...] What is there, what is present is instrumentalized, transformed into the material and the object of our own projections and desires."
[VERBATIM: Rosa, *Uncontrollability of the World*, Polity 2020 | ch. 2 "Four Dimensions of Controllability", pp. 14–16 | "(1) ... making it visible ... knowable; (2) ... physically reachable or accessible; (3) ... making it manageable; (4) ... making it useful, pressing it into service"]

The summative recapitulation (the cleanest single-sentence list):

> "These four dimensions of making the world controllable — rendering it **visible, reachable, manageable, useful** — are solidly entrenched in the institutions that form the basis of modern society."
[VERBATIM: Rosa, *Uncontrollability of the World*, Polity 2020 | ch. 2, p. 17 | "These four dimensions of making the world controllable — rendering it visible, reachable, manageable, useful — are solidly entrenched in the institutions..."]

> **NB — matches the prompt's hypothesised gloss almost exactly.** Prompt anticipated: visible/knowable · reachable/accessible · manageable/controllable · useful. Rosa's actual axes: **visible (knowable) · reachable (accessible) · manageable · useful**. Use Rosa's four nouns verbatim; do NOT label the third "controllable" (the whole set is "controllability"; the third is *manageable*).

### 1d. Constitutive uncontrollability of THINGS — "semicontrollability" (= the *halbverfügbar* idea, stated in the 2020 book)

The 2020 book gives Rosa's own English compound that is the conceptual twin of *halbverfügbar*:

> "Hence, despite not being able to fully demonstrate it here argumentatively, it seems to me that there must be an aspect of **inherent uncontrollability** not only in our experiences or in our relationship to the world, but also **in things themselves**, if we are to be able to enter into a resonant relationship with them.
> **Thesis 2: Things we can completely control in all four dimensions lose their resonant quality. Resonance thus implies semicontrollability.**"
[VERBATIM: Rosa, *Uncontrollability of the World*, Polity 2020 | ch. "Five Theses on the Controllability of Things", p. 44 | "Thesis 2: Things we can completely control in all four dimensions lose their resonant quality. Resonance thus implies semicontrollability"]

> "Hence, here again, the admittedly awkward term 'semicontrollability' seems to hit on just what it is that makes resonance possible. [...] The line between what is controllable (in the sense of being 'technically possible') and what is uncontrollable remains intact, but consistently shifts in favor of the former."
[VERBATIM: Rosa, *Uncontrollability of the World*, Polity 2020 | ch. "Five Theses...", p. 55 | "the admittedly awkward term 'semicontrollability' ... The line between what is controllable ... and what is uncontrollable remains intact"]

The three-fold locus of the constitutive uncontrollability (subject / object / process):

> "The uncontrollability constitutive of resonant relationships in fact has a subject dimension, an object dimension, and a process dimension. [...] On the object side, uncontrollability means that what we encounter must resist us in at least one of the four dimensions of calculation and control. There must be at least one 'obstinate remainder' that has something to say to us."
[VERBATIM: Rosa, *Uncontrollability of the World*, Polity 2020 | ch. "Five Theses...", p. 53 | "uncontrollability ... has a subject dimension, an object dimension, and a process dimension ... at least one 'obstinate remainder'"]

## Head-word verdict (controllability vs availability)

**VERDICT: CONTROLLABILITY.** The authorized Wagner/Polity translation renders the negative title-noun *Unverfügbarkeit* as **"uncontrollability"** and uses **"controllable/controllability"** as the running English term for the positive *verfügbar* drive throughout both volumes.

- This is stated by Rosa himself, on the record, in the Author's Note: *"Uncontrollability: This is the term that Jim, the masterful translator of this book, found to capture all the aspects of Unverfügbarkeit"* (p. ix). The translator explicitly **rejected "unavailability"** ("I tried 'unavailability.' But I never liked this one") and "unpredictability"/"elusiveness" en route to "uncontrollability."
- **"Availability" and "disposability" are NOT the head-word.** They appear only as two items inside Rosa's enumerated cluster for *verfügbar* — "engineerable, predictable, **available, accessible, disposable** (i.e. verfügbar)" (p. viii). The book's spine, chapter titles ("Four Dimensions of **Controllability**"; "The Monstrous Return of the **Uncontrollable**"), and running argument all use control-, not avail-.
- Independent scholarship confirms: Peters & Majid (2023, p. 19) gloss *Unverfügbarkeit* as "(translated as 'uncontrollability')."

➡ **Recommendation for Ch IV:** name the mode the **"universalizing CONTROLLABILITY mode."** This is the choice the authorized translation underwrites. If a single positive head-noun for the *drive* is wanted, use **controllability** (not availability/disposability). Reserve "availability / accessibility / disposability" for naming the *sub-dimensions* (they map onto Rosa's four: visible/knowable, reachable/accessible, manageable, useful). "Availability" as head-word would actively contradict the translator's documented rejection of "unavailability."

## How this feeds Ch IV (anti-intended = Unverfügbarkeit; the squeeze)

**1. 意のままにならない ("the anti-intended") ⇄ *Unverfügbarkeit* / "uncontrollability."** The Japanese phrase — "that which does not go as one intends/wills" — lands precisely on Rosa's *Unverfügbarkeit*. Rosa's own gloss of why "uncontrollability" beat the alternatives is built on exactly the will/intention nexus: resonance "is something comparable to the moment of falling asleep: we cannot bring it about simply by willing it. The more we want it, the less we get it" (Author's Note, pp. vii–viii). So 意のままにならない = the uncontrollable = *Unverfügbarkeit*, and the opposing mode (意のままにする / making-go-as-one-wills) = the *verfügbar*/controllability drive. The terminological anchor is clean and authorized.

**2. The squeeze is in Rosa, almost verbatim, as a structural inversion.** Ch IV's "the given reasserts against the controllability mode" maps directly onto Rosa's two-part paradox (Author's Note, p. ix; ch. "The Monstrous Return of the Uncontrollable"):
   - (a) The drive itself **alters and deadens** the relation: "A fully engineerable world eventually would be a dead world" (p. viii).
   - (b) The drive **produces its own opposite**: "this desire for control produces, behind our backs, a world that in the end is utterly uncontrollable" / "modernity's program of making the world controllable threatens to produce a new, radical form of uncontrollability, one that is categorically different from and worse than the original" (pp. ix, ~115).
   This is the squeeze figure: the universalizing controllability mode does not abolish the given (the uncontrollable) but forces its **monstrous return**. For the thesis's framing, note the *valence difference*: Rosa's returned uncontrollability is "frightening"/"monstrous" (alienated), whereas Rosa's *constitutive, resonant* uncontrollability (§1d) is the GOOD uncontrollable that the controllability mode squeezes out. Ch IV can use both poles: the controllability mode squeezes the resonant/constitutive uncontrollable (the world as meaning-space) AND boomerangs into the monstrous uncontrollable.

**3. "Constitutive and reasserts" — both halves are secured (primary).**
   - *Constitutive:* "human life and human experience are defined by uncontrollability" (p. 3–4); "there must be an aspect of inherent uncontrollability ... in things themselves" (p. 44); **Thesis 2: "Things we can completely control in all four dimensions lose their resonant quality. Resonance thus implies semicontrollability"** (p. 44). → The given cannot be made fully *verfügbar*; total controllability is self-defeating (kills resonance).
   - *Reasserts:* "Uncontrollability has returned ... as a self-made monster" (p. ~110); the "Monstrous Return" chapter in full (§1e).

**4. Head-word consequence, stated plainly for the decision doc.** Adopt **controllability** as Ch IV's mode head-word; it is the authorized English of the *verfügbar* pole and keeps the thesis's term in lockstep with Rosa's title-pair (controllability ↔ uncontrollability). The four sub-dimensions to cite (Rosa's exact nouns): **visible/knowable · reachable/accessible · manageable · useful**. The "semi-controllable" (*halbverfügbar*) middle term is available if Ch IV wants Rosa's positive thesis that a *liveable* world must remain only semi-controllable — i.e. harmony as the refusal of total controllability, which dovetails with the thesis's "harmony = squeeze-prevention" note.
