## (a) Section-by-Section Argument Arc

### §1 人間存在における〈共同〉の概念 (p. 245)
**Argument function:** Set up the problem by distinguishing the new concept 〈共同〉 from ideologically contaminated prior usage.

Uegaki opens by noting that the question of how beings who bear the inescapable "burden" (負担) of relationality with "others who do not bend to one's will" (意のままにならない他者) can nonetheless form cooperative relationships — i.e., the problem of *the conditions for communality* — is what this chapter is about. He then clears the conceptual ground.

He begins from the dictionary etymology: *共同* = "two or more persons doing something together" (共 = together; 同 = the same), meaning a *joint action* (共同行為). He distinguishes two senses already present in ordinary usage:
- **Sense ①**: Active — emphasis on *doing something jointly*
- **Sense ②**: Stative — emphasis on *being in a state of sameness/homogeneity* ("同等の資格")

Uegaki stipulates that his 〈共同〉 concept tracks **sense ①** — i.e., 〈共同〉 = *共同行為*, joint action — and that the stative/homogeneity reading (sense ②) is an ideologically loaded residue that must be bracketed.

He shows that in philosophy/sociology dictionaries, "communality" (共同性) is described as "the mutually supportive and empathetic mode of social members," and is understood both as a *natural human property* and as *lost under modernity*, recoverable as ideal. This already encodes the structure he will critique. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 247–248]`

He notes a further dimension: 〈共同〉 has been bound to a historical narrative linking Marxist "alienation" theory → dissolution of pre-modern community under capitalism → dialectical recovery via new forms of solidarity. This is the "ideology" he will name the *idyllic-dialectical theory of communality*.

### §2 「牧歌主義的-弁証法的共同論」批判 (pp. 249–256)
**Argument function:** Demolish the Rousseau→Kant positive-liberty synthesis as the principal obstacle to a realistic understanding of 〈共同〉.

Uegaki identifies three nested sub-positions within the overall "idyllic-dialectical theory of communality" (牧歌主義的-弁証法的共同論), each with its own flaw:

#### Sub-position 1: 「自然主義の共同論」 (Naturalist Theory of Communality)
**Claim**: Communality is rooted in humanity's natural essence as a social being — "mutually supportive and empathetic" by nature. Obstacles to communality (atomization, alienation under capitalism) are external distortions of this nature; remove them and communality flows naturally.

**Uegaki's critique**: This position makes communality *unconditional* — as if human beings, once freed from obstacles, would automatically achieve joint action. But the entire analysis of 〈関係性〉 in Chapter 7 showed that the other is *irreducibly* "not as one wills them to be," that 〈間柄〉 and 〈距離〉 are required to *manage* rather than eliminate the burden. The naturalist position "simplistically" (あまりに単純化) short-circuits this reality, assuming pre-established harmony. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 251]`

#### Sub-position 2: 「共同体批判の共同論」 (Community-Critique Theory)
**Claim**: Pre-modern community (*Gemeinschaft*, "むら") had genuine communality, but it was built on "personal dependence" and homogeneity — suppressing individual distinctiveness. Hence the path is not raw restoration but *critique* of community followed by a new form.

**Historical diagnosis**: In postwar Japan (Otsuka Hisao, Maruyama Masao), the village-community was simultaneously (a) an idealized locus of lost communality and (b) the locus of prewar Japanese irresponsibility and pre-modern suppression. This produced a contradictory double valence: the village as both romanticized ideal *and* overcome past.

**Uegaki's critique**: The "loss of individuality" charge is empirically contestable — people in pre-modern communities had real wills, joys, and sorrows, even if constrained. More fundamentally: the appearance of "homogeneity" from outside masked enormous social cost — "忍従と悲しみ" (endurance and sorrow) `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 253]` — that is, communality was never unconditional even then. The community critique inherits the fantasy that communality-in-community was effortless, and then merely inverts the evaluative sign.

#### Sub-position 3: 「自由連帯の共同論」 (Free-Solidarity Theory of Communality)
**Claim**: The dialectical synthesis — preserve modern freedom/individuality (acquired in modernity) AND recover communality in new form. The Aufhebung (止揚) of individual freedom and communality: each side contributes its best, resolves its contradiction in a higher unity. This was the ideological content of 1990s–2000s association (アソシエーション) discourse, volunteer/NGO/NPO movements, and "public sphere" (公共性) theory in Japan.

**Uegaki's historical account**: Fromm (*Escape from Freedom*, 1941) argued that modernity gave people freedom but stripped them of "primary bonds," and the weight of freedom pushed people toward authoritarian submission. The antidote is not to flee freedom but to build new solidarity on autonomous will. This framework powered the association-society optimism of the 2000s, which portrayed civil associations as the dialectical Aufhebung of Gemeinschaft heteronomy and market atomism.

**Uegaki's critique — the key move**: Dialectics here functions as **"魔術的なレトリック"** (magical rhetoric) `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 254–255]`. The problem: *why should contradictory things be synthesizable*? The free-solidarity theorists never answered this because they assumed that once obstacles (suppression, non-participation) are removed, synthesis follows automatically. Concretely:

- If the association is grounded in freedom and voluntariness, what is the status of the *non-participant* — the person who freely chooses not to join? Suppressing their non-participation would contradict the very freedom being claimed.
- The optimistic association discourse was full of "予定調和を前提とした賛美" (praise premised on pre-established harmony) `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 256]`, with no realistic account of how association sustains itself against the burden of joint action.

**Unifying diagnosis**: All three sub-positions share **one root failure**: they have "ほとんど顧みてこなかった" (barely paid attention to) the burden (負担) that people must accept in order to make joint action possible. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 257]`

Therefore: **"負担なき共同とは虚構である"** — *Communality without burden is a fiction.* `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 257]` This is the foundational proposition Uegaki places at the base of his entire reconstruction.

He then makes the key logical point explicit via two thought experiments.

**Thought experiment 1 — The Village of 100**: Imagine a village of 100 people where everyone is guaranteed unconditional freedom of self-realization — no suppression, no compulsion, only voluntariness. This is the "ideal village" of the idyllic-dialectical theory. Uegaki argues it would quickly collapse: there are always things "everybody benefits from but nobody voluntarily wants to do." In a village premised on self-directed voluntariness, anyone asked to shoulder such tasks can legitimately refuse ("this is not what I chose"; "it conflicts with my inner truth"; "I have not concluded it is necessary"). There is no possible rejoinder that does not itself constitute the very suppression the village forbids. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 258–259]`

**Thought experiment 2 — The Cleaning Rota**: What if the 100 villagers *cannot leave*, and there is a shared commons (e.g., a common well) that will become a health hazard unless cleaned — yet no one wants to clean it voluntarily? Now the village must distribute the burden. This requires: negotiation about who does what and how much; accommodation of unequal capacities; collective enforcement against free-riders; and persistent judgment-calls under uncertainty. The village has now forfeited its premise: it is no longer free of compulsion, social sanction, collective demand, and imperfect consensus. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 259–260]`

Crucially, Uegaki notes: there is *one* scenario in which unconditional free self-realization is possible — where some **非人間的な〈社会的装置〉** (non-human social apparatus — perhaps "slaves," perhaps "robots") performs all the burden-laden tasks nobody wants. This, he observes with irony, is precisely what he has been calling 〈自己完結社会〉 and 〈生の自己完結化〉 taken to its logical extreme. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 259]`

**→ THESIS RELEVANCE (Ch IV §A, paradox spine)**: This is the hinge. The Enlightenment demand for positive liberty (freedom-of-each-through-all) is internally contradictory because: (a) genuine joint action requires unconditional bearing of burden; (b) positive liberty as commonly theorized requires that all action be voluntarily self-chosen; (c) these two are irreconcilable in any real community without either suppression or the outsourcing of burden to a non-human apparatus. The 自己完結化 trajectory is thus *immanent* to the positive-liberty demand, not a deviation from it.

### §3 〈共同〉概念の再定義 (pp. 256–261)
**Argument function**: Establish the revised concept of 〈共同〉 with burden at its core.

Building on the thought experiments, Uegaki offers his reconstruction:

〈共同〉 = 「複数の人間が何かを一緒に行うこと」 — "multiple persons doing something together" — specifically as **共同行為** (joint action), which may involve:
- Simultaneous action or temporally/spatially distributed coordinated action
- Shared tasks, including role-differentiated participation

But this *minimal* definition is insufficient. The essential addition: 〈共同〉 is joint action **in which participants must together bear the burden that necessarily arises from that joint action**. The most important burden is not just the tasks themselves, but:

> The necessity of confronting "others who do not bend to one's will" (意のままにならない他者) and the burden of the very *process* of working out how to distribute that burden with such others. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 261]`

This means: the heart of 〈共同〉 is not the tasks, nor even the coordination, but the irreducible confrontation with human otherness in the process of negotiating shared responsibility.

### §4 〈共同〉が成立するための諸条件 (pp. 261–264)
**Argument function**: Establish three necessary and jointly sufficient conditions for 〈共同〉 to become possible at all.

Given that burden is essential and not something that positive-liberty/voluntariness can dissolve, Uegaki asks: what acts as **centripetal force** (求心力) strong enough to overcome the **centrifugal force** (遠心力) of burden? His answer: three conditions must be met.

#### Condition 1: 「〈共同〉のための事実の共有」 (Shared Facts for Communality)
Participants must share the *factual recognition* that the joint action in question is important for each other's 〈生〉 (life/existence). In the village thought-experiment, this was the condition built in by stipulating that the villagers cannot leave — making the fact of mutual dependency manifest. More broadly: from the earliest human history, the success or failure of 〈共同〉 was directly tied to 〈生存〉 (survival), and *this factual linkage* was the primordial centripetal force. In everyday life this applies to any joint action — meeting, going out together — not only survival situations: what matters is that participants *share the recognition* that there is a clear benefit in doing it together. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 261–262]`

Uegaki cautions against reading this as "self-interest reduction" of communality. Rather: demanding universal *altruism* (博愛主義) from all participants is unrealistic given human reality. Confusing episodic "altruistic acts" with *sustained communality* is a category error. Communality requires that participants see benefit; demanding pure selflessness makes it fragile. The self-interest/altruism binary is the wrong frame. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 262–263]`

He also takes aim at positive liberty (積極的自由) as formulated from Rousseau through Kant and Fromm: the idea that a sufficiently mature self can reach a "higher will" (高級な意志) in which "the freedom of all is experienced as one's own freedom." He grants that this *does* sometimes occur. But: requiring it as the general condition of communality is equivalent to demanding universal altruism (博愛主義) from everyone — manifestly contrary to human reality. Positive liberty is "another version of the magical rhetoric," making burden-less 〈関係性〉 and burden-less 〈共同〉 *appear* possible when they are not. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 263]`

#### Condition 2: 「〈共同〉のための意味の共有」 (Shared Meaning for Communality)
Participants must share *meaning* — not just factual recognition but an understanding of *why* this joint action is worth the burden. This condition is especially important when the benefit of the joint action is indirect, delayed, or invisible in the immediate situation: e.g., when the "payoff" circulates rather than being directly returned to the individual. Sustained communality requires that participants understand the value of the joint action from multiple perspectives and share that meaning. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 263–264]`

#### Condition 3: 「〈共同〉のための技能の共有」 (Shared Skills for Communality)
Participants must share the *skills* (技能) required to realize joint action — above all the skills of burden distribution. These include:
- **Speech skills** (発話の技能): communicating across differences of position, interest, and feeling to reach mutual understanding
- **Ingenuity skills** (工夫の技能): designing burden-distributions that avoid overloading specific people, prevent free-riding, and accommodate individual differences

These skills must be collectively maintained as group competence, not confined to individuals. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 264]`

### §5 「〈共同〉のための作法や知恵」としての〈役割〉〈信頼〉〈許し〉の原理 (pp. 264–273)
**Argument function**: Establish the three "craft-and-wisdom" principles that make real communality possible — each a specific mechanism for managing the burden of confronting "the other who does not bend to one's will."

Uegaki prefaces this section by noting that throughout human history, 〈共同〉 has been *inescapable* (逃れられない). It is *because* communality was inescapable that human beings generated these three principles as the practical wisdom for living through unavoidable communality. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 265]`

#### 〈役割〉 (Roles) — the principle

**Standard definition**: "Allocating duties to each person; the allocated duty."

**Sociological background**: Uegaki distinguishes his concept from the sociological "role" (Mead, Blumer, Goffman) — type of behavior expected in a particular social relation. His concept of 〈役割〉 is related to but not reducible to 〈間柄〉 (relational position/standing). The key additional content: 〈役割〉 = that which is *taken on* or *shouldered* (担う) as one's part in 〈共同〉.

**Why 〈役割〉 matters for communality**: 〈間柄〉 "formalizes" the 〈我-汝構造〉 (I-Thou structure) in 〈関係性〉. Analogously, in 〈共同〉, 〈間柄〉 "formalizes" the distribution of burden — it provides a *pre-established framework* for who does what, reducing the need for fresh negotiation. This allows participants to keep direct confrontation between singular persons out of the joint action and focus on the task. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 265–266]`

**Two forms of 〈役割〉**:

1. **〈間柄〉を引き受けるものとしての〈役割〉** ("Role as taking on 〈間柄〉"): Finding positive meaning in one's assigned position within the unavoidable. Since assigned 〈間柄〉 cannot always be chosen, participants who find meaning *within* rather than only against their position contribute to communality. Some find meaning by gradually shaping the role toward what they want; others by finding joy in the work itself. Either way: when someone carries a 〈役割〉 in this sense, they become the *bearer of* (担い手としての) 〈生〉 — the inescapable is taken on actively rather than passively resisted. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 266–267]`

2. **世間や世俗、時代を超えた〈役割〉** ("Role transcending worldly convention and era"): Some persons find meaning in something that transcends the here-and-now social world — a vocation, a "thought," an aesthetic ideal — which puts them at odds with socially assigned 〈間柄〉 expectations. These "eccentric" people (奇人) are often marginalized. But Uegaki argues that it is precisely such people who become the *turning-point figures* in closed eras: their words and ways of life, left behind, become sources of liberation and social transformation. They are, in a sense, practicing communality across time and place. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 267–268]`

**Limit of 〈役割〉**: Not all persons can find meaning in their assigned position; the transmission of individual meaning to a collective takes enormous effort; 〈役割〉 is not omnipotent. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 268]`

#### 〈信頼〉 (Trust) — the principle

**Background (Luhmann)**: Uegaki cites Luhmann's definition of trust as "complexity reduction" — we reduce the overwhelming uncertainty of possibilities by choosing to trust, accepting the risk of betrayal in exchange for the ability to act. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 268]`

**Uegaki's multi-layered typology of trust** (departing from Luhmann's purely functional account):
- Trust in the regular world (*素朴世界への信頼*): e.g., the sun will rise tomorrow
- Trust in shared meaning (*共有された意味に対する信頼*): e.g., others understand "red" as "stop"
- Trust in function (*機能に対する信頼*): e.g., the alarm clock will ring

All three of these are **non-personal trust** (非人格的な信頼). `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 268–269]`

The 〈共同〉-relevant form of trust is entirely different: **人間に対する〈信頼〉** (trust toward human beings), which has three sub-forms:

1. **具体的な他者に対する信頼** (Trust toward the concrete other): Built through the process of "revealing the 'weight' of the I behind 〈間柄〉" — i.e., through real confrontation via the 〈我-汝構造〉. This requires risk (vulnerability, possible betrayal) but when it succeeds, it leaves irreplaceable trust. Trust is mutual: when I feel trusted, I trust back. This forms a "chain of trust" (信頼の連鎖) with binding power (絆). `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 269–270]`

2. **集団的に共有された人間一般に対する〈信頼〉** (Collectively shared trust toward people in general): Built through "solidarity/cohesion" (結束) — creating an environment where honesty and diligence benefit one and dishonesty costs one. Uegaki argues: in communities where "primitive evil" (素朴な〈悪〉) — the seeds of avarice, envy, deception latent in all humans — is not countered, the honest are disadvantaged and the dishonest thrive, making communality impossible. Collective trust requires the shared will to maintain an environment that rewards honesty. But "solidarity" is also dangerous when evil is introduced — can produce unjust group coercion. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 270–271]`

3. **人間という存在に対する〈信頼〉** (Trust toward human existence itself): Trust in the *meaning* of what human beings are — not just the people around me, but humans through history: those who bore burdens, confronted evils, lived strenuously, and built the human world. This is the trust that makes it possible to carry something beyond worldly convention (the "role that transcends era") because one trusts that someone, somewhere, has done similarly. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 271–272]`

#### 〈許し〉 (Forgiveness) — the principle

**Etymology**: Japanese 許す derives from "loosening what is firmly bound" — hence: releasing vigilance, granting permission, releasing from guilt/duty.

**Why 〈許し〉 is indispensable to 〈共同〉**:

First, in 〈関係性〉: smooth relations require that people maintain appropriate 〈距離〉 — neither intruding fully nor demanding total transparency from the other. This requires: understanding that everyone has things they need to keep private; accepting the 〈間柄〉 framework as the legitimate "partition" behind which the "I" can hide; and being willing to show the "weight" of one's "I" in measured ways. This is **〈距離〉の自在さに関わるものとしての〈許し〉** ("forgiveness related to the fluency of distance"). `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 272]`

Second, in 〈共同〉 itself: human life necessarily involves failure. A group that can forgive failure-among-participants can through that forgiveness also cultivate "trust toward people in general." Conversely: a group that cannot forgive failure becomes one where everyone fears failing, no one willingly bears burden, and communality's sustainability is destroyed. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 272–273]`

**Critical qualification**: 〈許し〉 must not be confused with 博愛主義 (universal altruism). Unlimited forgiveness leads people to take each other for granted, destroying trust. Forgiveness as craft-and-wisdom requires that **both parties bear responsibility for what was forgiven and what one was forgiven for** — the forgiver must at times act with firmness rather than taking the easy road of blind tolerance. Forgiveness without this responsibility becomes mere flattery (媚び), damaging both 〈関係性〉 and 〈共同〉. And forgiveness mechanisms can escalate into violence if the "sanction" maintaining forgiveness goes too far. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 273]`

**Table (p. 274)**: Uegaki presents a summary table of the three principles and their "inverted/distorted forms":
- 〈役割〉: inverted as "bearing 〈生〉 as a burden" — roles as oppressive imposition rather than taken-on meaning
- 〈信頼〉: inverted as (i) personal trust degrading into "flattery" / "unreasonable cohesion with primitive evil" / "unreasonable risk escalation"; (ii) impersonal trust that is indifferent to persons
- 〈許し〉: inverted as "forgiveness that forgets its accountability" `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 274]`

**→ THESIS RELEVANCE**: The three principles are not free-standing virtues. They are *responses to the inescapability of communality*. Because communality was unavoidable, humans developed these craft-and-wisdom mechanisms. The argument running through §5 establishes that all three require confronting "the other who does not bend to one's will" — i.e., they presuppose real alterity, real burden, real risk. This is exactly what the social apparatus takes over: when 〈役割〉, 〈信頼〉, 〈許し〉 are outsourced to 〈社会的装置〉, the condition that gave rise to them (the inescapability of encountering the genuinely other) is dissolved.

### §6 〈共同〉破綻と「不介入の倫理」 (pp. 273–282)
**Argument function**: Show how, under 〈生の自己完結化〉, all three conditions and all three craft-and-wisdom principles have collapsed — leading to 「不介入の倫理」 as a "strategic" coping mechanism, which is itself already in breakdown.

#### The structural collapse of 〈共同〉 conditions in contemporary society

**Condition 1 (shared facts)**: In a 〈ユーザー〉-society, people realize the three moments of "human life" (生存/現実存在/継承) substantially through 〈社会的装置〉, without real-flesh others. The people who "matter" are reduced to those mediated by 〈社会的装置〉 (economic partners, digitally arranged contacts); the vast majority of "others" are disconnected. Moreover: with money and 〈社会的装置〉, life can be "self-completed" — there is simply no *reason* (理由) to take on the burden/risk of 〈共同〉 with others. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 274–275]`

**Condition 2 (shared meaning)**: The 〈ユーザー〉-subject reduces the purpose of 〈生〉 to "self-realization" premised on the "authentic self." In this frame, 〈他者存在〉 is either a *resource* for self-realization or an *obstacle* suppressing the "authentic me." What is desired is the "other who bends to one's will" — an other who responds instantly, accepts everything without critique, never intrudes. In a society where everyone fantasizes about this "compliant other," no one can find meaning in confronting the genuinely "unruly other" as 〈共同〉 requires. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 275]`

**Condition 3 (shared skills)**: People raised in a 〈ユーザー〉 society have simply not developed the skills of burden-sharing with the genuinely other. They are unaccustomed to working through position-interest-feeling differences, to finding mutually acceptable distributions. In practice, people often cannot even bear the burden *of the process of burden-sharing itself* — and when they face "primitive evil" in others, they collapse, unable to act. The collective "skills for communality" that groups once maintained have become atrophied. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 275–276]`

#### The collapse of 〈役割〉, 〈信頼〉, 〈許し〉

- **〈役割〉**: The "zero-attribute ethics" (ゼロ属性の倫理) established in Ch. 7 — the ethic that denies bringing any social attribute into 〈関係性〉, demanding that each person face every other as "the irreplaceable this-I" — makes all 〈間柄〉 a form of oppression. Where every assigned 〈間柄〉 is suppression, there is no space for "taking on the inescapable as 〈役割〉" and thus no bearer-of-life (担い手としての〈生〉). `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 276]`

- **〈信頼〉**: The dominant trust in contemporary society is powerful *impersonal trust* (非人格的な信頼) — trust in the reliable function of 〈社会的装置〉. Against this baseline, personal human beings are *relatively* unreliable (unpredictable, inconsistent). The rational response is to improve the apparatus, not to trust persons. In this environment, no one wants to "trust the unruly other." `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 276–277]`

- **〈許し〉**: The "zero-attribute ethics" produces "0-or-1 〈関係性〉" — relations with no graduated distance, no legitimate partition. People cannot maintain appropriate 〈距離〉; they either annihilate it (total self-disclosure demanded) or erect total barriers. There is no space for the "forgiveness related to fluency of distance." Moreover: contemporary people cannot forgive others' failures. For the "self-completed 〈ユーザー〉," 〈関係性〉 is always temporary, lacking inherent continuity — so the connection between "I forgive / I am forgiven" and mutual responsibility is not directly felt. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 277]`

**Summary verdict (p. 278)**: We live in an era in which *communality has become more difficult than ever before*. But — and this is the pivot — from another angle: **we live in an era in which we are more liberated from communality than ever before**. The 〈ユーザー〉-life has dramatically shrunk the occasions on which we *must* engage in communality. The "utopia" of the 100-person village — once impossible — has in a certain sense been realized.

#### 「不介入の倫理」 — the ethics of non-interference

**Definition**: The ethics of non-interference = "refusing mutual interference with one another in exchange for each bearing full responsibility for one's own life." Colloquially: "I am not troubling anyone, so you must not trouble me" / reflexively: "I am not troubled by anyone, so I must not trouble you." `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 278–279]`

**Context of operation**: Operative primarily in face-to-face encounters between 〈ユーザー〉-subjects outside the 〈社会的装置〉 framework. Also operative within 〈社会的装置〉 contexts as the posture of keeping "economic activity 〈間柄〉" insulated from 〈我-汝構造〉 intrusion.

**Uegaki's characterization**: 
- This ethics is appropriate to the contemporary era — it fits the conditions of a society in which avoiding the burden of 〈共同〉 is actually possible for most people.
- Its background causes: (i) strong impersonal trust makes personal humans relatively untrustworthy → people become increasingly reluctant to build new 〈関係性〉 or confront others via 〈我-汝構造〉; (ii) "0-or-1 〈関係性〉" exhausts people with "bottomless consideration" and "survival-stakes mutual destruction" → people give up on building "meaningful 〈関係性〉" and eventually abandon all 〈関係性〉 not mediated by 〈社会的装置〉.
- This is not necessarily a choice these people *wanted* to make. But for those worn out by the "monstrous other," distancing from 〈他者存在〉 to secure safety is not irrational. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 279–280]`
- Through 〈共同〉 burden-avoidance and 「不介入」, people achieve liberation from the burden of 〈関係性〉 itself — they become, in a sense, "truly free." `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 280]`

**The breakdown of 「不介入」 itself**:
Uegaki argues that even this last-resort strategy is already in collapse. The reason: however liberated people may appear, communality and the encounter with the genuinely unruly other *cannot be fully eliminated from life*. "Liberation is still incomplete." There will always come a time when one must shoulder the burden of 〈共同〉 or 〈関係性〉.

Contemporary society is already full of the "fraying" (ほころび) that 不介入 produces:
- People cannot distribute burden with others and try to carry everything alone, collapsing under the weight.
- They cannot accept others' "intrusion/help" because in a society that forbids interference, why would someone else accept my "intruding on them" just because I am in difficulty?
- The pain of sharing burden in 〈共同〉 feels greater than the pain of carrying it alone. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 280–281]`

**The final irony** (p. 281): Caught in the "0-or-1 〈関係性〉," failing there, fleeing into 不介入, failing there too — *this is the end-state of a society without 〈役割〉, 〈信頼〉, and 〈許し〉*. And there is a further irony: because we live in an era liberated from 〈共同〉, when 〈共同〉 does arrive, we are *even less able to bear its weight* than people who never escaped it. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 281]`

**Closing vision** (p. 282): Uegaki asks: in a world ordered by 〈社会的装置〉, in which 〈間柄〉, 〈役割〉, 〈信頼〉, 〈許し〉 no longer exist, where people still fantasize about the "other who bends to one's will" for small consolation — *is there salvation (救い) for human existence?*

**→ THESIS RELEVANCE (Ch IV §A + §F)**: This is the argument's payoff for the thesis. The Enlightenment's positive-liberty demand cannot be realized in real communality without either suppression or the outsourcing of burden. When outsourcing wins — through the social apparatus — 役割/信頼/許し are displaced from the human into the apparatus. The 不介入 ethics is the *cultural symptom* of this displacement: the attempt to manage what remains of unavoidable human contact by minimizing it to the point of mutual non-existence. That it is itself collapsing shows that self-finishing is *propelled* by the Enlightenment paradox — it is the immanent trajectory, not an aberration. This is Uegaki's own framing of the dynamic. **Ch IV §F** (immanent reframe) should draw directly on this: self-finishing is not the failure of positive liberty but its completion — the only consistent realization of the demand for unconditional self-directed freedom.

### §7 第三中間考察 ── 諸概念の整理 (pp. 282–288+)
**Argument function**: Integrate the 〈関係性〉 analysis of the chapter with the earlier 〈自己完結化〉/〈脱身体化〉 analysis, producing a unified anthropological diagnosis.

Key integrations:

1. **〈根源的葛藤〉 reread as 〈関係性〉 burden**: What the book previously called "primal conflict" (根源的葛藤) — the tension between individual biological interest and collective-survival interest — is now re-described at the 〈関係性〉 level as "内的緊張" (internal tension) in the 〈我-汝構造〉. The mechanisms of 〈間柄〉 and 〈距離〉 exist to *manage* this tension. In modernity, the "zero-attribute ethics" disables 〈間柄〉, leaving the primal conflict "naked" (むき出し) — this is the background of 〈関係性の病理〉. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 283–284]`

2. **〈間柄〉 as the relational expression of 「意味体系=世界像」**: The vast system of 〈間柄〉 and 〈間柄規定〉 in use is not arbitrary — it is the accumulated historical transmission of the "meaning-system = world-image" that forms the 〈社会〉 as "the stage apparatus of 〈生〉." The rise of functionally specialized 〈社会的装置〉 shrinks the "meaning-system = world-image" and with it disables most 〈間柄〉 except the powerful "provider/consumer" type. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 284]`

3. **Why 〈共同〉の動機 (motivation for communality) has disappeared**: Communality requires all three conditions; those conditions require the "human basis" of 〈役割〉/〈信頼〉/〈許し〉. 〈生の自己完結化〉 has broken all three conditions AND eroded the craft-and-wisdom. So of course there is no motivation for communality. This is not a failure of individual virtue but a structural consequence. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 284–285]`

4. **〈関係性の病理〉 diagnosed in full**: It is the result of: (a) spread of "0-or-1 〈関係性〉" in the absence of the human basis for communality, plus (b) the generalization of 「不介入の倫理」 as its reaction-formation. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 285]`

5. **〈存在の強度〉 and self-existence**: A self (〈自己存在〉) can exist as "this-I" only because it has "meaningful 〈関係性〉" with countless others — this is the 〈関係性の場〉. Being "rooted" in 〈存在の連なり〉 means being a "point" (点) within the temporally and spatially extended field of 〈他者存在〉. The 〈ユーザー〉-subject loses this rootedness and thus loses 〈存在の強度〉 — the capacity to affirm one's own existence with conviction. `[VERIFY-PAGE: book p. 285–287]`

6. **Closing warning** (p. 287–288): The fundamental problem of 〈生の混乱〉 is not the expansion of body-modification technology in itself but the *transformation of human self-understanding* that the 〈ユーザー〉-life produces — the ideology of "infinite 〈生〉" that believes all desires can and must be realized, and that *anything blocking this* must be eliminated. This is the human-worldview mutation that makes 〈生の混乱〉 dangerous, not the technology as such.

## (d) Relevance Tags

### Relevance to Ch IV §A — The Paradox Spine

The entire chapter *is* the argument for the thesis Ch IV §A spine:

1. **The Enlightenment demand** (positive liberty: freedom-of-each-through-all) is concretely analyzed by Uegaki as the "free-solidarity theory of communality" — which is the Rousseau→Kant→Fromm line claiming that a mature will can achieve a "higher will" in which self-freedom and social-freedom are harmonized.

2. **The dual demand unrealisable**: Uegaki demonstrates, through the thought experiments, that in *any* real communality, burden must be shouldered by persons against their immediate will. Positive liberty requires that all action be voluntarily self-chosen. These cannot coexist without either coercion or the outsourcing of burden.

3. **The "magical rhetoric" judgment**: Uegaki explicitly calls the dialectical synthesis of freedom-and-communality a "magical rhetoric" (魔術的なレトリック) that conceals the unavoidability of burden. This is the exact language needed for Ch IV §A to characterize the theoretical tradition the thesis is critiquing.

4. **The necessity of 博愛主義**: Uegaki makes explicit that positive liberty, if generalized, requires universal altruism — and this is manifestly contrary to human reality. The thesis can cite this as the precise demonstration that the positive-liberty program is *not a realization but a demand for superhuman virtue*.

5. **Self-finishing as immanent**: The social apparatus "utopia" passage (point 6 above) is the key textual locus: Uegaki *himself* points out that the only world in which unconditional freedom-with-communality is possible is one in which 〈社会的装置〉 shoulders all burden — i.e., 〈自己完結社会〉 taken to the limit. This makes self-finishing *the immanent logical endpoint of the positive-liberty demand*, not a deviation. The thesis can cite this directly.

### Relevance to Ch IV §F — Immanent Reframe

1. **不介入の倫理 as propelled outcome**: The non-interference ethics is not an aberration or a failure of virtue. It is the logical "strategy" that emerges from (a) the collapse of communality conditions + (b) the generalization of 〈ユーザー〉-life. Uegaki himself frames it as a "战略" that people "编みだしてきた" — compiled under pressure. The thesis Ch IV §F should use this to argue: non-interference is the cultural correlate of self-finishing; both are the Enlightenment's own children.

2. **Liberation from communality = Enlightenment's dream**: Uegaki says explicitly that the 〈ユーザー〉-society has realized the "dream" of liberation from "endurance and sorrow" that people have longed for since antiquity — the "utopia" of the 100-person village. This is a *positive* characterization, not dismissal. Ch IV §F needs to hold this: self-finishing is not simply failure; it is the partial realization of a genuine human aspiration — which is why it is so hard to critique from within Enlightenment terms.

3. **The final question as Ch IV §F's opening**: Uegaki closes §6 with the question: "Is there salvation for human existence in this world?" Ch IV §F's task is to answer — not from outside the Enlightenment but from its own deepest structure (the squeeze ontology, the remaining necessity of communality, the possibility of 〈役割〉/〈信頼〉/〈許し〉 being re-inhabited rather than outsourced).

## Final Report: 10 Bullets — Step-by-step Argument

1. **The idyllic-dialectical theory of communality** (牧歌主義的-弁証法的共同論) is the dominant ideological formation Uegaki targets: it unifies (a) a naturalist view that communality is humanity's essence, (b) a critique of pre-modern community as suppressing individuality, and (c) a dialectical claim that modern freedom and communality can be synthetically unified (Aufhebung). Its contemporary form is the 1990s–2000s association/public-sphere discourse. [VERIFY-PAGE: p. 249–256]

2. **The "magical rhetoric" of dialectics**: The Aufhebung of freedom and communality *looks* compelling but provides no answer to the fundamental question: why should mutually contradictory things be synthesizable? The association discourse was full of pre-established-harmony praise with no realistic account of how associations sustain themselves. Dialectics here is "magical rhetoric." [VERIFY-PAGE: p. 254–256]

3. **The two thought experiments expose the core failure**: The "100-person village" shows that a community premised purely on voluntariness/freedom cannot sustain itself because burden-bearing has no necessary ground. The "cleaning rota" shows that once such a community must distribute burden, it necessarily generates compulsion, sanction, imperfect consensus — the apparatus of the very suppression positive liberty sought to eliminate. [VERIFY-PAGE: p. 257–260]

4. **Foundational proposition**: "Communality without burden is a fiction." The one world where burden-free communality is possible is one where 〈社会的装置〉 shoulders all unwanted tasks — 〈自己完結社会〉 at its limit. This means: self-finishing is not an exception to the positive-liberty ideal but its logical *completion*. [VERIFY-PAGE: p. 257, 259]

5. **Three conditions for communality**: (1) Shared facts — participants must recognize mutual benefit from the joint action; (2) Shared meaning — participants must understand *why* the joint action is worth the burden; (3) Shared skills — participants must collectively maintain the communicative and organizational competencies for burden distribution. None of these is guaranteed by voluntariness alone. [VERIFY-PAGE: p. 261–264]

6. **Positive liberty exposed as requiring 博愛主義**: The Rousseau→Kant thesis that a mature will reaches a "higher will" in which others' freedom is one's own requires, if universalized, that *every person* achieve this disposition — i.e., requires universal altruism. This is "manifestly contrary to human reality" and is just another form of magical rhetoric. [VERIFY-PAGE: p. 263]

7. **The three craft-and-wisdom principles — 役割/信頼/許し**: These are not moral virtues but practical mechanisms generated *because communality was inescapable*. 〈役割〉 = actively taking on one's inescapable position as something to find meaning within; 〈信頼〉 = accepting the risk of betrayal in order to build the chain of mutual trust that makes joint action possible; 〈許し〉 = maintaining the graduated distance that makes 〈関係性〉 inhabitable AND forgiving failures in joint action while holding both parties responsible. Each requires confronting the genuinely unruly other. [VERIFY-PAGE: p. 264–273]

8. **Collapse of all three conditions and all three principles under 〈生の自己完結化〉**: In 〈ユーザー〉-society: shared facts dissolve (no need for real others); shared meaning dissolves (others are resources or obstacles); shared skills atrophy (no practice of burden-distribution). Simultaneously: 〈役割〉 is blocked by zero-attribute ethics; 〈信頼〉 is displaced by powerful impersonal trust in 〈社会的装置〉; 〈許し〉 is blocked by 0-or-1 relations. [VERIFY-PAGE: p. 274–277]

9. **不介入の倫理 as propelled strategic response**: Non-interference is not chosen freely but compiled under pressure — the response of people exhausted by "0-or-1 〈関係性〉" who give up on "meaningful 〈関係性〉." It delivers "liberation from communality" which is the realization of an ancient human aspiration. But liberation is structurally incomplete: communality cannot be fully eliminated from life. When unavoidable communality arrives, those who have lived under non-interference ethics are *less capable of bearing it* than anyone who ever lacked that "freedom." [VERIFY-PAGE: p. 278–281]

10. **The final irony and thesis implication**: Failing in "0-or-1 relations," fleeing to non-interference, failing there too — *this is the end-state of a society without roles, trust, and forgiveness*. And it is the end-state *propelled* by positive liberty's own demand: because positive liberty cannot be realized in real communality, it drives toward outsourcing to the social apparatus; because that outsourcing dissolves the craft-and-wisdom of communality, the non-interference ethics becomes the only available coping mechanism; because that mechanism itself breaks down when unavoidable communality arrives, the human being who is "most free" is also "most fragile." Self-finishing is not the corruption of the Enlightenment project but its immanent completion. [VERIFY-PAGE: p. 281–282]
