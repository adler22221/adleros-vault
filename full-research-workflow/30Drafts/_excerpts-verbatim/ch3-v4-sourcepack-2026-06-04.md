### 1.1 — Goodman, *Ways of Worldmaking* — plurality + making of worlds
Route: **NLM** notebook "Nelson Goodman: Worldmaking and Critical Review" (`413ef2ca-...`), source `7ddb391b` = *Ways of Worldmaking*; one quote from source `46f9cb7a` = Putnam Foreword to *Fact, Fiction, and Forecast*. **HELD via NLM.** Citekey: see Zotero queue.

**(a) Multiple ACTUAL worlds — not merely possible alternatives** `[NLM-VERBATIM]` `[VERIFY-PAGE p.2]`
> "Let it be clear that the question here is not of the possible worlds that many of my contemporaries, especially those near Disneyland, are busy making and manipulating. We are not speaking in terms of multiple possible alternatives to a single actual world but of multiple actual worlds. How to interpret such terms as 'real', 'unreal', 'fictive', and 'possible' is a subsequent question."
— *Ways of Worldmaking*, "Words, Works, Worlds" §I,2 (p.2)

**(b) In what non-trivial sense are there many worlds** `[NLM-VERBATIM]` `[VERIFY-PAGE p.4]`
> "Since the fact that there are many different world-versions is hardly debatable, and the question how many if any worlds-in-themselves there are is virtually empty, in what non-trivial sense are there, as Cassirer and like-minded pluralists insist, many worlds? Just this, I think: that many different world-versions are of independent interest and importance, without any requirement or presumption of reducibility to a single base."
— *Ways of Worldmaking* (p.4)

**(c) "Worlds in the making" — from a world fixed-and-found to worlds made** `[NLM-VERBATIM]` `[VERIFY-PAGE p.x, Foreword]`
> "The movement is from unique truth and a world fixed and found to a diversity of right and even conflicting versions or worlds in the making."
— *Ways of Worldmaking*, Foreword (p.x)

**(d) Worldmaking = remaking; always starts from worlds already on hand** `[NLM-VERBATIM]` `[VERIFY-PAGE p.6]`
> "The many stuffs—matter, energy, waves, phenomena—that worlds are made of are made along with the worlds. But made from what? Not from nothing, after all, but from other worlds. Worldmaking as we know it always starts from worlds already on hand; the making is a remaking."
— *Ways of Worldmaking* (p.6)

**(e) If worlds are as much made as found, knowing is as much remaking** `[NLM-VERBATIM]` `[VERIFY-PAGE p.22]`
> "Furthermore, if worlds are as much made as found, so also knowing is as much remaking. All the processes of worldmaking I have discussed enter into knowing."
— *Ways of Worldmaking*, "Notes on Knowing" §1,7 (p.22)

**(f) Truth cannot be correspondence with a ready-made world** `[NLM-VERBATIM]` `[VERIFY-PAGE p.17]`
> "But truth cannot be defined or tested by agreement with 'the world'; for not only do truths differ for different worlds but the nature of agreement between a version and a world apart from it is notoriously nebulous. Rather—speaking loosely and without trying to answer either Pilate's question or Tarski's—a version is taken to be true when it offends no unyielding beliefs and none of its own precepts… [hence] truth must be otherwise conceived than as correspondence with a ready-made world."
— *Ways of Worldmaking*, §5 "Trouble with Truth" (p.17). [The closing clause "truth must be otherwise conceived…" is from NLM's answer text spliced to the verbatim block — `[VERIFY-PAGE]` confirm the exact join against PDF.]

### 1.2 — Descola — the four ontologies (ILLUSTRATIVE, not the exhaustive scheme)
Route: **NLM** notebook "Philippe Descola" (`e29675a3-...`). **HELD via NLM.** Multiple source texts (see citekeys table). A FEW key quotes; in the thesis these illustrate that naturalism is *one* mode among several, NOT the master template.

**(a) The four modes named (the canonical definition)** `[NLM-VERBATIM]` `[VERIFY-PAGE p.121]`
> "The recognized formulae for expressing the combination of interiority and physicality are very limited. Faced with some other entity, human or nonhuman, I can assume either that it possesses elements of physicality and interiority identical to my own, that both its interiority and its physicality are distinct from mine, that we have similar interiorities and different physicalities, or, finally, that our interiorities are different and our physicalities are analogous. I shall call the first combination 'totemism,' the second 'analogism,' the third 'animism,' and the fourth 'naturalism' (fig. 1)."
— *Beyond Nature and Culture* (p.121)

**(b) Naturalism = OUR ontology = single nature + multiplicity of cultures** `[NLM-VERBATIM]` `[VERIFY-PAGE pp.39-40]`
> "The last mode of identification, naturalism, corresponds to our own ontology. […] Now, if one considers naturalism—the coexistence of a single unifying nature and a multiplicity of cultures—not as the all-embracing template which allows the objectification of any reality, but as one among other modes of identification, then its contrastive properties appear more clearly. In particular, naturalism inverts the ontological premises of animism since, instead of claiming an identity of soul and a difference of bodies, it is predicated upon a discontinuity of interiorities and a material continuity."
— "Modes of being and forms of predication" (pp.39-40)

**(c) Naturalism as one mode among several (1996/2009 restatement)** `[NLM-VERBATIM]` `[VERIFY-PAGE pp.152-153]`
> "If one considers naturalism – that is the coexistence between a single unifying nature and a multiplicity of cultures – not as the all-embracing template which permits the objectification of any reality, but as one among several other modes of identification, then its contrastive properties appear much more starkly. First, it becomes clear that naturalism inverts the ontological premises of animism since, instead of claiming an identity of souls and a difference of bodies, it is predicated upon a discontinuity of interiorities and a material continuity."
— "Human natures" (2009), *European Association of Social Anthropologists* (pp.152-153)

> USAGE NOTE for drafting: present these as ILLUSTRATIVE — Descola's scheme shows the modern West's "naturalism" is provincial, one option among four. Do NOT present the four-fold scheme as the thesis's own exhaustive ontology.

### 1.5 — Harmony-as-squeeze-prevention (天人合一 / wu-wei)
Route: **NLM** notebook "Joseph Needham" (`1c1bfe7d-...`). **HELD via NLM.** Sources: *Science and Civilisation in China* vol.2 (`8d2da12e`); *The Grand Titration* / SCC vol.7.2 (`8eb70551`, `7c55bfb3`). Reading: wu-wei = NOT going against the grain of Nature → harmony that *prevents* the universe↔world mismatch (the opposite of the "disposition to domination").

**(a) wu-wei = "refraining from activity contrary to Nature"** `[NLM-VERBATIM]` `[VERIFY-PAGE vol.2 p.68]`
> "Practically every translator and commentator has adopted the unmodified word 'action', so that the expression wu wei, which became one of the greatest Taoist slogans, appears as 'non-action' or 'inactivity'. I believe that the majority of sinologists have been wrong here, and that the meaning of wu wei was, so far as the early proto-scientific Taoist philosophers were concerned, 'refraining from activity contrary to Nature', i.e. from insisting on going against the grain of things, from trying to make materials perform functions for which they are unsuitable, from exerting force in human affairs when the man of insight could see that it would be doomed to failure, and that subtler methods of persuasion, or simply letting things alone to take their own course, would bring about the desired result."
— *Science and Civilisation in China*, vol.2, §10d (p.68)

**(b) wei = forcing against intrinsic principles; wu wei = letting things follow them** `[NLM-VERBATIM]`
> "Wei, then, was 'forcing' things, in the interests of private gain, without regard to their intrinsic principles, and relying on the authority of others. Wu wei was letting things work out their destinies in accordance with their intrinsic principles."
— *Science and Civilisation in China*, vol.2

**(c) "production without possession, action without self-assertion, development without domination"** `[NLM-VERBATIM]` `[VERIFY-PAGE]`
> "Wei meant application of the force of will-power, the determination that things, animals, or even other men, should do what they were ordered to do, but wu wei was the opposite of this, leaving things alone, letting Nature take her course, profiting by going with the grain of things instead of going against it, and knowing how not to interfere. This was the great Taoist watchword throughout the ages, the untaught doctrine, the wordless edict. It was summarised in that numinous phrase which Bertrand Russell collected from his time in China, 'production without possession, action without self-assertion, development without domination'."
— *The Grand Titration* / SCC vol.7.2

**(d) Harmony as cosmic pattern from internal natures — NOT external command** `[NLM-VERBATIM]`
> "The Chinese world-view depended upon a totally different line of thought. The harmonious co-operation of all beings arose, not from the orders of a superior authority external to themselves, but from the fact that they were all parts in a hierarchy of wholes forming a cosmic pattern, and what they obeyed were the internal dictates of their own natures."
— *The Grand Titration*

**(e) 天人合一 textual anchor: opposing Heaven = fighting one's own nature** `[NLM-VERBATIM]` `[VERIFY-PAGE vol.2]`
> "And about +300 Kuo Hsiang, in his commentary on Chuang Tzu, wrote: 'Non-action does not mean doing nothing and keeping silent. Let everything be allowed to do what it naturally does, so that its nature will be satisfied.' Huai Nan Tzu couples this with a statement of the macrocosm-microcosm doctrine, saying, 'To promote plans which do not comply with the will of Heaven, is to fight against man's own nature.'"
— *Science and Civilisation in China*, vol.2

## §2 — Collapse + regeneration base

### 2.1 — Tainter, *The Collapse of Complex Societies* (1988) — declining marginal returns on complexity
Route: **NLM** notebook "Collapse literature" (`8ea4351e-...`), source `a818c8d7` = Tainter 1988. **HELD via NLM.** Page-pins are by chapter (NLM did not surface folio numbers) — `[VERIFY-PAGE]`.

**(a) Definition of collapse = rapid loss of established sociopolitical complexity** `[NLM-VERBATIM]` `[VERIFY-PAGE ch.1]`
> "Collapse, as viewed in the present work, is a political process. It may, and often does, have consequences in such areas as economics, art, and literature, but it is fundamentally a matter of the sociopolitical sphere. A society has collapsed when it displays a rapid, significant loss of an established level of sociopolitical complexity. […] The collapse, in turn, must be rapid - taking no more than a few decades - and must entail a substantial loss of sociopolitical structure."
— *The Collapse of Complex Societies*, ch.1

**(b) The thesis: returns on complexity follow a curve → diminishing returns** `[NLM-VERBATIM]` `[VERIFY-PAGE ch.4]`
> "It is the thesis of this chapter that return on investment in complexity varies, and that this variation follows a characteristic curve. More specifically, it is proposed that, in many crucial spheres, continued investment in sociopolitical complexity reaches a point where the benefits for such investment begin to decline, at first gradually, then with accelerated force. Thus, not only must a population allocate greater and greater amounts of resources to maintaining an evolving society, but after a certain point, higher amounts of this investment will yield smaller increments of return. Diminishing returns, it will be shown, are a recurrent aspect of sociopolitical evolution, and of investment in complexity."
— *The Collapse of Complex Societies*, ch.4

**(c) After a point, marginal returns decline and marginal costs rise** `[NLM-VERBATIM]` `[VERIFY-PAGE ch.4]`
> "It is suggested that the increased costs of sociopolitical evolution frequently reach a point of diminishing marginal returns. […] After a certain point, increased investments in complexity fail to yield proportionately increasing returns. Marginal returns decline and marginal costs rise. Complexity as a strategy becomes increasingly costly, and yields decreasing marginal benefits."
— *The Collapse of Complex Societies*, ch.4

**(d) Least-costly solutions exhausted first → further complexity ever more costly** `[NLM-VERBATIM]` `[VERIFY-PAGE ch.6]`
> "Thus, while initial investment by a society in growing complexity may be a rational solution to perceived needs, that happy state of affairs cannot last. As the least costly extractive, economic, information-processing, and organizational solutions are progressively exhausted, any further need for increased complexity must be met by more costly responses. As the cost of organizational solutions grows, the point is reached at which continued investment in complexity does not give a proportionate yield, and the marginal return begins to decline."
— *The Collapse of Complex Societies*, ch.6

**(e) Collapse as an economizing process; return to "normal" lower complexity** `[NLM-VERBATIM]` `[VERIFY-PAGE ch.6]`
> "Collapse then is not a fall to some primordial chaos, but a return to the normal human condition of lower complexity. The notion that collapse is uniformly a catastrophe is contradicted, moreover, by the present theory. To the extent that collapse is due to declining marginal returns on investment in complexity, it is an economizing process. It occurs when it becomes necessary to restore the marginal return on organizational investment to a more favorable level."
— *The Collapse of Complex Societies*, ch.6
> [Bridges to §2.2: "return to lower complexity," not annihilation — i.e. there is a floor to fall back to.]

> "Could the rebel armies of Li Zicheng have taken control of the Yellow River valley in 1641 had an epidemic not wiped out 70 percent of the population earlier that year, leaving the region undefended, for example? To decide which destroyed the dynasty—fiscal insolvency? rebellions? Manchu military might? the weather?—would exclude more truths than it would illuminate. At this particular conjuncture in China's past, their combination was what brought down the house of the Ming. Perhaps the greater puzzle than deciding which event destroyed the dynasty is asking how the Ming managed to survive as long as it did."
— Ch. 10, end of climate section (p.255)

### B.4 — Yuan / Ming / Qing as distinct orders (supports "Qing as a different AOS/world")

**(a) Three conquest/indigenous orders, two ruptures** `[PyMuPDF p.1]`
> "Two dynasties ruled China between the middle of the thirteenth century and the middle of the seventeenth. The first was the Yuan, founded in 1271 not by a Chinese but by a Mongol, the Great Khan Khubilai … The second was the Ming, created by the brilliant and ruthless Zhu Yuanzhang in 1368 and overthrown in 1644 by the next conquerors from the northern grasslands, the Manchus."
— Introduction (p.1)

**(b) The Yuan as a *rupture* — a different order, not continuous with the Ming** `[PyMuPDF p.81]`
> "Later political philosophers such as the Ming loyalist scholar Huang Zongxi (1610–1695) liked to isolate the Yuan dynasty as the most significant rupture since the unification of China by the Qin dynasty in 221 bc. … Huang was not wrong in regarding the Mongol invasion as a major rupture … But it was not a rupture that, once completed, somehow closed and disappeared when the Ming took over. The Mongols changed the course of imperial history precisely because those who defeated them perpetuated some of their norms."
— Ch. 4 "Khan and Emperor" (p.81)

**(c) 1644 = end of the Ming, NOT end of the imperial system; the Qing is the *next* order** `[PyMuPDF p.23]`
> "The end of the dynasty arrived in 1644 not from the ocean but, once again, from the steppe. But it was only the end of the Ming, not the end of the imperial system or the culture that supported it."
— Ch. 1 "Dragon Spotting" (p.23)

**(d) The people pass into "a new dynasty" — the Qing as a distinct regime, marked by bodily submission** `[PyMuPDF p.255]`
> "The people of the Ming came out the other side of the Chongzhen Slough to find themselves the subjects of a new dynasty. The routes to the world beyond 1644 were many … The vast majority accepted their fate, submitted to Manchu authority, and, if they were men, displayed their personal submission to the new dynasty by adopting the nomad hairstyle of shaving the front of the head and growing a queue at the back."
— Ch. 10 "Collapse," §"Living through the End" (p.255). Cf. Dorgon's framing of the Qing as claiming "the mandate of Heaven without having to exterminate the previous mandate-holders" (p.240): the *new Qing regime* is treated throughout as a discrete successor order, not a continuation of the Ming.
