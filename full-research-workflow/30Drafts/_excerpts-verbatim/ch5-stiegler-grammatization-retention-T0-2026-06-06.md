## 1. GRAMMATIZATION — definition + Auroux origin

### 1a. Canonical main-text definition (printed p.19)

[VERBATIM: Stiegler, *Automatic Society, Vol. 1* | p.19 | "Digital tracking technologies are the most advanced stage of a process of grammatization that began at the end of the Upper Palaeolithic age, when humanity first learnt to discretize and reproduce, in traces of various kinds, the flux and flow running through it and that it generates: mental images (cave paintings), speech (writing), gestures (the automation of production), frequencies of sound and light (analogue recording technology) and now individual behaviour, social relations and transindividuation processes (algorithms of reticular writing)."]

[VERBATIM: Stiegler, *Automatic Society, Vol. 1* | p.19 | "These traces constitute hypomnesic tertiary retentions."]

### 1b. Compressed restatement — grammatization AS discretization into tertiary retentions (printed p.29)

[VERBATIM: Stiegler, *Automatic Society, Vol. 1* | p.29 | "This tendency is the pharmacological expression of a process of grammatization of which digital technology is the most advanced stage. Grammatization, as noted above, is itself a technical tendency that goes back to at least the end of the Upper Palaeolithic, consisting in the duplication and discretization of mental experiences (that is, temporal experiences) in the form of hypomnesic tertiary retentions."]

### 1c. The "discretize" operation defined (translator's note, printed p.254, note 4 to pp.19–20)

[VERBATIM: Stiegler, *Automatic Society, Vol. 1* | p.254 n.4 | "Translator's note: The author uses the term 'discretize' to refer to every process that makes the continuous discrete, by taking something temporal and making it spatial, that is, material, such as, for example, the discretization of spoken language in the form of alphabetic writing, or the discretization of the gestures of the human hand made possible by the invention of the mechanical loom."]

### 1d. Auroux as the source of the term (printed p.260, note 66 to pp.32–6)

> This is the explicit attribution the target asked for: Stiegler takes "grammatization" (*grammatisation*) from Sylvain Auroux.

[VERBATIM: Stiegler, *Automatic Society, Vol. 1* | p.260 n.66 | "It is indeed because the question of technological shock lies at the heart of the genesis of tertiary retention that Sylvain Auroux can title his book on the relations between writing and the sciences of language *La Révolution technologique de la grammatisation* (Liège: Mardaga, 1994)."]

> Note: in *Automatic Society* Stiegler does not re-define the term as "from Auroux" in a single sentence in the body; the attribution is carried in this note (and the index entry "grammatisation … Auroux", pdf p.269). The conceptual debt is standard in the Stiegler literature: Auroux, *La révolution technologique de la grammatisation* (1994), originally analysing the discretization of speech into writing/grammar; Stiegler generalizes it to all discretization of temporal flux into reproducible traces. If a single-sentence "Stiegler borrows the term from Auroux" citation is wanted in clean prose, cite **`stieglerAutomaticSocietyVolume2016`, p.260 n.66** (+ Auroux 1994 as the primary origin if that book is later acquired — it is NOT on P:\books and NOT in Zotero).

## 2. TERTIARY RETENTION — definition + Husserl primary/secondary contrast

### 2a. Canonical definition (printed p.31)

> This is the tight, citable definition for [[chV-sim_v2]] §VC footnote `[^c3]` (currently "Page TBD"). It defines tertiary retention AND states its relation to Husserl's primary/secondary retention in one place.

[VERBATIM: Stiegler, *Automatic Society, Vol. 1* | p.31 | "Tertiary retention, whatever its form or material may be, artificially retains something through the material and spatial copying of a mnesic and temporal element, and thereby modifies – in a general way, that is, for all human experience – the relations between the psychic retentions of perception that Husserl referred to as primary retentions, and the psychic retentions of memory that he called secondary retentions."]

[VERBATIM: Stiegler, *Automatic Society, Vol. 1* | p.31 | "In the hyper-industrial stage, the noetic faculties of theorization and deliberation are short-circuited by the current operator of proletarianization, which is digital tertiary retention – just as analogue tertiary retention was in the twentieth century the operator of the proletarianization of life-knowledge, and just as mechanical tertiary retention was in the nineteenth century the operator of the proletarianization of work-knowledge."]

### 2b. Tertiary retention as pharmakon / mediation of retention–protention (printed p.32)

[VERBATIM: Stiegler, *Automatic Society, Vol. 1* | p.32 | "Any tertiary retention is a pharmakon in that, rather than creating new transindividual arrangements between psychic and collective primary retentions and secondary retentions, and therefore between retentions and protentions – which constitute the expectations through which objects of attention appear …"]

### 2c. Digital tertiary retention as the infrastructure of automatic society (printed p.15)

[VERBATIM: Stiegler, *Automatic Society, Vol. 1* | p.15 | "Since 1993, a new global technical system has been put in place. It is based on digital tertiary retention and it constitutes the infrastructure of an automatic society to come."]

## 3. THREE-STAGE GENERALIZED PROLETARIANIZATION (savoir-faire → savoir-vivre → savoir-théoriser)

### 3a. The three stages stated in one sentence (printed p.25)

> This is the definitive statement for [[chV-sim_v2]] §VD footnote `[^d2]`. Note Stiegler's English-translation triad is **work-knowledge (savoir-faire) → life-knowledge (savoir-vivre) → theoretical knowledge (savoir-théoriser)**, mapped to the 19th / 20th / 21st centuries.

[VERBATIM: Stiegler, *Automatic Society, Vol. 1* | p.25 | "The industrial societies that emerged from the Aufklärung are now being disintegrated by hyper-industrial societies, because the latter constitute the third stage of accomplished proletarianization. After the loss of work-knowledge in the nineteenth century, then of life-knowledge in the twentieth century, there arises in the twenty-first century the age of the loss of theoretical knowledge – as if the cause of our being stunned was an absolutely unthinkable becoming."]

### 3b. The three stages restated as the operators of each century (printed p.26)

[VERBATIM: Stiegler, *Automatic Society, Vol. 1* | p.26 | "… hyper-industrial societies are undergoing the proletarianization of theoretical knowledge, just as broadcasting analogue traces via television resulted in the proletarianization of life-knowledge, and just as the submission of the body of the labourer to mechanical traces inscribed in machines resulted in the proletarianization of work-knowledge."]

### 3c. Translation key for the three savoirs (printed pp.13, 20, 255)

- savoir-faire = work-knowledge / knowledge of how to do; savoir-vivre = life-knowledge / knowledge of how to live:

[VERBATIM: Stiegler, *Automatic Society, Vol. 1* | p.13 | "… it presumes life-knowledge [savoir-vivre, knowledge of how to live] and work-knowledge [savoir-faire, knowledge of how to do] that always exceed it …"]

[VERBATIM: Stiegler, *Automatic Society, Vol. 1* | p.255 n.12 | "savoir-vivre [knowledge of how to live, manners, mores, and so on]"]

- the savoir-faire → savoir-vivre sequence in producers vs consumers (printed p.255, note 12 to pp.20–1):

[VERBATIM: Stiegler, *Automatic Society, Vol. 1* | p.255 n.12 | "From the beginning of the twentieth century, and under the control of marketing, these instruments short-circuit the savoir-vivre of consumers, proletarianizing them, just as the short-circuits of savoir-faire proletarianized producers in the nineteenth century."]

> **Terminology note for the author:** Stiegler's third stage in *this* (Ross) translation is rendered "**theoretical knowledge**" / "the proletarianization of theoretical knowledge," NOT the French "savoir-théoriser" — that French form does not appear in the *Automatic Society* English text (a whole-document search returned zero hits). The scaffold §VD names it "*savoir-théoriser*"; if the author wants to keep the French triad savoir-faire / savoir-vivre / savoir-théoriser as a parallel set, that is a defensible reconstruction but should be flagged as the author's normalization, since Stiegler's published English says "theoretical knowledge." Safe citation: `stieglerAutomaticSocietyVolume2016`, p.25.

### 3d. "Algorithms quicker than reason" — the §VD `[^d2]` quote, verbatim (printed pp.113–114)

> The scaffold paraphrases this as algorithms "quicker than reason" that "short-circuit the work … in which any work of transindividuation consists." The exact sentence spans pp.113–114:

[VERBATIM: Stiegler, *Automatic Society, Vol. 1* | pp.113–114 | "Algorithms, which are quicker than reason and which therefore amount to automated understanding, short-circuit the work of creating the more or less discursive and deliberative nomenclatures, categorizations and indexations in which any work of transindividuation always consists …"]

Supporting (the short-circuiting of reason as synthetic faculty by digital tertiary retention, printed p.32):

[VERBATIM: Stiegler, *Automatic Society, Vol. 1* | p.32 | "… digital tertiary retention and the algorithms that allow it to be both produced and exploited thereby also enable reason as a synthetic faculty to be short-circuited thanks to the extremely high speeds at which this automated analytical faculty of understanding is capable of operating."]

### 3e. Theoretical-knowledge stage = proletarianization of the noetic faculties (printed pp.26, 31)

[VERBATIM: Stiegler, *Automatic Society, Vol. 1* | p.26 | "The proletarianization of theoretical knowledge is more precisely that of the noetic functions, conceived by Kant as intuition, understanding …"]

[VERBATIM: Stiegler, *Automatic Society, Vol. 1* | p.31 | "The proletarianization of minds and spirits, that is, of the noetic faculties enabling theorization and deliberation, is the proletarianization of the conditions of consistence of the life of the rational spirit in general …"]
