## TARGET 2 — Brentano, three-fold classification of mental phenomena

> The thesis-relevant point: intentional directedness is the **common mark of all three classes**, so "intended" reaching cognition + affect + conation is warranted by Brentano's own taxonomy. The famous §5 passage already enumerates the three modes in its examples — *presentation* (something presented), *judgement* (something affirmed/denied), and *love/hate–desire* (loved/hated/desired). The dedicated classification is Bk II, ch. 6.

> **[VERIFY: three-class structure — SEP "Franz Brentano" §, paraphrase of *PES* Bk II ch.6 | classes = presentations (Vorstellungen) / judgements (Urteile) / phenomena of love and hate (Phänomene der Liebe und des Hasses)]**
>
> SEP "Franz Brentano" (paraphrase, not block-quote): *"The fact that we can be directed towards one and the same object in different ways allows Brentano to introduce a classification of mental phenomena. He distinguishes three basic kinds: presentations, judgments, and emotions (which he refers to as 'phenomena of love and hate')."*
>
> Brentano's own summary form (standard translation, as reproduced in secondary literature — `[VERIFY-PAGE]`, confirm folio in Bk II ch. 6/ch. 8): *"There are three basic classes of mental phenomena: presentations [Vorstellungen], judgements [Urteile], and the phenomena of love and hate [or: emotions / movements of the feelings and the will]."* Every member of every class exhibits intentional reference to an object; the classes differ only in the **mode** of that reference (presented vs. affirmed-denied vs. loved-hated/desired). This is the load-bearing warrant: the directedness that defines the mental is **not** confined to cognition or to volition — it spans presentation (≈ cognition/attention), judgement (≈ assent/belief), and love–hate–desire (≈ affect + conation). → cognition, affect, AND conation, exactly the three reaches 意 must carry.

#### Authoritative secondary statement (SEP "Franz Brentano"), verbatim — use these to cite the three-class warrant safely:

> **[VERIFY: SEP "Franz Brentano" (Kriegel et al.), verbatim secondary statement of Brentano's *PES* Bk II three-class division]**
> - *"He distinguishes three basic kinds: presentations, judgments, and emotions (which he refers to as 'phenomena of love and hate')."*
> - *"Presentations are the most basic kind of acts; we have a presentation each time when we are directed towards an object."*
> - *"The two other categories, judgments and phenomena of love and hate, are based on presentations. In a judgment we accept or deny the existence of the presented object. A judgment, thus, is a presentation plus a qualitative mode of acceptance or denial."*
> - *"The third category … comprises emotions, feelings, desires and acts of will. In these acts we have positive or negative feelings or attitudes towards an object."*
>
> → The third class explicitly bundles **emotions/feelings (affect)** with **desires and acts of will (conation)** — both founded on, and directed like, presentation (cognition). That is the whole 認知/情/意 trilogy under one intentional roof. `[VERIFY]` because this is SEP's exposition, not Brentano's verbatim sentence; treat as authoritative-secondary. For a primary-text page cite, the dedicated division is *PES* Book Two, ch. 6 ("On the Classification of Mental Phenomena" / "The Principal Differences"); confirm folio in your edition before final.

## TARGET 3 — Kant, *Gemüt* + the three faculties

> Source used: **Cambridge Edition, *Critique of the Power of Judgment*, trans. Guyer & Matthews (CUP 2000)** — extracted verbatim from the PDF text layer (Ak. marginal pagination preserved). Two strata: the **published Introduction** (Ak. vol. 5) and the **First Introduction** (Ak. vol. 20, the unpublished draft printed in the same Cambridge volume). Both are citable; the published Intro (5:197–198) is the standard locus.

### 3a. Published Introduction, §IX — the faculties of the soul + the master table (Ak. 5:197–198)

> **[VERBATIM: Kant, *Critique of the Power of Judgment*, trans. Guyer & Matthews, CUP 2000 | Introduction | Ak. 5:197 | "In regard to the faculties of the soul in general…"]**
>
> "In regard to the faculties of the soul in general, insofar as they are considered as higher faculties, i.e., as ones that contain an autonomy, the understanding is the one that contains the constitutive principles a priori for the faculty of cognition (the theoretical cognition of nature); for the feeling of pleasure and displeasure it is the power of judgment …; for the faculty of desire it is reason …"
>
> Master table of the three faculties (Ak. **5:198**), verbatim layout:
>
> | **All the faculties of the mind** | **Faculty of cognition** | **A priori principles** | **Application to** |
> |---|---|---|---|
> | Faculty of cognition | Understanding | Lawfulness | Nature |
> | Feeling of pleasure and displeasure | Power of judgment | Purposiveness | Art |
> | Faculty of desire | Reason | Final end | Freedom |
>
> — `[VERBATIM]` (Cambridge text layer). The phrase **"faculties of the soul"** = Kant's *Gemütskräfte / Vermögen der Seele*; the table header **"All the faculties of the mind"** renders *Alle Vermögen des Gemüts* — i.e. **Gemüt names the whole inclusive mind that bears all three**. Threefold-division footnote (Ak. 5:197): *"It has been thought suspicious that my divisions in pure philosophy almost always turn out to be threefold. But that is in the nature of the matter. If a division is to be made a priori … the division must necessarily be a trichotomy."*

### 3b. First Introduction, §III — the canonical "three faculties" sentence (Ak. 20:206)

> **[VERBATIM: Kant, *Critique of the Power of Judgment*, First Introduction §III "On the system of all the faculties of the human mind", trans. Guyer & Matthews, CUP 2000 | Ak. 20:206 | "We can trace all faculties of the human mind without exception back to these three…"]**
>
> "We can trace all faculties of the human mind without exception back to these three: the faculty of cognition, the feeling of pleasure and displeasure, and the faculty of desire."
>
> — `[VERBATIM]` (Cambridge text layer, First Introduction = Ak. vol. 20). Title of the section is itself the warrant: **"On the system of all the faculties of the human mind"** (*System aller Vermögen des menschlichen Gemüts*). NB a Kantian marginal note in this section substitutes "of the subject" for "of the state of mind (*Gemüthszustandes*)" (Cambridge editorial note, Ak. 20:208) — direct textual evidence that *Gemüt* is the operative term for the mind/heart in this division.

### 3c. Bernard translation cross-check (J. H. Bernard, *Critique of Judgement*) — public-domain wording

> **[VERBATIM: Kant, *Critique of Judgement*, trans. J. H. Bernard | Introduction §III | (Ak. 5:198) | via Project Gutenberg eBook #48433]**
>
> "For all faculties or capacities of the soul can be reduced to three, which cannot be any further derived from one common ground: the *faculty of knowledge*, the *feeling of pleasure and pain*, and the *faculty of desire*."
>
> — `[VERBATIM]` (Bernard public-domain translation). This is the most quotable single-sentence form of the trichotomy and is the wording most often cited in the literature; pair with the Guyer&Matthews table (3a) for the modern citable edition. *(Bernard renders Erkenntnisvermögen as "faculty of knowledge"; Guyer&Matthews as "faculty of cognition".)*

## How this feeds Ch IV §IV.3.0

The chapter locks **意のままになる生 = "the as-intended life"** and stipulates that "intended" must carry the **full Sinic 意** — the mind-heart inclusive of **cognition, affect, AND conation** — not the narrow English "will" (per [[ch4-translation-decisions-2026-06-06]], ★ NEW LOCK, rationale #1). The mandatory first-use gloss names **Kant's Gemüt** and **Brentano's intentionality** as the twin Western anchors. These excerpts supply the one-time citation warrant:

1. **Gemüt = the whole inclusive mind that bears all faculties.** Kant's own master table (Target 3a, Ak. 5:198) is headed **"All the faculties of the mind"** (*Alle Vermögen des Gemüts*) and divides that whole into exactly three: **faculty of cognition / feeling of pleasure and displeasure / faculty of desire**. The First-Introduction section title — **"On the system of all the faculties of the human mind"** (Target 3b, Ak. 20:206) — and the editorial *Gemüthszustandes* note make explicit that *Gemüt* is the term naming the mind-heart as the seat of all three. → This is precisely the "mind-heart" gloss for 意: not one faculty but the systematic whole. **Cognition** ↔ 認知/理(reason); **feeling of pleasure/displeasure** ↔ 情(affect/emotion); **faculty of desire** ↔ 意/志(will/conation, motive). Kant gives the *architecture* of the inclusive mind; he licenses "intended" reaching all three faculties rather than collapsing to *Begehrungsvermögen* (desire/will) alone.

2. **Intentionality = the single thread that runs through all three.** Where Kant *divides*, Brentano *unifies*: the mark of the mental (Target 1, *PES* p. 88) is **directedness toward an object** — "reference to a content, direction toward an object." And that directedness is **not** peculiar to thinking or to willing: the §5 passage itself runs the modes — "in presentation something is presented, in judgement something is affirmed or denied, in love loved, in hate hated, in desire desired" — and the three-class division (Target 2) shows the **same** intentional structure in **presentations (cognition), judgements (assent), and the phenomena of love/hate (emotions, feelings, desires, acts of will = affect + conation)**. → Brentano is the gloss that *prevents* "intended" from sliding back to "will": intentionality is exactly the common form of cognitive, affective, AND conative acts. So 意のままになる ("reality coming to conform to the intending mind-heart") means reality conforming to the whole directed mind — what I cognize, what I feel/love-hate, AND what I will — not merely to my volition.

**Citation economy for the chapter:** cite **once at first use** of "the as-intended life":
- Brentano [1874] 1973: 88 (intentionality as the directedness common to every mental act) — Target 1a/1b;
- Kant, *Critique of the Power of Judgment*, Ak. 5:198 (Gemüt as the inclusive mind, divided into cognition / feeling / desire) — Target 3a; optionally the cleaner Bernard sentence (3c) or First-Intro sentence (3b, Ak. 20:206).
This discharges the `[VERIFY: Brentano … verbatim-check before prose]` flag in [[ch4-translation-decisions-2026-06-06]] rationale #1. (The Rosa *Unverfügbarkeit* anchor for the *anti-intended* is a separate flag, NOT covered here.)

> **Caveat for the author (mode vs. faculty):** Kant's third faculty is *Begehrungsvermögen* ("faculty of desire"), and his "feeling of pleasure and displeasure" is a *receptivity*, not itself a cognition. The thesis maps 意 onto the **whole Gemüt** (all three), which is correct; just don't equate 意 with the **desire faculty alone** (that would re-narrow it to "will" — the very error the lock guards against). Brentano's intentionality is the better single-word gloss for "the directedness of the whole 意"; Kant's Gemüt is the better single-word gloss for "the inclusive seat." Use them as a *pair*, as the lock already specifies.
