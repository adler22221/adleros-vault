## 1. Luciano Floridi (DEEPENED — page locators)

Floridi is the pivotal figure: he asserts a **marriage** of physis and technē (symmetric framing), but his deeper directional arrow runs **natural ← artefactual** (the *opposite* of the author's technē → phusis). Yet his **re-ontologizing** + **third-order, human-out-of-the-loop technologies** give the made an *internal, self-running principle* — which is precisely Aristotle's diagnostic mark of phusis. So Floridi is simultaneously the author's closest structural ally (on *mechanism*) and his sharpest directional rival (on *which term absorbs which*).

### 1a. *The Logic of Information* (2019) — "the natural is in itself artefactual" `[NLM: Luciano Floridi]`

> "I shall conclude that **the natural is in itself artefactual** (a semantic construction), and that the information revolution is disclosing a tension not between the natural and the non-natural, but a deeper one between a user's and a producer's interpretation of knowledge."
> — *The Logic of Information: A Theory of Philosophy as Conceptual Design* (OUP 2019), **Ch. 3 "Constructionism as Non-naturalism," §1 "Introduction: A Plea for Non-naturalism," p. 54.**

> "The naturalization of the non-natural turns out to be an expression of **the artefactual nature of the natural**. Ironically, naturalization is really a second-order semanticization. This seems to me the same road taken by John McDowell, with the only, profound difference that we need to take the opposite direction: **the non-natural is our first nature, and the natural is actually our second nature.** And this means that what we need is **a genealogy of the natural from the non-natural, not vice versa.**"
> — *The Logic of Information* (2019), **Ch. 3, Conclusion "The Artefactual Nature of the Natural," p. 67.**

> Surrounding context (p. 67): "either the natural is only a very minimalist and uncontroversial way to refer to data in themselves (noumenal) as constraining affordances, and this is uninteresting; or the natural—as we conceive and perceive it in a rich and multifarious way—is a construction, a model of the noumenal (reality in itself)."
> — *The Logic of Information* (2019), p. 67.

**Directional reading.** This is the explicit *artefactual → natural* genealogy: the non-natural (semantic, constructed) is *first*; the "natural" is a *second-order* product of our constructive (semantic) activity. The arrow is the inverse of the author's. NB the symmetry with — and inversion of — Adorno's "second nature" (§8): for Adorno, the historically-made *masquerades* as nature (critically, as imprisonment); for Floridi the genealogy is *affirmative* (constructionism as the human condition).

### 1b. *The Fourth Revolution* (2014) — re-ontologizing, infosphere, third-order tech, inforgs/onlife `[NLM: Luciano Floridi]`

**(i) ICTs modifying the very nature of reality (the re-ontologizing thesis, described though the exact term is not in these excerpts):**

> "In short, ICTs make us think about the world informationally and make the world we experience informational. The result of these two tendencies is that ICTs are leading our culture to conceptualize the whole reality and our lives within it in ICT-friendly terms, that is, informationally… **ICTs are modifying the very nature of, and hence what we mean by, reality, by transforming it into an infosphere.**"
> — *The Fourth Revolution: How the Infosphere Is Reshaping Human Reality* (OUP 2014), **p. 40.**

> "The infosphere will not be a virtual environment supported by a genuinely 'material' world. **Rather, it will be the world itself that will be increasingly understood informationally, as an expression of the infosphere.** … At the end of this shift, the infosphere will have moved from being a way to refer to the space of information to being synonymous with reality itself."
> — *The Fourth Revolution* (2014), **p. 49.**

> `[NOTE — term provenance]` The NLM corpus for *The Fourth Revolution* did not surface the literal token "re-ontologizing" in these excerpts; Floridi's explicit *definition* of re-ontologizing is in his other works (e.g. *The Ethics of AI*, and the *Philosophy of Information*). `[VERIFY: literal "re-ontologize/re-ontologizing" definition — not retrieved verbatim from Fourth Revolution; locate in Floridi 2011 PI or 2023 Ethics of AI before quoting the term as 4th-Rev language.]`

**(ii) Third-order technologies — no human in the loop (technology–technology–technology):**

> "For technology starts developing exponentially once its in-betweenness relates technologies-as-users to other technologies-as-prompters, in a **technology–technology–technology scheme** (see Figure 15). Then we, who were the users, are no longer **in** the loop, but at most **on** the loop… Or perhaps we are not significantly present at all, that is, we are **out of the loop entirely**…"
> — *The Fourth Revolution* (2014), **pp. 28–29.**

> "Essentially, **third-order technologies (including the Internet of things) are about removing us, the cumbersome human in-betweeners, off the loop.** … Technologies as users interacting with other technologies as prompters, through other in-between technologies: this is another way of describing hyperhistory as the stage of human development when **third-order technological relations become the necessary condition for development, innovation, and welfare**."
> — *The Fourth Revolution* (2014), **p. 30.**

> "ICTs can process data autonomously and in smart ways, and so be **in charge of their own behaviours**. Once this feature is fully exploited, the human user may become redundant. … we can already conceive a fully automated, computational system that may not need human interactions at all in order to exist and grow. … **A hyperhistorical society fully dependent on third-order technologies can in principle be human-independent.**"
> — *The Fourth Revolution* (2014), **p. 31.**

**(iii) Infosphere (definition):**

> "Minimally, infosphere denotes the whole informational environment constituted by all informational entities, their properties, interactions, processes, and mutual relations… Maximally, infosphere is a concept that can also be used as **synonymous with reality, once we interpret the latter informationally**. In this case, the suggestion is that **what is real is informational and what is informational is real.**"
> — *The Fourth Revolution* (2014), **p. 40.**

**(iv) Inforgs / onlife:**

> "The digital-online world is spilling over into the analogue-offline world and merging with it. … I prefer to refer to it as **the onlife experience**. … **We are increasingly living onlife.**"
> — *The Fourth Revolution* (2014), **p. 42.**

> "Rather, we are **informational organisms (inforgs)**, mutually connected and embedded in an informational environment (the infosphere), which we share with other informational agents, **both natural and artificial, that also process information logically and autonomously**."
> — *The Fourth Revolution* (2014), **p. 93.**

**Directional reading.** The third-order/human-out-of-the-loop passages are where Floridi most *supports* the author: a technosphere whose elements run *autonomously*, "in charge of their own behaviours," "human-independent," is a made-world that has acquired an **internal principle of motion and rest** — Aristotle's διαφορά of φύσις from τέχνη (φύσει vs. ἀπὸ τέχνης, *Physics* II.1). This is technē-becoming-phusis in *mechanism* even where Floridi's *labelling* runs the other way (he calls the result "infosphere/reality," not "second nature").

## 2. Bensaude-Vincent & Newman (eds.), *The Artificial and the Natural: An Evolving Polarity* (MIT 2007) — DEEPENED `[DISK]`

Citekey `bensaude-vincentArtificialNaturalEvolving2007` (Zotero key `9VRWG6VT`; attachment `XCDBPIP5`). The volume's whole thesis is that the polarity is *not stable but historically evolving* — neither simply collapsing nor simply holding.

### 2a. Introduction — the boundary is becoming less distinct

> "**With each passing day the traditional boundary between the natural and the artificial becomes less distinct.** … Are such living entities rendered 'artificial' by the human intervention that modified their genetic makeup? If so, does it not follow that hybrids produced by old-fashioned cross-breeding are human-made as well, and that **every tomato or pear that we eat is an 'artificial' product?**"
> — Bensaude-Vincent & Newman, "Introduction: The Artificial and the Natural: State of the Problem," **p. 1.** `[DISK: Zotero storage XCDBPIP5, PDF p. 9]`

> "But in reality this dichotomy has always been confounded by human activities… At the same time, **artifacts are never really unnatural. As physical and chemical systems they belong to nature** and generate a number of effects independent of the intentions of their designers. … '**Native forest**' in the sense of woodland absolutely untouched by humans **exists nowhere but in the human imagination.**"
> — Introduction, **p. 2** (PDF p. 10).

### 2b. Introduction — the "evolving polarity" thesis (the boundary shifts, does not vanish)

> "Perhaps the most striking result that emerges from the following chapters is the fact that while the opposition between art and nature is itself a major leitmotif throughout the history of the West, **the forms that this dichotomy takes are themselves far from stable or constant over the centuries. On the contrary, the divide is continuously challenged and reassessed.**"
> — Introduction, **pp. 3–4** (PDF pp. 11–12).

> "The pseudo-Aristotle of the *Mechanical Problems*… remarked that **mechanics works 'against nature' (para physin).** Centuries later, the spell of machines had grown so much in power that Descartes and his followers would reject this now-hoary dictum, claiming that **machines were not only not contrary to nature, but that nature itself was mechanical.**"
> — Introduction, **p. 4** (PDF p. 12).

### 2c. Introduction — Aristotle: technē imitates *vs.* perfects nature (the two-arts passage)

> "[in] book 2, chapter 1 of Aristotle's *Physics*. The Stagirite argues there that a bed cannot be natural… **The physis—or 'nature'—of the wood itself remains unaffected by the artificial form imposed on it by the carpenter.** But in a later passage… (chapter 8) Aristotle undermines this clear distinction, pointing out that there are **two sorts of arts—those that imitate nature and those that lead it to a greater state of perfection.**"
> — Introduction, **p. 5** (PDF p. 13).

> (Alchemical inversion, same page:) "the scholastic practitioners of that discipline were often wont to say that **they did not make artificial precious metals but genuine natural ones**, since they merely perfected the base metals, achieving what nature could have done… **products could sometimes serve in turn as macrobiotic medicines for humans.**"
> — Introduction, **p. 5** (PDF p. 13).

### 2d. Heinrich von Staden, "Physis and Technē in Greek Medicine" (Ch. 2)

> "**Natures differ greatly from natures.** —Hippocrates, *On Joints*" (chapter epigraph)
> "The recurrent Hippocratic distinction between a thing's visible, external form or appearance (its *idéa* or *eîdos*), its invisible but inferentially knowable capacities and susceptibilities (its *dynameis*), and its invisible '**nature**' (*physis*) is of considerable significance for some Hippocratics' understanding of the relation between nature and **technē**."
> — von Staden, Ch. 2, **opening** (PDF pp. 29–30).

> "In ancient Greek the semantic range of *physis* extends from 'origin,' 'appearance,' and 'natural constitution' to 'genitalia'… But in certain cultural niches, **physis became emblematic of nontraditional ways of investigating or conceptualizing the physical and biological worlds.**"
> — von Staden, Ch. 2 (PDF p. 30).

> **Counter-topos (technē as violence, not imitation):** "Here there is no trace of the view that **technē is a servant of nature, imitates nature, is a benign, co-operative extension of nature**… Rather, the representative of technē here argues in favor of the use of **violent compulsion** (*biastheîsa, biázetai, ekbiâtai*) by technē against nature."
> — von Staden, Ch. 2 (PDF p. 38).

**Reading for the thesis.** This volume is the author's best *historiographical* warrant that "technē becomes phusis" is not a category mistake but a *recurrent inversion type*: alchemists claiming their products are "genuine natural ones"; Descartes' "nature itself was mechanical." The "technē imitates physis" topos (Aristotle, *Physics* II.8, "ἡ τέχνη μιμεῖται τὴν φύσιν") is the classical seed; von Staden shows the Hippocratic counter-current (technē *against* physis). The volume's discipline: the polarity **evolves**, it does not simply terminate — a caution the author should absorb when claiming a *completed* collapse.

> Environmentalists' identification of environment with separate "nature" "**is itself a symptom of alienation**" — **(p. 89).**

The last line ("processes whose workings we do not fully understand") interestingly grants artifacts an *opacity/autonomy* that edges toward the author's "made acquiring its own principle." Vogel is a **rival on direction** but a *partial ally on the autonomy of the made*.

## 4. Keekok Lee, *The Natural and the Artefactual* (Lexington 1999) `[WEB]`

> `[NOTE — provenance]` The monograph is not in Zotero/P:\books/NLM (a *different* Keekok Lee item — a Laozi article and a blog post — is in Zotero). Verbatim below is from a published essay that quotes Lee directly (Casey Walker discussion PDF); these are **quotes-of-Lee**, not first-hand pagination. `[VERIFY: confirm exact wording + page against the 1999 Lexington print before citing.]`

Lee's distinction is explicitly **ontological** (not moral-only), turning on *independence from human intention*:

> Natural: "**comes into existence entirely independent of human intention.**"
> Artefactual: "**comes into being—into existence—solely by virtue of our human intention.**"
> Ontological definition: the natural is "**what is *not* the material embodiment of deliberate human intention**" and is independent of humans.
> "Every natural being has a '**trajectory**,' by which I mean the whole fact and history of a natural being… **independent of human manipulation and human control, and therefore of human intention.**"
> Technology's threat: our technologies allow us to "**transform the natural being and divert its natural trajectory in order to force it to do our bidding—to become the artefactual**"; an "ultimate process of '**humanization**': thus transformed, nature would… no longer be 'natural' but '**artefactual**.'"
> — Keekok Lee, *The Natural and the Artefactual* (1999), as quoted. `[WEB: squarespace Walker/Lee PDF]`

**Directional reading.** Lee is the **conservative pole**: she *defends* a sharp ontological criterion (independence-of-intention ⇒ natural; embodiment-of-intention ⇒ artefactual) and reads deep technology/biotech as **erosion by humanization** — i.e. natural → artefactual *as loss*. Crucially, Lee gives the author his sharpest *criterion*: if the diagnostic of the natural is "**not the embodiment of human intention**" plus an **independent trajectory/internal principle**, then the author's claim that the metaverse becomes phusis is the claim that the made has acquired *exactly these two marks* (intention-transcending autonomy + self-running trajectory). Lee is a **rival on valuation and (default) direction** but the **best supplier of the ontological test** the author must meet.

This is the *idea-level* end of the given. He is the genre-founder ("after the end of nature" is Vogel's subtitle). Direction is the inverse of the author's; mood is loss, not actualization.

## 6. Bruno Latour — *We Have Never Been Modern* (1993), *Politics of Nature* (2004), *Facing Gaia* (2017) `[NLM: Bruno Latour]`

Latour dissolves the nature/society poles *as poles*: the modern "Constitution" *purifies* nature from society while *proliferating hybrids*; therefore "nature" and "society" were never two distinct ontological zones to begin with.

### 6a. *We Have Never Been Modern* — purification vs. hybrids; the Constitution

> "The first set of practices, by 'translation', creates mixtures between entirely new types of beings, **hybrids of nature and culture**. The second, by 'purification', **creates two entirely distinct ontological zones: that of human beings on the one hand; that of nonhumans on the other**."
> — *We Have Never Been Modern* (1993), §1.1.

> "**Third guarantee: Nature and Society must remain absolutely distinct: the work of purification must remain absolutely distinct from the work of mediation.**" — §2.8 (Fig. 2.1).

> "the modern Constitution renders the work of mediation that assembles hybrids invisible… On the contrary… **the modern Constitution allows the expanded proliferation of the hybrids whose existence, whose very possibility, it denies.**" — §2.9.

> "As soon as we are on the trail of some **quasi-object**, it appears to us sometimes as a thing, sometimes as a narrative, sometimes as a social bond, **without ever being reduced to a mere being**." — §3.14.

> "**No one has ever been modern. Modernity has never begun. There has never been a modern world**" — §2.14.
> — *We Have Never Been Modern* (Harvard 1993). `[NLM: Bruno Latour]`

### 6b. *Politics of Nature* — "let go of nature"; nature plays no role

> "if '**nature**' is what makes it possible to recapitulate the hierarchy of beings in a single ordered series, **political ecology is always manifested, in practice, by the destruction of the idea of nature**." — **Ch. 1 "The End of Nature," p. 24.**

> "**There has never been any other politics than the politics *of* nature, and there has never been any other nature than the nature *of* politics.**" — **Ch. 1, p. 27.**

> "The modern world… **does not have the characteristics commonly attributed to it because it lacks nature entirely. *Nature plays no role in either world.***" — **Ch. 1, p. 45.**
> — *Politics of Nature* (Harvard 2004). `[NLM: Bruno Latour]`

### 6c. *Facing Gaia* — nature/culture are one concept, not two domains

> "the very expression 'relation to the world'… presupposes two sorts of domains, that of nature and that of culture, **domains that are at once distinct and impossible to separate completely.** … we are not dealing with domains but rather with **one and the same concept divided into two parts**, … bound together… by a sturdy rubber band." — **First Lecture, p. 15.**

> "**nature does not exist (as a domain); it exists only as one half of a pair pertaining to one single concept.** We must thus take the Nature/Culture opposition as the topic on which to focus our attention and not at all, any longer, as the resource that would allow us to get out of our difficulties." — **First Lecture, p. 15.**
> — *Facing Gaia* (Polity 2017). `[NLM: Bruno Latour]`

**Directional reading.** Latour is **symmetric-by-dissolution**: he does *not* run an arrow from one pole to the other; he denies the poles. For the author this is the **deep methodological caution AND opportunity**: Latour would say "technē → phusis" still presupposes the two terms the moderns invented. The author can either (a) accept Latour and re-describe the squeeze without the purified poles, or (b) hold that the metaverse re-instates, at a higher order, exactly the phusis/technē distinction Latour dissolved — i.e. a *neo-*artificiality that behaves like a *new given*. Latour is neither ally nor rival on *direction*; he is the **frame-level challenger** of the question's terms.

## 7. Gilbert Simondon, *On the Mode of Existence of Technical Objects* (1958 / Univocal 2017 trans.) `[NLM: Gilbert Simondon]`

**This is the author's CLOSEST ALLY on the directional claim.** Simondon's *concretization* is explicitly technē-becoming-phusis: the evolved technical object approaches "the mode of existence of **natural** objects," develops an *internal* circular causality, and "becomes naturalized."

> "the concrete technical object, which is to say the evolved technical object, **comes closer to the mode of existence of natural objects, tending toward internal coherence, toward a closure of the system of causes and effects that exert themselves in a circular fashion within its bounds**, and it moreover incorporates a part of the natural world that intervenes as a condition of functioning… **As it evolves, this object loses its artificial character.**"
> — *On the Mode of Existence of Technical Objects* (Univocal 2017), **Pt. I, Ch. 1 "Genesis of the technical object: the process of concretization."** `[NLM: Gilbert Simondon]`

> "Conversely, **technical concretization makes the primitively artificial object increasingly similar to a natural object.**" — Pt. I, Ch. 1.
> **Editor's footnote / manuscript variant to this exact sentence:** "**Variant: the object frees itself and becomes naturalized.**" — Ed. note (1958 manuscript correction).

> "since **the mode of existence of the concretized technical object is analogous to that of natural spontaneously produced objects, one can legitimately consider them as one would natural objects**; in other words, one can submit them to inductive study… They are no longer mere applications of certain prior scientific principles." — Pt. I, Ch. 1.

> "This simultaneously technical and natural milieu can be called an **associated milieu**. It is that through which **the technical object conditions itself in its functioning**. This milieu is not fabricated… it is a certain regime of natural elements surrounding the technical being… **The technical object is thus its own condition**, as a condition of existence of this mixed milieu which is simultaneously both technical and geographical. This phenomenon of **self-conditioning**…"
> — Pt. I, Ch. 2, §II "Technical invention: ground and form in the living and in inventive thought." `[NLM: Gilbert Simondon]`

**Directional reading.** Simondon runs **artefactual → natural** but *affirmatively and ontologically*: through concretization the made object acquires the **internal recurrent causality** and **self-conditioning** ("its own condition," "associated milieu") that are the hallmarks of φύσις (internal *archē*). This is *almost exactly* the author's mechanism — except Simondon predicates it of the discrete *technical object*, whereas the author predicates it of an *emerging total layer* (the metaverse/technosphere). The author's move = **Simondon scaled to a world-layer** and projected forward (emerging, not yet "fully actualized"). The author should foreground Simondon as the precedent that makes "technē → phusis" philosophically respectable rather than metaphorical.

## 8. Adorno / Lukács — "second nature" (brief, optional) `[NLM: Theodor Adorno]`

Included because it is the *canonical inverse precedent*: the made/historical coming to *appear* as nature. Direction = **historical(made) → (apparent) nature**, but **critically/illusorily**, not really.

> "But the **second nature**, philosophically raised again for the first time in Lukács's *Theory of the Novel*, **remains the negation of any nature that might be conceived as the first.** What is truly θέσει—produced by the functional context of individuals…—**usurps the insignia of that which a bourgeois consciousness regards as nature and as natural.** … The more relentlessly socialization commands all moments of human and interhuman immediacy, … **the more irresistible its natural appearance.** … **nature turns into an irresistible parable of imprisonment.**"
> — Adorno, *Negative Dialectics*, Pt. III "World Spirit and Natural History," **p. 357f.** `[NLM: Theodor Adorno]`

> "He calls **this world of things created by man, yet lost to him, the world of convention.** … Lukács envisioned the metamorphosis of the historical qua past into nature; **petrified history is nature**… **This second nature is a nature of semblance in that it presents itself as meaningful and its semblance is historically produced.** … **Second nature is, in truth, first nature.**"
> — Adorno, "The Idea of Natural-History" (1932 lecture), **pp. 261–267.** `[NLM: Theodor Adorno]`

> "**Under the essay's gaze second nature recognizes itself as first nature.**" — Adorno, "The Essay as Form," *Notes to Literature* I, **p. 20.** `[NLM: Theodor Adorno]`

**Directional reading.** "Second nature" is the **made-appearing-as-given** — structurally parallel to the author's claim, but Adorno's valence is *critical* (the appearance is *ideological semblance*, "imprisonment"). The author's thesis differs by asserting that the metaverse does not merely *appear* natural but acquires the *operative* marks of phusis (autonomy, self-running principle) — i.e., a *real* (not merely semblant) re-naturalization. Adorno is the precedent the author must *distinguish himself from*: not "the made looks given" (Adorno/Lukács) but "the made *operates* as given" (the stronger, Simondon-Floridi-flavored claim).

## 10. SYNTHESIS MATRIX — author × collapse-direction × accomplished/emerging

Reading conventions:
- **Direction** = which term ends up doing the work of the other. `N→A` = the (given) natural is absorbed into / re-described as the artefactual. `A→N` = the (made) artefactual acquires the character of the natural (**= the author's arrow**). `SYM` = symmetric marriage/reconciliation. `DISSOLVE` = both poles denied as poles.
- **Modality** = does the author present the collapse as already **accomplished**, currently **underway/emerging**, or merely **threatened/normative**.

### Where the author sits
- **The author's arrow (A→N, "technē → phusis") is the MINORITY direction.** The dominant contemporary move is **N→A** (McKibben, Vogel, Lee, Preston) — the *given* is absorbed/abolished by the *made*. Floridi's *genealogy* is also A→N but semantic, not ontic-operative.
- **Closest ALLY (directional + mechanism): Gilbert Simondon** — concretization = the made acquiring an internal, self-conditioning, "naturalized" mode of existence (φύσις's internal *archē*). The author's thesis ≈ **Simondon's concretization scaled from the technical object to an emerging total layer (metaverse/technosphere), projected as not-yet-"fully-actualized."** Secondary ally on *mechanism*: **Floridi's third-order, human-out-of-the-loop, autonomous technologies.**
- **Closest RIVAL on the directional claim: Luciano Floridi** — his explicit "**genealogy of the natural from the non-natural, not vice versa**" is the *named inverse* of the author's arrow; he is also the rival the author most needs to engage because they share the *re-ontologizing* mechanism but disagree on which term is primitive.
- **Frame-level challenger (neither ally nor rival on direction): Bruno Latour** — would reject the purified phusis/technē poles the claim presupposes; the author's defensible response is that the metaverse re-instates, at a higher order, a *new given* (neo-artificiality behaving as a new phusis), which is not the moderns' purified Nature but a *re-naturalized made layer*.
- **The author's distinctive signature** vs. all of the above: **asymmetric** (one arrow, A→N-as-real-phusis, not "marriage"/"dissolution"), **emerging** (not accomplished — the metaverse is "yet-to-be-realized"), and **driven by a specific technology** (the metaverse/technosphere, not biotech, not climate, not information-in-general).
