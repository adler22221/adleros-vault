## Q1 — Does "organizing the inorganic" engage physis / technē / nomos? **YES — but mind which book.**

**Key clarification (load-bearing for citation):** The organic / inorganic / **organized-inorganic / organizing-inorganic** vocabulary is Stiegler's (after Simondon — "organized inorganic matter"), and Hui's own developed statement of the **shift** lives in ***Art and Cosmotechnics* (2021)**, NOT in QCT-China. The systematic engagement with the Greek **physis / technē / (nomos / dikē)** triad lives in ***QCT-China* (2016)**. The NLM answer fused the two books; below they are kept apart so each citation is anchored correctly.

### 1a. The "organizing inorganic" itself (Art-Cosmo) — the cleanest verbatim definition

> "Since the rise of cyber­netics in the mid-twentieth century, critiques based on this dualism seem increasingly suspicious. The relation of technical objects to human beings, which we can call **the organized inorganic** (in the sense that it is the human being who organizes the inorganic and integrates it as its organ), becomes **the organizing inorganic**, meaning that instead of humans integrating tools, humans are integrated into technical systems, which have the tendency and capacity to totalize. The organism, as that which organizes the inorganic (through the invention of tools, which for Bergson is also that which distinguishes intelligence from instinct) seems to be losing its significance in face of technical systems."
> — Art-Cosmo, PDF p.247 (printed p.227). [VERIFY: confirmed | Hui2021ArtCosmotechnics | nlm-excerpt direct-PDF p.247]

> "From micro to macro physics, technology now forms a gigantic **'organizing inorganic' force or power**. **The inorganic is no longer organized by the human body as was the case with simple tools, but rather constitutes an enormous technical system we can only live inside of, while submitting to its rules.**"
> — Art-Cosmo, PDF p.244 (printed p.224). [VERIFY: confirmed | Hui2021ArtCosmotechnics | direct-PDF p.244]

NLM also surfaced the **Introduction** statement of the shift (attributed in NLM to the *Art and Cosmotechnics* source, source_id `b324ee9a`):

> "Organicism and organology, developed from the concept of organic, are constantly challenged by technological development. **What we are witnessing today is a shift from the organized inorganic to the organizing inorganic, meaning that machines are no longer simply tools or instruments but rather gigantic organisms in which we live.** [...] Instead, we are observing the becoming of an 'artificial earth,' and we are living within a gigantic cybernetic system in the process of forming."
> — Art-Cosmo, Introduction. [VERIFY: confirmed | Hui2021ArtCosmotechnics | nlm-excerpt:"What we are witnessing today is a shift from the organized inorganic to the organizing inorganic"]

And the "inorganic organicity" coinage (smart objects / milieu / profiling):

> "...the concept of the milieu is extended beyond human organs to analog and digital organs, constituting a new milieu characterized by **an inorganic organicity**."
> — Art-Cosmo. [VERIFY: confirmed | Hui2021ArtCosmotechnics | nlm-excerpt:"a new milieu characterized by an inorganic organicity"]

### 1b. HOW it ties to **physis** and **technē** — the Greek scheme (QCT-China)

Hui's physis/technē account is the comparative backdrop against which "organizing inorganic" becomes legible. The canonical statement:

> "The Greek word **physis** refers to 'growth', to 'bringing forth', 'the process of natural development,' the roman translation also carrying the connotation of 'birth', while **zi ran** doesn't necessarily carry this connotation of productivity — it also applies to decay or stasis. **For the ancient Greeks, technics imitates nature and at the same time perfects it. Techne mediates between physis and tyche (chance or coincidence).** This idea that technics might supplement and 'perfect' nature could not possibly occur in Chinese thought, since for the latter technics is always subordinate to the cosmological order..."
> — QCT-China, PDF p.88 (printed §, "physis"). [VERIFY: confirmed | Hui2016QCTChina | direct-PDF p.88]

The bridge from the Greek physis→technē relation to the modern "organizing inorganic": for Hui the Greek scheme is technē *mediating/perfecting* physis under simple-tool conditions; the modern threshold is where the inorganic ceases to be organized BY the human and instead organizes the human — the technical system becomes a "gigantic organism," an "artificial earth" (Art-Cosmo, above). I.e., the organized→organizing shift is the *collapse* of the Greek technē-mediates-physis arrangement.

### 1c. HOW it ties to **nomos** — Schmittian spatial order / "Nomos of the Digital Earth"

In **Art-Cosmo** the nomos link is explicit and tied to technology:

> "Schmitt's lament comes out of his aspiration for **a new nomos of the earth, while it also seems clear to Schmitt that such a new nomos is determined by technology.** Without atomic bombs and cyber­netics, however, the US wouldn't be able to maintain the nomos of the Western Hemisphere..."
> — Art-Cosmo, PDF p.78 (Introduction §6). [VERIFY: confirmed | Hui2021ArtCosmotechnics | direct-PDF p.78]

In **QCT-China** the nomos↔technology↔legitimacy link (via Schmitt's *Nomos of the Earth*):

> "**Technology, as nomos (appropriation and division), corresponds to a legitimacy that is prior to legality** in the sense that rationality is thus strongly tied to science and technology. [...] 'The second nomos of the earth arose from such discoveries of land and sea.'"
> — QCT-China. [VERIFY: confirmed | Hui2016QCTChina | nlm-excerpt:"Technology, as nomos (appropriation and division), corresponds to a legitimacy that is prior to legality"]

The Greek nomos–dikē–geometry nexus (the precise sense in which nomos is, for the Greeks, an *artificial* fitting-into a divine order) is given verbatim in QCT-China:

> "...if we refer to the ancient Greek moral theory, law (**nomos**) is closely related to **dike** in a geometrical sense. **Dike** means something can be fitted into the divine order, which suggests a geometrical projection: 'The **nomoi**, the body of rules introduced by the legislators, are presented as human solutions aimed at obtaining specific results: social harmony and equality between citizens. However, these nomoi are only considered valid if they confirm [conform] to a model of equilibrium and geometric harmony of more than human significance, which represents an aspect of divine dike.' [Vernant]"
> — QCT-China, PDF p.95–96 (printed §, "nomos" 75, 77–78). [VERIFY: confirmed | Hui2016QCTChina | direct-PDF p.95-96]

> **Answer to Q1:** Yes. Hui develops "organizing the inorganic" (Art-Cosmo) against, and as the modern *undoing* of, the Greek arrangement in which **technē** mediates/perfects **physis** and **nomos** (allied to **dikē**) imposes a geometrically-fitted human order (QCT-China). HOW: under the Greek scheme the inorganic is *organized by* the human (technē serving physis; nomos as human geometric order); the modern threshold inverts this — the inorganic *organizes* the human, and **technology itself becomes nomos** (the new "nomos of the earth," determined by technology). The triad is engaged across both books, with physis/technē/nomos-dikē worked out systematically in **QCT-China §§8–10** and the organized→organizing-inorganic shift stated in **Art-Cosmo (Introduction + Ch.3)**.

## Q2 — 器 (Qi) / 道 (Dao) cosmotechnics as a NON-Greek metaphysics of the artificial

All Q2 quotes are from **QCT-China** unless noted. Cross-checked against PDF text layer.

### 2a. Definitions: Dao (above form) / Qi (below form)

> "In the Xi Ci, a commentary on the I Ching, we read that '**what is formless (or above form) is called Dao; what has form (or is below form) is Qi** (形而上者謂之道，形而下者謂之器)'. In the same text, we read that 'if there is appearance, then we call it phenomena; if there is form, we call it Qi'. It is important to note that **xing er shang** (形而上, 'above form'), is used to translate the English word 'metaphysical'; and **xing er shang xue** (形而上學) is the study of it — **metaphysics**."
> — QCT-China. [VERIFY: confirmed | Hui2016QCTChina | nlm-excerpt:"what is formless (or above form) is called Dao; what has form (or is below form) is Qi"] (OCR in attachment renders the I Ching line as 形之上者為之道...形之下者為之器; the standard text is 形而上者謂之道，形而下者謂之器.)

> "Dao is what gives form and phenomenon; it is what is above them, as superior being. However, **Dao does not mean the 'laws of nature'** as this term was understood in seventeenth-century Europe; **it is rather the ungraspable that could yet be known.** [...] **Qi is also a container, a carrier, but not only a physical one; it also means specificity and generosity.** In The Analects of Confucius, one reads 'junzi bu qi (君子不器)' [...] 'the gentleman is not a utensil'..."
> — QCT-China. [VERIFY: confirmed | Hui2016QCTChina | nlm-excerpt:"Dao does not mean the 'laws of nature'...it is rather the ungraspable that could yet be known"]

### 2b. The Qi–Dao relation: inseparable; "obtain/manifest the Dao through Qi"

> "**Dao and Qi are not separable, just as shadow cannot be detached from shape.** The later scholars believed that the teaching of Confucius comes from the six classics, and thought that the six classics are where Dao dwells, **without knowing that the six classics are actually only Qi** [...] **how can one talk about Dao without Qi? — how can shadow exist without shape?**" [Hui quoting **Zhang Xuecheng**, *Wen Shi Tong Yi* / "Original Dao"]
> — QCT-China. [VERIFY: confirmed | Hui2016QCTChina | nlm-excerpt:"Dao and Qi are not separable, just as shadow cannot be detached from shape"]

> "...the relation between Qi and Dao has varied throughout the history of Chinese thought, following continual attempts to reunify Dao and Qi (**道器合一**), each with different nuances and different consequences: **Qi enlightens Dao (器以明道), Qi carries Dao (器以載道) or Qi in the service of Dao (器為道用), Dao in the service of Qi (道為器用), and so on.**"
> — QCT-China. [VERIFY: confirmed | Hui2016QCTChina | nlm-excerpt:"Qi enlightens Dao (器以明道), Qi carries Dao (器以載道) or Qi in the service of Dao (器為道用)"]

> "Thus the two basic philosophical categories of **Dao** and **Qi** are inseparable: **Dao needs Qi to carry it in order to be manifested in sensible forms; Qi needs Dao in order to become perfect (in Daoism) or sacred (in Confucianism)**, since Dao operates a privation of the determination of Qi."
> — QCT-China. [VERIFY: confirmed | Hui2016QCTChina | nlm-excerpt:"Dao needs Qi to carry it in order to be manifested in sensible forms; Qi needs Dao in order to become perfect (in Daoism) or sacred (in Confucianism)"]

### 2c. The definition of cosmotechnics (the single best verbatim)

> "Technics is not anthropologically universal; technologies in different cultures are affected by the cosmological understandings of these cultures, and have autonomy only within a certain cosmological setting — **technics is always cosmotechnics.** [...] I will give a preliminary definition of cosmotechnics here: it means **the unification between the cosmic order and the moral order through technical activities.**"
> — QCT-China. [VERIFY: confirmed | Hui2016QCTChina | nlm-excerpt:"it means the unification between the cosmic order and the moral order through technical activities"]

Restated mid-book:

> "...the concept of cosmotechnics developed in the introduction, which hinges on **the relation between the cosmos and the moral, as mediated by technics**..."
> — QCT-China, PDF p.135. [VERIFY: confirmed | Hui2016QCTChina | direct-PDF p.135]

### 2d. WHY this is a *different* metaphysics of the artificial than the Greek physis/technē/nomos scheme

The contrast quotes (QCT-China), verbatim:

> "**For the ancient Greeks, technics imitates nature and at the same time perfects it.** Techne mediates between physis and tyche (chance or coincidence). **This idea that technics might supplement and 'perfect' nature could not possibly occur in Chinese thought, since for the latter technics is always subordinate to the cosmological order: to be part of nature is to be morally good, since it implies a cosmological order which is also a moral order.** [...] Nor is there need of any violence in order to reveal the truth, as claimed by Heidegger of the ancient Greek conception; **one can only embody the truth through harmony, rather than discovering it through external means, as is the case for techne.**"
> — QCT-China, PDF p.88. [VERIFY: confirmed | Hui2016QCTChina | direct-PDF p.88]

> "...in the absence of this conception of 'the uncanniest of the uncanny' of the human, the violence of techne, and the excessive violence of Being, **in Chinese thought we find harmony** — [...] this fittingness resides in another kind of relation between humans and other cosmological beings, **one which is based on resonance rather than war (polemos) and strife (eris).**"
> — QCT-China, PDF p.~108. [VERIFY: confirmed | Hui2016QCTChina | direct-PDF "resonance rather than war (polemos) and strife (eris)"]

**Hui's own strongest formulation of incommensurability (directly load-bearing for the thesis reframe):**

> "**the Chinese have no equivalents of the categories that the Greeks called techne and physis.** Hence in China the force of technology dismantles the metaphysical unity of practice and theory, and creates a rupture, which still awaits unification."
> — QCT-China, PDF p.52. [VERIFY: confirmed | Hui2016QCTChina | direct-PDF p.52]

And the methodological warning against symmetry (Hui explicitly refuses to find "the equivalents of techne and physis in Chinese culture"):

> "...three things that I seek to avoid: Firstly, a symmetry of concepts, where one starts with corresponding concepts in European philosophy and Chinese philosophy — for example, identifying the equivalents of techne and physis in Chinese culture. [...] it is dangerous to take these as symmetrical relations."
> — QCT-China, PDF p.71 (§6, "Some Notes on Method"). [VERIFY: confirmed | Hui2016QCTChina | direct-PDF p.71]

## How this supports the thesis's comparative reframe

The thesis proposes to drop the formula "**nomos has always been technē**" in favour of: **nomos and technē are the two major categories the ancient *Greeks* treated as *artificial*** — and **other civilizations had different metaphysics of the artificial** (e.g., ancient China's **器/qi**, per Hui's cosmotechnics). Hui's material underwrites this in three ways:

1. **It localizes the Greek scheme as ONE cosmotechnics, not the universal grammar of the artificial.** Hui's axiom "**technics is always cosmotechnics**" and his claim that "technics is not anthropologically universal" do exactly the de-universalizing work the reframe needs: the physis/technē(/nomos–dikē) arrangement is *the Greek* metaphysics of the made, not the metaphysics of the made. The Greek nomos+technē can be presented, with Hui's warrant, as one civilization's way of marking off "the artificial."

2. **It supplies a concrete *different* metaphysics of the artificial — the 器/道 scheme — where the "made/instrumental" (Qi) is internal to, and obtains its meaning from, the cosmic-moral order (Dao), via "obtaining/manifesting the Dao through Qi."** Where the Greek artificial (technē/nomos) is *violent supplementation/perfection* of, and a *geometric fitting imposed on*, a nature/divine order it stands against, the Chinese artificial (Qi) is *subordinate-resonant*: technical activity unifies cosmic and moral order rather than conquering chance (tyche) or imposing dikē. So "artificiality" is carved differently — the Qi/Dao pair is a genuine alternative ontology of the made.

3. **Hui himself blocks the lazy equivalence the thesis is trying to retire.** His "the Chinese have no equivalents of the categories that the Greeks called techne and physis," plus his explicit refusal of conceptual *symmetry*, is precisely the lever against treating nomos↔technē (or technē↔physis) as a civilizationally neutral pair. This is direct textual support for "Greek nomos+technē = ONE civilization's metaphysics of the artificial, among others."

> Caveat for drafting: Hui frames the contrast primarily as **technē/physis** (and technē/dikē/nomos as the Greek *legal-cosmic* order); he does NOT himself phrase the Greek pair as "the two categories the Greeks treated as artificial." That framing is the thesis's own synthesis — defensible on Hui's material but [NOTE: the "two artificial categories" formulation is the thesis author's, extending Hui; cite Hui for the *cosmotechnics* claim and the *non-universality of technics*, not for the precise "artificial categories" wording].
