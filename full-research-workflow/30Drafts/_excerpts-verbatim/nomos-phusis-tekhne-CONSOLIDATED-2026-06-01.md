# SECTION A — ETYMOLOGY (the made / given / instituted, at the root)

> The single most consequential datum: the roots of τέχνη ("build") and φύσις ("grow/be") are **maximally opposed**, so "tekhne is becoming phusis" is a category-crossing of the deepest lexical kind. νόμος ("distribute/allot") sits on the made/instituted side with τέχνη.

## A.1 — φύσις ← *bʰeh₂u- "grow, arise, be"

**LSJ — sense-glosses (Perseus TEI `entry=fu/sis`, `<tr>` strings, retrieved 2026-06-01)** [A; fuller than BASE §3c]:
> **[I]** "origin," | "birth," · **[I.2]** "growth," · **[II]** "the natural form" | "constitution" | "as the result of growth" (**II.1** "nature, constitution"; **II.2** "outward form, appearance"; **II.3** "constitution, temperament"; **II.4** "nature, character" / "natural disposition" / **II.4.b** "instinct"; **II.5** "natural") · **[III]** "the regular order of nature," | "by nature, naturally," (**III.1** "nature as an originating power" / "principle of growth" / "Nature"; **III.2** "elementary substance" / "atoms"; **III.3** "creation, 'Nature'") · **[V]** "creature," | "creatures" · **[VI]** "kind, sort, species," | "class" · **[VII]** "sex,"
> — LSJ s.v. **φύσις**. Etymology head: **(φύω)** — built on φύω/φύομαι "to grow, bring forth, be born."

**Beekes — root + gloss (OCR-cracked from Brill 2-vol copy, English content clean, printed p. 1597)** [A §2a; supersedes BASE §3.6 "not retrieved"]:
> "φύομαι [v.] intr. med. 'to grow, arise, spring up, become', perf. (and aor.) 'to exist or be endowed by nature, be there', trans. act. (factitive) 'to make grow, beget, bring forth' (Il.). **<IE \*bʰeh₂u- 'grow, arise, be'>**"
> "4. **φύσις [f.] 'growth, character, descent, nature, being, etc.'** (χ 303) … Hence φυσικός 'belonging to nature, naturalist, physical, physician'…"
> — Beekes 2010, s.v. **φύομαι** (deriv. 4 = φύσις). Cognates: Skt. *abhūt* "he became", Lat. *fui*; Kortlandt: "the root had the form **\*bʰeh₂u-**."

**Dunshirn (OEPN "Physis", 2019) — etymology** [B §3.1]:
> "The abstract term physis is derived from the verb phyesthai/phynai, which means 'to grow', 'to develop', 'to become' (Frisk 2006: 1052; Caspers 2010b: 1068)."

**Barney (Encyclopedia.com "Nomos and Phusis", 2005)** [B §4.1]:
> "Phusis is the ancient Greek word for 'nature,' cognate with the verb 'to grow' (phuein)…"

**Cassin, *Dictionary of Untranslatables* (2014) — NATURE entry, Aristotle's own etymology (book pp. 703–704)** [E §2a; the single most on-point lexicon per the brief]:
> "'Nature' [phusis] means (1) the genesis [genesis (γένεσις)] of growing things [tôn phuomenôn (τῶν φυομένων)]—the meaning that would be suggested if one were to pronounce the u in phusis long. (Metaphysics, 5.4. 1014b 17–19)"
> — Aristotle, *Metaphysics* Δ.4 1014b17–19, quoted in Cassin 2014, "NATURE" (Pascal David). *Phusis at root = the genesis/growth of growing things — Aristotle's own gloss, independent of LSJ/Beekes.*

**HWPh — "Natur" (Bd. 6, Sp. 421–441; dig. [Sp. 21620]), the two pre-philosophical senses** [E §1c]:
> "Zwei Grundbedeutungen, die der Begriff ‹Physis› (Ph.) auch schon in vorphilosophischem griechischem Kontext hat … nämlich einerseits die Bedeutung (Beschaffenheit, Wesen) und andererseits die Bedeutung (Werden, Wachstum, Wuchs). Die bekannteste Bedeutung von Ph., wonach der Begriff das Insgesamt aller natürlichen Seienden in ihrem Wesen und dem Gesetz ihres Werdens und Wachsens meint, … ist eine Schöpfung der Philosophie des ARISTOTELES mit Anklängen schon bei PLATON."
> — HWPh "Natur," Bd. 6. *Two core senses — (a) constitution/essence; (b) becoming/growth — with the "totality of natural beings" sense a comparatively late Aristotelian creation.*

**Load-bearing:** φύσις = *that-which-has-grown / the self-arisen / what-there-is-by-being.* The etymological backbone of the "given/self-arising" pole. Cassin/Aristotle (*genesis of growing things*) and HWPh (the *Werden/Wachstum* sense) corroborate the LSJ/Beekes "grow/be" root from the lexicographic tradition.

## A.2 — νόμος ← νέμω ← *nem- "distribute, allot"

**LSJ — sense-glosses (Perseus TEI `entry=no/mos2`)** [A §1c; fuller than BASE §3a]:
> **[A]** "that which is in habitual practice, use" | "possession," · **[I]** "usage, custom," | "law, ordinance," (**I.b** "law" / NT usage; **I.c** "custom" / "by custom, conventionally,"; **I.d** "statute, ordinance made by authority," / "general laws,"; **I.e** "under martial law,") · **[II]** "melody, strain,"
> — LSJ s.v. **νόμος**. Built on **νέμω** ("distribute, allot, assign").

**Beekes — νόμος as sub-lemma B of νέμω (OCR, printed pp. 1005–1006)** [A §2c; supersedes BASE §3.6]:
> "**νέμω, -ομαι [v.] 'to allot, dispense, distribute, appropriate, possess; to inhabit, manage; to pasture, graze, consume'** (Il.). **<IE \*nem- 'dispense, distribute; take'>**"
> "B. **νόμος [m.] 'custom, usage, law; (musical) key, tone'** (since Hes.) … νόμιμος 'customary, lawful' … νομίζω 'to use customarily, … observe (a custom), believe' … νόμισμα [n.] 'custom, received or current institution, (valid) coin'…"
> ETYM: "**Benveniste 1948: 79 stresses … the phenomenon of lawful and regular distribution that characterizes the verb νέμω.**"

**Preus (2015) — etymology in the NOMOS entry** [A §10a / BASE §3.5a, deduped]:
> "(Noun formed from nemo, distribute; nemesis is another noun based on the same verb.)"

**HWPh — "Nomos" (Bd. 6, Sp. 893–895; dig. [Sp. 23075]), the *Nahme* / pasture-division root** [E §1b]:
> "Zur indogermanischen Wurzel ‹*nem› gehörend, ist ‹N.› ein nomen actionis von νέμειν, das a) weiden, weiden lassen und b) verteilen, zuteilen, teilen bedeutet. Zur Zeit der nomadischen Landnahme könnte demnach mit ‹N.› die ‹Nahme› des Landes und die «erste Messung und Teilung der Weide» gemeint gewesen sein. Bei HESIOD ist N. schon die «Ordnung, die den Gang der Arbeit auf den Feldern für alle bestimmt» …"
> — HWPh "Nomos," Bd. 6 (Th. Rentsch). *νόμος = nomen actionis of νέμειν ("graze; distribute/allot"); at nomadic land-taking, the "Nahme" of land and "first measurement and division of pasture"; in Hesiod already an order governing field-work. (This is the Schmittian Nomos der Erde root.)*

**Cassin (2014) — THEMIS/DIKÊ/NOMOS entry, nomos as *spatial division* (book p. 1124)** [E §2c]:
> "… and finally, starting in the fifth century, **nomos [νόμоς], 'division,' which signals a spatial notion of the city.** … the law is established through procedure and does not preexist it."
> — Cassin 2014, "THEMIS / DIKÊ / NOMOS." *Cassin foregrounds the *spatial* sense of nomos as "division" of the city — the apportioning/allotting root again, now read politically: law is *instituted through procedure*, it does not pre-exist.*

**Load-bearing:** νόμος = *what-is-humanly-apportioned* → custom → law. The made/instituted pole, etymologically grounded in human distribution. Cassin ("division," spatial) and HWPh ("Nahme," division of pasture) independently sharpen the *apportioning/allotting* root from νέμω, and both stress that nomos is *instituted* (procedure, not pre-existing).

## A.3 — τέχνη ← *tek- "build" / *te-tk- "build, timber", cf. τέκτων "carpenter"

**LSJ — sense-glosses (Perseus TEI `entry=te/xnh`)** [A §1b; supersedes BASE §3b PARAPHRASE]:
> **[A]** "art, skill, cunning of hand" · **[A.2]** "craft, cunning" | "arts, wiles" | "trick" · **[A.3]** "way, manner" | "means whereby a thing is gained" · **[II]** "an art, craft" | "trade" · **[III]** "an art" | "craft" | **"a set of rules, system"** | **"method of making"** | **"doing"** · **[IV]** "work of art, handiwork" · **[VI]** "treatise"
> — LSJ s.v. **τέχνη**. Etymology head: τέχνη ← **τέκτων** (carpenter/builder). *Sense III is the load-bearing one: technē as a systematic method of making.*

**Beekes — root (OCR, printed p. 1476)** [A §2b; supersedes BASE §3.6]:
> "**τέχνη [f.] 'craftsmanship, handicraft, business, art; artifice, trick'** (Il.). **<IE \*tek- 'produce', \*te-tk- 'build, timber'>**"
> ETYM: "Derived from \*tek-sneh₂- … from the reduplicated IE root **\*te-tk- 'to build', whence τέκτων is derived.**"

**Cassin (2014) — ART entry: ars = technē, root *teks- "construct" (book p. 43)** [E §2b]:
> "For the Ancients, and so long as people thought with Latin, art and technique were one and the same thing: Latin ars (from the root \*er-, …) equals Greek technê [τέχνη] (from the root **\*teks-, 'construct,' 'make'**). Since art and technique are defined by the production of an object, the question is what guarantees the success of the finished product …"
> — Cassin 2014, "ART." *Cassin states outright that for the Ancients ars = technē, and gives the technē root as \*teks- "construct/make" — corroborating Beekes's *tek-/*te-tk- "build" and the identification of technē with making-an-object.*

**HWPh — "Technik" (Bd. 10; dig. [Sp. 42325]), the *tek- "bauen, zimmern" root** [E §1d]:
> "Bei den frühesten Belegen ist zwar die etymologische Herkunft aus dem Wortstamm **\*tek (bauen, zimmern)** bemerkbar …; immer geht es dabei um ein regelgeleitetes, sachverständiges, also an bestimmtes Wissen gebundenes praktisches oder theoretisches Können. … Der Werkzeuggebrauch und die Künste (τέχναι, artes) als Ausdruck von Kultur **gegenüber der bloßen Natur** sind seit der Antike in Mythos und Philosophie verschiedener Kulturentstehungstheo[rien] …"
> — HWPh "Technik," Bd. 10. *Root \*tek- "to build, to carpenter"; τέχνη = goal-directed competent skill bound to determinate knowledge; and tool-use/the arts figure since antiquity as the **expression of culture over against mere nature** — the technē-vs-physis axis stated in the lexicon itself.*

**Load-bearing:** τέχνη = literally *the act/skill of building-and-fashioning*; same root as τέκτων "the carpenter." The strongest lexical warrant that *technē = the made-by-construction*. Cassin (*teks- "construct") and HWPh (*tek- "bauen, zimmern") both confirm the *build* root, and HWPh additionally records the arts as "culture over against mere nature" — the very axis the thesis's reframe targets. **The crossing *tek- "build" → *bʰeh₂u- "grow" is the most compact statement of how radical "tekhne becoming phusis" is** — though, per the v2 reframe, that crossing is wielded as a *provocation* exposing the categories' instability, not as a literal directional claim (see [[nomos-FINDINGS-and-implications-2026-06-01_v2]]).

## A.4 — Latin corroboration: natura ← nascor; ars ← *h₂r-ti- "fit"; lex ← *leg- "collection"

**de Vaan (2008), clean Latin-script extraction** [A §4]:

The made/given line is triply cross-linguistic.

# SECTION B — THE NOMOS / PHUSIS / TECHNĒ DISTINCTIONS

## B.1 — The Aristotelian internal-vs-external archē (the canonical wall)

**Aristotle *Physics* II.1 (192b8–193a)** [C §A1, fuller than BASE §2]:
> "Of things that exist, some exist by nature, some from other causes. 'By nature' the animals and their parts exist, and the plants and the simple bodies … Each of them has within itself a principle of motion and of stationariness … On the other hand, a bed and a coat and anything else of that sort, qua receiving these designations i.e. in so far as they are products of art—have no innate impulse to change."
> — *Physics* II.1, 192b8–193a (Hardie & Gaye).

> "None of them [artificial products] has in itself the source of its own production. But while in some cases … that principle is in something else external to the thing … 'Nature' then is what has been stated."
> — *Physics* II.1, 192b–193a.

**The Antiphon "planted bed" fragment, embedded at 193a12** [C §A1]:
> "Antiphon points out that if you planted a bed and the rotting wood acquired the power of sending up a shoot, it would not be a bed that would come up, but wood—which shows that the arrangement in accordance with the rules of the art is merely an incidental attribute, whereas the real nature is the other, which … persists continuously through the process of making."
> — *Physics* II.1, 193a.

**Aristotle *NE* VI.4 (1140a10–17) — the origin contrast** [C §A-NE]:
> "All art is concerned with coming into being … whose origin is in the maker and not in the thing made; for art is concerned neither with things that are … by necessity, nor with things that … [come to be] in accordance with nature (since these have their origin in themselves)."
> — *NE* VI.4, 1140a10–17 (Ross).

**Aristotle *Metaphysics* Z.7 (1032a32–b2) — "the form is in the soul"** [C §A-Z]:
> "…from art proceed the things of which the form is in the soul of the artist. (By form I mean the essence of each thing and its primary substance.)"
> — *Metaphysics* Z.7, 1032a32–b2 (Ross).

**Preus (2015) — the contrast stated three ways** [A §10; deduped with BASE §3.5]:
> NOMOS: "Law; convention. … legislated laws tended … to be seen as **arbitrary and variable from one society to another**." (§10a)
> PHYSIS: "'Source or cause of change or rest in that to which it belongs primarily' (Physics II.1)." (§10b)
> TECHNĒ: "**For Aristotle, technē is contrasted with nature (physis), in that the source of change for a product of technē is external to that which is produced, but the source of change for a natural production is within the thing that changes.** … epistēmē is about 'being' and art is about 'becoming' (genesis)." (§10c)
> TECHNIKOS: "Skillful, but also often '**artificial**.'" (§10d)

**Encyclopedia / SEP corroboration** [B]:
> SEP "Artifacts" §1: artifacts "have their origin in the craftsperson … whereas [natural things] have their origin in themselves" (rooted in *Metaph* 1033a, *NE* 1140a, *Phys* 192b).
> SEP "Aristotle's Natural Philosophy" §1: "Nature … is an inner principle of change and being at rest (*Physics* 2.1, 192b20–23)."
> SEP "Philosophy of Technology" §1.1: "the former have their principles of generation and motion inside, whereas the latter, insofar as they are artifacts, are generated only by outward causes."

**Heidegger *QCT* (1977), pp. 10–11 — the en heautō / en allō hinge** [BASE §1b = C §H3, deduped]:

For what presences by means of physis has the bursting open belonging to bringing-forth … in itself (en heautoi). In contrast, what is brought forth by the artisan or the artist … has the bursting open … not in itself, but in another (en alloi), in the craftsman or artist."
> — Heidegger 1977, *QCT*, pp. 10–11 (PDF + NLM cited_text, exact match).

## B.3 — The Sophistic nomos–phusis antithesis (made vs given)

**Antiphon, *On Truth* (POxy 1364 = DK 87 B44), Ostwald trans.** [C §ANT1 — the primary fragment]:
> "For the dictates of the laws (tà tôn nómōn) are adventitious, whereas the dictates of nature are inescapable; dictates of the laws, based on agreement as they are, are not natural growths, whereas the dictates of nature (tà tês phúseōs), being natural growths, are not based on agreement."
> "The reason for our inquiry … is that the majority of things sanctioned as just in terms of the law are enactments inimical (polemíōs) to nature."
> — Antiphon, *On Truth*, col. 1–2 (Ostwald 1990).

**Plato *Gorgias* (Callicles) — nomos as "that artificial law, which we invent and impose"** [C §P2]:
> "Convention and nature are generally at variance with one another…" (482e)
> "…whereas nature herself intimates that it is just for the better to have more than the worse…" (483c–d)
> "Nay, but these are the men who act according to nature … not, perhaps, according to that artificial law, which we invent and impose upon our fellows…" (483d–484a)
> — *Gorgias* (Jowett; Project Gutenberg #1672).

**Encyclopedia statements of the antithesis** [B; deduped — one per source]:
> SEP "The Sophists" §2: "were such norms ever in some sense part of or grounded in the reality of things, or were they in every case mere products of human customs, conventions or beliefs? … both sides agreed in seeing nature as authoritative…"
> IEP "The Sophists" §3a: "The distinction between *physis* (nature) and *nomos* (custom, law, convention) was a central theme in Greek thought in the second half of the fifth century B.C.E."
> Barney 2005: "For the Sophists, nomos and phusis are polar terms, roughly equivalent (respectively) to the socially constructed and the universally, objectively given." / "nomos-phusis is best understood as a catch phrase for the general sophistic inquiry into the institutions of human society."
> Dunshirn 2019 §3: "'like is akin to like by nature, but convention, which tyrannizes the human race, often constrains us contrary to nature'" (Hippias in *Protagoras* 337c–d).
> Strauss, *Natural Right and History*: "The men who discovered 'nature' conceived of nature as the opposite of convention or law. Natural things … are everywhere the same, but the conventions vary…"

**Preus (2015) — the canonical list of Sophistic exponents** [A §10a / BASE §3.5a]:
> "Antiphon provides a particularly good example … Thrasymachus in Republic I, Callicles in the Gorgias, and Protagoras in the Protagoras … Democritus … 'By convention (by nomos) sweet … in reality atoms and void.'"

**HWPh — "Physis/Nomos, Physis/Thesis" (Deitz, Bd. 7; dig. [Sp. 27512 ff.]) — origin + radicalization of the antithesis** [E §1a; the single most on-point HWPh entry]:
> "Es ist weder bekannt, wann N. … als '(schriftlich festgelegtes) Recht' oder '(ungeschriebene) Sitte' zum ersten Mal der Ph. … gegenübergestellt wurde, noch wer diesen Gegensatz als erster formuliert hat. … wie der fast selbstverständliche Gebrauch in Tragödie, Medizin und Historiographie zeigt, muß die Gegenüberstellung begrifflich schon in der ersten Hälfte des 5. Jh. geprägt worden sein … So zeigt der Autor der hippokratischen Schrift Περὶ ἀέρων … daß die allgemein menschliche Natur durch langjährige Volkssitte entscheidend verändert werden kann … während HERODOT die Totensitten der Hellenen und Inder miteinander kontrastiert …"
> "Aber erst mit der Sophistik des 5. Jh. wurde der Gegensatz auf alle Bereiche des öffentlichen und privaten Lebens angewendet. So äußert Hippias in PLATONS ‹Protagoras›: «Ich bin der Auffassung, daß ihr alle Verwandte … seid, [und zwar] von Natur (φύσει), [wenn auch] nicht durch Gesetz (νόμῳ)». Das Gesetz, der Tyrann der Menschen, regele vieles gewaltsam gegen die Natur. Schärfer noch heißt es in einem ANTIPHON-Fragment, daß «die Gebote der Gesetze willkürlich, die der Natur dagegen notwendig» sind … den «natürlich gewachsenen» (φύν〈τα〉) Vorschriften wird hier der Vorzug gegenüber den «vereinbarten» (ὁμόλογη〈θέν〉τα) gegeben."
> — HWPh "Physis/Nomos, Physis/Thesis," Bd. 7 (L. Deitz). *The antithesis is first attested in 5th-c. medicine/ethnography (Hippocrates *Airs Waters Places*; Herodotus), generalized to all of public/private life by the Sophists (Hippias: kin "by nature" not "by law"; Antiphon: the commands of law are arbitrary/conventional, those of nature necessary — the "naturally grown" [φύντα] preferred over the "agreed" [ὁμολογηθέντα]). Independent lexicon confirmation of the §B.3 antithesis, with the Greek terms.*

> [!note] HWPh on Aristotle's deflation + the Stoic/Pauline reuse (Deitz, [Sp. 27514–27516])
> Deitz records that "Bei ARISTOTELES ist die Auseinandersetzung um den Gegensatz von nebensächlicher Bedeutung" (Aristotle *deflates* the opposition, admitting ἄγραφοι νόμοι / νόμοι τῆς φύσεως, e.g. Antigone's appeal), and that the Stoa (Chrysippus: the Logos as ὅς ἐστι φύσει νόμος, "a law that is by nature") and Paul (Rom. 2:14, the Gentiles "a law to themselves") later *fuse* the poles into "νόμος φύσεως / law of nature." *This is a lexicon-attested ancient line where nomos and phusis are explicitly recombined — relevant to the porosity argument (§C) and to the v2 reframe's "the categories were always unstable."*

**Heraclitus B114 / B123 — the divine-nomos and the hiding-phusis** [A/B/C]:
> "All human nomoi are nourished by the one divine (nomos)." — Heraclitus f. 114 (in Preus 2015).
> **Φύσις κρύπτεσθαι φιλεῖ** — "Nature loves to hide." — Heraclitus B123 DK (the title-aphorism of Hadot's *Veil of Isis*) [C §M2a].

## B.4 — nomos and technē sit together on the *made* side (Brague) — GROUPING, not identity

**Brague, *The Wisdom of the World* (2004), p. 14** [BASE §5a = B §6.1, deduped — the single most load-bearing Brague passage]:
> "the idea of 'nature' (phusis), even if it seeks to grasp what is original, is not originary, but derived; it is already the result of a reflection, more precisely of a separation between that which has its principle in the human activity of fabrication or estimation, i.e., the artificial (tekhnē) and the conventional (nomos), and that which grows by itself, spontaneously, the natural."
> — Brague 2004, p. 14.

> [!warning] Brague guard
> Brague *groups* tekhnē + nomos as one class ("the human activity of fabrication or estimation"); he does **NOT** say "nomos = technē." That identity is the author's reading. Also: Brague p. 45 ("Greece … named [nature], by contrasting nature to artifice and the conventional") and p. 47 ("it unites the two principles, artifice and convention, which contrasted with the Greek phusis") [BASE §5b/§5c].

# SECTION C — ANCIENT POROSITY (the canon runs the boundary in BOTH directions)

> Three ancient **phusis→technē** vectors + the one ancient **technē→phusis** inversion (Laws X) + the II.8 counterfactual that names the single remaining barrier. The boundary is already porous in the canon; only the *metaverse-driven, asymmetric* direction is the author's own.

## C.1 — phusis→technē vector 1: technē *completes* phusis (Aristotle II.8) — ★ KEYSTONE

**Aristotle *Physics* II.8 (199a15–18)** [C §A8]:
> "Each step then in the series is for the sake of the next; and generally **art partly completes what nature cannot bring to a finish, and partly imitates her.** If, therefore, artificial products are for the sake of an end, so clearly also are natural products."
> — *Physics* II.8, 199a15–18 (Hardie & Gaye).

**Cassin (2014) — ART entry: the same II.8 passage with the Greek, framed as technē's *dependency + superiority* (book p. 44)** [E §2b; the most useful Cassin technē/physis passage]:
> "[art] consists of bringing into existence things 'whose origin is in the maker and not in the thing made,' Nicomachean Ethics, 6.4.1140a13–14). But that always also implies that art provides the concepts necessary for thinking about nature. … From this comes the famous complement: '**Generally art partly completes what nature cannot bring to a finish** [epitelei ha hê phusis adunatei apergasasthai (ἐπιτελεῖ ἃ ἡ φύσιϛ ἀδυνατεῖ ἀπεϱγάσασθαι)], and partly imitates her' (Physics 2.8.199a15–16). **Art displays both its dependency on the model by imitating it, and a certain superiority in realizing what the model, even though it is prior, was not able to produce.**"
> — Cassin 2014, "ART" (book p. 44). *Cassin supplies the Greek of "completes what nature cannot finish" (ἐπιτελεῖ ἃ ἡ φύσις ἀδυνατεῖ ἀπεργάσασθαι) and reads II.8 as technē being at once *dependent on* nature (imitation) and *superior to* it (realizing what nature could not) — the lexicon's own statement of the porosity at the II.8 keystone.*

**Cassin (2014) — ART entry: the breadth of the field of technē (book p. 44)** [E §2b]:
> "The field of technê can thus include all values from divine demiurgy (artifex mundi, the Romans called it) to human power or faculty, which is rational and useful, but obviously susceptible to Promethean excess and trickery."
> — Cassin 2014, "ART." *(Connects the II.8 keystone to the domination/Promethean vector of §C.3.)*

## C.2 — phusis→technē vector 2: technē *imitates* phusis (Aristotle II.2 / II.8)

**Aristotle *Physics* II.2 (194a21)** [C §A2]:
> "But if … art imitates nature, and it is the part of the same discipline to know the form and the matter up to a point…"
> — *Physics* II.2, 194a21–22. (Bare slogan ἡ τέχνη μιμεῖται τὴν φύσιν; the "and partly imitates her" of II.8 above is the fuller statement.)

**von Staden, "Physis and Technē in Greek Medicine" (in Bensaude-Vincent & Newman 2007) — the ancient "physis as technē" inversion** [BASE §6c]:
> "In early Hellenistic medicine … nature (physis) itself often was explicitly depicted as working like, acting like, or being like technē … Erasistratus … repeatedly characterized nature as technikē … Here it is not a case of technē imitating physis, but rather of physis displaying characteristics that are best understood by their similarity to technē."
> "Aristotle's claim that 'technē imitates physis' … has received much attention, but in his biological works it is technē that repeatedly provides a model for understanding aspects of nature…"
> — von Staden, in Bensaude-Vincent & Newman 2007.

## C.3 — phusis→technē vector 3: technē *conquers/overcomes* phusis (Antiphon via Hui)

**Antiphon fr. 65 (Pendrick), via Yuk Hui** [C §ANT2]:
> "Each *techne* is a remedy for overcoming chance occurrences (*tyche*) … as Antiphon says: 'we conquer by techne things that defeat us by physis'."
> — Yuk Hui, *The Question Concerning Technology in China* (2016), quoting Antiphon. *(Hadot's "Promethean" forcing-of-nature, §C.6, is the deep-historical pedigree of this domination vector.)*

## C.4 — technē→phusis inversion: Plato *Laws* X — technē/nous/soul PRIOR to phusis — ★ HIGHEST closeness

**Plato *Laws* X (892b)** [C §P1b]:
> "Then thought and attention and mind and art and law will be prior to that which is hard and soft and heavy and light; and **the great and primitive works and actions will be works of art; they will be the first, and after them will come nature and works of nature, which however is a wrong term** … these will follow, and will be under the government of art and mind."
> — *Laws* X, 892b (Jowett).

> "if the soul turn out to be the primeval element … then in the truest sense and beyond other things **the soul may be said to exist by nature**…" (892c)

**The materialist thesis Plato is rebutting (889b–e)** [C §P1a]:
> "Art sprang up afterwards and out of these, mortal and of mortal birth, and produced … partial imitations of the truth … legislation is entirely a work of art … the principles of justice have no existence at all in nature…"
> — *Laws* X, 889b–e.

> [!important] Why *Laws* X is the closest ancient anticipation
> Plato **inverts the priority**: soul/mind/art/law are *prior*, and "nature" *follows* them and is *governed by art and mind*. The order made→given is reversed into made-grounds-given; that which is most technē/nous-like (soul) is said to *be phusis* in the truest sense. ⚠ But Plato's "art" is divine cosmic *nous*, his "nature" is *demoted* (not displaced by a new artificial layer), and there is no temporal/technological *becoming*. The directional, metaverse-conditioned claim is the author's; the *form of the inversion* is Platonic.

## C.5 — The II.8 counterfactual: "if the ship-building art were in the wood…" — names the single un-crossed barrier

**Aristotle *Physics* II.8 (199b26–31)** [C §A8]:
> "Art does not deliberate. **If the ship-building art were in the wood, it would produce the same results by nature.** If, therefore, purpose is present in art, it is present also in nature. The best illustration is a doctor doctoring himself: nature is like that."
> — *Physics* II.8, 199b26–31.

> [!important] The hinge
> Aristotle's own counterfactual: were technē *internal to its matter* (the very mark II.1 reserved for phusis), it would simply BE a natural process. The ONLY thing separating technē from phusis, on Aristotle's own statement, is the *externality of the principle of motion*. ⚠ Aristotle uses the conditional to argue the *reverse* (that nature, like art, acts for an end); he does NOT say the barrier is crossed. But the conditional itself states the threshold the author's claim says the metaverse satisfies.

**Heidegger, *Physis* essay (1939) — the same barrier, most precisely stated** [C §H1–H2; `[VERIFY: via Riis/Dahlstrom — primary Pathmarks not retrieved]`]:
> "In the case of every artifact … the origin of the making is 'outside' the thing made." (WM 255/PA 195, via Riis)
> "Physis … is the presencing of the absencing of itself, one that is on-the-way from itself and unto itself." (WM 299/PA 228, via Riis)

**The Prometheus correlate — technē as constitutive supplement (Plato *Protagoras* 321c–322a)** [C §P3]:
> "man alone was naked and shoeless … Prometheus … stole the mechanical arts of Hephaestus and Athene, and fire with them … and gave them to man."
> — *Protagoras* 321c–d (Jowett). *(The mythic correlate of II.8's "art completes what nature cannot finish"; the locus classicus behind the Stiegler "technics as originary supplement" reading — cf. [[thesis_stiegler_obara_triangulation]].)*

> "**For the ancient Greeks, technics imitates nature and at the same time perfects it.** Techne mediates between physis and tyche (chance or coincidence). **This idea that technics might supplement and 'perfect' nature could not possibly occur in Chinese thought, since for the latter technics is always subordinate to the cosmological order: to be part of nature is to be morally good, since it implies a cosmological order which is also a moral order.** […] one can only embody the truth through harmony, rather than discovering it through external means, as is the case for techne."
> — Hui, *QCT-China*, PDF p.88. [VERIFY: confirmed | huiQuestionTechnologyChina2016 | direct-PDF p.88]

*Note the convergence with §C: Hui's "technics imitates nature and at the same time perfects it" is the same II.8 "art completes/imitates nature" keystone, now read comparatively — the *completion/perfection* arrangement is specifically Greek and "could not possibly occur in Chinese thought."*

## G.4 — The alternative metaphysics of the made: 道 (above form) / 器 (below form), inseparable [HUI / QCT-China]
> "In the Xi Ci, a commentary on the I Ching, we read that '**what is formless (or above form) is called Dao; what has form (or is below form) is Qi** (形而上者謂之道，形而下者謂之器)'. […] **xing er shang** (形而上, 'above form'), is used to translate the English word 'metaphysical'…"
> — Hui, *QCT-China*. [VERIFY: confirmed | huiQuestionTechnologyChina2016 | nlm-excerpt:"what is formless (or above form) is called Dao; what has form (or is below form) is Qi"] (OCR artefact: attachment renders the line 形之上者為之道…形之下者為之器; standard text is 形而上者謂之道，形而下者謂之器.)
> "**Dao does not mean the 'laws of nature'** as this term was understood in seventeenth-century Europe; **it is rather the ungraspable that could yet be known.** […] **Qi is also a container, a carrier** […] 'junzi bu qi (君子不器)' […] 'the gentleman is not a utensil'…"
> — Hui, *QCT-China*. [VERIFY: confirmed | huiQuestionTechnologyChina2016 | nlm-excerpt:"Dao does not mean the 'laws of nature'...it is rather the ungraspable that could yet be known"]
> "**Dao and Qi are not separable, just as shadow cannot be detached from shape.** […] how can one talk about Dao without Qi? — how can shadow exist without shape?" [Hui quoting Zhang Xuecheng]
> "**Dao needs Qi to carry it in order to be manifested in sensible forms; Qi needs Dao in order to become perfect (in Daoism) or sacred (in Confucianism)**…"
> — Hui, *QCT-China*. [VERIFY: confirmed | huiQuestionTechnologyChina2016 | nlm-excerpt:"Dao needs Qi to carry it in order to be manifested in sensible forms"]

**Load-bearing:** in the 器/道 scheme the "made/instrumental" (Qi) is *internal to and obtains its meaning from* the cosmic-moral order (Dao) — "obtaining/manifesting the Dao through Qi" (器以載道). Where the Greek artificial (technē/nomos) is *violent supplementation/perfection* of, and a *geometric fitting imposed on*, an order it stands against, the Chinese artificial (Qi) is *subordinate-resonant*. So "the artificial" is carved differently — a genuine alternative ontology of the made, exactly what synthesis-1's comparative move requires.

# SECTION H — THE UNIVERSE IS NOT A KANTIAN NOUMENON BUT AN INTRANSITIVE REAL (the realist-pole positioning) — new in v3

> **Why this section exists (v3).** The thesis's realist pole — the **universe** — must be positioned against the two standing "realist floors" in play: Kant's **noumenon** and Floridi's **dedomena**. The verdict (from [KANT-UNIV]): the universe is **not** a Kantian noumenon (it is causal, spatiotemporal, knowable-in-effects) and is **thicker than** Floridi's dedomena; it is best called a **realist, causally-efficacious substrate accessed only perspectivally through the AOS — a Uexküllian–Bhaskarian "intransitive real."** This section supplies the **Bhaskar primary verbatim** that grounds the "intransitive real" label (replacing the earlier *unsupported* use of the term), plus the thin→thick ordering. Provenance tags **[KANT-UNIV]** (thesis/Kant verbatim) and **[BHASKAR]** (RTS primary, extracted from the Zotero PDF 2026-06-01).

## H.1 — The thesis's "universe" (verbatim, Ch.1) [KANT-UNIV / docx P0104–P0105]
> [P0104] "the fundamental substrate of all, which **interacts with all things and carries the total effect of all interactions, therefore transforms with all things** – manifested in its spatial-temporal diversity (… blackholes, stars, to planets …)"
> [P0105] "the foundational ontological level of all, which is **the source of laws, energies, or matters that are indifferent and unaffectable by anything** (e.g., the real referents behind what is referred as 'gravitation' or 'thermodynamics' **operate whether any AOS is present** or if any AOS's members can perceive them)."
> — thesis Ch.1 §E, `docx-extract-20260530.txt`. *The universe is mind-independent (P0105) yet causally engaged and spatiotemporally thick (P0104) — and accessed only perspectivally: members access it through their Umwelt; the Umgebung "can never be fully perceivable and acted upon by any of its members" (P0090).*

## H.2 — Bhaskar's transitive/intransitive distinction — PRIMARY VERBATIM [BHASKAR / RTS 2008, item SSDEPDHZ]

**The neologism + the canonical "if men ceased to exist" passage (Ch.1, printed p.11):**
> "The other is that knowledge is 'of' things which are not produced by men at all: the specific gravity of mercury, the process of electrolysis, the mechanism of light propagation. None of these 'objects of knowledge' depend upon human activity. **If men ceased to exist sound would continue to travel and heavy bodies fall to the earth in exactly the same way, though ex hypothesi there would be no-one to know it. Let us call these, in an unavoidable technical neologism, the intransitive objects of knowledge.** The transitive objects of knowledge are Aristotelian material causes. They are the raw materials of science—the artificial objects fashioned into items of knowledge by the science of the day."
> — Bhaskar, *A Realist Theory of Science* (2008 Routledge ed.), Ch.1 §1, **p.11**. [VERIFY: confirmed | bhaskarRealistTheoryScience2008 | p.11 | direct-PDF idx 43]

**The "world without science" statement of intransitivity (printed p.12):**
> "We can easily imagine a world similar to ours, containing the same intransitive objects of scientific knowledge, but without any science to produce knowledge of them. In such a world … reality would be unspoken for and yet things would not cease to act and interact in all kinds of ways. … The tides would still turn and metals conduct electricity in the way that they do, without a Newton or a Drude to produce our knowledge of them. … In short, **the intransitive objects of knowledge are in general invariant to our knowledge of them: they are the real things and structures, mechanisms and processes, events and possibilities of the world; and for the most part they are quite independent of us.** They are not unknowable, because as a matter of fact quite a bit is known about them. … But neither are they in any way dependent upon our knowledge, let alone perception, of them. **They are the intransitive, science-independent, objects of scientific discovery and investigation.**"
> — Bhaskar, *RTS*, Ch.1 §1, **p.12**. [VERIFY: confirmed | bhaskarRealistTheoryScience2008 | p.12 | direct-PDF idx 44]

**The two-dimensions definition (Introduction, printed p.6):**
> "These two aspects of the philosophy of science justify our talking of two dimensions and two kinds of 'object' of knowledge: **a transitive dimension, in which the object is the material cause or antecedently established knowledge which is used to generate the new knowledge; and an intransitive dimension, in which the object is the real structure or mechanism that exists and acts quite independently of men and the conditions which allow men access to it.** … the aim of science is the production of the knowledge of the mechanisms of the production of phenomena in nature … **These mechanisms, which are the intransitive objects of scientific enquiry, endure and act quite independently of men.** … they … would act in a world without men, where there would be no experiences and few, if any, constant conjunctions of events."
> — Bhaskar, *RTS*, Introduction, **p.6**. [VERIFY: confirmed | bhaskarRealistTheoryScience2008 | p.6 | direct-PDF idx 38]

**The editor's (Hartwig) precise gloss of the two objects (2008 Introduction, printed pp.x–xi):**
> "scientific knowledge has two kinds of object, **an intransitive object (the enduring structures and mechanisms of the natural world), and a transitive object (the Aristotelian material causes—existing theories, technologies, social practices and so on—that scientists work on and with in the process of production of scientific knowledge)**."
> — Hartwig, editor's Introduction to *RTS* (2008), [VERIFY: printed page xi | direct-PDF idx 16]. *Secondary gloss; cite the primary p.6/p.11/p.12 passages above for the definition itself.*

> [!important] Why "intransitive real" is the right label for the universe (and noumenon is the wrong one)
> Bhaskar's **intransitive dimension** = "the real structure or mechanism that exists and acts **quite independently of men and the conditions which allow men access to it**" (p.6) — yet is **knowable** ("they are not unknowable … quite a bit is known about them," p.12). That is *precisely* the thesis's universe: mind-independent **and** causally active **and** knowable-in-its-effects, but never given *as a totality* to any positioned subject. The structural fit is: **the universe = the intransitive object; each AOS/Umwelt = a transitive mode of access.** This is why the [KANT-UNIV] dossier concludes the universe is "a Uexküllian–Bhaskarian intransitive real, not a Kantian noumenon" — Kant's noumenon fails on three of its own marks (it is causally bracketed, non-spatiotemporal, and strictly unknowable), whereas Bhaskar's intransitive object matches the universe on all three. **The earlier use of "intransitive real" was an unsupported label; it is now grounded in Bhaskar's primary text (p.6, p.11, p.12).**

# What the verbatim establishes vs. what stays the author's synthesis

**Building blocks (stated by the sources):** nomos = the made/instituted (LSJ, Preus, SEP, IEP, Barney, Antiphon, Iwanami 人為的につくられた); phusis = the given/self-arising (LSJ, Beekes, de Vaan, Aristotle II.1, Heidegger, Iwanami); technē = making/built (LSJ, Beekes *tek-*, Preus, Aristotle NE VI.4); nomos + technē grouped on the made side (Brague — GROUPING only); the boundary porous in BOTH directions and anciently so (Aristotle II.2/II.8, von Staden, Laws X, Antiphon-via-Hui); the made can acquire ontological weight + autonomy (Floridi re-ontologizing + third-order; Simondon concretization); **technics is not anthropologically universal and the Chinese have no equivalents of technē/physis** (Hui §G — warrant for synthesis-1's comparative move); **the universe-pole is an *intransitive real* — mind-independent, causally active, knowable-in-effects** (Bhaskar §H — "the real structure or mechanism that exists and acts quite independently of men," RTS p.6).

**The author's original synthesis (NOT asserted by any source):**
1. **REFRAMED (v3, per [FRAME] §6c): "nomos and technē are the two major categories the ancient Greeks treated as *artificial* (the made / human-instituted)."** — AUTHOR'S SYNTHESIS. This **replaces** the earlier identity-claim slogan "*nomos has always been tekhne*" (which over-read Brague). The reframe asserts not an identity but a **grouping into one civilization's class of "the artificial"** — and *other civilizations carve the artificial differently* (China's **器/qi** within Hui's cosmotechnics — see §G). *Evidenced-by*: Brague (pp. 14, 47) — who **himself groups** tekhnē + nomos as "the human activity of fabrication or estimation"; LSJ/Preus/SEP/IEP/Antiphon/Iwanami (人為的につくられた) place nomos on the made/instituted side; Cassin (nomos = "division," "established through procedure, does not preexist it") + HWPh (*Nahme* / division of pasture, the *instituted*) sharpen the placement. ✅ **The Brague guard is now *satisfied by the reframe itself*** — the reframe asserts exactly Brague's *grouping*, never an identity. ⚠ The "**two categories the Greeks treated as artificial**" *wording* remains the **author's** synthesis extending Hui (Hui supplies cosmotechnics + non-universality, not that phrasing — see §G caveat).
2. **"Tekhne is becoming phusis through the metaverse."** — AUTHOR'S SYNTHESIS, original to the thesis; **NON-LITERAL** (a provocation against the inherited categories, per [FRAME] §1 — see [[nomos-FINDINGS-and-implications-2026-06-01_v3]]). *Evidenced-by*: Aristotle II.1's barrier (the made lacks the internal archē) + II.8's counterfactual (if technē were *in the wood*…) + *Laws* X's inversion + Simondon's concretization + Floridi's autonomy/re-ontologizing. ⚠ No source — Aristotle, Plato, Heidegger, Brague, LSJ, Beekes, Preus, SEP, IEP, von Staden, Simondon, Floridi, Latour, Hui — asserts "tekhne becomes phusis"; Floridi's directional arrow runs the *opposite* way. The "real" at stake is the **substrate-neutral self-completing tendency (自足完成化)**, *nomos-by-origin / phusis-by-operation* — NOT a specific emerging technology (per [FRAME] §6b).
