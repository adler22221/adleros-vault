# Terminology Decision Dossier — "Enlightenment" vs. "Modernity" (vs. a non-temporal descriptor)

> Backlink: [[master-thesis]] · related: [[squeeze-ontology]]
> Compiled 2026-06-01. Verbatim only; every quotation is text actually retrieved, with citation. Unretrieved items flagged `[VERIFY]`.

## The decision in one paragraph

The thesis critiques a **trans-historical disposition** — a process/attitude by which a particular human construction (one "world") makes itself the only world. That disposition is being renamed **Domination → "universalization."** The label for the disposition itself is contested: **"Enlightenment"** (the author likes its substance/process-sense) vs. **"Modernity"** (the field's convention). The tension is real on both sides:

- **Against "Modernity":** it reads as a *time-period*, which invites the regress the author wants to avoid ("post-modern? neo-modern?"), and it *post-dates* the ancient-Greek (Platonic–Aristotelian) fault line in which the thesis roots the genealogy.
- **Against "Enlightenment":** by default it reads as the **17th–18th c. European period** — which *also* mis-dates a Greek genealogy and **risks the very Eurocentrism** a viva committee (劉/Liu on scope; 馬/Ma on comparative breadth) flagged.

So the choice is not "period-word vs. process-word." Both canonical terms carry a period default that must be actively overridden, and both have a strong scholarly tradition of being used *as* a trans-historical process/attitude. The dossier below gathers the verbatim that lets each term be defended **and** attacked on its own best evidence.

## Axis ① — Period vs. process/attitude

**"Enlightenment" as process/attitude (supports the author):**

> "For Enlightenment thinkers themselves, however, the Enlightenment is not an historical period, but a process of social, psychological or spiritual development, unbound to time or place."
> — *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy*, "Enlightenment" (entry, retrieved 2026-06-01)

> "Enlightenment is the human being's emergence from his self-incurred minority. Minority is inability to make use of one's own understanding without direction from another."
> — Kant, *An Answer to the Question: What is Enlightenment?* (1784), Mary J. Gregor trans., in *Practical Philosophy* (Cambridge UP, 1996), as rendered at marxists.org (retrieved 2026-06-01) `[VERIFY: confirm Gregor vs. Beck wording against the print edition — the host page labels it Gregor; the marxists.org plain-text has historically been the Lewis White Beck "tutelage/nonage" translation]`

> "Sapere aude! [dare to be wise] Have courage to make use of your own understanding! is thus the motto of enlightenment."
> — Kant (1784), same source.

> "the thread that may connect us with the Enlightenment is not faithfulness to doctrinal elements, but rather the permanent reactivation of an attitude — that is, of a philosophical ethos that could be described as a permanent critique of our historical era."
> — Foucault, *What is Enlightenment?* (1984), foucault.info English text (retrieved 2026-06-01)

> "This philosophical ethos may be characterized as a limit-attitude."
> — Foucault (1984), same source.

**"Modernity" used as attitude rather than period (cuts the other way — undermines the "only Enlightenment can be an attitude" claim):**

> "I wonder whether we may not envisage modernity rather as an attitude than as a period of history. And by 'attitude,' I mean a mode of relating to contemporary reality; a voluntary choice made by certain people; in the end, a way of thinking and feeling; a way, too, of acting and behaving that at one and the same time marks a relation of belonging and presents itself as a task."
> — Foucault (1984), same source.

> "To me the Enlightenment is not a historical notion and set of ideas but a process and dynamics where criticism, self-criticism, irony, and humanity play a central role."
> — Ulrich Beck, "Risk Society Revisited" (1999/2000), quoted verbatim from NLM notebook *Ulrich Beck* (source 39ae7020; cross-ref acd14ed5, "Beck, 1999: 152")

**Takeaway ①:** The author's intuition is correct that "Enlightenment" has a canonical *process/attitude* reading (Kant, Foucault, SEP, Beck) — but Foucault shows "modernity" can be carried as an *attitude* too, so the process-sense is not exclusive to "Enlightenment."

## Axis ② — Temporal-regress handling ("what comes after?")

**The field answers the regress WITHOUT "post-modern" — it qualifies "modernity" rather than abandoning it:**

> "This is what 'reflexive modernization' means: we are not living in a post-modern world, but in a more-modern world. It is not the crisis, but the victory of modernity, which, through the logics of unintended and unknown side-effects, undermines basic institutions of first modernity."
> — Beck, quoted verbatim from NLM *Ulrich Beck* (source 9ac9c999)

> "Reflexive modernization means not less but more modernity, a modernity radicalized against the paths and categories of the classical industrial setting."
> — Beck, *Risk Society* (1992 [1986]), NLM *Ulrich Beck* (source fbb14994)

> "…we reject the idea that so far this is a move from the modern to the postmodern… there has been no clear break with the basic principles of modernity but rather a transformation of basic institutions of modernity… We would suggest, therefore, that what we are witnessing is a second modernity."
> — Beck & Lau, in *The theory of second modernity* (NLM *Ulrich Beck*, source 116882b3)

**Latour shows the regress is a defect of the period-logic itself — and mocks it:**

> "They feel that they come 'after' the moderns, but with the disagreeable sentiment that there is no more 'after'. 'No future': this is the slogan added to the moderns' motto 'No past'."
> — Latour, *We Have Never Been Modern* (1991), NLM *Bruno Latour* (source 5a0e0526, §2.14)

> "…we no longer have to continue the headlong flight of the post-post-postmodernists… Hence the hint of the ludicrous that always accompanies postmodern thinkers; they claim to come after a time that has not even started!"
> — Latour (1991), same source.

**Takeaway ②:** "Modernity" does invite the regress the author fears — BUT the literature's standard fix is *qualified modernity* (late / liquid / reflexive / second), explicitly **NOT** "post-modern." The regress is handled by adjectives, not by a new noun. "Enlightenment," lacking a built-in "post-" reflex in ordinary scholarly usage, sidesteps the regress more cleanly at the level of the bare noun — which is exactly the author's point. (Liquid Modernity = Bauman 2000; Consequences of Modernity / late modernity = Giddens 1990 — `[VERIFY]`: not retrieved verbatim here; cited by attribution only.)

## Axis ③ — Eurocentrism / comparative breadth (a viva concern)

**"Modernity" has the explicit anti-Eurocentric apparatus the committee would want — multiple modernities:**

> "THE NOTION OF 'multiple modernities' denotes a certain view of the contemporary world — indeed of the history and characteristics of the modern era — that goes against the views long prevalent in scholarly and general discourse."
> — Eisenstadt, "Multiple Modernities," *Daedalus* 129(1), Winter 2000, p. 1 (full text via amacad.org PDF, extracted verbatim 2026-06-01)

> "The idea of multiple modernities presumes that the best way to understand the contemporary world — indeed to explain the history of modernity — is to see it as a story of continual constitution and reconstitution of a multiplicity of cultural programs."
> — Eisenstadt (2000), p. 2, same source.

> "One of the most important implications of the term 'multiple modernities' is that modernity and Westernization are not identical; Western patterns of modernity are not the only 'authentic' modernities, though they enjoy historical precedence and continue to be a basic reference point for others."
> — Eisenstadt (2000), pp. 2–3, same source.

**"Enlightenment" carries the opposite default — a singular European event:**

> "The Enlightenment is an event, or a set of events and complex historical processes, that is located at a certain point in the development of European societies."
> — Foucault (1984), foucault.info (retrieved 2026-06-01)

> "The heart of the eighteenth century Enlightenment is the loosely organized activity of prominent French thinkers of the mid-decades of the eighteenth century."
> — *SEP*, "Enlightenment" (retrieved 2026-06-01)

**Takeaway ③:** This axis favors **"Modernity"** for the committee. "Modernity" comes with a ready-made pluralizing, de-Westernizing vocabulary (*multiple modernities*) that directly answers Ma's comparative-breadth and Liu's scope concerns. "Enlightenment" has no equivalent "multiple enlightenments" convention in wide circulation `[VERIFY: a small "multiple/global Enlightenments" literature exists (e.g., Sebastian Conrad, AHR 2012) — not retrieved here; would need verification before relying on it]`, so choosing "Enlightenment" *increases* the Eurocentrism-rebuttal burden the author must carry in prose.

## Axis ④ — Valence (optimistic self-image vs. critical inversion)

The disposition is being renamed to **"universalization"** — a particular construction making itself the only world. The label should ideally already *contain* a critical/dominating sense.

**"Enlightenment" carries a built-in critical inversion AND a domination/universalization sense — Adorno & Horkheimer make the term mean exactly what the thesis means by "universalization":**

> "Enlightenment, understood in the widest sense as the advance of thought, has always aimed at liberating human beings from fear and installing them as masters. Yet the wholly enlightened earth is radiant with triumphant calamity."
> — Horkheimer & Adorno, *Dialectic of Enlightenment* (1947), Edmund Jephcott trans. (Stanford UP, 2002), "The Concept of Enlightenment," p. 1 — verbatim from the PDF extracted 2026-06-01 (also independently confirmed via NLM *Theodor Adorno*, source 88ed6270)

> "Just as myths already entail enlightenment, with every step enlightenment entangles itself more deeply in mythology." / "Myth is already enlightenment, and enlightenment reverts to mythology."
> — Horkheimer & Adorno (1947), p. xviii and pp. 8–9 — verbatim via NLM *Theodor Adorno* (source 88ed6270). `[VERIFY: the pithy "Myth is already enlightenment, and enlightenment reverts to mythology" is the Preface formulation; the body text at p. 8 reads "Just as myths already entail enlightenment, with every step enlightenment entangles itself more deeply in mythology." Both are genuine; cite the one matching the page you use.]`

> "Enlightenment is totalitarian." / "For enlightenment is totalitarian as only a system can be."
> — Horkheimer & Adorno (1947), p. 4, NLM *Theodor Adorno* (source 88ed6270)

**Directly relevant to "universalization" (everything made identical / the only world):**

> "Representation gives way to universal fungibility."
> "The identity of everything with everything is bought at the cost that nothing can at the same time be identical to itself. Enlightenment dissolves away the injustice of the old inequality of unmediated mastery, but at the same time perpetuates it in universal mediation, by relating every existing thing to every other."
> "Under the leveling rule of abstraction, which makes everything in nature repeatable… the liberated finally themselves become the 'herd'."
> — Horkheimer & Adorno (1947), pp. 7–9, NLM *Theodor Adorno* (source 88ed6270)

> "In their mastery of nature, the creative God and the ordering mind are alike. Man's likeness to God consists in sovereignty over existence, in the lordly gaze, in the command… Enlightenment stands in the same relationship to things as the dictator to human beings."
> — Horkheimer & Adorno (1947), p. 6, NLM *Theodor Adorno* (source 88ed6270)

**"Modernity" by default carries a neutral-to-progressive valence; the critique must be added (Habermas even defends it):**

- Habermas defends modernity as "an unfinished/incomplete project" to be *completed*, not abandoned — i.e., the bare term leans pro. `[VERIFY: exact sentence not retrieved verbatim; well-attested title "Modernity — An Unfinished Project" (1980) and the position that the project "has not yet been fulfilled" but should be renewed rather than abandoned. Pull the exact quotation from the print essay before citing.]`

**Takeaway ④:** Strongly favors **"Enlightenment"** for the thesis's purposes. Adorno & Horkheimer already weld "enlightenment" to *domination, mastery, universal fungibility, totalitarian unification* — almost a ready-made gloss for "universalization." "Modernity" arrives valence-neutral (or, via Habermas, positively), so the critical charge must be supplied externally.

## Axis ⑤ — Fit with an ancient-Greek (Platonic–Aristotelian) genealogy

**Both canonical terms post-date the Greeks at their *period* reading** — so on the bare periodization, *both* mis-date the genealogy:

> "…the devolution of the French Revolution into the Terror in the 1790s… can serve as a convenient marker of the end of the Enlightenment, conceived as an historical period."
> — *SEP*, "Enlightenment" (retrieved 2026-06-01) — i.e., Enlightenment-as-period is firmly 18th c.

**BUT Adorno & Horkheimer explicitly run "enlightenment" back to Homer/the Greeks and to the mythological substrate — giving "Enlightenment" an ancient reach "modernity" lacks:**

> "…reason has steadfastly indicated since the earliest critique of Homer."
> "Oedipus's answer to the riddle of the Sphinx — 'That being is man' — is repeated indiscriminately as enlightenment's stereotyped message…"
> "The mythologizing equation of Forms with numbers in Plato's last writings expresses the longing of all demythologizing: number became enlightenment's canon."
> — Horkheimer & Adorno (1947), pp. 4–6, NLM *Theodor Adorno* (source 88ed6270)

**Takeaway ⑤:** Favors **"Enlightenment"** — but only if it is used in the Adornian *trans-historical* sense ("the advance of thought," reaching back through Plato and Homer), not the SEP/period sense. *Dialectic of Enlightenment* is itself the canonical warrant for back-dating "enlightenment" to the Greeks. "Modernity" has no comparable scholarly tradition of being stretched back to antiquity; pushing it to Plato would read as idiosyncratic. This is arguably the strongest single argument for "Enlightenment," and it has a citable warrant.

## Axis ⑥ — Disciplinary convention (comparative education / critical theory / STS)

- **Critical theory:** "Enlightenment" is the load-bearing term (Adorno & Horkheimer; Foucault's "ethos"). Using "Enlightenment" signals lineage to *Dialectic of Enlightenment* and the Frankfurt-School critique of domination — congenial to a thesis about a dominating disposition.
- **Sociology of modernity / STS:** "Modernity" is the convention (Beck, Giddens, Bauman, Latour, Eisenstadt). The whole *reflexive / liquid / multiple / never-been* apparatus is built on the word "modernity."

> "…the word 'modern' designates two sets of entirely different practices… The first set of practices, by 'translation', creates mixtures… of nature and culture. The second, by 'purification', creates two entirely distinct ontological zones: that of human beings on the one hand; that of nonhumans on the other."
> — Latour (1991), NLM *Bruno Latour* (source 5a0e0526, §1.5)

> "We then discover that we have never been modern in the claim. No one has ever been modern. Modernity has never begun. There has never been a modern world."
> — Latour (1991), NLM *Bruno Latour* (source 5a0e0526, §2.14)

- **Comparative education** (the thesis's likely disciplinary home given Ma's "comparative breadth across cultures/traditions"): the field's reflex term for the trans-cultural condition is **"modernity / modernities,"** and the anti-Eurocentric move is *multiple modernities* (Eisenstadt). This is the strongest convention-pull toward "Modernity."

**Takeaway ⑥:** Split by discipline. If the thesis foregrounds **critical-theory critique of domination → "Enlightenment"** is the native register. If it foregrounds **comparative/sociological breadth → "Modernity"** is the native register. The thesis does both, so convention does not settle it; the author's chosen *primary* interlocutor-field should.

## Recommendation (the call is the author's)

On the verbatim, the decision turns on **which of two real liabilities the author would rather carry**, because no term is free:

1. **If the thesis's center of gravity is the *critique of a dominating disposition* with an *ancient-Greek* genealogy** → **"Enlightenment"** is the better fit, and it is *defensible from canon, not opinion*: Adorno & Horkheimer already (a) define enlightenment trans-historically as "the advance of thought," (b) weld it to *domination / mastery / universal fungibility* (a near-synonym for the thesis's "universalization"), and (c) run it back to Plato and Homer (axis ⑤'s citable warrant). The price is axis ③: the author must **explicitly override the 18th-c./European default** in prose and pre-empt the Eurocentrism charge — because, unlike "modernity," "Enlightenment" has no off-the-shelf pluralizing convention to lean on.

2. **If the thesis's center of gravity is *comparative breadth* and *committee reassurance*** → **"Modernity"** is safer: it ships with *multiple modernities* (Eisenstadt) for the anti-Eurocentrism (Ma/Liu) requirement, and the regress the author fears is, in practice, **already solved by the field without "post-"** (Beck's "more-modern, not post-modern"; Latour's mockery of the "post-post-postmodernists"). The price is axes ② and ⑤: "modernity" still *reads* as a period and cannot honestly be back-dated to the Greeks, so a Greek genealogy under a "modernity" label would itself need defending.

3. **A third, genuinely available option** the author may be undervaluing: **keep "Enlightenment" as the named historical *symptom/peak* of the disposition, but give the disposition itself a non-temporal descriptor** (the thesis is already doing this by renaming Domination → **"universalization"**). This dissolves the dilemma: the *disposition* = "universalization" (regress-proof, genealogy-agnostic, valence-loaded by stipulation), and "Enlightenment" / "Modernity" become *named episodes or intensifications* of it rather than the disposition's primary label. Adorno's own move — "enlightenment" as a trans-historical tendency that merely *peaks* in the modern period — is the template. This buys axis ② and ⑤ outright and converts axis ③ into a manageable framing point.

**My weighed read (advisory only):** Given that the disposition already has its own non-temporal name ("universalization"), the cleanest path is **option 3** — let "universalization" do the trans-historical work, and reserve "Enlightenment" (in the explicit Adornian sense, with a one-line disclaimer that it is *not* the 18th-c. period) for the disposition's modern *culmination*. If forced to pick a single label for the disposition, the evidence tilts toward **"Enlightenment"** for *internal* fit (axes ①, ④, ⑤, ⑥-critical-theory) and toward **"Modernity"** for *external/defensive* fit (axes ③, ⑥-comparative-education). The honest tradeoff: **"Enlightenment" minimizes conceptual friction but maximizes the Eurocentrism-rebuttal burden; "Modernity" minimizes the committee-friction but re-imports the period/regress problem the author started from.** This is the author's call to make.
