## 1. Heidegger, *The Question Concerning Technology* (1977)

**Source:** Martin Heidegger, *The Question Concerning Technology and Other Essays*, trans. William Lovitt (New York: Harper & Row, 1977).
**Retrieval route:** PDF on disk, read-only via `pypdf` text extraction — `C:\Users\adler-standard\Zotero\storage\E3XHEXIG\Heidegger - 1977 - Question Concerning Technology, and Other Essays, The.pdf`. **Cross-verified 2026-06-01** against NotebookLM notebook "Martin Heidegger" (`5bcb5a9c-d010-4338-8401-9562dc581b76`), source "Martin Heidegger - The Question Concerning Technology and Other Essays" — `cited_text` matched the PDF string exactly for §1b–1c.

### 1a. "The essence of technology is by no means anything technological" (printed p. 4)

> "Technology is not equivalent to the essence of technology. When we are seeking the essence of 'tree,' we have to become aware that That which pervades every tree, as tree, is not itself a tree that can be encountered among all the other trees. Likewise, the essence of technology is by no means anything technological."
> — Heidegger 1977, p. 4

### 1b. *Physis* is *poiēsis* in the highest sense; technē belongs to bringing-forth (printed pp. 10–11) — VERBATIM, NLM-confirmed

> "It is of utmost importance that we think bringing-forth in its full scope and at the same time in the sense in which the Greeks thought it. Not only handcraft manufacture, not only artistic and poetical bringing into appearance and concrete imagery, is a bringing-forth, poiesis. Physis also, the arising of something from out of itself, is a bringing-forth, poiesis. Physis is indeed poiesis in the highest sense. For what presences by means of physis has the bursting open belonging to bringing-forth, e.g., the bursting of a blossom into bloom, in itself (en heautoi). In contrast, what is brought forth by the artisan or the artist, e.g., the silver chalice, has the bursting open belonging to bringing-forth not in itself, but in another (en alloi), in the craftsman or artist."
> — Heidegger 1977, pp. 10–11 *(PDF + NLM `cited_text` 2026-06-01, exact match)*

On Plato's definition of *poiēsis* / *Her-vor-bringen* (same passage, p. 10):

> "Every occasion for whatever passes over and goes forward into presencing from that which is not presencing is poiesis, is bringing-forth [Her-vor-bringen]."
> — Heidegger 1977, p. 10 (rendering Plato, *Symposium* 205b)

### 1c. Technē is a name for the fine arts; it belongs to *poiēsis*; it is a mode of revealing (printed p. 13) — VERBATIM, NLM-confirmed

> "One is that techne is the name not only for the activities and skills of the craftsman, but also for the arts of the mind and the fine arts. Techne belongs to bringing-forth, to poiesis; it is something poietic."
> — Heidegger 1977, p. 13

> "Techne is a mode of aletheuein. It reveals whatever does not bring itself forth and does not yet lie here before us, whatever can look and turn out now one way and now another."
> — Heidegger 1977, p. 13 *(NLM `cited_text` 2026-06-01)*

> "Thus what is decisive in techne does not lie at all in making and manipulating nor in the using of means, but rather in the aforementioned revealing. It is as revealing, and not as manufacturing, that techne is a bringing-forth."
> — Heidegger 1977, p. 13

### 1d. Technē once named the bringing-forth of the true into the beautiful / the fine arts (printed p. 34)

> "There was a time when it was not technology alone that bore the name techne. Once that revealing that brings forth truth into the splendor of radiant appearing also was called techne. Once there was a time when the bringing-forth of the true into the beautiful was called techne. And the poiesis of the fine arts also was called techne."
> — Heidegger 1977, p. 34

> "In Greece, at the outset of the destining of the West, the arts soared to the supreme height of the revealing granted them. […] And art was simply called techne. It was a single, manifold revealing."
> — Heidegger 1977, p. 34

## 2. Aristotle, *Physics* II.1 (Bekker 192b8–193a)

**Source:** Aristotle, *Physics*, Book II, Part 1, trans. R. P. Hardie and R. K. Gaye.
**Retrieval route:** OA, read-only — MIT Internet Classics Archive, `http://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/physics.2.ii.html` (accessed 2026-05-30). *(Perseus Hopper reader returned HTTP 503 repeatedly on 2026-05-30; the MIT Classics Archive carries the same standard Hardie–Gaye translation.)* Corresponds to **Bekker 192b8–193a**.

> "Of things that exist, some exist by nature, some from other causes. 'By nature' the animals and their parts exist, and the plants and the simple bodies (earth, fire, air, water)—for we say that these and the like exist 'by nature'. All the things mentioned present a feature in which they differ from things which are not constituted by nature. Each of them has within itself a principle of motion and of stationariness (in respect of place, or of growth and decrease, or by way of alteration). On the other hand, a bed and a coat and anything else of that sort, qua receiving these designations i.e. in so far as they are products of art—have no innate impulse to change. But in so far as they happen to be composed of stone or of earth or of a mixture of the two, they do have such an impulse, and just to that extent—which seems to indicate that nature is a source or cause of being moved and of being at rest in that to which it belongs primarily, in virtue of itself and not in virtue of a concomitant attribute."
> — Aristotle, *Physics* II.1, 192b8–193a (Hardie & Gaye trans.)

**Load-bearing distinction:** things *by nature* (φύσει) carry the source of motion/rest **within themselves**; products of *art* (ἀπὸ τέχνης) have **no innate impulse to change** — their source is **external** (in the craftsman). This is the canonical phusis-vs-technē locus classicus.

### 3.5a. NOMOS — full entry VERBATIM (p. 267 region)

> "**NOMOS. Νόμος.** Law; convention. (Noun formed from nemo, distribute; nemesis is another noun based on the same verb.) There is some tension in the way this word is used in Greek philosophy and literature. On the one hand, the nomoi are clearly the laws governing the organization of the state; Plato's longest dialogue, outlining the constitution of an ideal state, is called the Nomoi (Laws). By extension, the rules governing human behavior universally, possibly as decreed by the gods, are often called agraphoi nomoi, unwritten laws (Xenophon, Mem. IV.4, 5–25; Sophocles, Oed. Tyr. 863–871; et al.). As Heraclitus says (f. 114, in part), 'All human nomoi are nourished by the one divine (nomos).' On the other hand, legislated laws tended, at least by the 5th century BCE, to be seen as arbitrary and variable from one society to another. Thus some introduced the idea of a contrasting nature (physis) that was not subject to social variability. Antiphon provides a particularly good example of a writer who emphasizes this contrast (DK 87A44). Plato often represents the Sophists arguing on the basis of this contrast. Thrasymachus in Republic I, Callicles in the Gorgias, and Protagoras in the Protagoras are just a few examples. Democritus took the contrast back into the scientific context, at least in epistemological terms, when he said, 'By convention (by nomos) sweet by convention bitter, by convention hot by convention cold, in reality atoms and void.' The Stoics supposed that nature operates according to divine reason, or logos, so that the tension or dialectic between nature and law is resolved: physics (physikē) and ethics are both part of the same rational system, the thought of God. […] Possibly the clearest exposition of this Stoic position is in Cicero's De Legibus II."
> — Preus 2015, s.v. NOMOS

### 3.5b. TECHNĒ — full entry VERBATIM (p. 380 region)

> "**TECHNĒ. Τέχνη.** Art, craft, skill. In Homer, examples of technai would be metalworking, shipbuilding, soothsaying, and general trickiness; the Hippocratic texts hold that medicine (iatrikē) is a technē. There is a sense that technē is in some measure opposed to nature (physis); see TECHNIKOS. In classical society there was a widespread belief that mastery of one or more arts was tantamount to sophia, or wisdom. In that context, the Sophists proposed to instruct in the 'arts' of speaking, use of language, and especially governance (politikē)—Protagoras, Gorgias, and Thrasymachus are all reported to have used 'Technē' in the title of an instructional book. […] In Republic I, Thrasymachus readily agrees with Socrates that there is an 'art' of governing, analogous to medicine and sheepherding […]."
> — Preus 2015, s.v. TECHNĒ

### 3.5c. Short glossary glosses (back-matter, p. 455–461) — VERBATIM

> "**NOMOS.** Law; convention." · "**PHYSIS, HISTORIA PERI PHYSEŌS.** Nature; the study of nature." · "**TECHNĒ.** Art, craft, skill." · "**TECHNIKOS.** Skillful, but also often 'artificial.'" · "**POIĒSIS.** Making, production." · "**POIEIN.** To make or do." · "**POIĒMA.** An object that is made."
> — Preus 2015, Glossary

## 4. The Sophistic nomos–phusis antithesis (SEP + IEP)

### 4a. Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — VERBATIM

**Source:** "The Sophists," *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy*, §2 *Nomos* and *Phusis*. **Route:** OA read-only — `https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/sophists/` (accessed 2026-05-30).

> "The debate was fundamentally about the status of moral and other social norms; were such norms ever in some sense part of or grounded in the reality of things, or were they in every case mere products of human customs, conventions or beliefs?"
> — SEP, "The Sophists," §2

> "Critics of traditional morality argued that since that morality was nothing more than a human invention it thereby lacked genuine authority (which belonged to the realm of nature)…"
> — SEP, "The Sophists," §2

> "Both sides agreed in seeing nature as authoritative for correct human behavior, and as the ultimate source of true value."
> — SEP, "The Sophists," §2

### 4b. Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy — VERBATIM

**Source:** "The Sophists," *IEP*, §3.a Nature and Convention. **Route:** OA read-only — `https://iep.utm.edu/sophists/` (accessed 2026-05-30).

> "The term *nomos* refers to a wide range of normative concepts extending from customs and conventions to positive law."
> — IEP, "The Sophists," §3.a

> "This recognition sets up the possibility of a dichotomy between what is unchanging and according to nature and what is merely a product of arbitrary human convention."
> — IEP, "The Sophists," §3.a

**Load-bearing point:** *nomos* = what is **humanly instituted / posited / made** (custom → convention → positive law); *phusis* = the **given**, the unchanging-by-nature. The antithesis is precisely a *made-vs-given* distinction.

### 6c. von Staden, "Physis and Technē in Greek Medicine" — the *ancient* "physis as technē" inversion (ch. 2)

Section heading in the chapter (von Staden's own): **"'Nature' as 'Art': Natural Technology and Technical Nature."**

> "In early Hellenistic medicine, as in the works of Galen, nature (physis) itself often was explicitly depicted as working like, acting like, or being like technē, rather than as resisting (or being opposed to) technē. One of the two third-century BCE pioneers of systematic human dissection, Erasistratus, for example, repeatedly characterized nature as technikē—that is, as doing things in a way that is characteristic of technē, or as 'capable of technē.' Here it is not a case of technē imitating physis, but rather of physis displaying characteristics that are best understood by their similarity to technē."
> — Heinrich von Staden, "Physis and Technē in Greek Medicine," in Bensaude-Vincent & Newman 2007

> "The characterization of the activities of nature in terms of technē has a long history. Plato, Aristotle, and the Stoics all depicted nature as operating in a manner characteristic of technē."
> — von Staden, ibid.

> "Aristotle's claim that 'technē imitates physis' (a view endorsed by his successor Theophrastus […]) has received much attention, but in his biological works it is technē that repeatedly provides a model for understanding aspects of nature (physis), notably of the nature of a living thing as end-directed. […] Physis and technē thus both display goal-driven, expert activity."
> — von Staden, ibid.

**Load-bearing for the thesis:** von Staden documents that the ancients already ran the relation in BOTH directions — "technē imitates physis" AND "physis [is] technikē / physis as art." The author's "tekhne is becoming phusis" is the *converse* directional move; §6c shows the conceptual traffic between the two terms is ancient, while the *metaverse-driven, asymmetric* direction is the author's own (see §8).

### 7a. The phrase itself — "the marriage of physis and techne"

It seems beyond doubt that a successful marriage between physis and techne is vital for our future and hence worth our sustained efforts. […] a divorce between physis and techne would be utterly disastrous both for our welfare and for the wellbeing of our habitat."
> — Floridi, *Information: A Very Short Introduction* (2010), Epilogue ("the marriage of physis and techne") — on-disk PDF

### 7d. Third-order technologies — technology-to-technology relations, the human "off the loop"

### 7g. Floridi vs. the author's *directional* claim — convergence & divergence (analysis, from verbatim only)

**What Floridi means by *physis*.** Two distinct senses appear, and they must be kept apart:
1. **Practical/ecological sense** (VSI Epilogue; *Green and Blue* §69): *physis* = "nature, reality," the **natural environment / habitat**; *techne* = "practical science and its applications" / "digital technologies." Here the "marriage" is a **normative, political project** — a *symbiosis to be achieved* between the green (environment) and the blue (digital), explicitly framed as "a practical problem, whose feasible solution needs to be devised" and as a *marriage that may be successful* "provided the right sort of commitment is made." This is a relation of **alliance/coexistence**, NOT one term becoming the other.
2. **Epistemic/ontological sense** (*Logic of Information* ch. 3): "the natural is **in itself artefactual** (a semantic construction)"; "the non-natural is our **first** nature, and the natural is actually our **second** nature"; the call for "a **genealogy of the natural from the non-natural, not vice versa**." Here Floridi is most radical: he does NOT say the made is *becoming* given; he says the "natural" was always already a *construction* (a model of the noumenal) — i.e. the natural is **downstream of** the artefactual/semantic. The infosphere "blurs" and "erases" the distinction; ICTs "re-ontologize" by transforming intrinsic natures and "artificializing/denaturalizing" the world.

**Convergence with the author.** (i) Both reject a sealed natural/artificial (phusis/technē) boundary. (ii) Both treat the made as ontologically heavyweight: Floridi's "re-ontologizing" — "fundamentally transforms the intrinsic nature […], that is, its ontology" — is close in spirit to the thesis's claim that a neo-artificial layer can take on the *ontological weight of a substrate*. (iii) Floridi's "third-order technologies" that put the human "off the loop" and can be "human-independent" / "in charge of their own behaviours" supply a verbatim warrant for technē acquiring an **autonomous, self-running** character — exactly the property Aristotle reserved for *phusis* (an internal "principle of motion," §2). This is the strongest single bridge to "tekhne is becoming phusis": Floridi documents the made acquiring **self-driving autonomy**, which is the formal mark of the *given* in Aristotle.

**Divergence (the load-bearing point).** Floridi's relation is **symmetric / mutual / erasing**, and where it is directional it runs the *opposite* way to the author's:
- The "marriage / alliance / synthesis" vocabulary is **bidirectional reconciliation** (green ↔ blue, natural ↔ artificial co-flourishing), not a one-way conversion.
- Where Floridi IS directional, his arrow is **natural ← artefactual**: "a genealogy **of the natural from the non-natural**, not vice versa"; "the natural is **in itself** artefactual." That is, for Floridi the *natural* was always already *made/semantic* — he collapses physis *into* technē/construction at the level of knowledge.
- The author's claim "**tekhne is becoming phusis**" is the **converse and explicitly temporal/directional**: the *made* (technē/metaverse) is *acquiring the character of the given* (phusis) — the made hardening into an unchosen substrate that confronts humans as nature does. Floridi gives no such *asymmetric, made→given, substrate-formation* thesis; his closest move ("re-ontologizing," "third-order autonomy") describes the *mechanism* without asserting the *directional ontological outcome* the author needs.

**Net:** Floridi is the strongest *evidential ally* for the thesis (he verifies (a) the boundary's collapse, (b) the ontological heft of the made via "re-ontologizing," and (c) the made's autonomy via "third-order technologies"), but he is **NOT a source for the directional claim itself**. "Tekhne is becoming phusis" remains the author's synthesis; Floridi's own directional arrow (natural-from-non-natural) actually points the other way, and his "marriage" is a normative symbiosis, not an ontological conversion.
