## A. Section Arc

### Part IV Preface (book p199–203)

The preface introduces the third analytical approach of the book: the *analysis of 〈relationality〉* (〈関係性〉の分析). The first approach was environmental-philosophical; the second was the *analysis of 〈life〉* (focusing on the human as 〈living-being〉 within 〈lifeworld〉). The third now shifts to the more ontological plane: human beings as constituted *through* the relational field — defined by the innumerable "meaningful 〈relationships〉" that bind them to others.

The preface lays out the programme for both Chapter 7 and Chapter 8. For Chapter 7: to construct the basic theoretical framework by (i) elaborating the *I–Thou structure* as the minimal unit of human relationality; (ii) deriving two further regulatory mechanisms — *aidagara* (betweenness/social-relational type) and *distance* — that reduce the burden inherent in all meaningful relationship; and (iii) demonstrating that this burden can be modulated but never eliminated, and that the contemporary attempt to eliminate it through the "zero-attribute ethics" is both philosophically confused and socially destructive. For Chapter 8: to address the conditions of 〈co-action〉 (共同). [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp199–203]

### Chapter 7 — Section-by-Section

#### §1: The concept of "human 〈relationality〉" (pp205–207)

Uegaki opens by defining *relationality* (関係性) ontologically: not merely psychological or social but a property of *how beings are bound to one another*. The chapter focuses specifically on "human 〈relationality〉" — i.e. the mode of binding that is specific to human existence. The inquiry therefore draws on both sociology (social action theory from Weber, Durkheim, Parsons, Luhmann, Mead/Blumer, Schütz) and philosophy (other-theory: Husserl's inter-subjectivity, Sartre's *conflit*, Levinas's *responsabilité*, Arendt's *space of appearance*). Uegaki acknowledges this background but marks his divergence: the sociological tradition focused on value/norm as systemic glue; the philosophical tradition focused on the threat to self-identity or the ethical demand of alterity. Neither, he argues, fully captures the structural burdens that constitute ordinary human relationship. The book's own framework must be ontologically grounded *and* connected to the prior environmental and life-analysis approaches. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp205–207]

---

#### §2: The I–Thou structure as the basic form of human 〈relationality〉 (pp207–211)

**Core definition.** Uegaki redefines the *other* (〈他者存在〉) as: that which is essentially *not at the disposal of* self (意のままにならない存在), and in relation to which a "meaningful 〈relationship〉" can be constituted. A meaningful relationship is one where the other's *otherness* (他者性) — its irreducible difference, its capacity to resist full comprehension — is not merely noted but *constitutive of the meaning*. [VERIFY-PAGE: book p208/184]

The grandfather example: The deceased grandfather is an *other* whose life cannot be made to conform to "my" will — there are facts in his past I cannot undo or know fully. Yet it is precisely *because* the relationship resists total mastery that it has deep *meaning*. [VERIFY-PAGE: book p209]

**The 〈I–Thou〉 structure** (〈我-汝〉の構造) is Uegaki's term for this basic unit: the mutually constitutive interaction between self (〈我〉=I) and other (〈汝〉=Thou) in which "meaningful 〈relationship〉" arises. It is explicitly a reading *through* but also a departure from Buber. [VERIFY-PAGE: book p209 / footnote 9 at p232] Buber's original I–Thou / I–It distinction maps onto: the I–Thou structure = proper relationship with the other as "not at disposal"; the I–It relation = treating the other as *at disposal*, as manipulable object. Uegaki adopts the structural term but generalises it beyond Buber's theological-dialogical context.

**〈Self-existence〉** (〈自己存在〉) is correspondingly redefined: *not* a pure, constant, pre-relational ego, but the loose aggregate of the many "I"s that emerge across the innumerable I–Thou structures. If the Thou changes, the I that appears also changes. "I" is therefore: that which arises through unavoidable encounter with others who are not at my disposal, comprehended only vaguely as a totality across all such encounters. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp209–210]

**〈The field of relationality〉** (〈関係性〉の場): the background web of all the mutually influencing I–Thou structures that constitute a given self-existence at any moment. Uegaki uses this concept (borrowed and reworked from Yoshida Norihiko's "media" concept) to capture the networked, resonant character of self: any interaction arrives not from the isolated other but through the entire field — past and present relationships "vibrating" together. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp210–211 / footnote 13–14]

**The first internal tension — "real image / mirror image"** (実像-写像の内的緊張): In any relationship with a "core other" (中核的他者 — the face-to-face, everyday significant others), the *I's image of the relationship* (real image / 実像の〈関係性〉) and the *other's image of the relationship* (mirror image / 写像の〈関係性〉) necessarily differ, because neither party can fully know the other's I–Thou structure. This non-coincidence is irreducible — it derives from the fundamental not-at-disposal character of the other — and it requires constant adjustment. Active *intervention* (介入) into the other's intent, and willingness to be intervened upon, is actually necessary to maintain a healthy relationship. When multiple persons are simultaneously present, each person's I–Thou structures interfere with all others, exponentially increasing the burden. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp212–213]

#### §3: The concept of 〈aidagara〉 (betweenness) (pp211–216)

**Problem:** The real-image/mirror-image tension, multiplied across complex multi-person settings, would be unmanageable without a regulatory mechanism. Uegaki turns to sociology — Durkheim's "collective representations" (集合意識), Mead's "generalised other" (一般化された他者) — and proposes his own re-reading of these as *aidagara* (〈間柄〉). [VERIFY-PAGE: book p213]

**Definition of 〈aidagara〉:** An *abstraction of a specific type of relationship* — a socially shared "relational template" — together with its associated *aidagara-prescription* (〈間柄規定〉), the normatively expected pattern of behaviour within that template. Examples: parent/child, teacher/student, colleague, employer/employee, buyer/seller, friends, lovers. These are not merely descriptive categories but *performative scripts* that pre-structure how one should act toward another in a given relational context. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp215–216]

**How aidagara regulates tension:** By invoking a shared aidagara, the two parties *formalise* their I–Thou structure. This means neither party needs to grapple with the full complexity of the other's relational field; they need only execute the prescribed role. The most powerful example: the buyer/seller relationship, where "personalising" the other is entirely irrelevant, and yet smooth interaction proceeds. Mead's "generalised other" is thus re-described in Uegaki's framework as the aggregate of available aidagara that a person has internalised. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp214–216]

**The second internal tension — the burden of aidagara:** Aidagara reduces the first tension but introduces a second. Because aidagara is externally assigned (by birth, social position, circumstance), it may override the individual's relational field and I–Thou structure entirely, rendering the person's "existence intensity" (存在の強度) unrecognisable. The individual is forced to perform a role that may be contrary to their own relational reality. Moreover, if aidagara were the *only* regulator — if all relationship were "formalised" — then no "meaningful relationship" could arise at all, because meaningful relationship requires somewhere that the I–Thou structure re-emerges from behind the aidagara mask. Aidagara, however, is modifiable over time (Blumer's point about the fluidity of meaning); the dilemma is that it changes slowly, as it must if it is to function at all. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp216–219]

---

#### §4: The concept of 〈distance〉 (pp216–220)

**Problem:** Neither total aidagara-formalisation nor total absence of aidagara is liveable. Something must regulate *when and how far* the aidagara "posture is relaxed" (間柄の構えを解く) — i.e., when and how the I–Thou structure re-emerges *within* the aidagara context. Uegaki introduces 〈distance〉 (〈距離〉) for this purpose. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp219–220]

**Critique of Goffman's "role distance":** Goffman's concept names the gap between expected behaviour and actual behaviour (e.g., the young rebel, the surgeon who jokes in the OR) — a *deviance* from aidagara-prescription. Uegaki's distance is different: it is not deviance from aidagara but the *degree to which the I–Thou structure is brought back* into a relationship that is already operating through aidagara. [VERIFY-PAGE: book p219]

**Definition of 〈distance〉:** The degree to which parties are willing to *de-formalise* their relationship — to release the aidagara posture and allow direct I–Thou encounter. "Close distance" means the two parties meet in many contexts via I–Thou structure, not just aidagara; "far distance" means they meet almost entirely through aidagara-prescription. This modulation is what Uegaki calls "de-formalisation" (脱形式化), the counterpart to aidagara's "formalisation." [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp219–220]

**The third internal tension — distance mismatch:** De-formalisation re-exposes the real-image/mirror-image tension. And crucially, the *desired distance* of the "I" and the *desired distance* of the "other" may not coincide. If "I" wish to drop the aidagara posture but "other" does not, this itself destabilises the relationship. Managing distance requires reading the other's signals — and this is a skill that can fail. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp220, and Figure 5 at p221 (partly garbled in OCR)]

**Summary statement (Uegaki's explicit claim, ~p222):** Human beings have been born into a world of "not-at-disposal others" from the very first moment of life. The I–Thou structure, aidagara, and distance are the inherited toolkit for navigating this constitutive burden. But that toolkit only *modulates* the burden; it cannot eliminate it. Therefore: **a burden-free 〈relationship〉 is impossible from the outset** (負担を伴わない〈関係性〉というものははじめから存在しえない). [VERIFY-PAGE: book p222]

#### §5: "Zero-attribute ethics" and the "at-disposal other" (pp220–230)

**Context — "0 or 1 〈relationship〉" (〇か1かの〈関係性〉):** Contemporary life in the 〈self-sufficient society〉 has produced a pathological polarity: either relationship operates entirely through aidagara (as in economic transaction) — fully formalised, depersonalised, no I–Thou contact — or it operates with *no aidagara at all* and demands total I–Thou exposure from the first moment. The middle ground — appropriately calibrated distance — has been lost. Internet-mediated relations exemplify the second mode: "infrastructure" (search engines, SNS, comment columns, blogs) provides the connectivity platform without any aidagara, so that encounters are either fully anonymous or demand immediate total self-disclosure. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp223–225]

**Definition of 〈zero-attribute ethics〉** (ゼロ属性の倫理): The ethical imperative that *every* relationship must be conducted without bringing in any social position, role, or bodily attribute whatsoever — treating each person exclusively as "this irreplaceable I" (かけがえのないこの私). No aidagara is to be invoked; all aidagara are understood as unjust oppression of individual uniqueness. The ethics demands that "the real me" and "the authentic other" meet unmediated. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp225–226]

**Genealogy:** This ethics is the direct extension of the 〈autonomous individual〉 ideal — freedom and equality demand liberation from all normative constraint. Aidagara, on this view, is recast as a manifestation of collective power, a totalitarian suppression of the unique person. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp225–226]

**Why it is pathological (Uegaki's argument):**

1. The very premise — an "authentic self" independent of relational field — is a fiction. 〈Self-existence〉 *is* constituted by the I–Thou structures; there is no "invariant pure I" beneath them. The "authentic self" celebrated by this ethics is in fact a fictitious self, swollen by self-consciousness, detached from the relational reality that makes meaningful selfhood possible. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp227–228]

2. Eliminating aidagara does not liberate relationship; it forces *all* relationship to operate through naked I–Thou structure from the start — which is exactly the condition human beings find unendurable. Human encounter *begins* through aidagara and *gradually* measures distance toward I–Thou; it cannot skip this sequence. [VERIFY-PAGE: book p226]

3. The zero-attribute ethics produces the "0 or 1 relationship" pathology: without aidagara and calibrated distance, only two outcomes remain — either no relationship at all ("parallel-world" strangers), or total mutual exposure (the "bottomless consideration" / 底なしの配慮) that exhausts both parties. Neither is a functioning relationship. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp225–226, 229–230]

4. The ethics systematically *underestimates the necessarily suppressive dimension of aidagara*. Some degree of aidagara-induced constraint — willing acceptance of a role not fully of one's choosing — is not merely an unfortunate external imposition but a *constitutive feature* of what it means to live among others. To eliminate aidagara entirely is to pretend that human existence can be freed from its finite, embodied, temporally and socially situated character. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp228–229]

**Definition of the "at-disposal other"** (意のままになる他者): The pathological figure demanded by the logic of the zero-attribute ethics and the "authentic self" ideology. When "I" demand that my "authentic self" be unconditionally accepted, I am covertly demanding an other who conforms entirely to my will — who imposes no burden, whose "authentic self" I am free to reject whenever inconvenient. This is the at-disposal other: an other reconstructed in the image of my own desire, incapable of genuine otherness, and therefore incapable of sustaining a "meaningful relationship." [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp226–228]

Uegaki's key inversion (~p227–228): We seek the at-disposal other as if it would give us fuller selfhood, but the opposite is true. Genuine fulfillment in relationship arises precisely *because* the other is *not* at disposal — when, after managing aidagara and distance across the irreducible internal tension, a moment arrives where the "misalignment" (ずれ) is temporarily resolved. Remove the not-at-disposal character and you dissolve both the relationship and the meaningful self. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp227–228]

**Consequence — self-reinforcing spiral:** The zero-attribute ethics, by promoting the at-disposal ideal, produces (a) repeated failure in 0-or-1 relationship; (b) retreat into the "ethics of non-intervention" (不介入の倫理, introduced earlier in the book), i.e. the decision to form no meaningful relationships at all; (c) deeper dependence on 〈social apparatus〉 as substitute for genuine 〈relationship〉. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp229–230]

**The correct response (Uegaki, ~p229):** Not to abolish aidagara but to *renovate* the content of aidagara-prescriptions that have become outdated (時代に合わなくなった〈間柄規定〉を修整する). The burden cannot be removed; the task is to redistribute it better. [VERIFY-PAGE: book p229]

## D. Relevance to Thesis Ch IV §B / §F

### D.1 Direct structural support for §B (Burden-free relationality is impossible)

Uegaki's Chapter 7 is the most systematic available argument that **every "meaningful relationship" is constitutively burdened**. The three-internal-tension sequence (real-image/mirror-image → aidagara's second tension → distance mismatch) provides a layered demonstration that burden is not a contingent feature of imperfect social arrangements but an ontological feature of the I–Thou structure itself. §B can cite this as the relational-anthropological basis for the claim that the positive-liberty synthesis (which implicitly promises burden-free self-actualisation through relationship) fails at the anthropological level before it fails at any political or institutional level.

Specific passage for §B: the explicit statement at ~p222 that "burden-free relationship cannot exist from the outset" — this is verbatim support for the §B claim. [VERIFY-PAGE: book p222]

### D.2 The zero-attribute ethics as the contemporary enactment of the positive-liberty synthesis

The zero-attribute ethics is the ethic that the positive-liberty synthesis (freedom as liberation from all heteronomy, equal recognition of all "authentic selves") naturally generates at the level of interpersonal ethics. Uegaki's critique therefore hits the same target as the thesis's §A/§F critique of the positive-liberty synthesis, but from the bottom up (anthropological structure) rather than from the top down (political philosophy). This parallelism strengthens the thesis's argument: the positive-liberty synthesis fails not only because it produces unstable political philosophy but because it requires a fiction about the self and about relationship that is empirically and ontologically unsupportable.

### D.3 The at-disposal other as the relational symptom of self-sufficient society

The "at-disposal other" concept connects directly to the thesis's analysis of the metaverse as a domain where the other *is* (or appears to be) at disposal — content can be curated, unwanted others unfriended, interactions terminated at will. This makes Uegaki an argument that the metaverse *gratifies* the pathological desire for the at-disposal other while further atrophying the capacity for genuine I–Thou encounter. §F can use this to argue that the squeeze is not merely spatial/ontological but relational: the metaverse squeezes out the "not-at-disposal other" by providing a substitute that satisfies the surface need for connection while systematically dissolving the conditions under which meaningful selfhood is formed.

### D.4 Non-intervention ethics as terminus ad quem

The cascade — zero-attribute ethics → 0-or-1 relationship → failure → non-intervention ethics → dependence on social apparatus — maps directly onto the thesis's macro-argument about the self-sufficient society. The final stage (abandonment of relationship, deeper apparatus-dependence) is the relational face of the thesis's "fully actualised" closure: the self-sufficient society produces not just an isolated self but a self that has renounced the relational foundation of meaningful existence.

### D.5 Aidagara as corrective: what the positive-liberty synthesis rejects

Uegaki's positive recommendation — renovate aidagara-prescription rather than abolish aidagara — parallels the thesis's implied move toward a "harmonious" (non-squeezed) mode of existence that is not the totalisation of either universe or metaverse but a maintained balance. The aidagara concept shows that *some* normative constraint on relationship is not oppression but a precondition of liveable human life. This is important for §F's account of what is lost when the metaverse offers frictionless at-disposal connection.

## E. Brief Report (5–8 bullets)

- **ゼロ属性の倫理 (zero-attribute ethics)** is defined at book pp225–226 as the imperative to relate to every person *without* invoking any social role, status, or bodily attribute — treating each person purely as "this irreplaceable I." It is genealogised as the interpersonal expression of the 〈autonomous individual〉 ideal (freedom = liberation from all heteronomous norm). Its pathology is that it misidentifies aidagara as oppression and therefore pursues a world without aidagara — which is anthropologically impossible and practically produces the "0-or-1 relationship." [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp225–226]

- **意のままになる他者 (the at-disposal / willed other)** is defined at book pp226–228 as the figure covertly demanded by anyone who insists that their "authentic self" must be unconditionally accepted: an other reconstructed to conform entirely to one's will, imposing no burden, refusing nothing. Uegaki argues this demand is self-defeating: the at-disposal other is no longer a genuine 〈other-existence〉 and therefore cannot sustain a "meaningful relationship"; without the not-at-disposal character of the other, the "meaningful self" also dissolves. [VERIFY-PAGE: book pp226–228]

- **The impossibility of burden-free relationship** is stated explicitly at ~p222: "a relationship without burden cannot exist from the outset." This follows from the ontological definition of 〈other-existence〉 as essentially "not-at-disposal," which guarantees the real-image/mirror-image internal tension as a permanent feature of all meaningful relationship. The aidagara and distance mechanisms modulate the burden but cannot remove it. [VERIFY-PAGE: book p222]

- **The I–Thou structure** (pp207–211) is the minimal unit of human relationality; 〈self-existence〉 is entirely constituted by the aggregate of I–Thou structures — there is no prior "pure I." This anti-essentialist anthropology is what grounds the pathology diagnosis: the zero-attribute ethics presupposes a pure self that does not exist and demands an at-disposal other that cannot exist.

- **Aidagara** (pp211–216) is a *necessary* regulatory mechanism, not a contingent cultural residue. It "formalises" I–Thou encounters by providing shared relational scripts (間柄規定), thereby reducing the first internal tension. But it generates a second tension (forced conformity to assigned role) that must be managed through 〈distance〉-calibration. All three internal tensions are irreducible — they constitute the burden that the zero-attribute ethics falsely promises to eliminate.

- **The 0-or-1 relationship** (pp223–225) is the contemporary structural outcome of the zero-attribute ethics *plus* the infrastructure of 〈social apparatus〉: either fully aidagara-scripted transactions (economic, bureaucratic) or totally unmediated I–Thou exposure (internet, "parallel world" strangers). The middle ground — appropriate distance, functioning aidagara — has been lost. This is the structural context in which the at-disposal other is sought.

- **The non-intervention ethics** (pp229–230) is the terminus ad quem of the cascade: after repeated failure in 0-or-1 relationship, many people renounce meaningful relationship altogether and retreat further into social apparatus dependence. This directly maps onto the thesis's account of the self-sufficient society's relational dissolution as the final relational expression of the "fully actualised" closure.
