## Chapter overview

Chapter 10 is the book's terminal synthetic argument. Uegaki opens by introducing the lens of「世界観=人間観」(worldview-cum-anthropology) and framing the chapter's dual task: (a) tracing the genealogy of〈無限の生〉("limitless life" / "life-as-one-wills") back through modern Western philosophy to Renaissance Christianity, showing that the ideal of the self-owning, disembodied rational subject is its root; and (b) introducing〈有限の生〉("finite life" / "life-not-as-one-wills") as the counter-concept and proposing its five principles as the ground for a liveable anthropology. The chapter then follows the logical terminus of〈無限の生〉into a reductio: the "brain-human" thought experiment, in which absolute disembodiment and total self-completion annihilate the human being they were supposed to perfect. The final two sections pivot to〈有限の生〉's affirmative content: living with each of the five irreducible constraints, cultivating〈世界了解〉(world-comprehension), and locating the possibility of human〈救い〉(salvation/relief) in finite existence rather than its overcoming.

## §1–§5 Section Summaries

### §1 (lines 3976–4049): 〈無限の生〉と〈有限の生〉―「世界観=人間観」という視座

Uegaki introduces the organizing lens: human perception and action are always shaped by a pre-thematized「世界観=人間観」internalized during socialization. He argues that the structural transformation of the〈生活世界〉driving the〈自己完結社会〉has a single underlying worldview: the conviction that〈無限の生〉—"life-as-one-wills"—is humanity's proper condition, and that "life-not-as-one-wills" must be overcome. He shows that this worldview connects both〈生の脱身体化〉(de-bodification of life) and〈生の自己完結化〉(self-enclosure of life), because both are liberation from irreducible〈座〉-premises of existence: the not-as-willed body and the not-as-willed other. The section closes by previewing the counter-concept〈有限の生〉and announcing the chapter's method of confronting the two worldviews.

**Key terms introduced/resolved:** 世界観=人間観, 〈無限の生〉, 「意のままになる生」/「意のままにならない生」, 〈生の脱身体化〉, 〈生の自己完結化〉, 〈有限の生〉

---

### §2 (lines 4034–4263): 〈無限の生〉と西洋近代哲学の深淵

This section traces the genealogy of〈無限の生〉. Two structural presuppositions of modern freedom are identified: (1)「時空間的自立性」—the assumption that the human subject pre-exists all relational embeddedness and is therefore a kind of「思念体」(thought-entity) without body, birth, death, or generational continuity; and (2)「約束された本来性」—the assumption that a "true/authentic human being" exists in the future, currently blocked by "iron chains," whose realization is humanity's moral mission. Rousseau's famous sentence is the exemplary formulation. Uegaki shows how Kant's autonomy-doctrine extended this from political to「存在論的自由」(ontological freedom), and how Hegel and Marx then embedded it in philosophies of history as collective self-liberation. The Renaissance Christian matrix is identified as the deeper source: the belief that humans, as God's image-bearers endowed with reason, could take "God's viewpoint" and know what the "completed human" should look like. This belief—not politics—is what made the foundation for〈無限の生〉possible.

**Key terms introduced/resolved:** 時空間的自立性, 約束された本来性, 本来の人間 / 完全な人間, 思念体, 存在論的自由, 存在論的抑圧, 積極的自由, 自律 (Autonomie) / 他律 (Heteronomie)

---

### §3 (lines 4237–4498): 〈無限の生〉の「無間地獄」

Uegaki demonstrates the immanent contradiction of〈無限の生〉via the "ideals that negate reality" / "ideals that accompany reality" distinction, and through two fables (the young vegetarian, the teacher with hot iron). Because〈無限の生〉takes "complete human" as its anchor point outside reality, every achieved liberation reveals a new unfreedom; the suffering is structural, not contingent. The section then shows that〈無限の生〉paradoxically gained its fullest traction when modern technology made partial realization possible: once "life-as-one-wills" appears achievable, the remainder of "life-not-as-one-wills" becomes intolerably "abnormal." The values of modern liberal society—freedom, equality, autonomy, coexistence—are each shown to have been realized precisely as〈ユーザー〉-mediated forms through the〈社会的装置〉, which means further pursuit of them deepens〈自己完結社会〉.

**Key terms introduced/resolved:** 「現実を否定する理想」vs.「現実に寄り添う理想」, 無間地獄, 〈ユーザー〉としての自由/平等/自律/共生, 「意のままにならないもの」としての身体

---

### §4 (lines 4469–4666): 究極の「ユートピア」―「脳人間」と「自殺の権利」

The reductio of〈無限の生〉is dramatized through two limit-cases: (a) the「脳人間」thought experiment—if〈生の脱身体化〉reaches its terminus, the brain-connected-to-apparatus human achieves total freedom within virtual space, but the remaining "not-as-willed" element is the brain itself; discarding it means discarding humanity entirely; (b) the right-to-suicide / euthanasia trajectory—in a society where self-determination is sovereign, the logical endpoint is publicly facilitated death facilities. Uegaki interprets this not as a reductio of individual choice but as the self-abolishing terminus of the worldview: realizing "the complete human" requires ceasing to be human. The「脳人間」ultimately cuts their power supply out of boredom—a figure for the nihilism inherent in the total actualization of〈無限の生〉.

**Key terms introduced/resolved:** 脳人間, 「自殺の権利」, 究極の〈自己完結社会〉, 「生命維持装置」, 「こうでなければならないこの私」

---

### §5 (lines 4640–5301): 〈有限の生〉とともに生きる / 〈世界了解〉―人間の〈救い〉について

The chapter's constructive half. Uegaki restates〈有限の生〉's five principles (生物存在の原則, 生受の条件の原則, 意のままにならない他者の原則, 人間の〈悪〉とわざわいの原則, 不確実な未来の原則), argues that each is structurally ineliminable, and shows why living with them rather than eliminating them is the only non-self-defeating path. He then introduces〈世界了解〉as the name for the attitudinal act of accepting "that which is not-as-one-wills"—including the not-as-willed body—as the irreducible condition of meaningful existence; this is supported by three pillars (personal engagement with reality, the not-as-willed other, and locating oneself in〈存在の連なり〉). The section closes with a meditation on death and〈存在の連なり〉: human death is unlike biological death because the traces of a human life persist in the social fabric; this is both the source of obligation and the ground of meaning.

**Key terms introduced/resolved:** 〈有限の生〉の五原則 (全五項), 〈世界了解〉, 〈救い〉, 〈存在の連なり〉, 「生受の条件」

## Argument Map

- **Premise 1:** Human action is shaped by a pre-thematized「世界観=人間観」that we internalize without noticing (lines 3981–3993).
- **Move 1:** The structural transformation of the〈生活世界〉driving〈自己完結社会〉is powered by a specific worldview: that〈無限の生〉= "life-as-one-wills" is the proper human form (lines 4001–4026).
- **Move 2:** Both〈生の脱身体化〉and〈生の自己完結化〉are expressions of this single worldview, since both aim at liberation from irreducible〈座〉-premises: body and other (lines 4013–4020).
- **Move 3 (genealogy):** This worldview originates in two structural presuppositions of modern Western philosophy: (a) temporal-spatial self-sufficiency (「時空間的自立性」→「思念体」), and (b) promised authenticity (「約束された本来性」→「本来の人間」/「完全な人間」) (lines 4057–4089).
- **Move 4 (genealogy):** These presuppositions were made possible by Renaissance Christianity's conviction that reason gives humans access to "God's viewpoint," enabling knowledge of the "completed human" (lines 4213–4226).
- **Move 5 (genealogy):** Kant extended this from political freedom to「存在論的自由」; Hegel and Marx historicized it; postmodern collapse of the "grand narrative" did not end it but re-privatized it (lines 4104–4138).
- **Move 6 (contradiction):** Because〈無限の生〉is an "ideal that negates reality" (rather than one that accompanies reality), each achieved liberation reveals a new unfreedom: structural infinite regress → the「無間地獄」(lines 4281–4297).
- **Move 7 (acceleration):** The contradiction intensifies when technology makes partial realization possible: "life-as-one-wills" becomes "normal" and its remainder becomes "abnormal/intolerable" (lines 4362–4366).
- **Move 8 (terminus):** The logical terminus is the「脳人間」: at maximum disembodiment, the remaining "not-as-willed" element is the brain → discard the brain → "true freedom" achieved → humanity abolished (lines 4521–4556).
- **Move 9 (parallel terminus):** The same logic drives the right-to-suicide: life that is no longer "life-as-one-wills" is "not worth living" → willed death as the final self-completion of〈無限の生〉(lines 4591–4611).
- **Move 10 (self-abolition thesis):** Realizing the "complete human" of〈無限の生〉means simultaneously ceasing to be human — the worldview is self-negating (lines 4553–4556, 4653–4656).
- **Pivot:** The alternative is not resignation but a different worldview: accepting "life-not-as-one-wills" as the irreducible condition of human existence — this is〈有限の生〉(lines 4641–4677).
- **Move 11 (five principles):** Five ineliminable constraints are enumerated: biological existence, birth-conditions, not-as-willed other, evil/misfortune, uncertain future; each is structurally beyond elimination (lines 4648–4892).
- **Move 12 (positive argument):** Meaning, reverence, joy, solidarity — all arise from finitude's constraints; an infinite life would have no meaning (lines 5066–5086).
- **Move 13 (world-comprehension):** The attitudinal act of accepting this world as it is — including the not-as-willed body — is〈世界了解〉; affirming〈有限の生〉just is achieving〈世界了解〉(lines 5175–5186).
- **Conclusion:** Human〈救い〉consists in the affirmation of〈有限の生〉itself — not its overcoming — because the suffering this entails is the very condition of meaning, relational depth, and generational continuity (lines 5141–5144, 5283–5302).

## Flags for Thesis §A

### (a) Locke as founder of the self-ownership lineage

Uegaki names Locke explicitly at line 4050 as one of the founders of「自由の人間学」alongside Rousseau, with Kant as the culmination (lines 4050–4056). The passage at P-04 (lines 4076–4089) shows that the Rousseau formulation is the exemplary expression of「約束された本来性」. Uegaki does NOT use the Lockean「自己所有」frame explicitly here; his account emphasizes the subject's temporal-spatial independence (「時空間的自立性」) rather than property-in-oneself. **For thesis §A's claim that Locke founds the self-ownership lineage**: Uegaki's Ch.10 provides genealogical support at the structural level (Locke as co-founder of disembodied-subject anthropology), but does NOT directly cite Locke's Second Treatise property argument. The thesis needs its own Locke source for the property/self-ownership claim; Uegaki here corroborates the philosophical genealogy without naming self-ownership. [VERIFY-PAGE]

### (b) The positive-liberty paradox

Uegaki's「積極的自由」appears at line 4154 in a list alongside「ゼロ属性の倫理」and「本当の私」as expressions of the same〈無限の生〉worldview. This is structurally close to Berlin's negative/positive liberty distinction but Uegaki uses「積極的自由」pejoratively as part of the〈無限の生〉genealogy (a reading closer to Berlin's warning about positive liberty's totalizing tendency). The「無間地獄」analysis at P-07/P-08 (lines 4281–4349) is the most relevant passage for a "positive-liberty paradox" argument: pursuing ontological freedom without limit produces a structural infinite regress of new unfreedoms. **For thesis §A**: directly usable as the description of the contradiction inherent in the positive-liberty aspiration; Uegaki's framing is compatible with — but not derived from — Berlin. The thesis should distinguish whether it is using Uegaki's「無間地獄」as the paradox's name or the Berlinian analysis. [VERIFY-PAGE]

### (c) Self-finishing as immanent completion

Uegaki does not use the term「自己完結」in the sense of "self-finishing" as a positive ontological concept; he uses〈生の自己完結化〉and〈自己完結社会〉as descriptive/diagnostic terms for the pathology. However, P-12 (lines 4605–4611) contains a crucial related passage: those who cannot accept "life-not-as-one-wills" attempt, through willed death, "to complete life-as-one-wills by their own hand" (「意のままになる主を完成させようとしている」). This is immanent self-completion — but as a figure of self-negation, not affirmation. **For thesis §A's "self-finishing as immanent completion"**: if the thesis uses this in a positive sense (life achieving its own internal end), Uegaki's usage is the inverse — he shows it as the self-abolishing move. The distinction is productive for thesis argumentation. [VERIFY-PAGE]

### (d) Disembodiment as twin of self-finishing

Directly and explicitly supported. P-02 (lines 4013–4020) states: de-bodification = liberation from the not-as-willed body; self-enclosure = liberation from the not-as-willed other; both are continuous as liberation from〈座〉-premises. The「脳人間」(P-10, P-11) extends this: the logical terminus of both processes is the brain-connected apparatus → discard brain → humanity abolished. **For thesis §A**: strong direct support for the twin structure claim; Uegaki provides both the conceptual parallel (P-02) and the limit-case demonstration (P-10/P-11). [VERIFY-PAGE]

### (e) The limitless-life worldview's self-abolishing terminus

Directly and explicitly stated at P-11 (lines 4553–4556): "what the series of stories shows is that when we realize the 'complete human' imagined by〈無限の生〉, it simultaneously means that we discard being human." This is the self-abolishing terminus thesis in its clearest formulation. P-12 adds the parallel terminus via willed death. Lines 4653–4656 restate it: "〈無限の生〉will not be realized unless we discard even the brain and become completely machine, or unless the imperfect 'this I' terminates its existence to complete the 'world' — and in either case there is no human left." **For thesis §A**: highest-confidence support passage; verbatim quotation of P-11 + lines 4653–4656 should be sufficient to anchor this claim. [VERIFY-PAGE]
