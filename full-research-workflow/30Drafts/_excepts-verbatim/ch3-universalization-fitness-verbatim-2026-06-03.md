#### (a) technodiversity = a plurality of cosmotechnics vs global homogenization onto a single (modern, Greek-derived) technology

> "Thesis: Technology is an anthropological universal, understood as an exteriorization of memory and the liberation of organs, as some anthropologists and philosophers of technology have formulated it; Antithesis: Technology is not anthropologically universal; it is enabled and constrained by particular cosmologies, which go beyond mere functionality or utility. Therefore, there is no one single technology, but rather multiple cosmotechnics."
— Hui, *Machine and Sovereignty* (2024), **folio 213** [§29]. *Route: NLM cited_text (source 55b9e730).*

> "Hence counter to the technological tendency that homogenizes and totalizes, technodiversity diverges and fragments."
— Hui, *Machine and Sovereignty*, **folio 220** (PDFp 231, end of §30). *Route: local PyMuPDF.*

> "The progress of the noosphere, however, does not produce a diversification but rather a convergence toward a superbrain. The noospheric reflection or the omega point is also the moment of apocalypse. Technodiversity opposes the technological apocalypse, and instead of convergence, it proposes a divergence or bifurcation."
— Hui, *Machine and Sovereignty*, **folio 225** (PDFp 236, §31). *Route: local PyMuPDF + NLM (source 55b9e730).*

> "The universality of technology has to be fundamentally questioned from the perspective of technodiversity. We did not intend to claim that a particular Chinese thought could or should succeed European philosophy to rescue the planet…"

Foundational framing of the term (the cosmotechnics move):
> "If one admits that there are multiple natures, is it possible to think of multiple technics, which are different from each other not simply functionally and aesthetically, but also ontologically and cosmologically? … I propose what I call cosmotechnics as an attempt to open up the question of technology and its history…"
— Hui, *The Question Concerning Technology in China* (2016), **PDFp 14** (Preface). *Route: local PyMuPDF.*

#### (b) the explicit ecological / biodiversity analogy (technodiversity ~ biodiversity; monotechnology ~ monoculture)

> "In contrast to the discourse on nature, and therefore also an ontogenesis of the earth, we suggest a new matrix of diversity: biodiversity, noodiversity, and technodiversity. — Hui, *Machine and Sovereignty*, **folio 223** (PDFp 234, §31). *Route: local PyMuPDF.*

### B5 · Daron Acemoglu & James Robinson — *Why Nations Fail* (2012) [+ *The Narrow Corridor*, 2019]
**NOT held locally.** *Route: web — Wikipedia (which quotes the book's category definitions), Harvard WCFIA, archive.org full text available if a page-anchor is later needed.*

**Actual claim:** Prosperity vs. poverty is decided by institutions — **inclusive** vs. **extractive** — not by geography, culture, or leaders' ignorance. "You can't get your economics right if you don't get your politics right."

The categories (as quoted from the book on Wikipedia):
> **Inclusive economic institutions** "protect the property rights of wide sections of society not just the elite" and "allow all citizens to participate in economic relations, in order to make a profit."
> **Extractive economic institutions** "exclude large segments of the population from the distribution of income from their own activities" and let elites "alienate the property of those who do not belong to the elite."
> **Inclusive political institutions** allow "wide sections of society to participate in governing the country and make decisions that are beneficial to the majority."
> **Extractive political institutions** "exclude large sections of the population from governing the country and concentrate all political power in the hands of a narrow stratum of society."
— Acemoglu & Robinson, *Why Nations Fail* (2012), per Wikipedia's quotation of the text. *Route: web.* **[VERIFY: confirm these exact strings against the book's own pages (archive.org PDF is available) before quoting verbatim — they are presented in quotation marks on Wikipedia and align with the book, but I did not open the primary PDF this pass.]**

Central thesis (representative): nations prosper with inclusive institutions and fail when institutions are extractive; institutions — not geography/culture/ignorance — are decisive. *Route: web (Wikipedia summary; Harvard WCFIA).*

> **Rebuttal hook (for the author, not a source claim):** All three are *fitness-of-the-package* arguments. Note the internal tension the section can exploit: Morris is explicitly **anti-essentialist** ("maps, not chaps") and predicts the **East re-overtakes** — i.e. "universalization" is reversible and geographic, not a verdict on a civilizational essence. Ferguson's own metaphor concedes the apps are **downloadable** (hence not intrinsic to the West, hence not evidence of *Western* fitness). Acemoglu/Robinson locate success in **institutions that can be inclusive or extractive anywhere** — again decoupling ascendancy from any mode's intrinsic superiority. The ecology/critique allies (Target C) then supply the positive counter-model: universalization-as-homogenization is a *fragility*, not a fitness.

### C9 · Resilience / diversity ecology — the defensible (response-diversity / insurance) version
> NB caveat (as flagged in the brief): the crude "diversity → stability" claim was contested; the defensible form is **response diversity** (Elmqvist et al.) and the **insurance hypothesis** (Yachi & Loreau). Both retrieved.

**Elmqvist et al. (2003), "Response diversity, ecosystem change, and resilience," *Front. Ecol. Environ.* 1(9):488–494** (DOI 10.1890/1540-9295(2003)001[0488:RDECAR]2.0.CO;2). *Route: web (abstract via Wiley/Semantic Scholar; not full-text retrieved).*
