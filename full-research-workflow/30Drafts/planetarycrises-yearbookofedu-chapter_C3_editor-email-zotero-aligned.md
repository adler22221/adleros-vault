## §2. Three Procedural Questions — Answers

### Q1: Brief introduction at the beginning of the email?

**Yes — one sentence, before the substantive paragraph.** Without it, the email opens cold and reads transactional. With it, you establish identity in time for the substantive content to land in the right register. Don't expand it; one sentence is the right size. The signature block does the institutional credentialing.

### Q2: Attach portfolio website to show alignment without flattery?

**Yes — include the URL, not as an attachment but inline at the closing.** The portfolio is the substantive evidence that your work intersects with theirs — it's how you make the alignment claim auditable without saying "I admire your work." Recommend: `https://awakening-coop-lab.gitbook.io/adleryang/` (or whichever URL is most current).

Frame it functionally, not deferentially. Something like: *"My ongoing thinking on these threads is at [URL] for reference."* — not *"I would be honored if you would look at..."*

### Q3: Should you ask about expected timeline for the full chapter?

**Yes, this is a standard practical question.** The CFP gives proposal deadline (May 30) and notification of outcomes (June 30) but is silent on:
- Full chapter due date
- Chapter word count
- Whether references are inside or outside the 400w abstract limit

Asking all three in one email is acceptable; phrasing them as practical-clarifications rather than help-requests keeps the register peer-to-peer.

### A note on "without flattery"

The way to show alignment without flattering is to **name specific works as load-bearing for the chapter's thinking, in the same register a peer would use with a peer**:

- ❌ *"Your important work on policy mobility has shaped my thinking…"*
- ✅ *"The chapter takes McKenzie's 2009 case for scholarship-as-intervention as its methodological warrant…"*
- ✅ *"The framework intersects with Silova, Rappleye and You's 2020 invitation to think beyond the Western horizon, attempting a substantive rather than gestural answer…"*

These do the alignment work *while* signaling that you've actually read them and not just searched for matches.

## §4. Why This Email Is Engineered to Land

**Three things this email does that matter for landing well:**

1. **Establishes peer-register before asking questions.** The intro line (1 sentence) places you in scholarly venues they recognize (*Synthetic Anthropology* + KAS *Panorama*) before any practical request. The substantive paragraph references *their* work as load-bearing for *yours*, not the other way around.

2. **Asks three questions, one of which is genuinely useful to them.** Q1 (word count + timeline) and Q2 (reference counting) are pure practical clarifications. Q3 (section fit) is a substantive question that signals you've engaged with the CFP's three-section architecture and have thought seriously about where your chapter belongs. Q3 also invites editorial guidance, which builds rapport without flattery.

3. **Demonstrates substantive familiarity with two specific works of theirs in one sentence each**, naming the function those works perform for your chapter:
   - McKenzie 2009 "Scholarship as intervention" → methodological warrant
   - Silova, Rappleye & You 2020 "Beyond the Western Horizon" → substantive answer rather than gestural

These two citations are surgical — they pre-empt the two most likely silent doubts (Is this scholar-practitioner methodologically serious? Is this another decorative East-Asian-flavor move?) by naming the editors' own positions and showing your chapter responds to them substantively.

## §6. What Does NOT Belong in the Email

- ❌ Statement that you lack a PhD or that your pathway is unconventional (the signature block + the publication venues do the work; saying it explicitly invites scrutiny)
- ❌ Request for editorial feedback on the proposal in advance ("would you be willing to read…") — uses up editor goodwill for no clear benefit
- ❌ Listing the documentary or media coverage — belongs in the bio/expertise statement, not the email
- ❌ Apology for any aspect of the proposal — apologies invite editors to find fault
- ❌ Long paragraph of personal background or origin story — the portfolio URL does that work for those who want it

## §8. After You Send

If they reply with practical answers (word count, timeline, references counted), use those to finalize the abstract.

If they reply substantively (e.g., "could you tell us more about how the East Asian framing operates?"), reply within 24 hours with a focused 80-150w response — that's a positive signal, treat it as such.

If they reply with section-fit pushback (e.g., "we think this fits §1 better"), accept the redirect and adjust the abstract structure accordingly. Editors offering this kind of guidance pre-submission is rare and very valuable.

If they don't reply before May 30, proceed with the proposal as planned. The email is also working to put your name in front of them before the slush pile lands; even an unreplied email achieves that.
