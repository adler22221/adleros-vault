# Care, Caring Pedagogy, and Planetary Education — Field Brief

Prepared as background context for a CFP chapter proposal to Routledge *World Yearbook of Education* Vol. 29, *Education and Planetary Crises* (eds. McKenzie & Silova). Goal: position the user's "Learn by Caring" framing against current care-pedagogy scholarship oriented toward climate, sustainability, and the more-than-human.

## 1. Genealogy (Noddings → Tronto → present)

- **Nel Noddings (1984), *Caring: A Feminine Approach to Ethics and Moral Education*** inaugurated the modern care-ethics tradition in education, articulating the carer/cared-for relational dyad as the moral primitive and recasting moral education around modelling, dialogue, practice, and confirmation rather than rule-following (infed.org; Wikipedia entry on Noddings).
- Noddings extended this in *The Challenge to Care in Schools* (1992) and *Educating Moral People* (2002), arguing schooling should be reorganised around "centers of care" — for self, intimate others, strangers, animals, plants, the earth, and ideas.
- **Joan Tronto (with Berenice Fisher, 1990; then *Moral Boundaries* 1993; *Caring Democracy* 2013)** politicised care, defining it as "everything we do to maintain, continue and repair our world" and identifying five phases — *caring about, taking care of, care giving, care receiving, caring with* — with "caring with" (2013) tying care to democratic solidarity, justice, and equality (Tronto 2013, NYU Press; ResearchGate diagram of the five phases).
- Tronto's intervention reframed care from a private/feminine virtue into a political question about how a society allocates caring responsibilities — opening the bridge to political economy that contemporary planetary-care scholarship now walks across.
- Contemporary extensions split into (i) **posthuman / more-than-human care** (Puig de la Bellacasa), (ii) **decolonial / Indigenous kinship** (Whyte, Styres, Bang), (iii) **feminist political ecology and degrowth** (Harcourt, Bauhardt), and (iv) **climate-justice organising as care work** (Hobart & Kneese; recent *Environmental Sociology* work).

## 2. Current Frontiers (past ~5 years)

### 2.1 Care + climate / environmental ethics
- **Maria Puig de la Bellacasa, *Matters of Care: Speculative Ethics in More than Human Worlds* (Minnesota, 2017)** is the pivot text — care as "a situated and committed form of speculation" that sustains liveable worlds across human/nonhuman entanglements (Puig de la Bellacasa 2017; societyandspace.org review).
- **"Care-full climate justice organising"** (*Environmental Sociology*, 2025) applies Tronto's five phases to grassroots climate organising, framing activist labour as material and emotional care work for collective survival (Tandfonline 2025).
- Arries-Kleyenstuber (2023, *Nursing Ethics*) and "Climate Conscious Clinical Medical Ethics" (PMC, 2024) extend care ethics into professional practice under climate stress — a pattern likely to be mirrored in teacher-professional ethics.

### 2.2 Care + posthuman / more-than-human pedagogy
- Anchor: **Puig de la Bellacasa (2017)**; **Karen Barad** (intra-action / response-ability, often paired with care in this literature).
- *Posthuman pedagogy: experiential education for an era of mutualism* (Lloro & Le Grange, *Env. Ed. Research*, 2023) and Taylor & Pacini-Ketchabaw's earlier *Common Worlds* program inform this.
- **"Beyond pedagogical intention: exploring more-than-human entanglements in early childhood education and care"** (Tandfonline, 2025) and *Posthumanism and Higher Education: Reimagining Pedagogy* (edited collection, 2023) are recent flagships.
- "Posthuman and Political Care Ethics for Reconfiguring Higher Education Pedagogies" (academia.edu, ca. 2023) explicitly fuses Tronto + posthumanism — closest neighbour to the user's likely move.

### 2.3 Care + decolonial / Indigenous kinship
- Anchors: **Kyle Whyte** (Potawatomi; environmental justice + kinship), **Sandra Styres** (*Pathways for Remembering and Recognizing Indigenous Thought in Education*, 2017), **Megan Bang**, **Vanessa Andreotti** (HEADS UP / GTDF collective).
- "Indigenous Pedagogies: Land, Water, and Kinship" (Bank Street, *Occasional Paper Series*) and "Decolonising environmental education: welcoming non-Mob back to Country" (*Env. Ed. Research*, 2025) typify the current wave.
- Distinctive move: kinship is not a *metaphor* for care — it is an ontological claim about the relational fabric within which "caring for Country" *is* sustainability (vs. Western sustainability framing). Cree concepts *wahkohtowin* and *wîcihitowin* recur as theoretical anchors (MDPI 2022).

### 2.4 Care + political economy / "caring democracy" under crisis
- Anchors: **Tronto (2013, *Caring Democracy*)**; **Bauhardt & Harcourt** (eds.), *Feminist Political Ecology and the Economics of Care* (Routledge, 2019); **Christine Bauhardt**, **Wendy Harcourt**, **Nancy Fraser** (crisis-of-care thesis).
- *Caring Communities for Radical Change: What Can Feminist Political Ecology Bring to Degrowth?* (Springer, 2023) and *Towards Caring Economies? Feminist Economic Visions and COVID-19 Recovery* (2025) extend Tronto to climate/degrowth governance.
- "The Ethics and Politics of Care: Reshaping Economic Thinking and Practice" (*Review of Political Economy*, 2023) marks care's arrival in mainstream heterodox economics — a cue that planetary education can plausibly anchor in this register.

## 3. Critique and live debates

- **Romanticisation critique** (long-running): treating care as inherently good obscures coercive, surveilling, paternalistic caring. Sevenhuijsen, Hamington, and Engster have pushed back; the romanticisation worry persists in any "caring pedagogy" pitch and must be pre-empted.
- **Gendered/racialised division of care labour**: who actually does the care work — disproportionately women, racialised migrants, the Global South? Fraser's "crisis of care" and feminist political-ecology work warn that pedagogies of care can re-naturalise this division unless explicitly politicised.
- **"Too soft to challenge structure"**: structural / Marxist critics argue care discourse risks substituting affective repair for political-economic transformation. Tronto's "caring with" is the standard rebuttal; planetary-care scholars (Bauhardt, Harcourt) push further to institutional / macroeconomic reform.
- **Anthropocentrism critique** (from posthuman side): Noddings' original carer/cared-for dyad presupposes a human subject; Puig de la Bellacasa and Indigenous scholars argue care must be decentred from the human — but then the relational asymmetry that grounded the original ethic becomes unstable.
- **Co-optation by neoliberal universities**: "pedagogy of care" risks being absorbed as wellness/retention rhetoric while the structural conditions producing unwellness intensify (cf. critical pedagogy of caring chapter in *Higher Education for Good*, OBP 2023).

## 4. Educational applications and cases the chapter could draw on

- **Common Worlds Research Collective** (Taylor, Pacini-Ketchabaw, Blaise) — early-childhood multispecies pedagogy as the most developed practical program.
- **Reggio Emilia** and **Forest School / Friluftsliv** traditions — frequently reframed via care + more-than-human lenses.
- **Land-based / Country-based education** (Australia, Canada, Aotearoa): caring-for-Country as curriculum; concrete case sites already in *Environmental Education Research* 2024–2025.
- **"Slow education"** (Maurice Holt; Carl Honoré inspirations) and slow scholarship (Mountz et al.) — practical resonance with deceleration arguments in climate pedagogy (NZARE blog; ResearchGate slow-pedagogy review).
- **University-level**: Bozalek & Zembylas's *Posthuman and Political Care Ethics* applied in South African higher ed; "Critical pedagogy of caring" chapter (Open Book Publishers 2023) in UK/Irish settings.

## 5. What "Learn by Caring" might mean — hypotheses for the user

Three positionings the user could plausibly stake (flagged for *user decision*, not yet endorsed):

- **H1 — Caring as epistemic act, not affective adjunct.** Most current scholarship still treats care as the relational/affective *condition* for learning, with cognition separately theorised. "Learn by caring" could invert this: caring as itself a mode of knowing (closer to Puig de la Bellacasa's "thinking-with-care" than to Noddings' relational ethics). Distinctive if pushed all the way; risks blurring into existing posthuman work unless sharpened.
- **H2 — Caring as planetary curriculum architecture, not pedagogical method.** Most pedagogy-of-care literature operates at the method/relationship level. The user could pitch caring as the *organising principle for what schools teach* under planetary crisis — closer to a Tronto-style "caring with" rendered as curriculum theory. This is institutionally legible to the Yearbook editors (McKenzie/Silova have governance/comparative-policy backgrounds) and underdeveloped in the current literature.
- **H3 — Caring as the East-Asian / transcultural counter-figure to Western individualist learning ontologies.** Almost no current care-pedagogy work draws on Confucian *ren* / Daoist relational ontologies / Buddhist *karuṇā* / Japanese *omoiyari* as primary theoretical resources — they appear only as comparative footnotes. Given the user's transdisciplinary/CJK competence, this is an underclaimed niche; risk is that the editors read it as area-studies rather than planetary-scope unless explicitly globalised.

*Recommendation note for user*: H2 is the most strategically legible to this venue; H1 is the most theoretically distinctive; H3 is the most personally distinctive. A strong proposal probably combines two — most plausibly H2 framed as the deliverable, with H1 or H3 as the theoretical engine.
