# Ch-3-4 v2: Evidenced By — Grounding the Disposition-to-Domination Claim

## Preamble: What This Section Must Survive (Annotation A1)

The disposition-to-domination claim that anchors the §III argument is exposed to a methodologically sharp objection: it could be read as cherry-picking. A historian of science can produce a counter-list of episodes in which Western Modernity looks recessive, accommodating, or pluralistic; a comparativist can produce non-Western counter-lists in which domination, sacrifice, conquest, and totalizing cosmologies appear at full strength. If the thesis's claim were that "Western Modernity dominates, and other traditions do not," any one of these counter-lists would refute it. The claim must therefore be reformulated and re-grounded in such a way that the counter-evidence is not a refutation but a constitutive part of the argument itself.

This section accordingly does three things at once. First, it re-states the disposition-to-domination claim at the *real level* in the critical-realist sense set out in ANNOT-L3 — as a claim about a generative tendency built into the Modern Western mode of artificiality, not a claim about which civilization's empirical record best fits a domination-rubric. Second, it engages four major bodies of counter-evidence — material/structural explanations of the Western technoscientific rise (§A2), non-Western non-Sinosphere comparative cases (§A3), and the Plato/Aristotle internal-critique question (§A5) — *as the argument's response* to the cherry-picking concern, not as concessions to be quarantined in a footnote. Third, it locates the metaverse-as-evidence claim placement question (§A4) for later resolution.

The CR framework established in ANNOT-L3 §1 is load-bearing here. Bhaskar's distinction between the real (`dr`), the actual (`da`), and the empirical (`de`) makes the necessary discrimination thinkable: the disposition is a `dr`-level mechanism whose actualizations (`da`) are co-determined by many other mechanisms in open systems, and whose empirical traces (`de`) underdetermine the underlying structure [VERIFY: confirmed | bhaskarRealistTheoryScience2008 | p.TBD | nlm-excerpt:"Mechanisms, events and experiences thus constitute three overlapping domains of reality, viz. the domains of the real, the actual and the empirical" — NLM source UUID 15591783 (add to SOURCE_META); locate page in Bhaskar Zotero PDF]. A real-level mechanism is not refuted by the discovery that any single empirical configuration could have actualized differently under different conjunctural conditions; it is refuted only by the demonstration that the mechanism itself is incoherent or that its existence is unnecessary to explain the *character* of what was actualized. The four engagements that follow are addressed to that higher standard.

## §A2: Material and Structural Explanations of the Western Technoscientific Rise

The Great Divergence literature is the single most concentrated site of disagreement about why a recognizably Modern Western technoscientific mode arose where and when it did rather than elsewhere or earlier. The thesis must engage this literature visibly because A2 demands acknowledgment that material and structural explanations of the empirical Western rise are non-trivial competitors to any cultural or onto-epistemic account. Four standing positions are particularly relevant.

The first is the **material-ecological** position canonically associated with Kenneth Pomeranz's *The Great Divergence* (Pomeranz 2021 [VERIFY: confirmed | pomeranzGreatDivergenceChina2021 | p.TBD | nlm-excerpt:"western European land, labor, and product markets, even as late as 1789, were on the whole probably further from perfect competition…than those in most of China and thus less suited to the growth process envisioned by Adam Smith" — NLM source UUID 53eef35c (add to SOURCE_META as pomeranzGreatDivergenceChina2021); locate page in Zotero PDF]). Pomeranz's argument is that the Yangtze delta and lower England were comparably "Smithian" market economies up to roughly 1750, and that what tipped Britain toward sustained industrial takeoff was the contingent coincidence of accessible coal near population centres and access to extra-European land and resource frontiers — the Americas — that absorbed the ecological pressure that would otherwise have throttled European growth as it had throttled comparable Chinese cores. Cultural and institutional differences, on this reading, were not the prime mover. Andre Gunder Frank's *ReOrient* extends the position globally, arguing that the long Asian centrality of the world economy is the rule of which the brief Atlantic ascendancy is the exception [LOOKUP: Frank 1998 | ReOrient: Global Economy in the Asian Age | UC Press | ISBN 978-0-520-21474-3].

The second is the **institutional** position associated with Daron Acemoglu and James Robinson's *Why Nations Fail* [LOOKUP: Acemoglu and Robinson 2012 | Why Nations Fail | Crown | ISBN 978-0-307-71921-8]. On this reading, neither coal nor culture is the prime mover; what matters is the formation of *inclusive* economic and political institutions that made productive innovation profitable rather than predatory. Where institutions remained extractive, even resource-rich societies failed to convert their material endowments into sustained technoscientific development.

The third is the **cultural-values** position associated most famously with Max Weber and updated in different registers by David Landes [LOOKUP: Landes 1998 | The Wealth and Poverty of Nations | Norton | ISBN 978-0-393-04017-3] and the earlier work of Joel Mokyr. Weber's *Protestant Ethic* — locally available in the Routledge 2001 reprint and pending Zotero entry — frames ascetic Protestantism as one constellation among several routes to a rationalised economic ethos, in which the cultural disposition of a particular religious-ethical formation shifted incentives toward systematic this-worldly accumulation [LOOKUP-ZOTERO: Weber Protestant Ethic 2001 Routledge | ISBN 978-0-415-25406-3].

The fourth is the **cultural-rhetorical** position of Joel Mokyr's *A Culture of Growth* and Deirdre McCloskey's *Bourgeois* trilogy [LOOKUP: Mokyr 2017 | A Culture of Growth | Princeton UP | ISBN 978-0-691-16888-3; LOOKUP: McCloskey 2010 | Bourgeois Dignity | Chicago UP | ISBN 978-0-226-55674-9]. Mokyr emphasises the "Republic of Letters" — the institutional culture of open intellectual contestation across early-modern European elites — as the differentiator. McCloskey, attacking both Pomeranz-style materialism and Acemoglu-style institutionalism, argues that the change was less about institutions or values per se than about a *dignity revolution* in how innovators and merchants were socially talked about.

A fifth, framing position is that of Joseph Needham, whose mature answer to his own celebrated question is mixed and institutional-epistemic: bureaucratic mandarinate, absence of merchant-state alliance, and a mathematisation gap (Needham et al. 2004 [VERIFY: confirmed | needhamScienceCivilisationChina2004 | p.17 | nlm-excerpt:"it is the rise of the bourgeoisie in Western Europe from the 15th century onwards which decided that Europe would not continue trying to circulate its classical past, nor evolve in Chinese fashion, improving its technology and making small advances in various sciences without actually coming to grips with scientific method, but would strike out in a new direction" — NLM source UUID 70e55943 (add to SOURCE_META as needhamScienceCivilisationChina2004); confirm p.17 in Zotero PDF]). The very *existence* of the Needham Question — why did modern science arise in early-modern Europe and not in China, despite China's earlier and longer technological lead? — already shows that the Western trajectory was historically improbable and cross-civilisationally non-standard. Foster's recent reading of Needham's Marxian-ecological intuitions (Foster 2023 [VERIFY: confirmed | fosterMarxianEcologyEast2023 | p.TBD | nlm-excerpt:"The Needham convergence thesis…was thus that Marxist materialist dialectics had a special affinity with Chinese organic naturalism as represented especially by Daoism, which was similar to the ancient Epicureanism that lay at the foundations of Marx's own materialist conception of nature" — NLM source UUID 38d7ba4f (add to SOURCE_META as fosterMarxianEcologyEast2023); locate page in Zotero PDF]) sharpens the point: even a broadly materialist Needham did not collapse into a single-factor account.

This thesis does not adjudicate between these four positions. The disposition-to-domination claim operates *underneath* the historiographical debate, not inside it. The claim is at the **real level** in the critical-realist sense set out in ANNOT-L3: a tendency built into the metaphysical infrastructure of the Modern Western mode of artificiality, not a prediction about which actual historical configuration would activate it first or most fully. The disposition is *underdetermined* by any single empirical/material configuration; it is consistent with multiple actual histories — including counterfactual ones in which coal, Atlantic windfall, or inclusive institutions had been distributed differently. What the disposition explains is not *that* the takeoff happened in northwest Europe between 1750 and 1850, but the *character* of what was unleashed once any sufficient material configuration came online: an artificiality oriented toward the displacement and re-engineering of the universe rather than toward dwelling-within. The Great Divergence debate is, on this reading, a debate about *triggers and timings*; the thesis's claim is about *what got triggered*.

This is why the thesis does not need any one of Pomeranz, Mokyr, Acemoglu, McCloskey, Frank, or Landes to be right. It needs only the conjunction: *some* material/institutional/cultural configuration sufficed to actualise a real-level disposition that was already metaphysically prepared in the Modern Western tradition. The historiographical debate is preserved in its full openness; the thesis's claim is shown to be compatible with any of its outcomes. Pomeranz's coal does not refute the thesis; nor does Mokyr's Republic of Letters confirm it. The disposition is what the coal and the Republic of Letters *both* actualise.

## §A2-bis: The Adaptational-Fate Stratum versus the Historiographical-Trigger Stratum

A further methodological clarification is in order. The Great Divergence debate engaged in §A2 operates at one specific stratum of the historical argument: the *trigger* stratum, which asks why Western Modernity arose where and when it did rather than elsewhere or earlier. The collapse-and-fragility literature engaged in §III-8 — Tainter, Diamond, Ponting, Brook, Schwartz & Nichols, alongside the natural-extinction record — operates at a different stratum, the *adaptational-fate* stratum, which asks what happens to a mode of artificiality across longer historical timescales once it is operating. These two strata are parallel, not in competition.

The critical-realist framing of the preamble bears equally on both strata. The disposition-to-domination claim operates at the *real* level (in Bhaskar's stratification — the domain of the real, the `dr` stratum where generative mechanisms reside, holding transfactually whether or not they are exercised in any given event); it is consistent with multiple empirical actualisations of the trigger. The adaptational-fate mechanisms — Tainter's complexity-maintenance equation, Diamond's response-capacity factor, Ponting's cumulative-extraction trajectory, Schwartz & Nichols's regeneration-base — likewise operate at the real level. They specify generative mechanisms whose actualisations are co-determined by conjunctural conditions in open systems; they are not refuted by single empirical configurations that fit alternative patterns.

The same defence against the cherry-picking objection (§A1 preamble) applies to the adaptational-fate stratum: a real-level mechanism is not refuted by the discovery that any single empirical configuration could have actualised differently under different conjunctural conditions; it is refuted only by the demonstration that the mechanism itself is incoherent or that its existence is unnecessary to explain the *character* of the actualisations. §III-8 develops the adaptational-fate stratum with the civilizational-collapse evidence and the natural-extinction evidence at the empirical level (`de` — Bhaskar's domain of the empirical); the present section's preamble has defended the disposition-to-domination claim at the real-level trigger stratum. The two operate in parallel within the same critical-realist methodology.

## §A3: Non-Western Non-Sinosphere Engagement

The §III argument has been doubly exposed: first to the West-versus-Sinosphere comparison that Needham authorises (engaged in §IIIA), and second to the further comparative range of *non-Western non-Sinosphere* traditions whose existence is required to demonstrate that the disposition is genuinely distinctive rather than locally parochial. Annotation A3 demands engagement with this further comparative range, and demands that the engagement neither romanticise non-Western traditions as uniformly non-dominating nor concede the genealogical specificity of the Modern Western mode.

The companion draft Ch-3-2 already develops three non-Sinosphere alternatives in moderate depth — Islamic astronomy (al-Biruni, Ibn al-Haytham, the Maragha school), Pacific wayfinding traditions, and Indigenous North American land-management practices. The treatment here supplements Ch-3-2 by foregrounding the theoretical scaffolding (Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics, Philippe Descola's four-mode typology) that allows the comparative material to do *systematic* rather than merely illustrative work; by adding the counter-evidence that Ch-3-2 does not engage; and by stating the *combinatorial-structure* synthesis that the comparative argument requires.

### Counter-evidence: traditions with significant domination-dispositions

The naive form of the comparative argument — "the West dominates; the rest do not" — is empirically false and would be caught immediately by any reader familiar with the comparative civilisational record. Aztec sacrificial cosmology, Mongol imperial expansion, Ottoman territorial conquest, Mesopotamian and Egyptian state-building, and the long history of extractive social relations across many civilisations together constitute a body of evidence that domination, hierarchy, and large-scale state violence are *widespread* in human history rather than uniquely Western.

David Graeber and David Wengrow's *The Dawn of Everything* [RESOLVED 2026-05-05: Zotero R2HAEJHX | nlm:synced ✅] is the most recent systematic survey of pre-modern political variation, and explicitly argues that domination, hierarchy, and large-scale state violence existed in many pre-modern non-Western contexts (Cahokia, Teotihuacan, Shang) but did *not* produce modernity. David Graeber's *Debt: The First 5000 Years* [VERIFY: pending-T0-retrieval | UBN7Y9MV — no NLM query run; locate verbatim in Zotero UBN7Y9MV PDF] provides the long history of extractive social relations across many civilisations. Davíd Carrasco's *City of Sacrifice* [RESOLVED 2026-05-05: Zotero 59JD4WDP | nlm:pending], Marshall Hodgson's *The Venture of Islam* [RESOLVED 2026-05-05: Zotero B9BH3RQD (Vol.1) | nlm:pending], Thomas Allsen's *Culture and Conquest in Mongol Eurasia* [RESOLVED 2026-05-05: Zotero 2DZBGZQZ | nlm:pending], and Barry Kemp's *Ancient Egypt: Anatomy of a Civilization* [RESOLVED 2026-05-05: Zotero 2KP2C5FM | nlm:pending] would supply detailed grounding for the specific counter-cases.

The Hui–Viveiros de Castro "Strategic Primitivism" dialogue is methodologically essential here precisely because it refuses to romanticise non-Western traditions as uniformly non-dominating, arguing instead for tactical rather than essentialist deployment of non-Western modes. The thesis must adopt the same methodological position.

### The combinatorial-structure synthesis

The disposition to domination is widespread; what is genuinely distinctive about the Modern Western mode is its *combinatorial structure*. The compound has at least five interlocking components, each with non-Western analogues but no other tradition that integrates all five:

1. **Christian universalism** — the claim of a valid-for-all salvation history, supplying the imperative to convert the world to one form;
2. **Greek metaphysics-of-substance** — the subject/object split that Descola classifies as "naturalism," supplying the ontological licence to treat nature as standing-reserve;
3. **Colonial extractive material base** — the post-1492 silver, sugar, and labour circuits, supplying the energetic and capital surplus that Pomeranz's account isolates;
4. **Newtonian mechanism** — a mathematisable, predictable nature, supplying the operational schema;
5. **Capitalist accumulation imperative** — the M-C-M' loop as structural compulsion, supplying the perpetual self-driving dynamic.

Each component, taken alone, has non-Western analogues. Aztec sacrificial cosmology was totalising but lacked Newtonian mechanism. Mongol conquest was extractive but lacked the capitalist accumulation logic. Islamic Maragha-school astronomy possessed mathematical sophistication of the highest order but was integrated into a contemplative cosmology rather than an interventionist one (Hui's cosmotechnics frame; Poskett). Indigenous American polities had hierarchy and at times large-scale state violence (Cahokia, Aztec) but operated within the relational ontologies that Descola and Viveiros de Castro analyse, which did not produce the subject/object decomposition. It is the *systemic technospheric outcome* — the planet-scale infrastructural lock-in that the thesis subsequently calls the metaverse-substrate — that no other tradition produced, because no other tradition assembled all five components into a self-reinforcing loop.

This formulation concedes the comparative-breadth point that domination exists widely; preserves the genealogical specificity of the Modern Western mode that the squeeze hypothesis's causal claim requires; makes the argument falsifiable in principle (find a non-Western tradition that assembled all five components and produced a comparable technosphere); and avoids essentialism (the Western compound is itself contingent and historical, not a fixed civilisational essence).

## §A5: The Plato/Aristotle Fault Line — Critical Genealogies, Classical-Tradition Defenders, and the Strand-Genealogical Position

[*This sub-section absorbs the Cycle 11 Ch-3-6 material and is substantial enough to be extractable as `III-6_v2_platonic-aristotelian-fault-line.md` if the chapter architecture later requires it.*]

The §III argument identifies a "Platonic-Aristotelian fault line" as one of the philosophical conditions of possibility of the Modern Western disposition to domination. This is a high-stakes claim. The Platonic and Aristotelian traditions stand behind most subsequent Western philosophy, and an under-evidenced or totalising claim against them invites immediate counter-attack from any reader sympathetic to the classical heritage. Annotation A5 demands a more nuanced engagement that (i) clarifies the load-bearing concepts, (ii) engages the major critical genealogies that support the claim, (iii) engages the major defenders of the classical tradition who reject it, (iv) incorporates Miura Nagamitsu's non-Eurocentric voice, and (v) defends a *strand-genealogical* version of the claim rather than the totalising indictment that the looser reading would produce.

### Concept clarification: fault line, totalisation, exclusion

Three concepts must be clarified before the evidence can be properly assessed.

The first is what "fault line" means in this context. It does not name a single philosophical event or a biographical fact about Plato and Aristotle. It names a *structural tension constitutive of Western philosophy's development* — the unresolved oscillation between two poles that Bhaskar's analysis in *Plato Etc* traces across the full span of Western thought [VERIFY: confirmed | bhaskarPlatoEtc | p.4 | nlm-excerpt:"without the concept of the multi-tiered stratification of being, or ontological depth...it was clear neither how Aristotelian essences differed from Platonic Forms nor how they evaded Humean inductive scepticism...Philosophy was thus stuck—on what I am going to characterize as the Platonic/Aristotelian fault-line." — NLM UUID 5f7e7ef5 (A-Sources c68ca15e)]. The Platonic pole privileges universal form: reality is most fully actual in its formal, universal, necessary dimensions; knowledge proceeds from particular cases to universal principles; the universal is the telos of knowledge and the ground of being. The Aristotelian pole insists on the immanence of form in particular matter — but, on Bhaskar's reading, remains within the Platonic problematic, accepting formal cause as the organising principle while relocating it from a transcendent realm to the interior of particular substances. Neither pole resolves the underlying problem of the relationship between universal and particular; Western philosophy's characteristic oscillation — from medieval universals debates through rationalism vs. empiricism, through idealism vs. positivism — is not progress toward resolution but repeated variation on this unresolved structural tension.

The second clarification is **totalisation**. Bhaskar's concept of the *epistemic fallacy* — the reduction of ontological questions (what is real?) to epistemological ones (what can we know, and how?) — names the mechanism through which the Platonic pole produces domination [VERIFY: confirmed | bhaskarPlatoEtc | p.201 (Glossary) + pp.103-104 | nlm-excerpt:"Epistemic fallacy. The analysis or definition of statements about being in terms of statements about our knowledge (of being)...the ontification of knowledge, overseen by the ontic fallacy...characteristically spawning what, following Adorno, can be called 'identity-thinking'" — NLM UUID 5f7e7ef5 (A-Sources c68ca15e)]. When knowledge is taken as the measure of reality, what cannot be known in the privileged epistemological mode (formal, universal, calculable, experimentally reproducible) is denied full reality. Totalisation is the practical expression of this fallacy at the level of institutions and practices: the drive to render all of nature and society available to the categories of technical-scientific reason, so that what resists — the contextual, the contingent, the irreducibly particular — is treated as a residue of pre-scientific disorder to be eliminated rather than a feature of reality to be respected.

The third clarification is **exclusion** — totalisation's complement. What the totalising framework cannot accommodate is not simply left unexplained; it is actively marginalised: denied ontological status, epistemologically dismissed as irrational or pre-scientific, and socially deprecated as backward or underdeveloped. The metaverse instantiates exclusion technically: algorithmic systems constitute reality from what can be digitised, tracked, and processed; the undatafiable — embodied competence, contextual relational meaning, non-commodifiable value — is progressively excluded from operational reality. The Platonic fault line appears in its contemporary technical expression not despite the distance from ancient philosophy but because the same logic — formal universalisation as the condition of reality — has been institutionalised in the infrastructure through which social reality is now constituted.

### Critical genealogies supporting the strand-claim

A substantial twentieth-century philosophical literature anticipates and authorises a genealogical reading on which the Modern Western disposition to domination is rooted in a particular strand of the Greek metaphysical inheritance.

Adorno and Horkheimer's *Dialectic of Enlightenment* (Adorno and Horkheimer 2002 [VERIFY: confirmed | horkheimerDialecticEnlightenment2002 | p.TBD | nlm-excerpt:"What human beings seek to learn from nature is how to use it to dominate wholly both it and human beings. Nothing else counts." — NLM source UUID 88ed6270 (Adorno notebook; add to SOURCE_META as horkheimerDialecticEnlightenment2002); locate page in Zotero JCJ3EXYF PDF]) provides the locus classicus. The "Concept of Enlightenment" essay and "Excursus I: Odysseus" together argue that mythical and early-rationalist reason already contain the seed of instrumental domination. Odysseus' cunning — *mētis* — prefigures the subject who masters nature by self-mastery and renunciation. Adorno's *Negative Dialectics* (Adorno 1973 [VERIFY: confirmed | adornoNegativeDialectics1973 | p.TBD | nlm-excerpt:"The principle of dominion, which antagonistically rends human society, is the same principle which, spiritualized, causes the difference between the concept and its subject matter; and that difference assumes the logical form of contradiction because, measured by the principle of dominion, whatever does not bow to its unity will not appear as something different from and indifferent to the principle, but as a violation of logic" — NLM source UUID 312f57bc (Adorno ND notebook; add to SOURCE_META as adornoNegativeDialectics1973); locate page in local PDF]) sharpens the argument philosophically: identity-thinking — the metaphysical impulse to subsume particulars under concepts, with deep Platonic-Aristotelian roots — is the philosophical correlate of social domination.

Heidegger's *The Question Concerning Technology* (Heidegger 1977 [VERIFY: confirmed | heideggerQuestionConcerningTechnology1977 | p.TBD | nlm-excerpt:"Enframing means the gathering together of that setting-upon which sets upon man, i.e., challenges him forth, to reveal the real, in the mode of ordering, as standing-reserve" — NLM source UUID 56f08cb6 (Heidegger notebook; add to SOURCE_META); locate page in Zotero QSYD445D PDF]) provides the framework for arguing that the disposition to domination is the historical unfolding of a possibility latent in Greek metaphysics. *Gestell* — enframing — is read as the destinal essence of modern technology, with Greek (Platonic-Aristotelian) origins in the conception of *technē* and the metaphysics of presence. The broader genealogical critique of Western metaphysics as forgetfulness of Being, set out in *Being and Time*, supplies the substrate enabling the *Question Concerning Technology* argument [LOOKUP: Heidegger Being and Time Macquarrie/Robinson 1962 OR Stambaugh 1996/2010].

Derrida's *Of Grammatology* (Derrida 2016 [VERIFY: confirmed | derridaOfGrammatology2016 | p.TBD | nlm-excerpt:"the history of the metaphysics…has always assigned the origin of truth in general to the logos: the history of truth…has always been…the debasement of writing, and its repression outside 'full' speech" — NLM source UUID c774be6d (A-Sources; add to SOURCE_META); locate page in Zotero KE3YPPJ2 PDF]) traces logocentrism — the Western metaphysical privileging of speech and presence over writing and trace — explicitly to Plato. The grammatological critique reframes the Platonic legacy as a structural disposition that organises Western thought toward mastery and self-presence. Derrida's "Plato's Pharmacy," in *Dissemination* (Derrida 1981 [VERIFY: absent-from-A-Sources | derridaDissemination1981 | citekey VYNZTA5S — *Dissemination* PDF absent from A-Sources notebook; pharmakon concept partially available via Spivak Preface to *Of Grammatology*: "The pharmakon is neither remedy nor poison, neither good nor evil, neither the inside nor the outside, neither speech nor writing" (source UUID 27610fcb); add Dissemination PDF to A-Sources for full T0 retrieval, or locate page in local PDF]), develops the *pharmakon* analysis of *Phaedrus*: writing as both poison and remedy, the classical text already containing the deconstructive resources it tries to suppress. Marcuse's *One-Dimensional Man* (Marcuse 2009 [VERIFY: confirmed | marcuseOneDimensionalMan2009 | p.TBD | nlm-excerpt:"the technological society is a system of domination which operates already in the concept and construction of techniques. ### The non-Eurocentric voice: Miura Nagamitsu

The critical genealogies above are conducted from within the Western philosophical tradition. Annotation A5 specifically requests engagement with a non-Eurocentric voice that traverses the Western philosophical genealogy from the outside, in order to demonstrate that the strand-claim is not a parochial intra-Western quarrel.

Miura Nagamitsu's *環境思想と社会—思想史的アプローチ* (Environmental Thought and Society: A Thought-Historical Approach) (Miura 2006 [VERIFY: confirmed | miuraKankyoShisoTo2006 | pp.88-89, 99 | nlm-excerpt:[pp.88-89]: Locke undervalued nature's productivity vs. human labor → stimulated "conquering the earth"; [p.99]: "Locke thus played the role of a theorist who justified the legitimacy of British colonization of America...the ownership of nature (especially land) is linked to the domination of others (lower classes and different ethnic groups)." — NLM source UUID a4f2260b (A-Sources c68ca15e)]) is the most directly relevant text. The subtitle — *thought-historical approach* — signals the methodological move: a Japanese philosopher working *through* the Western philosophical genealogy on the human/nature/society triad rather than around it. Miura's affiliation with the 総合人間学会 (Synthetic Anthropology Association) and his sustained engagement with Locke's property theory and the colonial ontology that follows from it together constitute the kind of non-Eurocentric philosophical voice that the strand-claim requires for its non-parochial standing. Miura's more recent essays on the contemporary view of the human (Miura 2017 [VERIFY: pending-T0-retrieval | SanPuYongGuangGendaishakaitosononingenkansogoningengakunotamenoshiron2017 — no NLM query run; `nlm:synced` status of this source in A-Sources needs confirmation; run T0 retrieval or locate verbatim in Zotero PDF]) connect the long thought-historical analysis directly to the *displacement of the human* framing that the squeeze hypothesis develops.

The bridge Miura provides is significant in two ways. First, his non-Eurocentric standpoint shows that the genealogical critique need not be a Western-internal self-flagellation; it can be conducted from a position that does not stake itself on the survival of any particular Western philosophical school. Second, the *thought-historical* framing — neither analytical-philosophical decomposition nor sociological reduction — is methodologically congruent with the strand-genealogical position the thesis adopts.

### Classical-tradition defenders

Against the critical genealogies, a substantial body of philosophical work defends the classical tradition by arguing that the Adornian-Heideggerian-Derridean reading mistakes the destination of one strand for the character of the whole.

Pierre Hadot's *Philosophy as a Way of Life* and *What Is Ancient Philosophy?* [LOOKUP: Hadot 1995 | Blackwell | ISBN 9780631180333; LOOKUP: Hadot 2002 | Harvard Belknap | ISBN 9780674007338] argue that ancient philosophy — including Platonism — was not primarily a system of theoretical propositions destined to become instrumental reason, but a set of *spiritual exercises* — *askesis* — aimed at the transformation of the practitioner. Hadot's reading directly resists the Heideggerian-Adornian genealogy by showing that the *practice* of ancient philosophy was therapeutic and self-transformative, not proto-dominative.

Alasdair MacIntyre's *After Virtue* (MacIntyre 2007 [VERIFY: macintyreAfterVirtue2007 | local PDF available]) and *Whose Justice? Which Rationality?* (MacIntyre 1988 [VERIFY: absent-from-A-Sources | macintyreWhoseJustice1988 | citekey NZSP5WTW — MacIntyre *Whose Justice? Which Rationality?* absent from A-Sources notebook; add PDF to A-Sources for T0 retrieval, or verify passage in local PDF]) defend the Aristotelian-Thomist tradition as the *resource for diagnosing and repairing* modern moral and political failure rather than its source. The collapse into emotivism and managerial-instrumental reason is, on MacIntyre's reading, precisely a *consequence of abandoning* the Aristotelian framework, not its fulfilment.

Hans-Georg Gadamer's *Truth and Method* [RESOLVED 2026-05-05: Zotero N2DNAE3L (2011 Bloomsbury ed.) | NOT nlm:synced — add nlm:pending] develops the hermeneutic recovery of Aristotelian *phronēsis* — practical wisdom — against the modern reduction of reason to method and instrumental rationality. Gadamer's Aristotle is the antidote to instrumentalism, not its precursor.

Leo Strauss's *Natural Right and History* and *The City and Man* [RESOLVED 2026-05-05: Zotero Z6U9GMBM (*Natural Right and History*) | nlm:synced ✅; Zotero ZHDHGT5A (*The City and Man*) | nlm:pending] argue that the classical tradition — and Plato in particular — is the highest achievement of political-philosophical reflection and the *standard* against which modernity must be measured. The "lowering of the horizon" — modern political philosophy reducing the human good to security, comfort, and mastery — is, on Strauss's reading, a *departure* from rather than an extension of the classical project. Eric Voegelin's *Order and History* [LOOKUP: NOT IN ZOTERO 2026-05-05 — *Eric Voegelin Reader* (SWPU8SIN, nlm:synced ✅) is in Zotero as secondary; *Order and History* (5 vols, LSU/Missouri) not as standalone entry; cite via Reader or add individual volumes manually] extends the line in a different register: the classical tradition articulates the experience of order in being, against modern "Gnostic" projects of immanentising the eschaton (which Voegelin sees as the genuine root of modern domination).

### The strand-genealogical position

The thesis adopts a strand-genealogical position rather than a totalising-indictment position. This is the load-bearing move that allows the §III argument to survive A5.

First, the defenders' point is conceded substantively. The Platonic-Aristotelian tradition is internally plural and contains genuine resources against domination. Hadot's *askesis* shows that the transformative-practice strand of Platonism is irreducible to logocentrism. MacIntyre and Gadamer show that the Aristotelian *phronēsis* tradition runs orthogonal to instrumental rationality. Strauss shows that classical political philosophy contains its own critique of tyranny and of the lowering of the human horizon. The thesis does not deny these strands; it does not need to deny them.

Second, the genealogical claim is defended at strand-level rather than tradition-level. What Adorno-Horkheimer, Heidegger, Derrida, and Marcuse diagnose is not the whole of the classical tradition but a *specific strand* within it — call it the **productionist-substantialist strand**. This strand has three identifiable components. (1) The Platonic tendency to subordinate the sensible particular to the eidetic universal — what Adorno terms identity-thinking and what Derrida terms logocentrism. (2) The Aristotelian conception of *technē* as knowledge-of-how-to-produce, which when abstracted from its embedding in *phronēsis* becomes the formal template for instrumental reason. (3) The metaphysics of *ousia* — substance — as bounded, masterable presence, which Heidegger reads as the foundation of *Gestell*.

Third, the historical argument is conditional rather than necessary. The disposition to domination is not the necessary outcome of the classical tradition *as such*, but the historically actualised outcome of one strand of it, when (a) detached from the corrective practices Hadot describes, (b) institutionally fused with Christian-providentialist domination-of-nature theology in the medieval and early-modern Latin West, and (c) technologically scaled in modernity through the combinatorial structure analysed in §A3 above. The metaverse — as the thesis develops in §V and §VI — is the latest displacement of this strand, not its origin.

This strand-genealogical position lets the thesis hold the genealogical insight (which is real and well-attested in the critical tradition) without committing to a thin total indictment that would crumble under any committee reader sympathetic to Hadot or MacIntyre. It also gives the comparative-breadth, philosophical-precision, and scope-discipline requirements of the three viva committee members the explicit response they require. The argument identifies *what is argued* (a strand-genealogical claim about the productionist-substantialist component of the Platonic-Aristotelian heritage) as distinct from *what is merely mentioned* (a total indictment that the thesis does not need and does not advance).

## Closing Synthesis

The four engagements above — material/structural counter-explanations (§A2), non-Western non-Sinosphere comparative cases (§A3), and the Plato/Aristotle internal-critique question with Miura's non-Eurocentric voice (§A5) — together constitute the thesis's response to the cherry-picking concern raised in §A1. None of them is treated as a concession to be quarantined; each is incorporated as part of the argument's structure.

The disposition-to-domination claim survives the engagement because it is reformulated at the right level. It is a real-level claim about a generative tendency in the Modern Western mode of artificiality, underdetermined by any single empirical configuration and consistent with multiple actual histories. The combinatorial structure that gave the disposition its global scale is not present in any other tradition that exhibits domination as a local phenomenon, and the productionist-substantialist strand of the Platonic-Aristotelian heritage that contributed one of the disposition's philosophical conditions is not coextensive with the classical tradition as a whole. The thesis is therefore neither a totalising indictment of the West nor a parochial generalisation from a local case; it is a strand-genealogical and combinatorial argument about a specific historically actualised compound whose contemporary form is the metaverse.

The argument's falsifiability conditions are explicit: a non-Western tradition that assembled the five combinatorial components and produced a comparable technosphere would refute the combinatorial-structure claim; a demonstration that the productionist-substantialist strand is not philosophically traceable to Greek metaphysics would refute the strand-genealogical claim; and a demonstration that the disposition's actualisations across multiple histories do not share the *character* the thesis attributes to them would refute the real-level claim. The thesis stands or falls on these specific tests, not on the broader and easier tests that the cherry-picking objection imagines.
