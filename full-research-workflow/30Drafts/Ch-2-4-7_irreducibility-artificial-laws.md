## Part A: Terminology — "Laws" vs. "Rules"

### Mi's comment: *"rule比law更合適"* (rule is more appropriate than law)

Mi's terminological concern is philosophically precise. "Law" in the natural-sciences sense implies universality, necessity, and reduction: the laws of thermodynamics hold everywhere; they are not negotiable or chosen. "Rule" suggests something instituted, context-dependent, and revisable — it is the appropriate term for the normative structures that organize human practices (grammatical rules, rules of chess, institutional rules).

**The thesis's usage sits between these senses, and needs to be explicit about this.**

The "laws" the thesis attributes to artificial environments are *neither* natural laws (universal, observer-independent) *nor* purely conventional rules (arbitrary, fully revisable). They occupy a third ontological status: they are *emergent structural regularities* — stable patterns that arise from the organization of a complex system and that exercise causal constraint on the elements within it, but that are not reducible to the laws of the substrate from which they emerge.

**Recommended approach**: Use "laws" (not "rules") but add a terminological footnote distinguishing the thesis's use from both natural-law usage and conventional-rule usage. The argument for this follows in Part B below.

Alternatively, if the committee strongly prefers terminological consistency with the social-science context, adopt "governing principles" or "structural regularities" — but these are wordier and lose the conceptual specificity that "laws" carries when used carefully.

## Part B: The Irreducibility Argument

### 1. The General Claim

The thesis claims that artificial environments have laws "of their own" that are not reducible to the physical and biological laws of the universe. Mi's challenge is: *why* are these laws not reducible? What is the argument?

The answer proceeds in three steps: (a) what "reduction" means and what irreducibility requires; (b) Michael Polanyi's dual-control argument as the formal model; (c) why this applies to artificial environments specifically.

---

### 2. What Reduction Means: The Reductionist Claim

The reductionist claim about artificial environments would go: the "laws" governing human social environments (grammatical rules, legal norms, institutional procedures, market prices, platform algorithms) are ultimately just patterns in the physical behavior of matter and energy — neural firing patterns, electronic state changes, acoustic vibrations. In principle, a sufficiently complete description of the physical substrate would entail all the higher-level patterns. There is nothing "extra" at the higher level that is not already determined by the lower level.

This is the standard physicalist/reductionist position. It has serious advocates (Churchland, Dennett, at the extreme end). But it faces a problem that Michael Polanyi identified with particular clarity.

---

### 3. Polanyi's Dual-Control Argument

Michael Polanyi (*The Tacit Dimension*, 1966; *Personal Knowledge*, 1958) argues that complex systems operating through hierarchical organization exhibit what he calls *dual control*: at every level, the higher-level organizing principle operates *through* the lower level without being *determined by* it.

Consider a machine: its parts obey the laws of physics — friction, thermodynamics, electromagnetic force. But the *function* of the machine — what it does, what counts as it working correctly — is not determined by the physics of its parts. A gear can physically rotate in many directions; the machine's design selects one direction as functional. The machine's blueprint specifies a *boundary condition* that harnesses the physics of its parts without being dictated by that physics. The boundary condition is *irreducible* to the physical laws of the parts: you cannot derive the function from the physics alone.

Polanyi generalizes: *"A higher level can harness the principles of a lower level... The higher principle can be said to set the boundary conditions for the lower level, without itself being reducible to those conditions."*

The hierarchy runs upward: physics → chemistry → biology → psychology → sociology → culture. At each step, the higher level harnesses the lower without being reducible to it. A living cell obeys chemistry but has biological functions that chemistry alone cannot specify; a human institution obeys biology (human cognition, embodied action) but has social meanings that biology alone cannot specify.

**Key implication for the thesis**: Artificial environments introduce a *new level* in this hierarchy — one that operates through physical and biological substrates but whose organizing principles cannot be derived from those substrates. The "laws" of an artificial environment are *boundary conditions* that the environment imposes on human activity within it, just as the machine's blueprint imposes a boundary condition on its parts' physical behavior. They are real, causally operative, and irreducible.

---

### 4. Why Artificial Environment Laws Are Irreducible: Three Arguments

**Argument 1: The Multiple Realizability Argument**

The same artificial environment law — say, the rule that a posted message cannot exceed 280 characters — can be implemented in physically entirely different ways (different programming languages, different hardware, different data centers). The law is the same; the physical substrate varies. Since the same law is compatible with indefinitely many different physical instantizations, the law cannot be *identical to* any particular physical configuration. It is multiply realizable, and multiply realizable properties are not reducible to physical descriptions.

This is a version of Putnam's classic argument against the identity theory of mind, applied here to institutional and technical structures rather than mental states.

**Argument 2: The Semantic Gap Argument**

The laws of artificial environments are *normative* in a way that physical laws are not: they distinguish correct from incorrect behavior, valid from invalid actions, permitted from prohibited moves. Physical laws have no normative dimension — an electron cannot "violate" a physical law; it simply behaves as physics dictates. But a user can *comply with* or *violate* platform terms of service, or can be *sanctioned* for rule infractions. The normative dimension — the distinction between what *is* and what *ought to be* (or is permitted/prohibited) — cannot be derived from any physical description. It requires a level of analysis at which meanings, intentions, and social significance are operative.

**Argument 3: The Downward Causation Argument**

If artificial environment laws were fully determined by their physical substrate, they could not exert independent causal influence on the physical world. But they demonstrably do. The legal structure of a corporation causes physical outcomes (factories built, forests cleared, labor performed) that would not occur without the legal structure — not because legal structures are physical forces but because they organize and direct the physical behavior of agents who exist at both the physical and social levels simultaneously. This is downward causation: the higher-level law constrains and directs events at the lower level. Downward causation is incompatible with full upward reduction.

---

### 5. What "Laws of Artificial Environments" Are

Drawing on the above, the thesis can specify: the "laws" of artificial environments are *emergent boundary conditions* that:

1. **Operate through physical and biological substrates** (neural activity, social behavior, hardware) without being determined by them
2. **Are multiply realizable** — the same law can be implemented in physically different ways
3. **Have a normative dimension** — they distinguish permitted from prohibited, functional from dysfunctional, within the system
4. **Exert downward causal influence** — they constrain and direct the physical behavior of agents and systems within their scope
5. **Are not derivable from** the physical or biological laws alone — they require a level of analysis at which the system's organizing principles, purposes, and semantic contents are specified

These are the features that make the term "laws" (rather than "rules") defensible: they are not arbitrary conventions but structural regularities with genuine causal power — even though they are not natural laws in the physicalist sense.

---

### 6. Connection to the Self-Completion Argument

The irreducibility of artificial environment laws matters for the thesis's argument in two ways:

**a) Why the metaverse constitutes a genuine new environment**

If the metaverse's "laws" — its platform governance, its algorithmic rules, its UI affordances and constraints — were merely patterns in physical behavior, they would have no more significance than any other complex physical system. Their irreducibility is what makes them constitute a *world* in the relevant sense: a structured space with its own organizing principles, its own normative order, its own constraints on what counts as action, identity, and value within it. A physically different substrate implementing the same platform governance would produce the same metaverse — because it is the emergent law, not the physics, that defines what the metaverse is.

**b) Why the squeeze is not just "adaptation to a new tool"**

If the metaverse's laws were reducible to its physical substrate (hardware, electricity, photons), the "squeeze" would be no more significant than adapting to any new technology — learning to use a hammer, adjusting to the combustion engine. The irreducibility argument establishes that what is at stake is not a new *tool* but a new *environment* — a new level of organization with its own laws that harness and constrain the physical and biological levels below it. Organisms adapt to environments, not tools; the adaptive pressure is of a different order.
