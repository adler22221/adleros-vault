## ¶2 — Positive definition (UNCHANGED from existing line 197 — LOCKED VERBATIM)

> [KEEP existing line 197 verbatim]

> "I call this causally efficacious nonmaterial aspect of artificiality virtuality."

The clause "aspect of artificiality" — taken together with the ¶1 antecedents — does substantial work. It positions virtuality as a *dimension* of artificial things (their nonmaterial-causally-efficacious side), not as a separate ontological category. A coin has a material aspect (metal) and a nonmaterial aspect (its being-money); the virtuality is the latter. A scientific theory's material aspects are paper, ink, screens, lecture-hall walls; its nonmaterial aspect is the conceptual structure that does explanatory and predictive work in human practice. The clause "of artificiality" carries the framing-X commitment (per §IIA) that virtuality is a *lens* on the nonmaterial side of human artificiality, triangulated by Stiegler's broad sense of technē and Obara's 「モノ」.

### ¶3a — Against Peirce's "as if" reading (UNCHANGED from existing lines 198–199)

> [KEEP existing lines 198–199 verbatim]

These lines carry forward the *virtus* (power/efficacy) sense from Peirce's etymology while rejecting the "as if" component on Baudrillard's ground that many virtual things have no original to which they refer. A virtual *x* can be original in its own right — a point that matters most for an emerging stage in which the real-represented may no longer be locatable. Citations to Peirce 1902 [VERIFY: peirce1902 — not in Zotero snapshot 2026-05-16; LOOKUP needed before final draft] and Baudrillard 1994 [VERIFY: confirmed | baudrillardSimulacraSimulation1994 | Zotero key 5495YCYV] retained.

### ¶3b — Against Baudrillard's terminal-stage framing (NEW)

A reader fluent in Baudrillard's later writings will recognise that his usage of "virtuality" is more elaborate than the Phase-1 ally-against-Peirce use established in ¶3a. From *Passwords*, *The Impossible Exchange*, and *The Intelligence of Evil*, Baudrillard treats *the Virtual* as a terminal-eschatological category — the "final solution of the real," in which not only the real but the sign itself is annihilated and "Integral Reality" replaces the symbolic order. The thesis declines this framing on two grounds. First, Baudrillard organises his account of virtuality around a *historical-epochal carving* (symbolic → real → hyperreal → virtual) in which the virtual is a terminal stage exclusive to the digital-algorithmic phase; the thesis treats virtuality as a *trans-historical genus* present from oral ritual onward, of which the algorithmic stage is one substrate-instance and not the genus's terminal negation. Second, what Baudrillard calls "the symbolic" (gift, sacrifice, ritual obligation, reversibility — the pre-value relational order) is, on the thesis's account, a *part of* virtuality (the institutional-fact / customary-norm zone), whereas Baudrillard treats it as virtuality's opposite. The thesis adopts Baudrillard's specific diagnostic precision — the dissimulation/simulation distinction — without buying his terminal-stage framing. Baudrillard is, importantly, a co-belligerent against the narrow computer-based reading of virtuality: in *The Intelligence of Evil* he explicitly diagnoses the reduction of the Virtual "to an applied technology" as a "misunderstanding," a position the thesis shares in ¶4 against any computer-based-only reading. The "haunting" relation Baudrillard identifies — that the older symbolic order persists as a haunting within the new — is taken up positively in §IV where it informs the squeeze diagnosis.

### ¶3c — Against Searle's institutional-fact framing (NEW; condensed cross-reference to Ch-1-6)

The thesis adopts Searle's realism of the observer-relative — that collectively-recognised, causally-efficacious nonmaterial structures are real, not as-if, not fictional — and his constitutive-rule analytic in the schematic form "*X counts as Y in context C*." It declines, however, two features of Searle's broader framework: his language-requirement (status-function assignment requires linguistic articulation) and his human-uniqueness closure (only human collective intentionality grounds institutional reality). These rejections are developed in Ch-1-6 and are presupposed here. The virtuality this thesis defines is therefore broader than Searle's "institutional facts" in the directions both Ch-1-6 and the OS-spectrum elaboration lay out: the same realism-of-observer-relative obtains under pre-linguistic collective behavioural recognition (proto-virtuality at OS-spectrum Nodes 1–2) as under full linguistic collective acceptance (full virtuality at Node 3), and the structural criterion remains constant across the spectrum.

## ¶4 — The thesis adopts Chalmers's substrate-neutrality and simulation realism; differentiates at the analytical unit (REPLACES existing line 200 with a compressed steel-man + clean analytical-unit differentiation)

Chalmers (2022) defines virtual reality as an "immersive, interactive, and computer-generated space" and holds that, at a first approximation, "a virtual object is a structure of bits inside a computer" [VERIFY: confirmed | chalmersRealityVirtualWorlds2022 | UUID d672826d]. Read carefully, his account is substrate-neutral: "the same sequence of bits can be encoded in all sorts of substrates — punched cards, mechanical switches, transistors, or patterns of beer cans" [VERIFY: confirmed | chalmersRealityVirtualWorlds2022 | UUID d672826d], and grounded in causal structuralism, on which "a physical system implements a mathematical computation when the causal structure of the system mirrors the formal structure of the computation" [VERIFY: confirmed | chalmersRealityVirtualWorlds2022 | UUID d672826d]. Chalmers's project is anti-simulationist: he grounds virtuality in some computational substrate precisely so as to defend the *reality* of virtual entities against the "fake reality" intuition, arriving (notably) at the position that "money is what it is because we treat it as money" and that virtual money is real money on the same terms as physical money [VERIFY: confirmed | chalmersRealityVirtualWorlds2022 | UUID d672826d; cf. brey2003 | Zotero key J4QDKEKB].

**The thesis adopts Chalmers's substrate-neutrality and simulation realism without modification.** The disagreement lies elsewhere: where Chalmers's analytical unit is the entity within a virtual environment (his core question being "is the X inside a virtual environment real?"), the thesis's analytical unit is the *trans-historical layer of human-constructed nonmaterial structure* — stories, gods, ceremonies, laws, scientific theories, mathematical models, algorithms — that has always partly constituted human worlds. This is virtuality in the sense the thesis adopts. The thesis and Chalmers therefore agree about a great deal (the reality of virtual money; the substrate-neutrality of information; the inadequacy of fictionalism about virtual entities) and are doing different jobs with the word "virtuality" — Chalmers's job is the *defence of virtual entities against fictionalism*; the thesis's job is the *characterisation of virtuality as a constitutive layer of human worlds and the diagnosis of its operational transformation* under the metaverse-stage substrate-shift (developed in §IV).

### ¶4c — Non-computational virtuality: the money paradigm case (REVISES existing line 202)

> [KEEP existing line 202 prose on shells/grain/salt/metal/paper/digits as temporal mediums; APPEND the Searle/Bhaskar warrant at close]

Money is the cleanest illustration of virtuality on the thesis's account: causally efficacious, intersubjectively binding, substrate-promiscuous, and predating the computer by millennia. Money is still money, and money has always been virtual; shells, grain, salt, metal, paper, or digits are merely temporal mediums to operationalise money's virtuality. Searle puts the constitutive structure plainly: "it is only given the institution of money that I now have a five dollar bill in my hand. Take away the institution and all I have is a piece of paper with various gray and green markings" [VERIFY: confirmed | searleSpeechActs1969 | p.51]. In Searle's mature formulation, money is the textbook *status function* generated by collective acceptance of a constitutive rule of the form *X counts as Y in context C*: "such and such counts as a $20 bill in our society" [VERIFY: confirmed | searlePhilosophyNewCentury2008 | p.33]. Electronic money tightens the structural point: a bank issuing a loan performs a speech act in which "there need be no physical reality to the $1,000, other than the representation" [VERIFY: confirmed | searleLanguageSocialOntology2008 | p.457]. The same structural treatment extends, with the appropriate substitutions, to law, sovereignty, marriage, citizenship, ritual obligation, and customary norm — each is causally efficacious; each is collectively constituted; none is computational in any sense that does not vacate the term. This treatment is not idiosyncratic: it sits within the well-established social-ontological tradition that recognises money, law, sovereignty, and ritual obligation as causally-efficacious nonmaterial structures arising from collective recognition (Searle 1995, with the language-requirement modification developed in Ch-1-6) and operating as real-stratum mechanisms with their own emergent powers irreducible to events or experiences (Bhaskar 2008) [VERIFY: confirmed | searleConstructionSocialReality1995 | NLM UUID 8186ca58; bhaskarRealistTheoryScience2008 | A-Sources UUID 15591783].

## ¶5 — Historical continuum (REVISES existing line 201)

> [KEEP the trans-historical continuity claim and the worked example, with **mathematical models** inserted into the chain; DELETE the "biologically programmed and powered" sentences; REPLACE with ¶5c]

### ¶5a — Trans-historical continuity (KEEP)

As Durante observes, humans have always dealt with the virtual, ever since humans lived by the stories they collectively invented [VERIFY: durante2022 | Zotero key SBXRDXBB]. The claim is structural, not chronological: virtuality is not a recent invention that arrived with the computer but a feature of the meaning-spaces humans have always inhabited.

### ¶5b — Worked example, with substrate-shifts foregrounded (KEEP existing prose; INSERT mathematical models; ADD softer-framing sentence)

Initially, virtuality manifested as stories of gods and as the ceremonies that bound them to communities — providing the nonmaterial yet causally efficacious basis on which worlds could form. Over time, gods were displaced by the sciences and ceremonies by laws, each transition involving a *change of substrate* (oral to written to printed to computational) operating on the *same underlying virtuality*. Scientific theories and mathematical models entered the chain as humanly-constructed approximations of structures the world appears to exhibit — approximations that, even where their referents are mind-independent, function as real-stratum mechanisms shaping engineering practice, governance, and everyday cognition. Today, AI guidance and algorithmic regulation, mediated by computer-based interfaces, are the dominant operating layer. The substrate has shifted repeatedly; the virtuality has persisted.

### ¶5c — Future trajectory: embedded computational virtuality (REPLACES the deleted "biologically programmed and powered" sentences)

Some commentators imagine that the future of virtuality will move beyond computational substrates altogether — that, for example, biologically programmed and powered interfaces will mark the end of the computer-based. This expectation rests on a category mistake. As Chalmers himself recognises, computation is substrate-neutral: biological, quantum, neuromorphic, and not-yet-imagined substrates all qualify as computational provided they implement the requisite causal-computational structure. The actual trajectory is not the supersession of computational virtuality but its *embedding*. Brain-computer interfaces, ambient intelligence, augmented sensory prostheses, always-on agents — these dissolve the interface into the body schema and the everyday environment, making computational virtuality phenomenologically transparent rather than absent (Stiegler 1998 on tertiary retention thickening; on the body schema's absorption of the prosthesis, see §IIB.A). The squeeze the thesis diagnoses is therefore not a squeeze between virtual and non-virtual but between an emerging *embedded computational virtuality* and the older virtualities — ritual, institutional fact, customary norm, narrative — that the embedded computational layer is increasingly displacing or absorbing (§IV).
