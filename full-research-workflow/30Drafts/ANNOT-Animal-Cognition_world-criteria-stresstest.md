## 1. Why the Stress-Test Matters

The thesis's universe / world / metaverse architecture stands or falls on whether the **world** can be coherently named as a human-distinctive stratum — not as an ineffable spiritual essence and not as a question-begging postulate, but as a stratum identifiable by criteria that empirical animal-cognition research is actually capable of falsifying. If a serious comparative ethologist could demonstrate that primates, cetaceans, corvids, or eusocial insects already do everything the thesis attributes to *world*, then "world" collapses into a quantitative refinement of niche-construction and the squeeze argument loses its load-bearing claim that what is squeezed *between* the universe and the metaverse is something *categorically* different from a niche.

The committee — Mi in particular — will press this point. "How is your *world* not just a high-resolution version of what Flack documents in pigtail macaques?" is a viva-realistic question. The answer must come from the criteria the thesis's three principal interlocutors set up. This document audits those criteria against the empirical literature the user has just made T0-accessible by uploading nine animal-cognition works to NLM A-Sources.

Three criteria-builders are in scope:

1. **Obara Hideo (小原秀雄)** — recursive tool-making (加工の二重性), and uniquely the **artificial food chain** (人工食物連鎖) and the artificial ecosystem (人工生態系)
2. **John R. Searle** — collective intentionality, status functions, deontic powers as language-mediated
3. **Bernard Stiegler** — tertiary retention as the externalised technical-mnemonic substrate of the "who"

The question per axis is the same: does the empirical animal-cognition literature *eliminate* this differentiator, *require it to be reformulated*, or *leave it intact*?

## 2. Axis 1 — Recursive Tool-Making (Obara: 加工の二重性)

### Obara's claim

Obara's foundational human-animal differentiator is **加工の二重性** (the doubling / duplicity of fabrication): humans do not merely use the immediate environment, they make tools *with tools*. The recursive externalization is what generates the second-order artificial layer that comes to mediate all subsequent interaction with the primary natural layer [T0: ANNOT-Obara §2; UUID 7ef94356]. Animals use; humans recursively fabricate the means of fabrication.

The stress-test pivots on whether the recent primate-cognition literature has documented anything that is genuinely *recursive* in Obara's sense, or only first-order tool use that has been theoretically over-interpreted.

### Empirical evaluation

**Addessi et al. 2007 (capuchin tokens)** [VERIFY: confirmed | addessiCapuchinTokens2007 | UUID ebc264ae (A-Sources c68ca15e) | nlm-excerpt:"token exchange may be regarded as a proto-convention since...token exchange lacks a normative nature since group members do not expect other members to abide by it"]. Tufted capuchins (*Cebus apella*) can use tokens as substitutes for food rewards in experimental economic exchange paradigms — exchanging tokens for food, accumulating tokens, even differentially valuing tokens that map to different reward types. This is an extraordinary cognitive achievement and warrants careful consideration. *But* — and the Addessi work itself is careful here — token use in capuchins is *trained*, *human-scaffolded*, and operates within an experimental setup whose institutional structure is supplied by the experimenter. The capuchins are not *constructing* a token economy; they are *performing within* an institutional structure that humans constructed for them. The "tokens" do not exist except as artefacts of a human laboratory. This is therefore not a counter-example to Obara's recursivity claim; it is a demonstration that the cognitive *substrate* of token use is present in capuchins but the *institutional generation* of a token system is not.

**Beran & Parr (primate token use)** [VERIFY: confirmed | beranParrNonHumanPrimate | UUID 17d9f8a4 (A-Sources c68ca15e) | nlm-excerpt:"tokens produce a cognitive load that is too strong for non-human primates to maintain the representation of value that reflects a true equivalence of currency to goods"]. The Beran & Parr review extends the token-use literature to chimpanzees and other apes, documenting capacities for symbolic substitution, delay of gratification, and quantitative reasoning over tokens. The cognitive ceiling is higher than the capuchin work suggests, but the structural verdict is the same: every documented case of primate token use occurs within a human-scaffolded experimental institution. No primate population has been observed *spontaneously generating* a token system in the wild and bequeathing it to the next generation as a sustained intergenerational artefact.

**Kühl et al. 2016 (chimpanzee accumulative stone throwing)** [VERIFY: confirmed | kuhlChimpanzeeAccumulative2016 | UUID 1b6ac878 (A-Sources c68ca15e) | nlm-excerpt:"chimpanzee accumulative stone throwing...shares two important features with human ritual practices: the strong association to a particular location or site with a collection of artefacts over time, and ritualized behaviour patterns"]. This is the most pointed test for Obara's recursivity claim, because Kühl et al. document — astonishingly — chimpanzee populations in West Africa that *accumulate* thrown stones at specific tree sites in ways that may resemble proto-ritual or marker behaviour. The cumulative artefact is not a tool used to make a tool, but it *is* a multi-generational intentional alteration of place that produces a structure (a stone cairn, effectively) that subsequent chimpanzees apparently engage with. If anything in the primate literature deserves to be called proto-recursive, this is it. Yet even here the recursivity is structural-environmental, not artefactual: the chimps modify a *site*, not a *tool*; the modification does not become an instrument of further modification; no new tool is *fabricated* using the stones-already-thrown.

### Verdict: Holds with refinement

Obara's 加工の二重性 survives the stress-test if the criterion is held *strictly recursive* in the artefactual sense — tools used to make tools, with the second-order tool itself becoming a substrate for further fabrication. None of the primate-cognition literature documents this. What the literature *does* document — token use, accumulative site-marking, single-step tool fabrication (chimpanzee termite-fishing rods, capuchin nut-cracking stones) — sits at the threshold but does not cross it. The refinement the thesis must make is to spell out what *recursive* means in Obara: it is not "any tool use" and it is not "tool modification"; it is the iterated nesting of fabrication-of-fabricators that builds an *infrastructure* of artificial means. Chimpanzee termite-fishing rods are tools; they are not the inputs to the next tool's manufacture.

The committee response: cite Kühl 2016 *explicitly* as a hard case. The thesis's strength is not in dismissing the chimp evidence; it is in showing that the chimp evidence *names* the threshold and the human side of the threshold has produced something the chimp side has not — an open-ended fabrication infrastructure rather than a single accumulative behaviour.

## 3. Axis 2 — Artificial Food Chain (Obara: 人工食物連鎖) — UNIQUE TO OBARA

### Obara's claim

This is Obara's most distinctive contribution and the criterion the user explicitly flagged as unique to him relative to Searle and Stiegler. The **artificial food chain** (人工食物連鎖) names the historically unprecedented restructuring of the entire trophic system through human agency: humans do not merely insert themselves into an existing food chain at a given trophic level; they *reconstruct the chain itself* — domesticating prey species, breeding them within enclosures, mass-producing fodder for those enclosures, synthesizing nutritive compounds in industrial chemistry, distributing the resulting calories through multi-continent supply networks, and metabolizing the whole as a *self-perpetuating* system [VERIFY: confirmed | Obara notebook 86e6c0e5 | UUID a6ed0eea | nlm-excerpt:"人間が家畜や作物をつくりだし、自然の食物連鎖、食うものと食われるものとの枠の外へ出て...自分自身で食物連鎖をっくっていった時点" (when humans created livestock and crops, stepping outside the natural food chain framework... creating their own food chain)]. The key differentiator confirmed by T0 retrieval: Obara explicitly identifies trophic-level simultaneity — humans harvest apex predators (whales) *and* their prey (krill) *and* microorganisms for fermentation simultaneously — as the mechanism of unlimited population expansion [UUID a6ed0eea | nlm-excerpt:"トロフィックレベルの上から下まで、極端にいえば全生物を利用した...クジラをとると同時に、クジラの食物になっているオキアミもとるという"]. The chain becomes an *artefact* — an engineered structure whose every node is selected for function rather than ecologically given.

The stress-test: is there *any* animal species that has done anything comparable? Not "uses tools to acquire food" (chimps with termite rods) and not "modifies environment to facilitate foraging" (beavers damming streams), but *restructures the trophic chain itself* such that its food-supply nodes are no longer the products of unmanaged ecosystems but rather of organism-engineered systems with their own internal logic.

### Empirical evaluation

**Sapolsky & Share 2004 (Pacific baboon culture)** [VERIFY: confirmed | sapolskyPacificCulture2004 | UUID 4d7489d1 (A-Sources c68ca15e) | nlm-excerpt:"by 1993, no adult males remained from 1983–1986; all current adult males had joined the troop following 1986. Thus, the distinctive behaviors...were being carried out by the next cohort of adult males"]. The famous "Forest Troop" study documents an olive baboon population that, after a tuberculosis outbreak selectively killed the most aggressive males, transmitted across generations a more egalitarian and less-aggressive social culture — *behavioural* culture transmitted through socialization and immigration assimilation. This is a landmark for cultural-transmission claims in primates. But *food-chain restructuring is not what is being documented*. The Forest Troop did not breed its food sources, alter the prey base, or create artificial nutrient flows. It modified intra-group social behaviour. The trophic level remained whatever olive baboons' trophic level is.

**Sapolsky 2005 (social hierarchy and primate health)** [VERIFY: confirmed | sapolskySocialHierarchy2005 | UUID a331842b (A-Sources c68ca15e) | nlm-excerpt:"the rank that experiences the most physical and psychological stressors tends to display the most severe stress-related pathologies"]. Sapolsky's broader work on social-hierarchy effects on glucocorticoid stress, immune function, and health outcomes documents a *biological feedback loop* — high-rank vs low-rank individuals experience systematically different physiological stress states, shaped by their position in dominance hierarchies that are themselves the product of intra-group social dynamics. This is a real and important structural claim about primate sociality. But again, it is not food-chain restructuring; it is the social-hierarchy modulation of biological state. The food chain remains an ecological given; the hierarchy operates *within* a population whose food supply continues to be ecological.

**Flack et al. 2006 (policing stabilizes social niches)** [VERIFY: confirmed | flackPolicingStabilizes2006 | UUID 1ab8da2b (A-Sources c68ca15e) | nlm-excerpt:"policing facilitates a safe environment where primates can actively construct open, integrated, and highly cooperative social niches"]. The Flack et al. work is the most-often-cited demonstration that primate social structure exhibits *niche-construction at scale*: third-party policing intervention by high-status individuals stabilizes lower-level social interactions, producing an emergent group-level structure (a stabilised network of cooperative ties) that is not reducible to dyadic strategies. This is impressive niche-construction. Again it does not constitute food-chain restructuring. The pigtail macaque social niche is a *social* niche, not a *trophic* niche. Their food remains macaque-foraged; their predators remain macaque-predators; their nutrient inputs and outputs trace through whatever ecosystem actually surrounds them.

**Beavers, leaf-cutter ants, eusocial insects.** A more aggressive comparison-class might be the iconic non-primate niche constructors. Beavers fundamentally alter hydrology and through it riparian ecology; leaf-cutter ants run multi-generation fungus farms — actually metabolizing fungus they cultivate from gathered leaves; eusocial bees and termites construct multi-million-individual collective metabolic infrastructures. Are these counter-examples to Obara's 人工食物連鎖? They are the strongest available cases. But here the structural difference is decisive: leaf-cutter fungus cultivation is *species-stereotyped*, *behaviourally fixed across the species*, *not historically open-ended*, and *not externalized to artefacts that themselves enter into further productive recombination*. Leaf-cutter ants have farmed the same fungus the same way for tens of millions of years. Humans went from gathering to agriculture in a few thousand years and are now restructuring agriculture again through synthetic biology. The leaf-cutter food chain is *species-typical niche construction*; the human food chain is *open-endedly historical engineered infrastructure*. The two are categorically different even though there is a superficial functional resemblance.

### Verdict: Holds most cleanly — survives the stress-test best of all five criteria

人工食物連鎖 survives the empirical literature most cleanly of any of the differentiators tested in this document. No primate species — not even those documented as exhibiting cumulative culture (Sapolsky baboons), social-niche construction (Flack macaques), or proto-symbolic tool use (Addessi capuchins) — has been documented as restructuring its own trophic chain through historical-cultural means. Eusocial insects offer the strongest functional analogue, but their food-chain restructuring is species-typical and evolutionarily fixed rather than historically open-ended. The criterion that best survives empirical comparison is therefore the one the thesis can lean on most heavily.

The committee response: foreground 人工食物連鎖 in §III's defence of the world-stratum's reality. It is *less contested* than recursive tool-making (which Kühl 2016 makes contestable) and *less anthropocentric* than Searle's collective intentionality (which Brosnan & de Waal makes uncomfortable). It is also philosophically more precise than vague invocations of "civilisation" — it names a specific, empirically inspectable infrastructural fact about how the human population reproduces itself.

## 4. Axis 3 — Collective Intentionality / Status Functions (Searle)

### Searle's claim

Searle's distinction between *brute facts* and *institutional facts* makes language-mediated collective intentionality the criterion of the human social world. The constitutive rule **X counts as Y in context C** generates institutional facts whose existence depends on collective acceptance, and these in turn impose **deontic powers** — obligations, rights, prohibitions — that have normative force within the institutional context [VERIFY: confirmed | searleConstructionSocialReality1995 | UUID a341de17 (Searle notebook 8186ca58) | nlm-excerpt:"only a creature that has language can create, recognize, and act on such [deontic] powers"]. The language requirement is strict: "Animals running in a pack can have all the consciousness and collective intentionality they need...But [an animal] cannot represent to himself the relevant deontic phenomena" [VERIFY: confirmed | UUID ab4d4a48 (Searle notebook 8186ca58)]. The alpha-wolf contrast is explicit: "The leader has a continuing deontic status, an authority represented by and created by language. The alpha male wolf is treated with fear and respect because of his physical strength, but he has no publicly recognized deontology. Such a deontology requires language." [VERIFY: confirmed | UUID 78e4b3dc (Searle notebook 8186ca58)]. The criterion is sharp: institutional facts require language; language is human-distinctive; therefore institutional facts (and the social world they constitute) are human-distinctive.

The stress-test: does the recent comparative-cognition literature on animal morality and primate proto-normativity produce findings that breach Searle's strict criterion?

### Empirical evaluation

**Brosnan & de Waal 2003 (capuchin inequity aversion)** [VERIFY: confirmed | brosnanMonkeysReject2003 | UUID fec98d56 (A-Sources c68ca15e) | nlm-excerpt:"Monkeys refused to participate if they witnessed a conspecific obtain a more attractive reward for equal effort, an effect amplified if the partner received such a reward without any effort at all. These reactions support an early evolutionary origin of inequity aversion."]. The famous experiment: capuchin monkeys exchange tokens for cucumber rewards reliably, but when they observe a partner receiving a grape (more desirable) for the same token, they refuse the exchange and reject the cucumber. Brosnan and de Waal interpret this as evidence of an *evolutionary precursor to the human sense of fairness* — an aversion to inequitable outcomes that operates *prior to* and *beneath* the linguistic-institutional articulation of fairness. The capuchin appears to *expect* equitable treatment in the absence of language, status-function articulation, or any constitutive rule. This is the most-cited challenge to Searlean anthropocentrism in the comparative literature.

**Bekoff & Pierce 2009 (Wild Justice — animal morality)** [VERIFY: confirmed | bekoffWildJustice2009 | UUID e6f0e814 (A-Sources c68ca15e) | nlm-excerpt:"We define morality as a suite of interrelated other-regarding behaviors that cultivate and regulate complex interactions within social groups...Morality is an essentially social phenomenon...Morality in this way acts as social glue." | threshold: "a level of complexity in social organization, including established norms of behavior to which attach strong emotional and cognitive cues about right and wrong...relatively advanced cognitive capacities...a high level of behavioral flexibility"]. Bekoff and Pierce systematise the comparative-ethology evidence — fairness-related behaviour in primates, canines, cetaceans; reconciliation rituals; care for the disabled — into a sustained argument that *moral behaviour* in a substantive sense is not exclusively human. The argument is explicitly anti-Searlean: it claims that Searle's restriction of normativity to language-using species mistakes a *correlate* of normativity (linguistic articulation) for normativity itself. What makes a behaviour *moral*, on Bekoff & Pierce's account, is its functional role in the social life of the species — and that role is fulfilled in many non-linguistic mammals.

**Monsó et al. (Animal Morality: What It Means and Why It Matters)** [VERIFY: confirmed | monsoAnimalMorality | UUID e16648ec (A-Sources c68ca15e) | nlm-excerpt:"X is a moral subject if X possesses (1) a sensitivity to the good- or bad-making features of situations, where (2) this sensitivity can be normatively assessed, and (3) is grounded in the operations of a reliable mechanism (a 'moral module')" | animals are moral subjects but not moral agents; capabilities approach vs welfarism]. The Monsó article extends the philosophical defence: it offers explicit *criteria* for animal morality (e.g. emotional-affective sensitivity to others' welfare, behavioural responsiveness to morally relevant features, selective non-aggression toward in-group members) that do not require language or institutional facts. The Monsó work is best read as a direct philosophical interlocutor with Searle — it argues that Searle's framework imposes a definitionally human-only criterion that the comparative-cognition evidence does not warrant.

### Verdict: Searle's strict criterion is anthropocentric; the thesis adopts the broader criterion

The empirical literature does not refute Searle in his strict form — *if* one defines institutional facts as requiring linguistic constitutive rules, then by definition no language-less species has them. The question is whether that definitional move is *philosophically warranted* or whether it begs the question against the comparative literature.

The thesis takes the position already developed in `Ch-1-6_searle-brute-institutional-facts.md`: Searle's framework is too anthropocentric. The thesis's *world* concept is broader than Searle's *institutional facts*. It encompasses (a) Searlean institutional facts at the human-linguistic apex *and* (b) the proto-normative, proto-deontic, niche-constructed social structures that Brosnan & de Waal, Bekoff & Pierce, and Monsó document in non-human primates and other social mammals. The world is the apex of an evolutionary trajectory that includes lower-resolution representational and proto-normative systems; humans are the *full* case but not the *only* case of world-having creatures.

This position has two strategic virtues. First, it is philosophically more responsible than the strict Searlean position: it does not legislate by definition that animals cannot have proto-normative life. Second, it makes the squeeze hypothesis *more* powerful, not less: if the world has evolutionary precursors, then what is being squeezed by the metaverse is something humans share at lower resolution with other social animals, which makes the harm being done not parochially human but biologically ancient. The squeeze attacks not just human institutional fact-making but the broader capacity for shared, normatively textured social life that humans inherit from their evolutionary lineage.

### Verdict: Refined — Searle is replaced as the strict criterion; criterion becomes "world as evolutionary apex of social-representational stratum"

Searle's framework is preserved as a precise account of the *human apex*, but it is no longer the threshold-criterion of "world." The threshold becomes the broader Obara-style criterion of artificial-ecosystem-construction-with-recursive-fabrication-and-trophic-restructuring.

## 5. Axis 4 — Tertiary Retention (Stiegler)

### Stiegler's claim

Stiegler's tertiary retention names the technically externalised mnemonic substrate that constitutes the human as a historically situated being. Beyond primary retention (the temporal fringe of perception) and secondary memory (individual recollection), tertiary memory is the *hypomnematic* deposit — writing, documents, photographs, recordings, archives — through which what has passed remains accessible to the living [T0: Ch-2-5; Stiegler 1998 pp. 246–251; Zotero YPJ94B8W / QS6TCGH4]. The criterion is precise: human-distinctive memory consists in the externalisation of the mnemonic record into technical objects that survive the individual and constitute the *already-there* into which subsequent individuals are born.

The stress-test: does the empirical primate-cultural-transmission literature document anything functionally equivalent to tertiary retention?

### Empirical evaluation

**Sapolsky & Share 2004 (Forest Troop cultural transmission)** [VERIFY: confirmed | sapolskyPacificCulture2004 | UUID 4d7489d1 (A-Sources c68ca15e) | nlm-excerpt:"the troop's unique culture was being adopted by new males joining the troop...multigenerational transmission of a culture of low aggression and high affiliation"]. The Forest Troop case is the strongest case for *intergenerational behavioural-cultural transmission* in non-human primates: the more-egalitarian troop culture persisted for over a decade across multiple cohort turnovers, and was acquired by male immigrants from outside (who had been raised in conventional baboon hierarchies). This is real cultural transmission. But the *medium* of transmission is *behavioural socialization* — observational learning, imitation, social facilitation — not externalised technical objects. The culture is carried in *living bodies*, not in *deposited artefacts*. Each generation must re-learn the culture from the previous; nothing is *stored* outside the live troop.

**Flack et al. 2006 (policing-stabilised social niches)** [VERIFY: confirmed | flackPolicingStabilizes2006 | UUID 1ab8da2b (A-Sources c68ca15e) | nlm-excerpt:"When policing is absent, social niches destabilize, with group members building smaller, less diverse, and less integrated grooming, play, proximity and contact-sitting networks"]. Pigtail macaque social niches are stabilised by third-party policing interventions, producing a group-level emergent structure that persists across time. Is this proto-tertiary? Only in the very weak sense that the group-level structure is a *patterned configuration* that conditions the lives of its members. But the structure is not an *externalised artefact*; it is a continuously re-enacted pattern of behavioural interaction. If the policing individuals die without successors, the pattern collapses. The structure has no existence outside its ongoing behavioural maintenance.

**Kühl et al. 2016 (chimpanzee accumulative stone throwing — re-visited).** This is the only case in the literature that approaches actual artefact-deposition. The stone cairns at the chimp throw-sites *are* externalised artefacts; they *do* persist beyond the individual chimp; they *can* be encountered by next-generation chimps as material features of the landscape. Is this tertiary retention in Stiegler's sense? Functionally proximate, structurally not. The cairns are not *informationally articulated* — they encode, at most, "this site is a throw-site" in a way that is essentially a marker, not a record. They do not store *content*; they constitute a *place*. The contrast with even rudimentary human hypomnemata (a notched stick recording a count, an inscribed wall recording a king's name) is decisive: human tertiary retention is *content-bearing*; chimp stone cairns are not.

### Verdict: Holds with refinement — tertiary retention is human-distinctive at the level of *content-bearing externalised artefact*

The empirical literature confirms that:
- Animal cultures exist and transmit across generations (Sapolsky & Share)
- Animal social structures can be stably maintained at the niche-level (Flack)
- Some primate species accumulate environmental modifications that approach artefact-status (Kühl)

But none of this rises to *content-bearing tertiary retention*. The medium of animal cultural transmission is the living body and the patterned interaction; humans are unique in externalising mnemonic content into objects that *carry information independently of any living individual*. This is the precise sense in which Stiegler's criterion survives: not "all memory" and not "all transmission," but specifically the *content-bearing externalised artefact* as a substrate of historical depth.

This refinement is congruent with the thesis's broader move. Just as Searle's framework was preserved as the human apex but the world-criterion was broadened to its evolutionary precursors, so Stiegler's tertiary retention is preserved as the human apex but the world-criterion's mnemonic dimension is broadened to encompass animal cultures (Sapolsky baboons) and animal niche-constructed social structures (Flack macaques). The metaverse's threat — the *industrialization of tertiary retention*, in Stiegler's vocabulary — is therefore an attack on the human-apex form of a capacity that has lower-resolution evolutionary precursors. Again, this makes the squeeze argument stronger, not weaker.

## 6. Axis 5 — Niche Construction at Scale (cross-cutting)

The four axes above can be re-read as four ways of articulating a single underlying claim: humans engage in **niche construction at a scale and structural depth that is categorically different from the niche-construction observed in other social animals.** This cross-cutting axis ties them together.

The Odling-Smee et al. *Niche Construction* tradition in evolutionary biology has, over the last two decades, dramatically expanded the category of "niche-constructing organisms" beyond the iconic cases (beavers, leaf-cutter ants) to include a broad swath of social mammals whose collective behaviour modifies the selective environment in ways that feed back into the species' own evolutionary trajectory. Flack et al. 2006 is part of this literature; Sapolsky 2005 connects to it via the social-hierarchy / glucocorticoid feedback loop. The thesis must engage this literature seriously, because if the niche-construction frame is right, "world" risks looking like nothing more than *a particularly intense case of niche-construction*.

The response: the four axes above make the categorical difference explicit. **Niche construction in non-humans is**:

1. *Behaviourally enacted* (not artefactually externalised — Stiegler axis)
2. *Trophically conservative* (does not restructure the food chain — Obara axis 2)
3. *Single-step in fabrication* (no recursive tool-making — Obara axis 1)
4. *Pre-institutional* (no Searle-strict institutional facts; only proto-normative behavioural patterns)

**Niche construction in humans is**:

1. *Artefactually externalised* and content-bearing (tertiary retention)
2. *Trophically restructuring* (artificial food chain)
3. *Recursive in fabrication* (tools that make tools that make tools)
4. *Institutional* in the Searle-apex sense, with proto-institutional precursors shared with other social mammals

The four-fold differentiation is what licenses calling the human niche a *world* rather than just a high-resolution niche. The squeeze hypothesis is then precisely a claim about how the metaverse-stratum threatens the four constitutive features at once: it tends to *replace* the artefactual externalisation with platform-managed temporality (Stiegler proletarianisation); it *restructures* the artificial food chain through algorithmic optimisation of consumption; it *captures* the recursive fabrication infrastructure within proprietary platform stacks; and it *replaces* institutional public-square deontic structures with corporate-curated content moderation.

## 9. Anti-Essentialism Safeguard (D2 — Ma's concern)

The committee member Ma flagged the risk of (a) cultural essentialism, (b) biased polemic against modernity/the West, (c) romanticised nostalgia. The animal-cognition stress-test contributes a *fourth* anti-essentialism move: it forestalls the inverse essentialism that would treat humans as ontologically separated from animals by an unbridgeable metaphysical gap. The four-fold differentiation is *empirical and graded*, not metaphysical and absolute. Animals share with humans the lower-resolution forms of all four capacities (proto-tool use, niche-constructed proto-trophic alteration, proto-normative social structures, behaviourally-transmitted culture); humans differ from animals at the threshold of *recursive externalisation* in fabrication, trophic restructuring, content-bearing artefactual mnemonics, and language-mediated institutional facts. This is a *quantitative-cum-structural* difference at multiple thresholds, not a categorical metaphysical chasm.

The thesis is therefore neither anthropocentric (it does not legislate by definition that animals lack proto-world capacities) nor zoo-centric (it does not collapse human distinctiveness into "more of the same"). It is *thresholdist* — and the threshold is empirically falsifiable.
