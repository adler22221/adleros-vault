# Why the thesis leads with Uegaki, and where Weber, Adorno, and Rosa stand
> Ch IV positioning passage (ai-draft, voice-matched). Placement + flags at foot. This is also the explicit **Rosa-subordination** flag.

A powerful and more familiar tradition runs alongside Uegaki's, and the thesis must say plainly why it does not lead with it. That tradition has a clear lineage — Max Weber, then Adorno and Horkheimer, then, in our own moment, Hartmut Rosa — and what unites the three across a century is that each diagnoses the modern predicament as a matter of the subject's **relation to a disenchanted world of objects**. It is the *object-pole* of the very rupture this thesis has been tracking. Uegaki works the *subject-pole*; and the difference between the two is the reason he leads.

Weber gave the lineage its founding term. The modern world is the *disenchanted* world — *die Entzauberung der Welt* — the progressive draining of intrinsic meaning and mystery from things until what remains is a field of the calculable: for there are, on this view, "no mysterious incalculable forces that come into play, but rather… one can, in principle, master all things by calculation" (Weber, "Science as a Vocation"). `[VERIFY: Weber locus + exact wording — no Weber T0 yet; "Wissenschaft als Beruf" 1917/1919, the Entzauberung / "alle Dinge durch Berechnung beherrschen" passage. Pull before prose.]` The world becomes the object of a calculating mastery; the subject is whoever calculates. Adorno and Horkheimer radicalised exactly this thought, making the disenchantment of nature the engine of an instrumental reason whose entire content is domination: what human beings "seek to learn from nature is how to use it to dominate wholly both it and human beings. Nothing else counts" (DoE, p.2). And Rosa — the contemporary heir of this Frankfurt tradition `[VERIFY: Rosa directs the Institut für Sozialforschung — confirm before asserting the institutional claim in prose]` — gives the lineage its late-modern form: the world rendered *verfügbar*, made "engineerable, predictable, available, accessible, disposable" in all its aspects (Rosa, 2020: viii), a controllability whose price is the silencing of the world's power to reach and address us, and whose recoil is what he calls the "monstrous return of the uncontrollable" (Rosa, 2020: ~115). Across all three, the diagnosis is a *stance toward the object*: disenchant it, master it, render it available — and forfeit something in the relation.

This lineage is not a foil to be refuted; the thesis draws on it, and most directly on Rosa, whose vocabulary of controllability names the *principle* of the mode (Chapter III) and whose returning uncontrollable supplies the universe-side image of the squeeze (§IV.4 below, and Chapter VI). But it works one pole, and the thesis's argument turns on the other. For the question Uegaki asks is not what the disenchanting subject *does to the world*, but what the liberated subject takes itself *to be*, and therefore *to demand*. His anthropology of freedom (自由の人間学) is a diagnosis of the **subject's self-constitution**: the human posited as a self-subsistent existence standing prior to and above its world (時空間的自立性), bearing a true nature it is owed and not yet given (約束された本来性), and entitled on that ground to a life that answers to its own 意 — the as-intended life. Where Weber, Adorno, and Rosa describe a relation to objects, Uegaki describes an elevation of the subject; and it is the elevated subject, not the disenchanted object, whose collision with the given this thesis will call the squeeze.

That this re-centring is more than a change of emphasis becomes visible the moment the two axes are laid across each other — because they cross. Rosa's axis runs from the controllable to the uncontrollable; Uegaki's from the as-intended to the anti-intended; and the as-intended is the *broader* category, because it annexes much of what Rosa files under the uncontrollable. The clearest case is the one Rosa himself most prizes: sport, and games. For Rosa these are paradigms of the good uncontrollable — we love a game precisely because its outcome cannot be commanded, and "the interplay between control and uncontrollability is constitutive… of games in general" (Rosa, 2020: 3). But sport and games, on demand and in abundance, are an enormous part of the self-finishing user's life — and they are entirely *as-intended*: chosen, willed, framed as the self's own diversion, and delivered by the very apparatus that has taken the rest of living off the user's hands. The as-intended life does not require that things be *verfügbar*; it requires only that the apparatus serve the subject's 意 — which it does as readily by *manufacturing* unpredictability (the match, the next level, the algorithmic surprise) as by guaranteeing convenience. The two diagnoses coincide at only one corner of the field — the genuinely anti-intended *and* uncontrollable: the body that ails, the other who refuses, the death that comes — which is exactly the corner the squeeze occupies.

So the relation between them is one of genus and species. Rosa's controllability is *one engine* of the as-intended life — the instrumental, appropriative engine that renders the object available; in the thesis's terms the Aristotelian pole, whose practical signature is Bhaskar's epistemic fallacy (being reduced to what can be known, calculated, controlled). Uegaki's as-intended life is the whole of which that engine is a part: it carries the instrumental engine *and* the idealist, soteriological engine of the elevated subject — the Platonic pole, the endowed nature, the engine Weber and Rosa do not theorise because their gaze is fixed on the object. To lead with Rosa would be to mistake a part for the whole, and to leave the thesis sounding like one more critique of controllability. To lead with Uegaki is to keep the subject in view, and to be able to say what neither the disenchantment lineage nor the controllability lineage can: that the squeeze is not first a fact about a world made too available, but about a subject that has taken its as-intended life to be its birthright, and so reads the given — whatever in existence will not answer to its 意 — as an oppression to be overcome. Rosa names the symptom; Uegaki names the cause; and the thesis leads with the cause.
