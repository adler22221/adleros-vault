## Source Passages — Verified in Stiegler (1998)

From the extracted Stiegler text (pp. 246–251), the following definitions are established:

**Primary retention (p. 247)**: The temporal fringe of living perception — what is retained within the *now* of experience. The "comet's tail" of the present moment: what was just heard lingers within the perception of what is being heard.

**Secondary memory (p. 247)**: Individual recollection — calling back to mind what has been previously experienced. "Secondary memory is a 'recollection'" (p. 247). It is re-presentation, not perception.

**Tertiary memory (pp. 247–251)**: Image-consciousness and technically externalized memory — the category Stiegler adds to Husserl's distinction. Writing, documents, recordings, photographs: these are the "supports of having-already-been" (p. 251) — the technical objects through which what has passed continues to be accessible. Stiegler calls this the already-there qua "bearer of Epimetheia": the inherited technical record that precedes any individual and constitutes the historical depth within which any world-inhabitation occurs.

At p. 249: "the specificity of image-consciousnesses and other tertiary memories is here lost" — Stiegler's diagnosis of Heidegger's inattention to the technical dimension of the already-there. Tertiary memory is constitutive of the who — of human being as historically situated — but Heidegger fails to acknowledge this because he subordinates the *what* (technical objects) to the *who* (Dasein).

At p. 251: tertiary memories are the "supports of the already-there" that give access to "having-been-already-there as such, to the as-such of the past."

## Prose Draft — Full Version

The concept of exosomatization — the externalization of biological functions and cognitive capacities into technical objects — acquires its full philosophical weight when it is understood not merely as a functional process but as the constitution of a *temporal inheritance*. What is externalized is not only labor (the use of tools to accomplish what the hand alone cannot) but *memory itself*: the accumulated stock of experience, knowledge, and practice that human communities transmit across generations. The technical object is not only an instrument; it is a carrier of the past.

Bernard Stiegler's analysis of memory in *Technics and Time, 1* (1998) provides the vocabulary for this claim. Stiegler follows Husserl in distinguishing two registers of lived memory:

*Primary retention* is the temporal fringe of living perception — the "comet's tail" of the present moment. When one hears a melody, the tones just past linger within the perception of the tones being heard; this lingering is not recollection but an immediate dimension of the *now*. It is memory as the thickness of the present.

*Secondary memory* is recollection — the calling back to mind of what has been previously experienced. It is a re-presentation: one recalls the melody one heard yesterday, hearing it "as it were" in imagination, running through it in a second-order temporal succession. This is memory as individual biography.

But Stiegler introduces a third category that Husserl and Heidegger each, in different ways, neglect: *tertiary memory* — what he calls "image-consciousness," the technically externalized record. Writing, documents, photographs, recordings, archives: these are the "supports of the already-there" (Stiegler 1998, p. 251) through which what has passed remains accessible to the living. Tertiary memory is not the retention of an individual's experience but the objectified record of experience — the *hypomnemata*, in Plato's term for written memory-aids — that precedes any individual and into which any individual is born. It is the technical deposit of the past.

The significance of tertiary memory for the thesis is this: the world — the human-made meaning-space through which a community organizes its inhabitation of the universe — is constituted not only through current practice but through the *inheritance* of this technical record. A community does not begin each generation from scratch; it inherits the technical deposit of all previous world-making — language, law, ritual, built environment, accumulated craft knowledge. This inherited deposit is what makes world-making a cumulative, trans-generational process rather than a Sisyphean repetition. Tertiary memory is the *material condition* for the world's historical depth.

The metaverse, in Stiegler's framework, represents the *industrialization of tertiary retention*. When the management, curation, and provision of tertiary memory is taken over by algorithmically managed commercial platforms — when the social archive, the communicative record, and the historical deposit are administered by Facebook, Google, TikTok, and their successors — the pharmacological character of all technics is intensified to an unprecedented degree. The platform does not simply *store* memory; it *selects* which memories are amplified, which are buried, which are associated with which others, and at what speed the historical record scrolls past. It imposes a single algorithmically managed temporality on all who depend on it: a temporality organized by engagement optimization, commercial interest, and network-effect dynamics rather than by the organic rhythms of communal practice and historical inheritance.

This constitutes a new form of what Stiegler calls "proletarianization" — the loss of savoir-faire that accompanies industrial standardization, extended here to the dimension of memory and temporal experience. Just as industrial production deskilled the craftworker by substituting machine-managed process for embodied craft knowledge, the platform-managed metaverse deskills the cultural memory-keeper by substituting algorithmic curation for communally maintained historical inheritance. The world's depth is not eliminated but *standardized*: what is remembered, and in what order, and at what speed, is determined not by the community's own temporal rhythms but by the platform's commercial logic.

## Condensed Version (if §IIA space is limited)

Bernard Stiegler's analysis of memory in *Technics and Time, 1* (1998) illuminates what is at stake in exosomatization. Beyond primary retention (the temporal fringe of living perception) and secondary memory (individual recollection), Stiegler identifies *tertiary memory* — the technically externalized record: writing, documents, recordings, the inherited archive. These are the "supports of the already-there" (Stiegler 1998, p. 251) through which the world's historical depth is maintained and transmitted across generations. Tertiary memory is not a supplement to the world; it is the material condition of the world's depth.

The metaverse represents the industrialization of this tertiary retention. When algorithmically managed platforms administer the social archive — selecting which records are amplified, which are suppressed, at what speed history scrolls — they impose a single commercially optimized temporality over the trans-generational inheritance through which world-making has always operated. This is Stiegler's "proletarianization of memory": the loss of communally maintained historical inheritance to platform-managed standardization — a new pharmacological intensification that the §VI squeeze argument must account for.
