## A. Reaffirming the thesis position: world as human-dependent totality

The viva-passed thesis already states the position clearly (passed draft):

> "'World,' a word with Germanic origin Welt or vereld, has its etymological roots from 'man' (Latin vir) and 'age' (German alt), i.e., 'age of man' (Oxford Dictionary Online, 2022). As historian of philosophy Rémi Brague has shown in his historical survey (2003), the meaning of 'world' does not come from the synthesis of what is contained in the totality, as we 'almost never use the word "world" any more to designate a physical reality as such.' Instead, it is anthropological, designating human phenomena, and its 'worldliness' comes not from the totality itself but from us."

This position must be *retained* in the revised §I and is corroborated by Brague's own framing. The relevant Brague passage is from the Introduction, *not* the Setting-the-Stage chapter that v1 quoted:

> "[T]he meaning of the word 'world' is often anthropological. It is clear that it is increasingly used to designate human phenomena... For us, therefore, 'the world' designates the entire earth, considered as the resting-place of humanity, the *oikoumenē*, or society, even a part of it, the most distinguished social layer ('a man of the world'); the word can also designate a milieu ('the little world of Don Camillo'). This meaning, whether or not it is accurate, has however become the most common. **For ultimately we almost never use the word 'world' any more to designate a physical reality as such. For that use other words, such as 'universe' or 'cosmos,' have more or less replaced it.**" (Brague 2003, pp. 5–6, emphasis added)

This is the passage the viva-passed thesis quotes. The bolded sentence is the operative claim: *the lexical division of labor in modern Western languages assigns "world" to human-dependent totality and "universe"/"cosmos" to physical-substrate totality*. The thesis's vocabulary follows this division.

Brague's full title — *La Sagesse du monde: Histoire de l'expérience humaine de l'Univers* (lit. "The Wisdom of the World: History of the Human Experience of the Universe") — encodes the very distinction the thesis relies on: *world* is the human side of the relation (the wisdom-of-the-world is what humans build); *universe* is the cosmic substrate that humans experience-and-reckon-with-via-world.

## B. The Kosmos lineage: Brague's reading

Brague's substantive historical thesis is that Western thought "experienced the universe" through the concept of *kosmos*, originated in Greece around the 6th–5th c. BCE. The genealogy has five stages:

### B1. *Kosmos* before philosophical use — "order" or "ornament"

> "The Greeks set out on a path where other civilizations did not follow... The innovation was in giving the world a name of its own. The Greeks did so by choosing the word *kosmos*. Its etymology is not clear, but its meaning is, beginning with its use in the *Iliad*: 'order,' always in the fixed expression *kata kosmon* 'in good order,' or 'ornament,' like the boss or stud of a horse's bit or Hera's jewels. The term denotes order and beauty, even more specifically the beauty resulting from order, the beauty that is still implied today by an activity that derives its name from the word — 'cosmetics.'" (Brague 2003, pp. 18–19)

**Reading:** *Kosmos* enters philosophical use as a metaphor: the universe is "in good order" *like* a well-fitted horse's bit. The metaphor was felt as such by the Ancients themselves; Brague cites Tertullian and Eriugena for the long memory of *kosmos* as "ornament" (p. 19).

### B2. Heraclitus Fragment 30 — the founding declaration of observer-independence

> "I am interested less here in the history of the word than in the conceptual decision that enabled the emergence of the idea of 'world.' And it is only with Heraclitus, perhaps, that we may find it formulated... It is the very familiar Fragment 30 that to me seems not only to contain, but in fact to create, the meaning of the word *kosmos*. I will hazard to translate it quite literally:
>
> *Kosmon [tonde] ton auton hapantōn, oute tis theōn oute anthrōpōn epoiēsen, all' ēn aei kai estin kai estai: pur aeizōon, haptomenon metra kai aposbennumenon metra.*
>
> 'This world, the same for all, it is neither a god nor a man who has made it, but always, it was, it is, it will be: an everliving fire, kindling in measures, and being extinguished in measures.'" (Brague 2003, p. 20)

**Reading (Brague's own):**

> "In any case we see the affirmation of the idea of *an ordered totality sufficient in itself and about which it is explicitly said that it does not require the intervention of an external influence*. As for the necessity of excluding an active role on the part of men, it is perhaps possible to understand this on the basis of an ancient conception, notably that of the Egyptians, mentioned above, according to which the order of the world was maintained by the intervention of men." (Brague 2003, p. 20–21, emphasis added)

**Critical interpretation for the thesis:** Heraclitus's *kosmos* is precisely *observer-independent ordered totality* — the very feature the thesis assigns to *universe*, not to *world*. Brague's translation as "world" follows the lexicographical convention of Western philosophy (in which Greek *kosmos* → Latin *mundus* → English/French/German "world"/"monde"/"Welt") but *the conceptual content* of Heraclitean *kosmos* is closer to thesis's "universe." This is the lineage that needs to be traced.

The negation "neither god nor man made it" is the philosophical signature: it stipulates that the totality exists *independently* of the agency of any inhabitant. In thesis terms: it stipulates that the totality is *real* in the critical-realist sense (independent of human cognition), not *world* in the thesis's sense (constituted in part by human meaning-making).

### B3. Plato's Timaeus — *kosmos* installed as the canonical word for "world"

> "Plato's work represents a decisive point of departure in the history I wish to recount, especially his *Timaeus*... it was with Plato that the word *kosmos* was installed definitively and without ambiguity in its meaning as 'world.' And at the same time, in this very usage, it reveals what was problematic in the underlying concept." (Brague 2003, p. 22)

> "The final words of [the *Timaeus*] sum up its meaning: '...this world (*hode ho kosmos*), has thus become a visible living creature embracing all that are visible and an image of the intelligible, a perceptible god, supreme in greatness and excellence, in beauty and perfection, this Heaven (*heis ouranos hode*) single in its kind and one.'" (Brague 2003, p. 22, citing Plato, *Timaeus* 92c)

**Reading:** Plato's intervention is to ouranize the kosmos — to identify the world with the sky, and to fold the substrate (heaven, *ouranos*) and the totality (*kosmos*) into one. This is the moment at which the Greek-philosophical "kosmos" stabilizes as a name for *the ordered, beautiful, observer-independent totality*. Plato's "this world" (*hode ho kosmos*) is the term modern translators render as "world" but which the thesis must read as *universe-conceived-as-orderly*.

### B4. The Latin transmission: *mundus*

> "Similarly, the Latin *mundus*, source of the French *monde*, or 'world,' is no doubt the same word as *mundus*, the French *toilette* or 'woman's ornamentation,' the cosmological meaning having been derived through imitation of the Greek. This is in any event what Pliny the Elder asserts: 'What the Greeks call *kosmos*, we call *mundus* due to its perfect and faultless elegance (*a perfecta absolutaque elegantia*).'" (Brague 2003, p. 19, citing Pliny, *Natural History* II.4.8)

**Reading:** The Latin *mundus* preserves the "ornament/elegance" sense of *kosmos*. Through Latin, the Greek concept enters all the Romance vernaculars (*monde*, *mondo*, *mundo*) and, via translation traditions, the Germanic vernaculars (*Welt*, *world*) where it competes with the *Welt-* family rooted in "age of man." The European modern languages thus inherit *two* senses of "world":

| Sense | Etymological lineage | Conceptual content | Thesis's term |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ordered observer-independent totality | Greek *kosmos* → Latin *mundus* → Romance *monde/mundo* (and into "world" via translation) | The cosmos as objective, beautiful, lawful order | **universe** |
| Human-dependent totality, "age of man" | Germanic *Welt*, *vereld* | The lived meaning-space; what is human about the totality | **world** |

The viva-passed thesis quote already invokes the Germanic lineage. The lookup needs to make explicit that what *Brague* tracks under the heading "world" — namely, the *kosmos* tradition — is, by the thesis's own vocabulary, the genealogy of *universe-conceived-as-ordered*.

### B5. The Greek "decision" as the disposition to domination — relevance to §III

> "It was [in Greece], and there alone, that that 'distanced' position would appear, that 'Archimedes point' from which man would be able, 'conscious of being a subject (*subjektbewußt*), submit nature to objective research.'" (Brague 2003, p. 15, citing Hans Blumenberg)

> "Greek scientists were aware that they not only had a knowledge of the *kosmos*, but that their use of the term had essentially constructed the *kosmos* as such, as a *kosmos*. We see the development of this awareness in a passage of Simplicius..." (Brague 2003, p. 23)

**Critical interpretation for the thesis:** The Greek invention of *kosmos* is not merely a lexical event; it is the constitution of a *dispositional stance* — the stance of standing back from the universe and treating it as a unified observer-independent ordered totality available for objective investigation. This stance is the seed of the "disposition to domination" the thesis tracks in §III as the signature of the modern Western mode of artificiality. The Greek *kosmos* is the philosophical-cosmological precursor of the cosmography and physics that became, in the modern era, the basis for treating the universe as material to be known, predicted, and controlled.

The cross-cultural contrast Brague develops sharpens the point. He shows that Egypt, Mesopotamia, and other ancient civilizations had *no word for "world" as a unified observer-independent totality* — they expressed totality through binary opposition (heaven/earth) or exhaustive list (Brague 2003, p. 12–13, already in v1). The Greek innovation was to detach the totality from the inhabitant and posit it as a *kosmos*, an order in itself. This is the philosophical fork that, multiplied through Christian-Aristotelian synthesis, modern science, and Enlightenment rationalism, becomes the modern Western mode of artificiality.

## C. Recommended §I prose passage (revised for v2)

> The thesis uses *world* in a specific sense, distinct from the technical usage of *world* in modal logic (footnote 2 of Ch-1-11) and distinct from the broader use that conflates "world" and "physical universe." The thesis's *world* is *human-dependent totality* — the lived, culturally constituted meaning-space through which a collective inhabits the universe. The vocabulary follows a lexical division of labor that is already canonical in modern Western languages, and which Rémi Brague has documented: "we almost never use the word 'world' any more to designate a physical reality as such. For that use other words, such as 'universe' or 'cosmos,' have more or less replaced it" (Brague 2003, pp. 5–6). The Germanic etymology of *world* (Welt, vereld, "age of man") corroborates the anthropological orientation: world is what humans constitute *as* world.
>
> This linguistic division of labor is not universal across traditions, and it is not even uniformly distributed in the Western inheritance. Brague's historical thesis is that the very concept of the universe as a *unified, observer-independent ordered totality* — the concept the thesis assigns to *universe* — is a specific Greek philosophical achievement, formulated under the name *kosmos*. Heraclitus's Fragment 30 is the founding declaration: "*This kosmos, the same for all, neither god nor man made it, but always, it was, it is, it will be*" (DK 22 B30; translated by Brague 2003, p. 20). The negation *neither god nor man made it* is the philosophical signature: the totality is stipulated to be independent of any inhabitant's agency — a stipulation precisely of what the thesis calls the *real* level of universe-as-substrate. Plato installed *kosmos* as the canonical Greek word for the universe-so-conceived, and through Latin *mundus* (Pliny, *Natural History* II.4.8) it entered the European vernaculars. The translation conventions of philosophy thereby produced the modern European ambiguity: *world* (Welt) and *world* (translation of *kosmos*) are different concepts under one word. The thesis's vocabulary disambiguates them.
>
> Egypt and Mesopotamia, and the early Semitic linguistic traditions, did not generate a comparable concept of unified observer-independent totality; they expressed totality through binary opposition ("heaven and earth") or exhaustive list. The Arabic ‘ālam, etymologically rooted in "unlimited time" before being inflected toward "world" via Aramaic and through later contact with Greek philosophy, illustrates the contrast (Brague 2003, p. 12). The Greek invention of *kosmos*, accordingly, is not a parochial linguistic event but a constitutive philosophical decision — the decision to occupy what Hans Blumenberg called the "Archimedean point" from which a subject might "submit nature to objective research" (Brague 2003, p. 15). §III will track the downstream effects of this decision through the Christian-Aristotelian synthesis, modern science, and the Enlightenment, into the modern Western mode of artificiality and its disposition to treat reality as material available for control. For the present argument it is enough to register that this dispositional stance — universe as *kosmos*, totality as observer-independent ordered object of knowledge — is one specific tradition's intellectual inheritance, not a universal feature of human cognition.
