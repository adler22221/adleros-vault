# §VI.2: Animals Already Squeezed — The Empirical Record

## §0 — Framing

[SCAFFOLD] §VI.1 defined the squeeze structurally as F1+F2. §VI.2 establishes that the structural condition F1+F2 names is *empirically not novel*. For many non-human species, F1+F2 has been the operative condition for at least fifty thousand years, with anthropogenic extinction as the historical record of the squeeze played out at the species scale. This serves three functions: (i) empirical anchoring of the F1+F2 structural claim before §VI.3 returns to anthropological diagnoses; (ii) preparation for §VI.5's symmetric-squeeze argument by establishing the animal squeeze as the structural precedent for the metaverse-stage human squeeze; (iii) refusal of any reading in which the thesis's squeeze claim is anthropocentric or unprecedented.

[PROSE-TENTATIVE]
> Before §VI.3 takes up the human-experiential signatures of the squeeze in industrial and cybernetic modernity, this section establishes a structurally prior point: the F1+F2 condition defined in §VI.1 is not novel. It is not new in nature. It is not unprecedented as a structural form of squeeze. What is new, in the metaverse-stage, is that humans — uniquely as the apex niche-constructor species — now face it.
>
> For at least fifty thousand years, F1+F2 has been the operative condition of many non-human species. Their universe-originated niches and the human-created artificial ecosystems that displaced or invaded those niches have been the two incommensurable environments between which their Animal OSes have been jointly constrained. The empirical record of this squeeze is the record of anthropogenic extinction — paleontological, ecological, and demographic.
>
> This is structurally important for §VI's argument for two reasons. First, it grounds the abstract F1+F2 diagnosis in empirical phenomena no committee member will dispute. Anthropogenic extinction is established science; the structural conditions that produced it are documented; the analogy to the metaverse-stage human predicament is not speculative but structural. Second, it provides the *precedent* against which the metaverse-stage human squeeze will be compared in §VI.5. The relevant historical analogy for what humans now face is not the within-environment squeezes of earlier modernity (industrialization, urbanization) but the symmetric squeezes that produced animal extinctions. The thesis's structural claim about the metaverse-stage is empirically anchored in a fifty-thousand-year record of what symmetric F1+F2 squeezes look like when they play out to their endpoint.

## §1 — The F1+F2 condition at the species scale [PENDING LOCK REVISION]

> *Note: This subsection extends the squeeze-ontology F1+F2 from the human case to the non-human-animal case. F1+F2 as locked do not specify the subject as human; they specify the structural condition of a being jointly subject to two environments with incommensurable laws. The extension is therefore consistent with the lock. The proposed lock revision is to add an explicit clause acknowledging that F1+F2 applies to non-human species, with anthropogenic extinction as the empirical record of the squeeze played out at species scale.*

### §1.1 — F1 applied to non-human animals

For a non-human species, F1 obtains when the species' Animal OS — the biological-behavioral repertoire it evolved over phylogenetic time — must simultaneously satisfy the selection criteria of *two* environments rather than one:

- **The universe-originated niche** — the environmental conditions the species' Animal OS was selected for. For megafaunal ungulates, this means the open-grassland/savanna ecosystem; for insular birds, the predator-free island ecology; for cold-adapted megafauna, the late-Pleistocene tundra-steppe; for forest-canopy primates, the intact-forest microhabitat. Each species' niche is universe-originated in the sense that it emerged through long evolutionary co-adaptation with universe-laws (gravity, climate, vegetation succession, predator-prey co-evolution).

- **The human-created artificial ecosystem** — the environmental conditions human worlds have superimposed on or substituted for the universe-originated niche. For Pleistocene megafauna, this means the cooperatively-hunting hominid presence on every landmass humans reached. For Holocene wild ungulates, this means agricultural landscapes that converted forest and grassland into cropland and pasture. For colonial-era island endemics, this means human-transported commensal predators (rats, cats, pigs) introduced into formerly predator-free ecosystems. For contemporary species, this means an Anthropocene biosphere with altered climate, fragmented habitat, introduced species, and ubiquitous chemical and plastic pollution.

When the species' Animal OS is jointly constrained by both environments — when survival requires capacities calibrated for the universe-originated niche *and* capacities to navigate the human-created artificial ecosystem — F1 obtains at the species scale.

### §1.2 — F2 applied to non-human animals

F2 obtains when the organizing principles of the two environments are *incommensurable* — when capacities developed for one do not transfer to the other.

The universe-originated niche selects for: predator-evasion calibrated against natural predator behavior, diet calibrated to native plant chemistry, reproductive timing calibrated to native phenology, dispersal patterns calibrated to landscape connectivity, body size/morphology calibrated to thermal regime, immune systems calibrated to native pathogen spectrum.

The human-created artificial ecosystem selects for: tolerance of human proximity, ability to forage in human-modified landscapes, ability to consume crop and refuse food, ability to evade firearms and traps, ability to navigate roads and fences, tolerance of light/noise/chemical pollution, immune capacity for human-translocated pathogens.

The two selection regimes are not commensurable. A species cannot trade off "universe-niche fitness" against "artificial-ecosystem fitness" along a single dimension — they are different *kinds* of fitness, operating in different ecological currencies. F2 obtains at the species scale.

### §1.3 — Why this is structurally identical to the human squeeze the thesis claims

The structural parallel to the human universe-metaverse squeeze is exact. Just as humans now face: (a) a universe-side (with its biological-ecological-physical selection criteria) whose manifestations have been disturbed by human worlds, and (b) a metaverse-side (with its computational-algorithmic-architectural selection criteria) whose laws are themselves engineered — so non-human species have long faced: (a) a universe-originated niche whose manifestations are increasingly altered by human presence, and (b) a human-created artificial ecosystem whose organizing logic is incommensurable with the species' inherited niche-criteria.

The thesis's structural claim about the metaverse-stage is therefore not a new claim about a new kind of phenomenon. It is a claim that *humans* now occupy the structural position that many non-human species have occupied for ≥50,000 years. What is new is not the squeeze; what is new is the species identity of the squeezed.

## §2 — The five-wave periodization of anthropogenic extinction

The empirical record of the animal squeeze is the record of anthropogenic extinction. Five identifiable waves are distinguishable, each corresponding to a different mode of human artificiality producing a different intensity and geographic scope of squeeze.

### §2.1 — Wave 1: Late Pleistocene megafauna (~50,000–10,000 BP)

The first identifiable wave of anthropogenic extinction coincides with the cognitive-cultural revolution that produced cooperatively-hunting hominids equipped with projectile weapons, fire-based landscape management, and migratory population dynamics.

The geographic-temporal pattern is well-documented: large-bodied mammals went extinct on each landmass shortly after human arrival.

- **Australia** (~46,000 BP): approximately sixty species of large vertebrates — including the rhinoceros-sized marsupial *Diprotodon*, the marsupial lion *Thylacoleo*, giant kangaroos, and large flightless birds — disappeared within a few millennia of human arrival. [VERIFY: pending acquisition — Roberts et al. 2001; Saltré et al. 2016 on Australia megafauna timing]
- **Americas** (~13,000 BP): mammoths, mastodons, ground sloths, glyptodonts, saber-toothed cats, dire wolves, North American horses, and camels disappeared shortly after Clovis-era human expansion. The Late Pleistocene extinction removed approximately 70% of large mammal genera in North America. [VERIFY: pending acquisition — Martin 1973; Surovell et al. 2016; recent dating work]
- **Madagascar** (~2,000 BP): giant lemurs, elephant birds (*Aepyornis*), pygmy hippos, and giant tortoises went extinct after human arrival. [VERIFY: pending acquisition — Burney et al. 2004; Crowley 2010]
- **New Zealand** (~700 BP): nine species of moa, Haast's eagle, and many other endemic birds went extinct within a few centuries of Polynesian settlement. [VERIFY: pending acquisition — Holdaway and Jacomb 2000; Perry et al. 2014]

The causal mechanism remains debated between "overkill" hypotheses (direct human predation as primary cause) and climate-change hypotheses (Pleistocene-Holocene transition as primary cause). The current consensus, in broad strokes, is *combined causation* — climate change set the conditions of vulnerability, but human arrival was the new variable each landmass introduced, and the timing correlation with human arrival is too consistent across continents and islands to attribute to climate alone. [VERIFY: pending acquisition — Koch and Barnosky 2006 review; Stuart 2015 update; Sandom et al. 2014 on global pattern]

**Squeeze diagnosis:** the human cognitive revolution produced a *proto-world* — a mode of artificiality dense enough to disturb planetary manifestations even at hunter-gatherer scale. Megafaunal Animal OSes had evolved against non-human predator pressures; the cooperatively-hunting, fire-using, weapon-armed human was an *incommensurable* predator type whose pressure could not be answered by the megafauna's inherited capacities. F1+F2 obtained at species scale; collapse-endpoint was extinction.

### §2.2 — Wave 2: Holocene agricultural era (~10,000 BP – ~1500 CE)

The second wave begins with the domestication of plants and animals and the establishment of agricultural civilizations.

- The aurochs (*Bos primigenius*), the wild ancestor of domestic cattle, went extinct in 1627 after several millennia of habitat loss and hunting pressure. [VERIFY: pending acquisition — Van Vuure 2005]
- Wild boar populations were extirpated from much of Western Europe by the medieval period.
- Megafaunal species in China, India, and the Mediterranean basin underwent severe range contractions; many local extirpations followed.
- Forest cover in the Mediterranean basin, China's lowlands, and Europe's plains was substantially reduced through agricultural conversion.

The mechanism is different from Wave 1: rather than direct predation, the squeeze operates through *habitat conversion*. Agricultural landscapes replaced wild ecosystems at landscape scale; the human-created artificial ecosystem (cropland, pasture, settled village, road network) became dominant; wild Animal OSes with less viable territory faced declining population trajectories. Some species adapted as *commensals* (rats, sparrows, house mice, crows, foxes); most could not adapt to the artificial ecosystem's selection criteria.

**Squeeze diagnosis:** the agricultural mode of artificiality (settled cultivation, domesticated livestock, urban concentration) constituted a *more developed* artificial ecosystem than hunter-gatherer landscape modification. The incommensurability of selection criteria deepened. F1+F2 intensified; collapse-endpoint (extinction) became the trajectory for many species over centuries-to-millennia timescales.

### §2.3 — Wave 3: Colonial / early industrial era (~1500 – ~1900)

The third wave begins with global colonial trade and accelerates with industrial production.

- The dodo (*Raphus cucullatus*) on Mauritius (extinct 1681) was driven to extinction by Dutch sailors' hunting, deforestation, and the predation of introduced rats, pigs, dogs, and macaques on dodo eggs and nests.
- The great auk (*Pinguinus impennis*) was hunted to extinction across the North Atlantic by 1844.
- The passenger pigeon (*Ectopistes migratorius*), once the most abundant bird in North America with population estimates of 3–5 billion, was hunted to extinction by 1914.
- Steller's sea cow (*Hydrodamalis gigas*) was hunted to extinction within 27 years of European discovery (1741–1768).
- The Tasmanian tiger (*Thylacinus cynocephalus*) was driven to extinction by 1936 through bounty-hunting and habitat conversion.

The mechanism combines mass-scale commercial exploitation (whaling, fur trade, market hunting) with *global biotic translocation* — colonial trade routes carried European, Asian, and African commensals (rats, cats, pigs, goats) to islands and continents where endemic Animal OSes had no evolutionary preparation for them. Island endemics, in particular, faced novel predator pressures their inherited Animal OSes could not answer.

**Squeeze diagnosis:** the colonial mode of artificiality globalized the artificial ecosystem. Previously isolated universe-originated niches were now subject to human-translocated species the endemics' Animal OSes had not co-evolved with. F1+F2 became *globally pervasive*; few terrestrial environments remained outside the artificial-ecosystem's reach.

### §2.4 — Wave 4: Industrial / Anthropocene proper (~1900 – present)

The fourth wave is the wave we are currently in — what is commonly called the *Sixth Mass Extinction* (Kolbert 2014, after Leakey and Lewin 1995). [VERIFY: pending acquisition — Kolbert *The Sixth Extinction* 2014]

Several quantitative benchmarks document the wave:

- **Extinction rate**: the modern extinction rate for vertebrates is estimated at 100–1,000 times the long-term background rate. [VERIFY: pending acquisition — Pimm et al. 2014 *Science* "The biodiversity of species and their rates of extinction, distribution, and protection"; De Vos et al. 2015; Ceballos et al. 2015]
- **Population declines**: the *Living Planet Index* documents approximately a 69% decline in monitored vertebrate populations from 1970 to 2018. [VERIFY: pending acquisition — WWF *Living Planet Report 2022*]
- **Species threatened**: the IPBES *Global Assessment* (2019) estimates that approximately 1 million species are threatened with extinction, many within decades. [VERIFY: pending acquisition — IPBES 2019 Global Assessment Summary for Policymakers]
- **Insect biomass loss**: Hallmann et al. (2017) documented a 75% decline in flying insect biomass in German protected areas over 27 years (1989–2016). [VERIFY: pending acquisition — Hallmann et al. 2017 *PLOS ONE*]
- **Biomass replacement**: Bar-On et al. (2018) calculated that wild terrestrial mammalian biomass is now approximately 4% of total mammalian biomass; humans plus livestock constitute approximately 96%. The proportion was inverted within historical memory. [VERIFY: pending acquisition — Bar-On, Phillips, Milo 2018 *PNAS* "The biomass distribution on Earth"]

The mechanism is multifactorial: habitat destruction and fragmentation; climate change (now a leading driver); invasive species; pollution; overexploitation; emerging infectious disease. Climate change is structurally distinctive within Wave 4 because it represents the human worlds *disturbing planetary manifestations of universe-laws* at scale — the universe-side of the squeeze, in the sense developed in §VI.1 §3.

**Squeeze diagnosis:** the industrial mode of artificiality intensified habitat conversion, added chemical pollution at planetary scale, and crucially began to alter the universe-manifestations themselves (climate, atmosphere, ocean chemistry). F1+F2 operates not only through artificial-ecosystem encroachment on universe-originated niches but increasingly through the disturbance of the universe-manifestations within which all species' niches are embedded. The squeeze tightens at both poles simultaneously.

### §2.5 — Wave 5: Digital / metaverse-stage acceleration (current → projected)

The fifth wave is in formation and projected to peak in the mid-21st century.

The mechanism integrates the prior four waves with new acceleration vectors:

- Data center power demand intensifies fossil-fuel emissions and water consumption.
- Rare earth mining for digital infrastructure destroys habitat at extraction sites.
- AI-optimized agriculture, fishing, and forestry extract more efficiently — increasing extraction rates per unit of human labor.
- Climate-driven tipping cascades (Lenton et al. 2019, in NLM Collapse notebook) project mid-21st-century peaks for climate-driven extinction.
- Algorithmic governance of land-use decisions accelerates and standardizes extraction patterns globally.

**Squeeze diagnosis:** the metaverse-stage of human artificiality does not displace the prior waves' mechanisms — it intensifies and accelerates them. The squeeze on non-human species continues to tighten, and is projected to peak in extinction rate during the lifetime of contemporary readers. The same metaverse-stage that imposes the F1+F2 condition on humans for the first time is simultaneously the wave whose impact on non-human species is most severe.

## §3 — When does the squeeze start, when does it peak?

The empirical record supports a clear narrative on timing.

**When did the animal squeeze begin?** Approximately 50,000 BP, with the first hominid populations capable of cooperatively-hunting, fire-using, weapon-armed niche construction at densities sufficient to destabilize universe-originated niches.

**When did it become global?** Approximately 1500 CE, with colonial-era biotic homogenization linking previously isolated landmasses and island ecosystems into a single human-mediated translocation network.

**When did the extinction rate peak?** This depends on the taxon and the metric:
- For Late Pleistocene megafauna, the rate peaked during each landmass's Wave 1 episode.
- For total species, the rate is currently rising and projected to peak in the mid-21st century under business-as-usual climate trajectories.
- For specific taxa (large mammals, amphibians, coral reef organisms, freshwater fish), peak rates may be ongoing or near-future.

**When did biomass replacement peak?** Now. The Bar-On et al. (2018) data place the wild-vertebrate biomass at approximately 4% of mammalian biomass; the inversion from a wild-dominated biosphere to a human-and-livestock-dominated biosphere is complete in mammalian terms and approaching completion in avian terms (domesticated poultry now dominates avian biomass globally).

**When will the squeeze peak for non-human species?** Projected to be mid-21st century, primarily driven by climate-change cascades on top of established habitat-fragmentation, invasive-species, and pollution pressures.

**When will the squeeze peak for humans?** This is the prospective question §VI.4 and §VI.7 take up.

## §4 — The structural lesson: F1+F2 squeezes do not always survive themselves

The point of the empirical record is structural, not merely descriptive. The animal squeeze record establishes three things relevant to the thesis's argument:

### §4.1 — F1+F2 is the structural form of many adaptive failures

A squeeze in the F1+F2 sense is not unique to the metaverse-stage human predicament. It is the structural form that many adaptive failures take when an organism's inherited capacities are calibrated for one environment but its immediate survival increasingly depends on another whose criteria are incommensurable. The thesis's contribution is not to identify a structurally novel phenomenon but to (i) name the structural condition precisely (F1+F2), (ii) demonstrate that humans now face this condition for the first time as a species, and (iii) specify the mechanism (the metaverse-stage threshold per Ch-5-3 v3 + Ch-5-5) by which humans came to face it.

### §4.2 — Symmetric F1+F2 squeezes tend toward extinction-class endpoints

Across the five waves of anthropogenic extinction, the species that went extinct were those whose squeeze was *symmetric* — those who could neither retreat to the universe-originated niche (humans had altered it) nor inhabit the artificial ecosystem (Animal OS incompatible). Symmetric F1+F2 squeezes lack a stable fallback substrate on either side. They tend, when not resolved by recognition and structural intervention, toward the collapse-endpoint of extinction.

§VI.5 will develop the symmetric-vs-asymmetric distinction in detail. The point here is that the animal record is the *empirical precedent* for what symmetric F1+F2 squeezes do when they play out. They do not produce recoverable transitions; they produce extinction.

### §4.3 — Humans are not exempt from the structural form

Humans are uniquely positioned in evolutionary history as the apex niche-constructor species — the species whose mode of artificiality is most disruptive of other species' universe-originated niches. Until the metaverse-stage, this position has been one of *causing* F1+F2 squeezes for other species while not experiencing one ourselves. The metaverse-stage is when this asymmetry breaks. Humans, having constructed the artificial ecosystem so thoroughly that it acquires the structural status of a second environment for our own species, now face the same F1+F2 condition we have imposed on other species since the late Pleistocene.

The thesis's distinctive claim about the metaverse-stage is therefore *not* that the squeeze is unprecedented in nature. It is that humans now face it. The structural form is identical to what the animal record documents; the species identity of the squeezed is what is new.
