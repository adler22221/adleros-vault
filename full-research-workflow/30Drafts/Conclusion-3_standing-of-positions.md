## Justification: Roy Bhaskar

### Who Bhaskar Is

Roy Bhaskar (1944–2014) was a British philosopher, Principal Research Fellow at the Institute of Education, University of London, and the founding theorist of *critical realism* — a major tradition in the philosophy of science and social science. His foundational texts include *A Realist Theory of Science* (1975) and *The Possibility of Naturalism* (1979), which established the critical realist framework as a systematic alternative to both positivism (which collapses the real to the empirical) and constructivism (which collapses the real to the conceptual).

His *Dialectic: The Pulse of Freedom* (2008, CDY2DJWM — confirmed in Zotero) and *Plato Etc: The Problems of Philosophy and their Resolution* (2009, 88YLH48Y — confirmed in Zotero) extend the framework to social ontology and the history of philosophy respectively.

### Why Bhaskar's Position Is Reliable

Critical realism is now one of the major frameworks in the philosophy of social science and is used across sociology, economics, education studies, health sciences, and interdisciplinary research. Its core commitments — stratified ontology, the real/actual/empirical distinction, retroduction as method — are defensible on their own philosophical merits (they are internally consistent and provide genuine advantages over their competitors) and are widely applied in the social science literature.

The thesis uses Bhaskar's stratified ontology for two specific purposes:
1. The real/actual distinction (Ch-4-1): to argue that the metaverse's causal mechanism is real and operative before fully actualized
2. Retroduction (Introduction-6): as the method by which the thesis infers from observed phenomena to underlying structural mechanisms

Both uses are standard applications of Bhaskar's framework and can be defended by reference to his texts and the secondary literature on critical realism.

### Why Bhaskar Rather Than Alternatives

**Rather than phenomenology** (Husserl, Heidegger, Merleau-Ponty): The thesis draws on phenomenology where appropriate (Stiegler, who is post-Husserlian; the concept of the "world" is phenomenologically inflected). But phenomenology's methodological focus on first-person experience is insufficient for the thesis's structural claim about emergent environmental conditions that operate whether or not any individual experiences or names them. Critical realism's distinction between the real (operating independently of observation) and the empirical (observed) is exactly what the thesis needs to argue that the squeeze is structurally real before it is fully experienced.

**Rather than pragmatism** (Dewey, Peirce): Pragmatism's criterion of truth by practical consequence is useful for policy analysis but not for the ontological distinction between universe and metaverse that the thesis requires. The thesis needs to say that the universe's laws are real independently of what proves useful — a realist rather than pragmatist claim.

**Rather than Kantian critical philosophy**: Kant's transcendental idealism would locate the universe's structural features in the conditions of possible experience rather than in mind-independent reality — which is exactly what the thesis's realist claim about the universe resists.

**Recommended footnote for Bhaskar:**

> *Roy Bhaskar (1944–2014) is the founding theorist of critical realism, a major tradition in the philosophy of science and social science developed from *A Realist Theory of Science* (1975). The thesis draws on his stratified ontology (the distinction between the real, the actual, and the empirical) and on retroduction as the method appropriate to structural-environmental analysis. Critical realism's meta-theoretical commitments are used because they are specifically suited to the thesis's task: arguing that a structural mechanism (the squeeze) is real and causally operative before being fully empirically manifest — a claim that positivist frameworks (which collapse the real to the observable) and constructivist frameworks (which collapse the real to the conceptual) cannot accommodate.*

## Justification: Daisuke Uegaki (上岡克己)

### Who Uegaki Is

Uegaki Katsumi (上岡克己) is a Japanese sociologist and cultural theorist, known primarily in Japanese academic contexts for his analysis of contemporary Japanese social transformations. His work on "自己完結社会" (self-finishing/self-enclosed society) and the five historical periods of postwar Japanese social development provides the thesis's §IV with its historical narrative of the progression toward life-as-willed and the emergence of the "Drifter" (漂流人) as the social-psychological subject of the squeeze. ### Why Uegaki's Position Is Reliable

**Context-specific expertise**: Uegaki's analysis is specifically Japanese, and §IV's argument is about the Japanese context of self-finishing and life-as-willed in the postwar period. A Japanese sociologist analyzing Japanese postwar social transformation is the most appropriate kind of expert for this specific claim — more so than a Western social theorist whose analysis of equivalent transformations in European or American contexts would require significant translation.

**Empirical grounding**: Uegaki's five-period historical schema is based on documented sociological trends in Japanese society (labor market transformation, demographic change, the emergence of hikikomori and the Drifter social type, the shift from group-oriented to individual-oriented social norms) rather than on purely theoretical speculation. The factual accuracy of his periodization can be assessed against other sources on Japanese social history of the postwar period.

**Corroborated claims**: The core social trends Uegaki identifies — the atomization of Japanese social life, the emergence of the self-enclosed individual, the withdrawal from social obligation — are corroborated by a substantial literature in Japanese social science and sociology (cf. the extensive literature on hikikomori, the "herbivore men" phenomenon, the *kodawari* (obsessive specificity) culture of individual consumption). The thesis is not relying solely on Uegaki but using his analytical framework to organize well-documented social trends.

**Recommended footnote for Uegaki:**

> *Uegaki's sociological analysis of postwar Japanese social transformation provides the historical framework for §IV's argument about the emergence of life-as-willed as a social ideal. Uegaki is a Japanese sociologist whose work is primarily addressed to Japanese academic readers; the thesis uses his five-period periodization because it provides the most systematic available analysis of the specific social transformations (atomization, self-enclosure, the emergence of the Drifter social type) that §IV analyzes. The factual claims underlying his periodization — labor market transformation, demographic change, the shifts in family structure and social obligation — are well-documented in the Japanese sociological literature and not dependent on Uegaki alone.*
