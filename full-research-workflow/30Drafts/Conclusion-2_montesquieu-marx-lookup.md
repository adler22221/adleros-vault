## Part A: Montesquieu — Liberty as Balance of Power

### The Framework

Montesquieu's *The Spirit of Laws* (1748) establishes the framework of political liberty as *freedom from the arbitrary exercise of power*. In Book XI, "Of Laws Which Establish Political Liberty, With Regard to the Constitution," he argues:

> "Political liberty in a citizen is that tranquility of mind arising from the opinion each person has of his safety. In order to have this liberty, it is requisite the government be so constituted as one man need not be afraid of another."

— Montesquieu, *The Spirit of Laws*, Book XI, Chapter 6 [NOTE: verify page against print edition]

The guarantee of this liberty is the *separation of powers*:

> "In every government there are three sorts of power: the legislative; the executive in respect to things dependent on the law of nations; and the executive in respect to matters that depend on the civil law. [...] There would be an end of every thing, were the same man or the same body, whether of the nobles or of the people, to exercise those three powers, that of enacting laws, that of executing the public resolutions, and of trying the causes of individuals."

— Montesquieu, *The Spirit of Laws*, Book XI, Chapter 6 [NOTE: verify page]

The principle that follows:

> "To prevent this abuse, it is necessary from the very nature of things that power should be a check to power."

— Montesquieu, *The Spirit of Laws*, Book XI, Chapter 4 [NOTE: verify exact chapter/page]

### The Montesquieuan Response to Big Tech

As the thesis notes, the regulatory response to the metaverse/digital platform threat has been largely *Montesquieuan*: the EU's GDPR, Digital Markets Act, and AI Act seek to impose *institutional checks* on the executive power of large technology platforms — treating platform power as analogous to an unchecked sovereign and applying the logic of constitutional balance.

This is not wrong. Montesquieu correctly diagnoses that concentrated power without checks is dangerous to individual liberty. The GDPR approach — requiring consent, creating data subject rights, empowering national supervisory authorities — is structurally a separation-of-powers intervention.

### Why This Is Insufficient for the Thesis's Argument

The Montesquieuan framework addresses *political liberty* — the freedom not to be arbitrarily coerced by power. But the squeeze the thesis describes is not primarily about coercion. The self-completing tendency of the metaverse does not (primarily) threaten freedom in the sense of external constraint; it threatens the *ontological preconditions* of having a world at all. A person can freely choose to enter the metaverse and freely choose all its offerings — without any violation of Montesquieuan liberty — while the "world" in the thesis's sense (the shared meaning-space nested within the universe) is nonetheless being displaced.

Put differently: GDPR can protect data subjects from unauthorized surveillance. It cannot protect the *lived world* from being replaced by a neo-artificial environment that is simply chosen, freely and repeatedly, by billions of users. The squeeze is a structural feature of the relationship between environments, not a violation of rights.

## Part B: Marx — Liberation as Change of Ownership

### The Framework

Karl Marx's *Capital* (Vol. 1, 1867) diagnoses the fundamental pathology of capitalist society as *alienation through the commodity form and the labor relation*. The worker's labor-power becomes a commodity that the capitalist buys and deploys; the worker is alienated from the product, the process, the species-being, and other humans. The relevant passages include:

On alienated labor (from the 1844 Manuscripts, foundational for Capital):
> "The worker becomes poorer the more wealth he produces, the more his production increases in power and extent. The worker becomes an ever cheaper commodity the more commodities he creates."

On commodity fetishism (*Capital*, Vol. 1, Ch. 1, §4):
> "A commodity appears at first sight an extremely obvious, trivial thing. But its analysis brings out that it is a very strange thing, abounding in metaphysical subtleties and theological niceties."

[NOTE: These are close to Marx's text but verify exact wording against a standard edition — e.g., Penguin/Ben Fowkes translation (1976), or the Moscow Progress edition.]

On real subsumption of labor (*Capital*, Vol. 1, Ch. 15, "Machinery and Large-Scale Industry"):
> "By means of its conversion into an automaton, the instrument of labour confronts the labourer, during the labour-process, in the shape of capital, of dead labour, that dominates, and pumps dry, living labour-power."

[NOTE: Ch. 15, §1 — verify against print]

### The Marxist Response to Platform Capitalism

As the thesis notes, the progressive-left response to digital capitalism has been largely *Marxist*: the problem is that platform companies extract value from user-generated data without compensation, concentrate ownership, and use algorithmic systems to exploit cognitive vulnerabilities. The proposed solutions — data cooperatives, data trusts, platform nationalization, algorithmic regulation — are structurally Marxist: change the *ownership structure* and *class relation* around data, and the exploitation will cease.

This too is not wrong. Platform capitalism does extract value asymmetrically; the labor theory of value can be extended to explain why data workers receive zero compensation for the data they generate.

### Why This Is Insufficient for the Thesis's Argument

The Marxist framework addresses *exploitation* — the unjust extraction of surplus from those who perform productive labor. But the squeeze the thesis describes is not primarily about exploitation. Even a *post-capitalist* platform — one owned cooperatively by its users, with equitable data revenue sharing — would still generate the same ontological squeeze if it operates by the same logic of world-completion.

The thesis's argument is not that users are exploited by the metaverse but that they are *adapted* to it in the evolutionary sense: the metaverse imposes adaptational pressures regardless of who owns it. A collectively owned metaverse with perfectly equitable governance would still displace vernacular world-making if it achieved the same degree of "self-completion." This is an evolutionary/environmental argument, not an economic one.

Put differently: if the metaverse were perfectly just — no surveillance, no exploitation, fair wages for content creators — the squeeze would remain, because the squeeze is a function of the *environment*, not of its ownership structure.

## Recommended Synthesis for Conclusion

> This thesis does not dispute the validity of political and economic critiques of the emerging metaverse. Montesquieu's framework of liberty-as-balance-of-power correctly diagnoses that the executive power of platform corporations poses a threat to individual freedom, and the regulatory responses this insight has generated — from the European Union's GDPR to the Digital Markets Act — represent genuine if incomplete remedies. Marx's framework of exploitation correctly identifies the asymmetric extraction of value from users whose cognitive labor generates platform revenue without compensation; the data-cooperative movement represents a structurally coherent response within this framework.
>
> What this thesis argues is that these frameworks, however adequate to their respective diagnoses, do not exhaust the threat. Political liberty — freedom from coercion — can be perfectly secured while the lived world is simultaneously displaced by a neo-artificial environment chosen freely by its inhabitants. Economic justice — equitable ownership and revenue distribution — can be perfectly achieved by a collectively owned platform that still operates by the logic of world-completion. The squeeze this thesis describes operates at the ontological-environmental level, below the political and economic: it is the evolutionary pressure that a new background environment imposes on the organisms that inhabit it, regardless of that environment's constitutional or ownership structure.
