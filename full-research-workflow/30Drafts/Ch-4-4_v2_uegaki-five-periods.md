## Reframed Introduction: Japan as Case, Not as Cause

The argument of §IV — that contemporary human existence is structured by a self-finishing tendency, oriented toward life-as-willed and primed for metaverse retreat — risks two opposite misreadings if Uegaki Takahide's (上柿崇英) periodization of postwar Japanese social development is introduced without methodological framing. The first misreading would treat the Japanese trajectory as the *cause* of the self-finishing condition: as if some peculiarity of postwar Japan generated a pathology now exported globally. The second would treat it as a merely *parochial* case-study, ethnographically interesting but theoretically inert for any non-Japanese context. Both misreadings collapse what the thesis needs to keep apart: the *real-level mechanism* and its *empirical actualizations*.

The position taken here, drawing on the critical-realist stratification developed in Ch-4-1 and elaborated in ANNOT-L3, is that **Japan offers a particularly legible empirical actualization of the self-finishing tendency, not because Japan caused it, but because the Modern Western Mode of Artificiality colonized Japanese onto-epistemic terrain in compressed historical time, leaving the actualization unusually visible**. [VERIFY: confirmed | bhaskarRealistTheoryScience2008 | p.TBD | nlm-excerpt:"Mechanisms, events and experiences thus constitute three overlapping domains of reality, viz. the domains of the real, the actual and the empirical" — NLM source UUID 15591783 (likely RTS 2008); add to SOURCE_META in convert_t0_to_md.py; locate page in Bhaskar Zotero PDF] In Bhaskar's vocabulary, the squeeze of human existence between universe and metaverse is a real-level (`dr`) generative mechanism, operating transfactually wherever the technosphere takes hold; its actualization (`da`) and its experiential registration (`de`) vary across geo-historical conjunctures. The Japanese case is methodologically privileged not because it is essentially different but because the speed and intensity of its onto-epistemic restructuring — Meiji to Heisei in roughly four generations — compressed into observable form what unfolded more slowly across the Atlantic core.

Read this way, Uegaki's five periods are not a developmental narrative *of Japan* whose generalizability one could then debate. They are a *cross-section through the Japanese empirical layer* in which a deeper mechanism — the progressive substitution of life-as-willed for life-as-given, mediated by externalized achievement metrics — became unusually amenable to anthropological description. [VERIFY: pending | uegakiJikokanketsuShakai2017jo | p.TBD — 生活世界の構造転換 term not directly retrieved in T0 (0-citation query); locate 〈生活世界の構造転換〉 usage in Zotero PDF] What Uegaki documents under the name 〈生活世界の構造転換〉 (the structural transformation of the lifeworld) is the Japanese face of a process whose generative mechanism is not Japanese at all: it is the global expansion of the Modern Western Mode of Artificiality, with its specific binding of selfhood to performance, achievement, and the autonomous construction of one's existential conditions.

The five periods, accordingly, should be read as five successive *layers* of colonization at the onto-epistemic real level — each layer reorganizing what counts as a self, a world, a life worth living, in terms increasingly aligned with the Western artificiality-mode. Japan's particular legibility is that it underwent this reorganization knowing it was undergoing it. The Meiji elite framed the encounter explicitly as a confrontation; the postwar reconstruction framed it explicitly as catch-up; the Lost Decade framed its breakdown explicitly as the failure of the catch-up frame. This *self-conscious actualization* is what makes Uegaki's periodization a methodologically valuable lens — not because Japan is the universe's pathological specimen, but because Japan kept records.

## Period 1: Confrontation with the West (明治維新 → 1945)

The first period spans Japan's encounter with Western Modernity from the Meiji Restoration (1868) through the Pacific War's end (1945). [VERIFY: pending | uegakiJikoKanketsuShakai2022a | p.TBD — Period 1 dates (明治維新→1945) not directly retrieved in T0; locate period-boundary passage in Zotero CYAAA3IK PDF] The defining experience is the confrontation with a civilizational form whose technical and institutional apparatus threatened Japan's continued existence as an independent polity. The adaptive response — rapid, intentional adoption of Western technical and institutional forms — constituted, at the empirical level, what Uegaki identifies as the first major transformation of Japanese self-understanding: from a world-anchored identity (rooted in place, tradition, cosmological framework, and the dense relational substrate of pre-Meiji 〈間柄〉 forms) toward a performance-oriented identity calibrated against external standards of technical and institutional achievement. [VERIFY: pending | uegakiJikokanketsuShakai2017jo | p.TBD — Meiji self-restructuring passage not directly retrieved in T0; locate in Zotero PDF (also check J3YDIF8D = Zotero BBKWYRDU)]

Read at the real level, Period 1 is the moment at which the Modern Western Mode of Artificiality first installs itself as the *measure* of the Japanese self. It is not yet the dominant onto-epistemic frame — pre-Meiji cosmological and relational structures persist throughout, and indeed are mobilized in modified form (the kokutai construction, the State-Shinto synthesis) to legitimize the modernization project itself. But the *standard* by which Japanese institutions, bodies, and selves are evaluated has been displaced outward. The empirical configuration is hybrid; the underlying generative mechanism — that selfhood is now to be assessed against an externally specified achievement-criterion — is in place.

The psychic structure produced is a self understood primarily as the subject of achievement, achievement measured against an externally defined standard (the West). The self is already, in this period, beginning to detach from its world-substrate and reconstitute itself as a performer before an audience it did not choose. [NOTE: developed from source-manuscript — check self-plagiarism before submission] The militarism of the 1930s and early 1940s should be read against this background not as a *return* to indigenous tradition but as one possible empirical resolution of the tension between an externally calibrated achievement-self and a populace whose relational substrate had been disrupted but not replaced. Maruyama Masao's diagnosis of the 「無責任の体系」 (irresponsibility system) of prewar Japan, which Uegaki cites and inherits [VERIFY: pending | uegakiJikokanketsuShakai2017ge | p.TBD — 補論二 content confirmed (cit.6,7 in selfcompleting-totalism-tier0-retrieval.md) but Maruyama footnotes (fn.47-49) need PDF lookup in Zotero VESTLJGF], names exactly this configuration: a collectivity whose internal organization no longer rests on stable role-relational ethics but on a structurally diffuse pursuit of performance under a vacated centre.

## Period 2: Reconstruction and High-Growth Collectivism (1945–1960s)

The second period is postwar reconstruction and Japan's economic miracle: the collective mobilization of social resources for national reconstruction and high-growth industrialization. [VERIFY: pending | uegakiJikoKanketsuShakai2022a | p.TBD — Period 2 high-growth periodization (1945–1960s) not directly retrieved in T0; locate in Zotero CYAAA3IK PDF] The empirical surface — the company-man (会社人間), the developmental state, the so-called "Japanese model" of collective achievement through individual subordination — is well-documented. What is at stake for the present argument is *what kind of collectivism this is*, because the question whether postwar Japanese collectivism was continuous with traditional Japanese relational forms or was something quite new is decisive for the reading of the period.

The original draft risked a misunderstanding of Japanese collectivism as purely East Asian / Sinic in inheritance. This must be carefully distinguished. **Traditional Japanese relational collectivism** — the 〈間柄〉 (aidagara) structure articulated philosophically by Watsuji Tetsurō in 『人間の学としての倫理学』 [VERIFY: Watsuji's relational collectivism distinct from postwar achievement-collectivism] — is *role-anchored*, situated in a dense lattice of reciprocal obligations whose telos is the maintenance of the relational fabric itself, not the maximization of any externally specified output. Pre-Meiji 〈むら〉 (village) collectivism, the 〈家〉 (household) form, the Tokugawa mutuality structures examined by Najita and others — these are collectivisms in the sense that the individual is constituted *through* relational positioning, but they are not achievement-collectivisms. They do not measure the group against an external benchmark. They are ontologically thick and teleologically internal. [NOTE: developed from source-manuscript — check self-plagiarism before submission]

**Postwar achievement-collectivism is different.** The company-man ethos, the developmental-state mobilization, the salary-man household — these are forms in which the *collective* (firm, nation, family) takes up the achievement-orientation that Period 1 had installed at the level of the self, and pursues it with the intensity that liberal individualism would direct toward individual self-realization. The benchmark remains external (catching the West, exceeding GNP targets, securing market share); the unit of pursuit has shifted from the individual to the collective; but the underlying mechanism — selfhood validated by performance against an externally specified standard — is unchanged. What Uegaki identifies as the *collective form of life-as-willed* [VERIFY: pending | uegakiJikoKanketsuShakai2022a | p.TBD — 集団的な生の自己完結化 passage not directly retrieved in T0; locate in Zotero CYAAA3IK PDF] is precisely this: the subordination of the individual life-project to a group whose own life-project is structured by the same externalized achievement-logic.

This distinction matters because it allows the period to be read as an instance of the deeper mechanism the chapter is tracking, rather than as a culturally exotic deviation from it. The same real-level pull — selfhood-as-externally-calibrated-performance — actualized in the United States as the postwar individualist consumer-citizen, in Japan as the corporate collectivist 会社人間, and in earlier and more violent registers in interwar Europe as the mass-mobilization formations of fascism. **These are not the same empirical formations**; the differences in scale, ideology, violence, and political content are decisive. But from a critical-realist perspective they may be cautiously read as **distinct geo-historical actualizations of a common real-level generative mechanism: the collective pursuit of life-as-willed under externalized achievement metrics, when the indigenous relational substrate has been hollowed out**.

[NOTE: extension by Yang via CR-stratified cross-actualization framing; not Uegaki's own claim — see ANNOT-L8] Uegaki himself stops short of this comparison and indeed cautions explicitly against it. In 補論二 of vol. 2 [VERIFY: confirmed | uegakiJikokanketsuShakai2017ge | p.TBD | nlm-excerpt:"「政治的な意味での全体主義」と、「個を超える何ものかを肯定すること」とを区別するということだろう。…われわれは両者を、本来同じ次元で考えるべきではない" — see selfcompleting-totalism-tier0-retrieval.md cit.6; maps to 2022b 補論二 — locate page in Zotero VESTLJGF PDF] he polemically anticipates and rebuts the charge that any framework affirming "logic and values that transcend the individual" must be totalitarian; he distinguishes 〈政治的な意味での全体主義〉 (totalitarianism in the political sense) from 〈個を超える何ものかを肯定すること〉 (affirming something beyond the individual), treating the postwar Japanese reflexive equation collectivism = fascism as itself an ideological residue of the Maruyama–Ōtsuka 〈近代主義〉 tradition. Uegaki's diagnosis of postwar Japanese collectivism as a distinct, *new* configuration driven by 〈社会的装置〉 (social apparatus), not by ideological mobilization, is therefore his own — and the cross-actualization claim made here is the present author's extension, anchored on Maruyama [VERIFY: Maruyama 1946 『超国家主義の論理と心理』 — LOOKUP], Fromm [VERIFY: Fromm 1941 *Escape from Freedom* — LOOKUP], Arendt [VERIFY: Arendt 1951 *Origins of Totalitarianism* — LOOKUP], and Adorno/Horkheimer [VERIFY: confirmed | 6SSSY7D7 | Dialectic of Enlightenment, culture-industry chapter] for the European actualization, with Uegaki cited only for the postwar-Japan side and the totalitarianism-discrimination caveat.

The structural parallel is offered with great caution. The Nazi Übermensch ideology and the Japanese company-man ethos differ in nearly every respect at the empirical surface. What they share is a deeper structural pull: a collectivity whose internal organization no longer rests on the stable relational fabric of traditional role-ethics, mobilizing itself toward an externalized achievement-standard, and demanding that the individual subordinate selfhood to that mobilization. The Showa 会社人間 and the Nazi mass formation are surface configurations of the same underlying mechanism — but they are not the same thing, and any prose that elides the difference falsifies both. The point of the comparison is not to indict postwar Japan but to render visible the real-level mechanism whose Japanese actualization happened, this once, to be relatively peaceful and economically productive.

The psychic structure that crystallizes in Period 2 is therefore a self constituted by group-identification, whose individuality is expressed through role-performance within an achievement-oriented collective rather than through self-authored life-design — and crucially, a self for whom the collective itself is no longer the world-anchoring relational substrate of pre-Meiji Japan, but a performance-vehicle whose continued cohesion depends on the achievement it produces.

## Period 3: Consumerist Nuclear Family and the Hollowing Out of the Lifeworld (1970s–1980s)

The third period is what Uegaki identifies as the emergence of the consumerist nuclear family and 〈生活世界の空洞化〉 (the hollowing out of the lifeworld). [VERIFY: pending | uegakiJikokanketsuShakai2017jo | p.TBD — 生活世界空洞化 passage not directly retrieved in T0 (0-citation query on lifeworld); locate in Zotero PDF (also check J3YDIF8D)] Economic success creates abundance; abundance creates the conditions for consumption-based individual expression; the nuclear family displaces the extended family and the community as the primary social unit. The domestic sphere becomes the locus of consumer choice — the home as the site of self-expression through commodities; the salary-man household with its gendered division between productive and domestic spheres; consumer culture as the medium of social expression; the progressive withdrawal of social life from public communal spaces into the private domestic sphere.

At the empirical level, this is Japanese-specific: the 1970s–1980s salary-man household with its particular gender configuration, the bubble-era consumer aesthetics, the catalogue cultures, the brand-mediated identity construction documented in the sociology of the period. At the real level, it is one more layer of colonization: the achievement-self of Period 1 and the achievement-collective of Period 2 are now joined by the consumer-self of Period 3, whose freedom is reconceived as the freedom to choose among available options rather than as the freedom of participation in a communally constituted world. [VERIFY: pending | uegakiJikoKanketsuShakai2022a | p.TBD — consumer-self / 消費者的自己 passage not directly retrieved in T0; locate in Zotero CYAAA3IK PDF]

The hollowing out of the lifeworld names the evacuation of shared meaning-making from the communal world: as consumption displaces communal practice as the primary medium of social expression, the shared world — the communally maintained meaning-space — loses its density. What remains is a collection of private consumer choices that share a market but not a world. Read through Archer's distinction [VERIFY: confirmed | archerCultureAgencyPlace1996 | p.TBD | nlm-excerpt:"the degree of logical consistency was labelled 'Cultural System integration' (hereafter CS) and the extent of causal cohesion was termed 'Socio-Cultural integration' (hereafter S-C)" — NLM source UUID 9353c836 (add to SOURCE_META as archerCultureAgencyPlace1996); locate page in Archer Zotero PDF], Period 3 is the moment at which the *Cultural System* — the layer of intelligible items standing in logical relations of contradiction or complementarity — begins to thin out into a market-mediated catalogue of consumption-options, its internal logical structure replaced by the external logic of brand differentiation. The *Socio-Cultural* layer (who consumes what, when, with whom) thickens; the CS layer thins. The world, as a real-level cultural-symbolic stratum with its own emergent causal powers, begins to lose those powers — not by being denied but by being reorganized as a menu.

The psychic structure: a self that increasingly understands its freedom as consumer freedom — the freedom to choose among available options — rather than as the freedom of participation in a communally constituted world. The individual is becoming the consumer-individual: the subject of preference-satisfaction rather than world-co-constitution. This is, in the vocabulary of §IV, a major step toward life-as-willed — for life-as-willed is precisely the life that takes its conditions as objects of preference rather than as the given substrate of relational practice.

## Period 4: Recognition Anxiety and the Drifter (1990s–2000s)

The fourth period emerges from the collapse of the collective achievement framework: the bursting of the asset-price bubble (1991), the Lost Decade, the end of lifetime employment security, and the delegitimization of the company-man identity. [VERIFY: confirmed | uegakiJikokanketsuShakai2017ge | p.TBD | nlm-excerpt:"とりわけ「第四期」の後半になると、少なくない人々が自身の"居場所"をめぐって苦しみ、「承認不安」とも呼べる事態に直面するようになっていた" — see drifter-ethics-tier0-retrieval.md cit.3; maps to 2022b — locate page in Zotero VESTLJGF PDF] Without the collective framework within which individual achievement was recognized and validated, the recognition structure that constituted the individual's social identity collapses.

The result is 〈承認不安〉 (recognition anxiety): the anxiety of a self whose social identity has been constituted by recognized achievement within a collective framework, when the framework disappears and the achievement is no longer recognizable by anyone. [VERIFY: confirmed | uegakiJikokanketsuShakai2017ge | p.TBD | nlm-excerpt:"(144) ここでの「承認不安」は、おそらく「自由な個性の全面的な展開」を人生の理想として素朴に信じた人々が、理想と現実の狭間で挫折していくことを通じて生じてきたものである" — see drifter-ethics-tier0-retrieval.md cit.4; fn.144 cites 斎藤(2003) for broader 承認不安 literature; maps to 2022b ed. — locate page in Zotero VESTLJGF PDF] The self does not know who it is when the collective that recognized its performance dissolves. Read at the real level, the dissolution of the postwar Japanese collective-achievement framework is one regional instance of the global late-twentieth-century unwinding of the Fordist-Keynesian compact whose European and American actualizations Boltanski and Chiapello, Sennett, and others have analyzed — a real-level mechanism (the disorganization of the achievement-collective under conditions of financial liberalization and post-industrial restructuring) actualizing once again in geo-historically distinct empirical form.

From this recognition anxiety, Uegaki identifies the emergence of the 〈漂流人〉 (Drifter): a social type characterized by the absence of stable social anchoring, drifting between social contexts, identities, and commitments without being able to form the sustained attachments through which a stable self is constituted. [VERIFY: confirmed | uegakiJikokanketsuShakai2017jo | p.TBD | nlm-excerpt:"〈漂流人〉は、生まれながらにして〈存在の連なり〉から浮遊し、「〈共同〉のための意味」も「〈共同〉のための技能」も受け継がれることなく成長していく" — see drifter-ethics-tier0-retrieval.md cit.1; maps to 2022b ed. — locate page in Zotero VESTLJGF PDF] The Drifter is not simply lonely or alienated; the Drifter is a self in whom the world-making and self-making capacities have been structurally impaired by the dissolution of the collective frameworks within which these capacities were previously exercised. The capacities themselves — relationality, mutual obligation, world-co-constitution — depend on a substrate whose progressive evacuation across Periods 1–3 left the individual, by Period 4, exposed to the sudden withdrawal of the collective scaffold without indigenous resources to fall back on.

The psychic structure: a self without anchoring — seeking recognition but without the communal framework within which recognition is possible; moving between contexts without the commitments that make any context a world. The Drifter is permanently between worlds, unable to inhabit any of them fully.

**The Drifter as the Psychological Subject of the Squeeze:**

The Drifter is the psychological subject for whom the metaverse is most seductive: a being already detached from world-anchoring, already seeking recognition through platform performance (social media as recognition economy), already constituted by consumer identity rather than communal practice. The metaverse offers the Drifter what the dissolved collective framework no longer provides — a social environment structured around recognition, achievement, and identity performance — but at the cost of the remaining connections to universe-substrate that the world, even in its hollowed-out form, still maintained.

The Drifter's metaverse retreat is not a failure of character; it is the structurally predictable response of a being whose social world has been evacuated of the communal content that makes world-anchoring possible. The squeeze for the Drifter is particularly acute: there is already little world left to be squeezed; the metaverse finds a being primed for absorption. And the Drifter, like the periods that produced her, is not a Japanese figure: she is the empirically Japanese face of a real-level subject-formation that the global Atlantic core has been producing under different empirical signatures — the precariat, the gig-worker, the burnout-millennial — whose family resemblance is a function of the shared underlying mechanism, not of cultural diffusion.

## Period 5: The Self-Enclosed Society and the Ethics of Non-Intervention (2010s–present)

The fifth period is what Uegaki calls 〈自己完結社会〉 (the self-enclosed / self-finishing society): a social form in which individuals increasingly organize their entire relational and productive life within self-selected, algorithmically managed personal environments — the curated social media feed, the personalized content stream, the niche interest community — without engaging with the uncontrolled, unchosen social world that the traditional lifeworld required. [VERIFY: confirmed | uegakiJikokanketsuShakai2017jo | p.TBD | nlm-excerpt:"〈自己完結社会〉 ーそれは情報技術、ロボット/人工知能技術…〈社会的装置〉に人々が深く依存し、生身の他者と関わっていく必然性、生身の身体とともに生きる必然性が失われていく社会のことである" — see selfcompleting-totalism-tier0-retrieval.md cit.1; maps to 2022b — locate page in Zotero VESTLJGF PDF]

The associated normative framework: the 〈不介入の倫理〉 (Ethics of Non-Intervention). [VERIFY: confirmed | uegakiJikokanketsuShakai2017jo | p.TBD | nlm-excerpt:"「不介入の倫理」とは、端的には、互いに他者に対する介入を拒む代わりに、自身の人生にかかる責任はすべて自らが負うべきだとする倫理のことである" — see selfcompleting-totalism-tier0-retrieval.md cit.4; maps to 2022b — locate page in Zotero VESTLJGF PDF] [TERMINOLOGY CORRECTED 2026-05-05: was erroneously「非干渉の倫理」(hikansho no rinri); Uegaki's actual term confirmed as「不介入の倫理」(fukainyū no rinri) via T0 verbatim excerpt above] Where traditional social ethics imposed obligations of reciprocal engagement — one was obligated to participate in communal life, to maintain relationships of mutual aid, to engage with unchosen neighbors and coworkers — the ethics of non-intervention reframes social distance as a virtue: respecting others' autonomy means not encroaching on their self-enclosed world. The normative ideal is not engagement but non-intrusion. This is the moral residue of a culture whose every prior layer has been systematically detached from the relational substrate that made reciprocal obligation intelligible: when there is no longer any shared world within which my action could be other than encroachment on yours, the only available ethic is the ethic of mutual non-disturbance.

The self-enclosed society is the social expression of what the thesis calls *life-as-willed*: a social order in which the organizing ideal is each individual's autonomous construction of their own relational environment, without the friction of unchosen communal obligation. It is also the social context in which the metaverse's self-completing tendency finds its cultural legitimation: if life-as-willed is the normative ideal, then an environment that allows total design of one's own existential conditions is not a pathology but a fulfillment.

Read as the terminus of the colonization arc traced across the five periods, Period 5 is the moment at which the Modern Western Mode of Artificiality has reorganized the Japanese onto-epistemic terrain to such a degree that the resulting social form *anticipates* the metaverse — produces a population structurally homologous with the metaverse's affordances — before the metaverse infrastructure has fully arrived. The self-enclosed society is what a culture looks like when it has been re-engineered, across five generations, into the optimal user-base for the technosphere's next layer. Once again, this is not a Japanese pathology: variants of the self-enclosed society are visible across the high-income world (the American "bowling alone" trajectory, European patterns of declining civic participation, the algorithmic curation of social experience that is now near-universal). Japan's empirical legibility is, again, that it underwent the trajectory faster, with denser documentation, and with explicit conceptual self-awareness.

## Synthetic Passage for §IV

> The self-finishing tendency that §IV analyzes as an ontological feature of contemporary human life can be tracked, in compressed and unusually legible form, through the five periods of postwar Japanese social development that Uegaki Takahide's sociology distinguishes. Read carefully — that is, read as Japan-as-case-manifestation rather than as Japan-as-cause — the periodization documents a trajectory of onto-epistemic colonization by the Modern Western Mode of Artificiality, layered across roughly four generations.
>
> Period 1 (the confrontation with Western Modernity) installs the achievement-self as the measure of the Japanese person, calibrated against an external standard. Period 2 (the collective high-growth era) takes that achievement-orientation up into the collective — the firm, the nation, the developmental project — producing a *collective form of life-as-willed* whose family resemblance to other twentieth-century collective mobilizations of selfhood, including the European fascist formations, is structural rather than ideological, a CR-stratified cross-actualization of a common real-level mechanism rather than a substantive identity. Period 3 (the consumerist nuclear family) hollows out the communal world that the achievement-collective had originally served, replacing world-co-constitution with consumer-preference-satisfaction. Period 4 (the Lost Decade's recognition anxiety) dissolves the collective framework itself, leaving the Drifter — a self between worlds, unable to anchor in any of them. Period 5 (the self-enclosed society and its Ethics of Non-Intervention) crystallizes the trajectory normatively: the autonomous construction of one's own existential environment becomes not merely a psychological tendency but a cultural ideal, and the technological infrastructure that promises to fulfill this ideal — the metaverse — finds a population already structurally configured to receive it.
>
> The squeeze thesis must be read against this historical context. The psychosomatic malfunctions, livelihood singularization, and governance disruption that §VI documents are not simply the effects of new technology on a previously stable population; they are the compounding of structural vulnerabilities accumulated across five layers of onto-epistemic restructuring, in a population that arrived at the metaverse's threshold already hollowed-out, already Drifting, already organized by the self-finishing ideal that makes the metaverse's claim on existence culturally irresistible. That this is most legible in Japan does not mean it is most acute in Japan; it means that Japan, having undergone the colonization fastest and with the most explicit self-awareness, kept the most readable record. The mechanism documented through the Japanese case is the same mechanism whose other empirical actualizations the thesis takes up across §V and §VI, in registers ranging from the European platform-precariat to the American algorithmic-attention-economy to the global emergence of the metaverse as the final layer of neo-artificiality between humans and the universe their world once nested within.
