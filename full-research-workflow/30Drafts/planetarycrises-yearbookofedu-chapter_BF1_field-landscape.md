# Climate / Sustainability / Planetary Education — Field Landscape (2021–2026)

Brief prepared to position a chapter proposal against Routledge *World Yearbook of Education* Vol. 29, *Education and Planetary Crises* (eds. McKenzie & Silova, 2026). The field is currently re-organising itself around four overlapping but identifiably distinct currents. I name them in descending order of dominance in Anglophone scholarly publication 2023–2026.

## 1. The ESD Paradigm and Its Critics (still the institutional centre of gravity)

UNESCO's Education for Sustainable Development (ESD) framework remains the dominant *policy* discourse — reinforced by the 2024 *Global Education Monitoring Report* on climate ([UNESCO 2024](https://www.unesco.org/gem-report/en/2024ccec)) and SDG 4.7 reporting infrastructure — but its scholarly legitimacy is contested. The critique now has two stable lines:

- **Neoliberal-capture critique.** ESD's instrumental, growth-compatible framing reproduces the very logics driving the crisis. Continues the line from Hursh, Henderson & Greenwood (2015); recent restatements in higher-ed contexts (Bessant et al. 2024, *Professional Development in Education*, [tandfonline](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/19415257.2024.2394879)).
- **Depoliticisation / "climate literacy is not enough" critique.** Knowledge-transfer models leave structural drivers untouched; UNESCO itself now concedes that ESD/CCE must move from cognitive to "social, emotional, action-oriented" learning (UNESCO 2024).

Key recent works:
- UNESCO. (2024). *Education and Climate Change: Learning to Act for People and Planet.* GEM Report. ([link](https://www.unesco.org/gem-report/en/2024ccec))
- Kwauk, C., et al. (2025). *Education and Climate Change: Synthesizing the Evidence.* EdWorkingPaper, Kraft et al. ([PDF](https://edworkingpapers.com/sites/default/files/Kraft%20et%20al.%202025%20Education%20and%20Climate%20Change%20v2.pdf)).
- Sterling, S. — anchoring "sustainability as transformation" critique; cited across the corpus.

## 2. Climate Change Education + Youth Agency / Affect

The single largest empirical literature 2023–2026. Two sub-streams:

- **Transformative Climate Change Education (TCCE).** Synthesising Mezirow-style transformative learning with CCE. Flagship: Læssøe & colleagues, "Towards a transformative climate change education: questions and pedagogies," *Environmental Education Research* 30(12), 2024 ([tandfonline](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/13504622.2024.2365983)); Sezen-Barrie et al., *Science Education* 2025 special section on "Transformative Climate and Environmental Education for a Just Future" ([Wiley](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/sce.21963)); Ibourk et al. (2025) on care-centred TCCE ([Wiley](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/sce.70054)).
- **Affect, hope, climate emotions, youth activism-as-education.** Maria Ojala's constructive hope work remains foundational. Recent: Verlie's *Learning to Live with Climate Change* (2022) is now the most-cited monograph anchor; Trott (2024, 2025) on activism-as-education (*British Educational Research Journal*, *Geographical Research*); Finnegan & Eaton (2024) "Hope Wheel" model (*Frontiers in Psychology*, [link](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2024.1347392/full)); Vamvalis (2023) on affective/collective climate-justice education.

Active debate: whether "hope" is a pedagogical good or a depoliticising anaesthetic (Bryan; Stein on "hospicing" vs. hope).

## 3. Decolonial / Indigenous / Climate-Coloniality Framings

The fastest-growing and most theoretically ambitious current — and the one most aligned with the McKenzie/Silova editorial frame. The pivotal move is from "include Indigenous knowledge in ESD" to **climate coloniality** as the analytic: education itself is implicated in the colonial-modern habits that produced the crisis.

Key authors and works:
- **Sharon Stein & Vanessa Andreotti** (with Suša, Ahenakew, Čajková) — *Gesturing Towards Decolonial Futures* collective; "From ESD to education for the end of the world as we know it" (2022); Andreotti (2024) "The task of education as we confront the potential for social and ecological collapse"; Stein (2023) on universities beyond sustainable development and solutionism (*Higher Education*).
- **Elizabeth Sumida Huaman & Sharon Stein (eds.)** (2025). Special Issue, *AlterNative*: "Indigenous and Decolonial Approaches to Environmental (Re)learning for Planetary Care" ([SAGE](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/11771801251369903)).
- **Climate coloniality / pluriversal pedagogies** — Sultana's "climate coloniality" thesis (2022) is now the field's most-cited theoretical hinge; applied to education in 2025 *CIES* and *Comparative Education Review* venues ([CICE paper](https://journals.library.columbia.edu/index.php/cice/article/download/13416/7713/39906)).

Active debate: whether "decolonisation" risks becoming a metaphor (Tuck & Yang 2012 still anchors this) absorbed by ESD machinery, or whether it requires institutional refusal.

## 4. Posthuman / More-than-Human / Planetary Pedagogies

The most theoretically distinct current, drawing on new materialism, Deleuze-Guattari, multispecies ethnography, and Earth-systems framing. Less institutionally dominant but agenda-setting for *this* Yearbook volume given its title.

Key recent works:
- Salonen, A. (2023). "A Theory of Planetary Social Pedagogy," *Educational Theory* ([Wiley](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/edth.12588)).
- Le Grange, L., and collaborators — "Towards posthuman climate change education," *Journal of Moral Education* 54(1), 2024/25 ([tandfonline](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/03057240.2024.2418124)).
- *Planetary pedagogies: reimagining geography and environmental education in the Anthropocene*, *International Research in Geographical and Environmental Education*, 2025 ([tandfonline](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10382046.2025.2519879)).
- Affifi, Snaza, Common Worlds Research Collective (Taylor, Pacini-Ketchabaw) — anchoring more-than-human early-childhood and outdoor education.
- *Post-Anthropocene Civic and Global Education Studies* (Springer, 2024).

Active debate: posthuman frames vs. decolonial critique that "more-than-human" can erase Indigenous relational ontologies by recoding them as European theory.

## Cross-Cutting Active Debates

- **Is ESD still useful, or is it the problem?** (Currents 1 vs. 3.)
- **Hope vs. hospicing / grief / collapse-acceptance.** Andreotti & Stein vs. hope-pedagogy scholars.
- **Climate literacy vs. climate justice.** Cognitive-behavioural CCE (meta-analysed 2025, *Frontiers in Education*) vs. systemic/affective/political framings.
- **Anticipation and futures.** UNESCO Futures of Education (2021) + Futures Literacy → empirical uptake 2024–25 (Sippl 2025; PISA 2025 adding sustainability/agency dimension).
- **Whose planetary?** McKenzie & Silova's framing pushes against universalising "planetary" by demanding *comparative* and pluriversal grounding ([substack preview](https://edforplanetaryfutures.substack.com/p/education-and-planetary-crises-comparative)).

## Foundational Anchors (pre-2021, still load-bearing)

- Mezirow on transformative learning.
- Sterling, *Sustainable Education* (2001).
- Orr, *Earth in Mind* (1994/2004).
- Bowers, eco-justice education.
- Tuck & Yang, "Decolonization is not a metaphor" (2012).
- Haraway, *Staying with the Trouble* (2016).
- Stengers, *In Catastrophic Times* (2015).
- Ojala on constructive hope (2012 onward).
- Latour, *Down to Earth* / Facing Gaia.

## Emerging-but-Buzzy Concepts (positioning surface for the chapter)

- **Climate coloniality** (Sultana) — has crossed from geography into education 2024–25; high citation velocity.
- **Hospicing modernity** (Andreotti) — book-length; now a movement-term.
- **Planetary health literacy** (PMC11395496) — bridging medical and education literatures.
- **Care-centred / communal-ecology pedagogies** (Ibourk et al. 2025).
- **Regenerative education / "education beyond green growth"** (Nordic Journal of CIE 2024).
- **Futures literacy** institutionalised via UNESCO + PISA 2025.
- **Climate grief / eco-anxiety pedagogy** (Verlie; Pihkala).

## Gaps & Opportunities

- **Regional voices.** East Asian (Sinophone, Japanese, Korean) theorisations of the human–nature–technology nexus are almost absent from the Anglophone planetary-education corpus, despite obvious resources (Watsuji's *fūdo*; Hui's cosmotechnics; Daoist relational ontologies). The corpus reads Indigenous and Latin American decolonial sources but rarely East Asian philosophy.
- **Theoretical integration.** The decolonial and posthuman currents largely talk past each other; few works hold both with serious tension (Stein gestures here but is one of few).
- **Ontology of "the planetary."** Most usage is rhetorical (synonymous with "global+ecological"). Few authors define it against universe/world/Earth/Gaia/biosphere — a conceptual gap a chapter could fill.
- **Technology / metaverse / digital education** is treated separately from climate education; almost no work asks what *neo-artificial* / digital infrastructures do to planetary pedagogies (an obvious chapter wedge given the editor's likely audience).
- **Methodology.** Heavy reliance on small-n qualitative + position papers; comparative policy work (McKenzie's own strength) and computational/scoping work (Kwauk/Kraft 2025) are still rare; philosophical-ontological work is rarer still.
- **Audience-bridging.** Almost nothing writes for both the ESD/policy reader *and* the posthuman/decolonial reader simultaneously — a methodological-rhetorical gap.
