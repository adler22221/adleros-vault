# Ch-1-11 v4 — The World as Operating System: A Structural-Functional Account

## §A. The OS analogy at the structural-functional level

The thesis turns to systems theory for an analogy, but at a level of abstraction that the literature on the metaverse has consistently failed to maintain. Mainstream definitions of the metaverse mistake contingent implementation — extended-reality headsets, blockchain ledgers, particular platforms — for the metaverse as such, just as one might mistake a particular kernel architecture or system call interface for what an operating system is. The thesis avoids that error in both directions: it treats the metaverse as a class of background environment (§I-1), and it treats the world as a class of operating system. What follows is a structural-functional definition of the latter, in five conjoined senses.

A *world* is an operating system in that (i) it is a totality to its members — the only environment they directly inhabit and respond to; (ii) it is simultaneously a bounded environment for those members and the members' collective systemic response to a deeper background environment, the universe; (iii) as such a bounded environment, it operates upon laws irreducible to the universe's laws and incommensurable, at the level of those laws, with other operating systems sharing the same background; (iv) components of one operating system can nonetheless be adopted into another without rendering their generative laws commensurate, a point the next sections will develop; and (v) it embodies a specific population-specific mode of collective survival and adaptational strategy — what the thesis calls a *mode of artificiality*.

This formulation is not a metaphor extracted from computing. It rests on three convergent biological frameworks. *Niche construction theory* establishes that organisms across the tree of life modify their selective environments in ways that feed back into their own evolution and the evolution of others (Odling-Smee, Laland & Feldman 2003; Laland, Matthews & Feldman 2016).[^1] *Umwelt theory* establishes that an organism's effective environment is constituted by the sensory-motor and semiotic structures through which it engages the world, not by the physical surroundings as such (von Uexküll 1934/2010). And the *extended evolutionary synthesis* (Laland et al. 2015) integrates niche construction with epigenetic and cultural inheritance, allowing constructed environments to be transmitted across generations independently of genetic transmission. Together they support the central claim: a world is a constructed, transmitted, and operationally bounded environment that mediates a collective's relationship to the universe.

The *spectrum of operating systems* across the tree of life clarifies what is and is not distinctive about human worlds. At one end, simple organisms such as cyanobacteria perform direct metabolic niche construction without coordinated social or artifact-mediated layers — although even here, niche construction is real: cyanobacterial photosynthesis transformed Earth's atmosphere during the Great Oxidation Event, a planetary-scale instance of the principle (Laland, Matthews & Feldman 2016). Social organisms with population-specific behavioural traditions operate through *proto-semiotic* OS: chimpanzees in different populations exhibit thirty-nine distinct cultural traditions in tool use, grooming, and courtship (Whiten et al. 1999), and four West African populations engage in habitual stone-throwing displays at specific trees that share key features with human ritual (Kühl et al. 2016). Tool-making organisms add a *proto-technological* layer through artifact construction: termite mounds organize by stigmergy, environmental traces coordinating coordinated construction without central plan; beaver dams are not tools the beaver uses but, in J. Scott Turner's formulation, *external physiological organs* that perform thermoregulation and chemical buffering for the colony (Turner 2000). Humans, finally, occupy a distinctive position: through self-domestication selecting for prosociality (Hare, Wobber & Wrangham 2012; Wrangham 2019) and through cumulative cultural evolution that no other species achieves at scale (Henrich 2016), they construct fully virtual *and* technological operating systems — *worlds* — that recursively shape their own learning environments, their bodies, their sociality, and their evolutionary trajectory.[^2]

[^1]: Following ethological convention, this thesis uses "proto-" as a placeholder for non-human analogs and reserves the unmodified terms (artificiality, virtuality, technology, world) for distinctively human formations. This is a working bracket on a real philosophical question — whether non-human creatures have artificiality of their own — that lies beyond the scope of the present argument and is itself worth reflective attention as a check on anthropocentrism. Importantly, "proto-" formations are *population-specific*, not species-specific: zoological evidence of within-species cultural variation, especially in primates and corvids, establishes that operating systems above the metabolic level are constituted by particular populations rather than genetically determined at the species level (Whiten et al. 1999).

[^2]: The thesis's "world" must not be confused with the technical term *possible worlds* in modal logic and analytic metaphysics, where it designates a logically consistent state of affairs (Leibniz, Kripke, Lewis). The two vocabularies are distinct: this thesis's world is a lived, culturally constructed, biologically real operating system; modal logic's possible worlds are abstract formal constructs. The phenomenological-civilizational lineage on which the thesis draws — Husserl, Heidegger, Goodman, Brague, and the niche-construction literature above — is also distinct from the individual-phenomenological lineage of "finite provinces of meaning" (Schutz), which operates at the wrong level of analysis: provinces are sub-regions of an individual's experience, not collective adaptational strategies.

## §B. Singular and plural: Goodman's worldmaking re-read

The thesis uses *the world* (singular) and *worlds* (plural) in different but coordinated senses. The singular is generic — it names the operating-system *type* whose criteria §A has just specified, in the way "the cell" is generic in biology and "the operating system" is generic in computer science. The plural names the *instances* of that type: the Sinosphere world, the Andean world, the medieval Latin world, and so on, each a different realization of the same five criteria, differing in content while sharing the same constitutive relation to the universe as their substrate.

Nelson Goodman's *Ways of Worldmaking* (1978) is the most precise philosophical articulation of this structure, and it can be re-read in critical-realist terms. Goodman's central observation — "worldmaking as we know it always starts from worlds already on hand; the making is a remaking" — positions worlds as ontologically real generative mechanisms whose products (the experienced furniture of any given culture) are the actual events and empirical observations of that culture. Worlds, in this reading, are *real* in the critical-realist sense: enduring, constitutive, generative; *actual* in their concrete instantiations; *empirical* in their lived experience. Goodman's "remaking" is the diachronic process by which worlds are reproduced and transformed across generations through what we now call cultural niche construction (Henrich 2016).

This reading also frames the empirical record §III will mobilize. Worlds rise and fall — the Easter Island world, the Western Roman world, the late-Ming dynastic world (Brook 2023), the Bronze Age Mediterranean world — under cosmic-ecological pressure that exceeds their adaptive range (Tainter 1988; Diamond 2005). The reoccurring apocalyptic collapse of the Santi civilization in *The Three-Body Problem* extrapolates from these real cases. Such collapses are empirical evidence that worlds are operating systems with adaptive limits, not eternal categories — that the *type* persists across human history while *instances* are mortal.

## §C. Fukuyama and Huntington as world-clash diagnoses, CR-corrected

Two large diagnoses of the contemporary world-condition operate at this level and require critical-realist correction. Fukuyama's *end of history* (1992) is best read not as a prediction about historical termination but as an articulation, *from within*, of the disposition of one currently dominant operating system — the modern Western mode of artificiality — toward global domination, what Chakrabarty (2018, 2023) names the displacement of "planet" by "globe." Fukuyama's specific failure is the linearization of evolution: he treats history as cumulative progress toward a single end-state, in the orthodox Hegelian-Marxist mould. But evolutionary theory across the synthesis from Archer's morphogenetic approach to the extended evolutionary synthesis (Laland et al. 2015; Wielecki 2020) takes diversity to be both a *necessary condition* and a *necessary result* of evolutionary processes; world-pluralism is not a transitional inefficiency but a structural feature of complex adaptive systems.[^3]

[^3]: A flag to be addressed throughout the thesis and unpacked fully in §III: the labels *modern* and *Western* — even in their long tradition of self-critique within modernity studies — risk essentializing the West as if internal diversity within it did not exist. The thesis treats "modern Western mode of artificiality" as a description of a currently dominant *configuration* of artificiality, not of a culturally bounded entity, and §III will develop the disaggregating moves that this requires.

Huntington's *clash of civilizations* (1996) gets one thing importantly right: tensions between worlds at the *real* level — for instance, the deep onto-epistemic incommensurability between traditional Chinese and modern Western medicine — exist, cannot be resolved by normative fiat, and have led and can lead to socio-political conflict if poorly handled. But Huntington's argument fails by *conflating* the three critical-realist levels. Defining civilizations geopolitically and treating the resulting boundaries as if they tracked real-level differences, he commits cultural essentialism (precisely Sen's critique, 2006), cultural determinism (precisely Said's critique, 2001), and an internal inconsistency that confuses onto-epistemic traditions with geographic or geopolitical entities. Yuk Hui's cosmotechnical question — is the People's Republic of China's social-credit-and-surveillance complex Western or Eastern? — exposes the inadequacy of the geopolitical taxonomy at the real level: the components circulating today are real-level Western in origin, even when they are mobilized against Western hegemony (Hui 2016). Real-level world-tensions are real; geopolitical maps are not their mirror. The thesis preserves Huntington's recognition of real-level incommensurability and discards his cartography.

## §D. A critical-realist reading of colonization

Component portability across worlds is empirically obvious: technologies, concepts, institutions, foods, and forms of writing have circulated across world-boundaries throughout recorded history. Yet portability is not frictionless transfer — and here the OS analogy reaches its sharpest point. To adopt a component from another operating system is to *re-embed* it in the receiving world's stratified ontology, and the cost of re-embedding scales with the ontological depth at which the imported component operates.

At the *empirical* level, components transfer with low conversion cost: a recipe, a tool, a textual genre can be incorporated with only superficial adjustment. At the *actual* level, conversion cost rises: an institution or practice (a parliamentary form, an examination system, a market mechanism) requires functional re-coding to operate within the recipient world's existing mechanisms. At the *real* level — the level of generative mechanisms, foundational ontologies, modes of artificiality as such — re-embedding is no longer adjustment but *replacement*: the recipient world's real is overwritten by the imposing world's real. *This is the ontological structure of colonization.* Colonization is not best understood as a contingent moral injury layered onto neutral cultural exchange; it is the name for what occurs when real-level imposition succeeds in displacing one world's generative mechanisms with another's.

The argument is intended as one — but not the only — key lens connecting this thesis to the de- and post-colonial literatures. It is operative in §I, where it grounds the world-as-OS account; in §II, in any treatment of cultural transfer or technology adoption; in §III, in any treatment of world-transformation by external imposition; and in the conclusion, as one of the thesis's contributions to debates about pluralism, hospitality, and the conditions of inter-world communicability without homogenization. The framing is critical-realist rather than purely normative because it identifies the *level* at which world-replacement occurs and thereby clarifies what is and is not at stake when components circulate.

## §E. Coda: From colonization to the squeeze — the metaverse as autopoietic outcome of one mode of artificiality

The §D argument returns, in transformed shape, in the case the thesis principally tracks. The metaverse is *not* external to the worlds analyzed thus far; it is the autopoietic outcome of one specific mode of artificiality — what the thesis calls (provisionally) the modern Western mode.[^4] §III, §IV, and §V develop this trajectory and need only be summarized here. §III shows the modern Western mode's *disposition to domination* — its tendency to displace other worlds' modes of artificiality at the real level, exactly the colonization structure §D specifies. §IV identifies, within the modern Western mode itself, a *self-finishing* tendency at the level of the human: the pursuit of *life-as-willed*, of an existence shaped to inner specification through controllable arrangements. §V shows the corresponding *self-completing* tendency at the level of artificiality: the drift of artificial systems toward operational independence from the human collectivities that built them. The metaverse is what emerges when the apparatus of controllability becomes self-sufficient relative to its human builders — when artificiality, originally a collective adaptation to the universe, achieves autonomy from the collective and becomes itself the dominant background environment to which the collective must adapt.

Read against §D, the metaverse extends the colonization structure rather than departing from it. The modern Western mode has been imposing its real upon other worlds for several centuries; the emergence of the metaverse is a further phase of the same trajectory, in which the imposed real now displaces even the originating world that produced it. In this strong sense, the metaverse can be considered a new kind of colonization — and one that the thesis's de- and post-colonial interlocutors should recognize.

But the metaverse departs from classical colonization in two respects sharp enough to require the squeeze hypothesis as a complement to the §D frame. First, *no human colonizer*. Classical colonization names what one human collectivity does to another, with coercion as either intent or consequence; the resistance and ethics of decolonization are organized around the identification of such an agent. The metaverse has no analogous agent. It is not the project of a colonizing collectivity imposed upon a colonized one; it emerges from the collective pursuit of *life-as-willed*, a normative inheritance internal to the modern Western mode itself, embraced — often willingly — by the very people whose worlds are being displaced. Second, the *form* of the pressure differs. Classical colonization operates through human coercion (overt or structural) directed at humans; the metaverse operates through *environmental adaptational pressure* exerted by an emerging second background environment upon its inhabitants. There is still imposition, and it is still real-level; but the imposing entity is no longer another human collectivity. It is an environment that has acquired sufficient operational independence to confront human life with its own selection criteria.

This is why the thesis requires both the critical-realist reading of colonization developed here *and* the squeeze hypothesis of §I-1 and §VI. The colonization frame supplies the genealogical structure: how the modern Western mode displaced and continues to displace other modes of artificiality at the real level, and how its present phase displaces even its own originating world. The squeeze hypothesis supplies the new ontological condition: human existence increasingly compressed between the universe — its given substrate, whose own stability has been destabilized in turn — and the metaverse, an emergent second background environment whose laws derive from one specific mode's autopoietic trajectory rather than from any collective adaptation to the universe. Together they articulate what is structurally novel about the present: colonization has become an ongoing environmental event, no longer requiring a colonizing collectivity to advance.[^5]

[^4]: "Modern Western" is treated throughout this thesis as a description of a currently dominant *configuration* of artificiality, not of a culturally bounded entity (see footnote 3 and §III). A sharper provisional candidate, to be considered in §III, is *Enlightenment* or *Neo-Enlightenment mode of artificiality*: the term would name the specific normative inheritance — the pursuit of *life-as-willed* through controllability — that drives the trajectory analyzed here, without conflating this normative inheritance with the geographic West or with the internal diversity of European-descended cultures. Settling the precise term is deferred.

[^5]: A note on *life-as-willed* and the place of controllability in the trajectory: the present coda summarizes only what is needed to position the metaverse against §D. The substantive argument that the modern Western mode pursues life-as-willed via increasingly automated controllability, and that this pursuit produces both the self-finishing tendency of its human and the self-completing tendency of its artificiality, belongs to §IV and §V.
