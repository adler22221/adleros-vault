## The Three Concepts: Preliminary Definitions for Intro §B

### 1. Universe

The *universe* is the totality of what exists independently of human mind and action: the background environment constituted by physical, chemical, biological, and ecological processes that preceded human existence, that will continue after it, and that human activity cannot suspend even as it transforms. The universe is *realist* in the sense that critical realism specifies: its structures and mechanisms operate whether or not any human being observes, conceptualizes, or intervenes in them. Its laws — thermodynamic, biological, gravitational — are not chosen; they are given.

For this thesis, the universe functions as the *ground*: the unrevisable substrate of all human existence. Whatever world human beings build, they build it within the universe; the conditions of the universe are the non-negotiable outer boundary of any possible human habitation.

*Key terms to establish:* universe as realist; universe as background environment; universe as non-negotiable ground.

### 2. World

The *world* is the human-made meaning-space that communities construct within the universe to render it inhabitable. It is *idealist* in the sense that it depends on human mind and practice for its existence: without human beings who act within it, attribute significance to it, maintain it through ritual, law, language, and built environment, and transmit it across generations, the world ceases to exist. But the world is not purely mental; it is materially embodied in technical objects, institutions, architectures, and practices — what Stiegler calls the "already-there" of technical inheritance.

The world is *nested within the universe* — smaller in extent but not in functionality. Everything the world contains is materially supported by the universe; but the world organizes that material support according to principles of meaning and value that are not derivable from the universe's physical laws alone. To inhabit a world is to be oriented within a meaning-space that transforms bare material existence into a structured form of life.

*Key terms to establish:* world as idealist but materially embodied; world as nested in universe; world as human-made meaning-space; world as trans-generational.

### 3. Human (as World-Making Animal)

The *human* is not simply a biological species — *homo sapiens* with a particular morphology and cognitive architecture — but the world-making animal: the being whose mode of existence requires the construction and maintenance of a meaning-space that mediates its relationship to the universe. Humans are not adapted to any specific ecological niche; they create their own niche — the world — through technical, linguistic, and institutional activity.

This is the sense in which Stiegler's Epimetheus myth is structurally accurate: the human is the being that was "forgotten" in the original distribution of capacities to animals, and whose constitutive lack is compensated by the prosthetic inheritance of technical objects. The human is not prior to its prostheses; it is *constituted by them*. World-making is not an optional activity for human beings; it is the condition of their biological survival.

*Key terms to establish:* human as world-making animal; human as constituted by prosthetic inheritance; world-making as existential necessity.

### The Thesis's Central Claim — Framed from These Definitions

Given these three preliminary definitions, the thesis's central claim can be stated precisely:

The *metaverse* is not another world within the universe (the way that different cultural worlds are different worlds within the same universe) but an emerging *second background environment* — a technically constructed system with its own governing logic, operating alongside and increasingly in conflict with the universe's laws, whose structural tendency is to displace the world as the mediating space between the human and its given substrate.

The *squeeze* is the adaptational double-bind that emerges when the human — as world-making animal — is simultaneously subject to the selection criteria of the universe (which demands embodied resilience, biological attunement, and ecological integration) and the metaverse (which demands platform legibility, algorithmic compliance, and data-generativity). These are not compatible demands; adaptation to the metaverse tends to reduce fitness for the universe, and vice versa.

## Recommended Passage for Intro §B

> This thesis is organized around three concepts that will be elaborated in §I but must be sketched here to orient the reader.
>
> The *universe* is the totality of what exists independently of human mind and action: the background environment constituted by physical, chemical, biological, and ecological processes that precede and exceed human existence. The universe is not chosen; its laws — thermodynamic, biological, gravitational — are given. It is the non-negotiable ground within which all human life occurs.
>
> The *world* is the human-made meaning-space that communities construct within the universe to render it inhabitable. Nested within the universe but not reducible to it, the world organizes material existence according to principles of meaning and value — embodied in language, law, ritual, built environment, and technical inheritance — that transform bare survival into a structured form of life. Without human beings who maintain and transmit it, the world ceases to exist; but it is not merely mental: it is materially embodied in the "already-there" of accumulated technical objects and institutions.
>
> The *human*, as this thesis treats it, is not simply a biological species but the *world-making animal*: the being whose mode of existence requires the construction and maintenance of a world as the mediating space between itself and the universe. World-making is not an optional activity for human beings; it is the condition of their biological survival. The human is constituted by its prosthetic technical inheritance — not prior to the world it builds, but shaped by it at every level.
>
> The *metaverse*, whose emergence is this thesis's occasion and object, is not analyzed here as another world alongside others. It is analyzed as an emerging *second background environment* — a technically constructed system with its own governing logic, operating alongside the universe but with laws that are not modifications of the universe's laws. The thesis's central claim — the "squeeze hypothesis" — is that the simultaneous subjection of the human to two incommensurable background environments (universe and metaverse) creates a structural double-bind that constitutes the defining civilizational predicament of the present moment. §I develops the empirical and philosophical basis for this claim; what follows in the Introduction sketches the methodological approach.
