# On method: the sovereign subject, and how to challenge the unchallengeable
> Methodological note (ai-draft, voice-matched). Placement map at foot. `[VERIFY before prose: 無我/anātman, 齊物/Zhuangzi, Taylor (buffered self), Macpherson (possessive individualism), Hume (bundle-self), Eckhart/apophatic — T0-before-characterizing discipline.]`

This thesis takes aim at what may be the deepest assumption of the modern world, and it must be honest from the outset about the difficulty that target presents. The assumption is the **sovereignty of the subject**: the conviction that the human being is, at bottom, a self-subsistent individual — standing prior to and independent of its world and its others, sovereign over its own life, owed its freedom, answerable in the last instance to nothing it did not choose. This is the self Charles Taylor calls *buffered*, against the porous, world-embedded self it displaced; it is the possessive individual and the autonomous rights-bearer; and it is what Uegaki, in the vocabulary this thesis adopts, names 存在論的自由 (ontological freedom). It is not one modern value among others. It is the ground on which the others stand.

The difficulty is that an assumption this deep is almost impossible to bring into view, let alone to question. To a person formed within it — which is to say, to nearly every reader this thesis can expect — the sovereignty of the subject does not appear as an assumption at all. It is the water. Asked to consider that the self might not be sovereign, the modern reader tends to hear one of three things: a triviality (*of course I am myself*), a provocation against everything decent (*are you against freedom, against rights?*), or plain unintelligibility (*what could it even mean for the self not to stand at the centre?*). Each reaction is evidence — not that the assumption is beyond question, but that it has been *universalized* so completely that questioning it has come to feel like questioning reality itself.

And that is the first thing to say about the difficulty: **it is not an obstacle to this thesis; it is the thesis's subject matter.** The invisibility of the sovereign subject is precisely what the word *universalization* names — the success-condition of the mode this thesis diagnoses. A configuration becomes universalized when it stops being seen as a configuration at all. So the reader's inability to see the sovereign subject as one way of being a self among others is not a wall the argument must climb; it is the very phenomenon the argument is about. This dictates the method, because it means the assumption cannot be challenged in the ordinary way — by argument, frontally. One does not argue a person out of the water they breathe. The thesis proceeds, instead, by two moves.

The first is to **denaturalize the assumption by contrast.** Set the sovereign subject beside ways of being a self that do not posit it, and it ceases to look like the human condition and becomes visible as a *selection* — one world's, not the world's. The sharpest such contrasts deny the separate self at its root: the Buddhist 無我, which holds that there is no abiding, self-subsistent self to be sovereign; and the Daoist 齊物, which levels the self with the myriad things, refusing it the privileged standpoint from which alone sovereignty could be claimed. The contrast must be drawn with care, and the care is itself instructive. It is *not* a contrast between a sovereign West and a self-dissolving East — a romance this thesis refuses. The no-self has its own subordinated Western lineages (Hume's bundle, Eckhart and the apophatic mystics), and the self-subsistent self has non-Western expressions; what is at issue is not a geography but three distinct *configurations* — the self-subsistent self that **masters** the given (the mode this thesis studies), the self-subsistent self that **withdraws** from it (the ascetic traditions, East and West), and the self that is **not separate from the given at all** (無我, 齊物). It is the third that does the decisive work, because it shows that the very *separateness* the sovereign subject takes for granted is something some traditions decline at the root. Drawn this way, the contrast denaturalizes without orientalizing.

The second move is to **refute the assumption immanently — through the squeeze.** Here the thesis does something stronger than denaturalisation, and it does it without ever asserting that the sovereign subject is *false*. It shows that the sovereign subject's own project undoes itself. The project is to be answerable to nothing one did not choose — to be free of the anti-intended, the part of existence that does not bend to one's will — and the means is to offload that anti-intended burden onto artificiality. But artificiality, absorbing what it is handed and completing itself, becomes a second given; and the first given, the universe, reasserts in the body and in death. The sovereign subject thus ends up answerable to *two* givens, precisely by the project through which it sought to answer to none. This is the squeeze, and it is the thesis's decisive move against an assumption it could not attack head-on: not a claim that sovereignty is *wrong*, but a demonstration that sovereignty's own pursuit produces a structural condition it cannot acknowledge within its own terms. Reality calls the bluff. The method is immanent critique — the same by which this thesis surfaces the emancipatory tradition's self-contradiction, and by which Habermas convicted the *Dialectic of Enlightenment* of a performative contradiction — and the squeeze is its culmination.

Two disciplines follow, and the thesis holds them throughout. The first is that the critique is **not normative in the ordinary sense.** One cannot moralise against an assumption that is at once foundational and genuinely good-delivering — the freedoms and equalities the sovereign subject has won are real, and this thesis neither asks anyone to surrender them nor pronounces them a mistake. It asks only that the sovereign subject be seen as a configuration rather than a fate, and that the predicament its universalisation produces be acknowledged. The second is **anti-prescription.** The contrasts that denaturalise the sovereign subject — 無我, 齊物 — are tools for *thinking* a self that is not sovereign; they are not a programme to be adopted, and the thesis counsels no one to become a Buddhist or a Daoist. The squeeze is a structural and collective condition, not one an individual dissolves by private enlightenment. What these traditions offer is not an exit but a vantage — the only vantage from which the water becomes visible as water.
