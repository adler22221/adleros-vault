## Recalibration of Part 2 (2026-05-30)

Part 2 — Obara's **animal proto-virtuality** (gorilla chest-beating, crane courtship dance, collective-excitement management) — is **not** present in the DOCX. It belongs in Chapter II, inside the virtuality section ("Virtuality as causally efficacious nonmaterial artificiality").

The recalibration is mainly conceptual vocabulary. The original draft spoke loosely of a "representational layer mediating between organism and environment" as the general definition of virtuality. That phrasing predates the canonical stance and risks two slippages the thesis now forecloses: (1) it sounds like the simulationist "stands-for / as if" reading that DOCX §II.B explicitly rejects (Pierce, Baudrillard), and (2) it floats free of the umbrella concept. The recalibrated frame is tighter:

- **Artificiality** is the umbrella (Stiegler's broad *technē* + Obara's 「モノ」). Animal proto-virtuality is, strictly, *proto-artificiality* in the nonmaterial register — the evolutionary floor beneath the human case.
- **Virtuality** is the §II.B lens on the **nonmaterial, causally-efficacious** subset of artificiality. What makes the chest-beat proto-*virtual* is not that it "represents" or "stands for" something (that would re-import the as-if view) but that it is a **nonmaterial** organizing principle whose **compelling power is given by its meaning rather than its physical instantiation**, and which is **causally efficacious** — it really moves other gorillas. The display's matter (the sound, the posture) is merely the temporal medium; the work is done by what the display *means* within the group.
- This is the same structure DOCX §II.B asserts of money — "shells, grain, salt, metal, paper, or digits are merely temporal mediums to operationalize money's virtuality." The chest-beat is to gorilla deference what shells are to money: a temporal medium for a causally-efficacious nonmaterial principle.

So the recalibrated claim is **not** "virtuality is representation." It is: *virtuality — causally-efficacious nonmaterial artificiality — has an evolutionary floor that predates the human lineage; the human case is the most-developed point on a continuum, not a discontinuous invention.* This is exactly the continuum DOCX §II.B already builds against Chalmers ("what he considers computer-based is no more than a temporal medium of a long historical continuum"; Durante: "humans have always dealt with the virtual"). The proto-virtuality material extends that continuum one step deeper than the DOCX currently does — from "as old as human storytelling" to "older than the human species."

One caution carried over from the canonical stance: animal proto-virtuality is *proto*-virtual, not fully virtual. As the Searle engagement (Ch-2-8) notes, the animal cases lack the linguistic-conventional and deontic articulation of human institutional facts. The claim is continuity-with-difference: the same nonmaterial-causal structure, at incomparably lower complexity. The passage below should not overclaim that animal displays are virtualities *tout court*; they are the evolutionary precursors of virtuality.

## Recalibrated draft passage (Chapter II §II.B)

> Virtuality does not begin with the computer, or the screen, or even with human storytelling. Just as the impetus to develop artificiality — the exosomatic extension of our functions — has an evolutionary depth that reaches below the human lineage, so too does its nonmaterial dimension. Obara identifies, in several animal behavioral repertoires, the structural precursors of what becomes, in the human case, virtuality [VERIFY: Obara's specific framing of these as proto-virtual; confirm which examples are Obara's own].
>
> The gorilla's chest-beating display is the paradigm case. When a silverback responds to a challenge by beating its chest rather than by fighting, the display does real causal work — it produces deference, social ordering, and the avoidance of costly combat — and it does so through a nonmaterial principle whose compelling power is given by what the display *means* within the group, not by the physical force of the chest-beat itself [VERIFY: Obara's use of this example; compare de Waal on primate display repertoires]. The matter of the display — the sound, the posture, the rhythm — is merely its temporal medium; were the same effect achievable through a different gesture, the underlying nonmaterial principle would be unchanged. This is virtuality in its minimal, pre-human form: a causally-efficacious nonmaterial artificiality whose power is meaning-borne rather than matter-borne.
>
> The crane's courtship dance follows the same logic in a different social register. The ritualized sequence of postures, calls, and movements does causal work — it conditions whether reproductive coupling proceeds — through a nonmaterial principle of species-identification and fitness-signalling whose force lies in its legibility to a prospective mate rather than in any physical compulsion [VERIFY: Obara's use of crane courtship; compare ethological literature on ritual display in Gruidae]. The dance organizes behavior the way money organizes exchange: by what it means within a shared field, not by what it physically is.
>
> The coordinated management of collective excitement — the synchronized alarm calls, sentinel behaviors, and ritualized dominance-and-submission gestures through which many social species regulate group responses to threat or tension — completes the picture [VERIFY: Obara's specific examples; which species?]. Here a nonmaterial principle integrates individual physiological states into coherent group action, again through meaning-borne rather than matter-borne efficacy.
>
> What these cases share is the early appearance, in non-human behavioral systems, of the very structure §II.B isolates in the human case: a nonmaterial artificiality whose compelling power is given by its meaning, operating through interchangeable temporal media, and yet fully causally real in its effects on behavior. In the human case this nonmaterial dimension achieves a constitutive complexity and civilizational reach that has no equivalent elsewhere — it becomes, with language and institution, the virtual fabric of the world itself (the gods, money, and laws of §II.B). But its evolutionary floor lies far below the hominid line. This depth is the most decisive answer to any suggestion that the virtual is a recent or merely technological phenomenon: long before Chalmers's computer-based virtual, and even before Durante's storytelling humans, the nonmaterial-and-causal had already organized animal life. Virtuality is not what computation added to the world; it is what the world has run on since long before there were humans to call it virtual.
