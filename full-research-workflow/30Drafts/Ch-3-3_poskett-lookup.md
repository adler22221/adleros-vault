## What Poskett Argues

*Horizons* is a popular-scholarly history of science that challenges the conventional narrative of the scientific revolution as a uniquely European event. Poskett's core argument:

1. **Modern science was not a European invention.** It was the product of global cultural exchange, from the fifteenth century to the present.
2. **The "Western science" narrative was a Cold War construction.** The mid-twentieth-century historians who promoted European exceptionalism were responding to Cold War political pressures.
3. **Non-Western scientific traditions were not merely "predecessors."** Ottoman, Chinese, Indian, African, and Indigenous American scientific cultures contributed to modern science not in some medieval "golden age" but continuously throughout the early modern and modern periods.

## Key Passages

### Passage 1 — The Central Argument (Introduction)

> "This story is a myth. In this book, I want to tell a very different story about the origins of modern science. Science was not a product of a unique European culture. Rather, modern science has always depended upon bringing together people and ideas from different cultures around the world."

— Poskett, James. *Horizons: A Global History of Science*. Viking, 2022. Introduction.

**Relevance:** This is Poskett's thesis statement. For §III, it provides a strong anglophone scholarly authority for the claim that Western modernity's scientific tradition is not a universal default but a *particular formation* — one that happened to become globally dominant, but not one that emerged in isolation. This directly supports the thesis's comparative framework.

---

### Passage 2 — The "Golden Age" as Imperial Construction (Introduction)

> "We often forget that the notion of a 'golden age' had originally been invented during the nineteenth century in order to justify the expansion of European empires. British and French imperialists promoted the false idea that the civilizations of Asia and the Middle East had been in decline since the medieval period, and so needed to modernize."

— Poskett, *Horizons*, Introduction.

**Relevance:** The standard narrative of non-Western science (China, Islam, India) as relevant only in a pre-modern "golden age" — followed by stagnation requiring Western modernization — was an imperial ideological construction. This is directly relevant to §III (Western modernity as a "disposition to domination"): the very historiography of science has been shaped by this disposition. Poskett's critique is not merely historical but meta-historical.

---

### Passage 3 — Cold War Politics and the European Science Myth (Introduction)

> "The politics of all this couldn't be clearer. These historians lived through the early decades of the Cold War, a period in which the struggle between capitalism and communism dominated world politics. They thought about the contemporary world in terms of a strict divide between East and West, and then — whether intentionally or not — projected this back onto the past. [...] The idea that modern science was invented in Europe therefore served as a convenient fiction."

— Poskett, *Horizons*, Introduction.

**Relevance:** The myth of European scientific uniqueness was manufactured during a specific geopolitical moment and for specific political ends. This supports the thesis's argument that the "disposition to domination" is not merely a neutral philosophical tradition but is entangled with material power relations. Poskett shows that even the historiography of science is not innocent of this disposition.

---

### Passage 4 — Global Scientific Exchange as the Historical Reality (Introduction)

> "I show how the development of modern science fundamentally relied on the exchange of ideas between different cultures across the world. That was true in the fifteenth century, just as it is true today."

— Poskett, *Horizons*, Introduction.

**Relevance:** Poskett's historical argument reframes the question of Western modernity: it is not the case that Western science developed internally and then spread outward. Rather, what we call "Western science" always already incorporated ideas, instruments, and knowledge from across the globe. The global is not posterior to the Western; it is constitutive of it. This has direct implications for the thesis's §III argument about how Western modernity "mechanistically intervened the globe and, by doing so, materially reinforced itself."

---

### Passage 5 — Science and Empire (Chapter 4 area, on natural history)

> "In the Calcutta Botanical Garden we can ultimately see Enlightenment science in microcosm. It was an institution, established by the expanding British Empire, for the purpose of economic gain. And it was built on land that the British had seized, by military force, from local Indian rulers. At the same time, the Calcutta Botanical Garden was also a place in which a diversity of cultures and scientific traditions came together."

— Poskett, *Horizons* (Chapter on Natural History, c. eighteenth century).

**Relevance:** Science and empire were co-constitutive. Poskett documents how the most emblematic institutions of Enlightenment science were simultaneously imperial extraction machines. This is relevant to §III's claim about the "disposition to domination" as structuring both Western science and its political economy.

## Disambiguation: How Poskett Differs from Needham

Both Poskett and Needham are cited by 馬 as relevant to comparative breadth. They address different questions:

| Author | Central question | Comparative scope | Methodology |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Needham | Why did modern science arise *only* in Europe? What did China achieve before that? | China vs. Europe | Deep historical + sociological analysis of a single non-Western tradition |
| Poskett | Why has the history of science been told as a European story? What did non-European cultures actually contribute? | Global (Ottoman, Chinese, Indian, African, Indigenous American) | Broad historical synthesis, many case studies |

**Recommended use:** 
- Needham: for the deep analysis of the Chinese/Mediterranean structural difference (§II/§III junction)
- Poskett: for the broader comparative claim that *no* mode of science is purely Western; for contextualizing the historiographical problem; for the Ottoman, Indian, African examples that go beyond the China/Europe binary
