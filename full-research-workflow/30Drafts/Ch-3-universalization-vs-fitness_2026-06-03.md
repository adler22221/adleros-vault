# Ch III — Is the universalization of the Enlightenment mode evidence of its fitness?

An objection arises here that must be met directly, because the very evidence this chapter relies on can be turned against it. If the Enlightenment mode of world-building has spread across the globe while other modes have collapsed or been absorbed into it, is this universalization not itself the proof of the mode's superiority — the demonstration that, of all the worlds humans have built, this one was the fittest? The sharpest version of the view is Niall Ferguson's. In *Civilization*, he credits the West's five centuries of ascendancy to six "killer applications" — competition, science, property rights, modern medicine, the consumer society, and the work ethic — that the West developed and "the Rest" lacked, and that the rest of the world has since been busy "downloading."[^f] What makes Ferguson the version this thesis must answer is that his six apps are not vague virtues but six specific *modes of artificiality*: forms of institution, knowledge, law, technology, and habit. He is claiming Western superiority in exactly the register this thesis works in — the superiority of an *artificiality-package* — so the rebuttal has to meet him on that ground rather than change the subject. Ian Morris makes a quieter, quantitative version of the same case, ranking civilizations on a single index of "social development" and finding the modern West at its historical peak;[^m] he is a more complicated interlocutor, an ally as much as an opponent, and I come back to him. In both hands the inference is the same: the mode that won is the mode that worked.[^ar]

The inference is tempting, and it is mistaken. More than one error is folded into it, and it is worth separating them. The first is the slide from *survival* to *superiority* — the assumption that because a mode prevailed (a fact about what *is*) it was the better mode (a claim about what *ought* to have prevailed). A mode can prevail by domination, by accident, or by the path-dependent advantages of an early lead, none of which makes it good. The second error is more interesting, and it is the one I want to press. The "fitness" of the winning mode is being measured on the winning mode's own yardstick. Ferguson's six "killer apps" are instruments of competition and accumulation; Morris's index counts energy capture, social organization, war-making capacity, and information technology. Each of these is a value internal to the Enlightenment mode itself. Naturally the mode that universalizes these values scores highest on a ranking built from them; the result is close to a tautology. Universalization does not merely spread a mode — it spreads the *scorecard* by which modes are judged, so that "the West won" reduces, on inspection, to "the West won by the West's own definition of winning." A mode that organized its world around relational harmony, or around the long-term regeneration of its land, would grade the Enlightenment mode not a triumph but a catastrophe of anomie and ecological exhaustion. This is the incommensurability of organizing principles — what Chapter I marked as the absence of a common metric across worlds — now applied to the writing of history. There is no view from nowhere from which one mode can be crowned the fittest; there are only the metrics each mode brings.[^f2]

The case is weaker than it looks even on its proponents' own terms. Ferguson's own metaphor gives the decisive point away: if the West's advantages are "killer applications" that the rest of the world can "download," then they were never intrinsic to the West, and their global spread is the diffusion of a portable toolkit rather than the triumph of a superior kind. An application that anyone can install is not the property of its first user; it is, precisely, universalizable — a fact about its portability, not a verdict on its maker.

Morris is the harder and more interesting case, because much of what he says is not an opponent's claim at all but an ally's. His central mechanism, the "paradox of development," is close to this thesis's own: "Rising social development," he writes, "generates the very forces that undermine further social development… Success creates new problems; solving them creates still newer problems."[^morris-paradox] That is the self-undermining dynamic the present argument attributes to universalization, stated by one of universalization's own celebrants. His account of how worlds end is congenial too: when a society fails to meet that paradox, "a terrible package of ills — famine, epidemic, uncontrolled migration, and state failure" (his "five horsemen of the apocalypse," once climatic change is added) turns "stagnation into decline," and decline into "disastrous, centuries-long collapses and dark ages."[^morris-horsemen] And his refusal to let geography harden into fate — "while geography drives social development, social development determines what geography means… It is a two-way street"[^morris-twoway] — is exactly the co-constitution of the made and the given that this thesis intends by the relation between artificiality and its universe; it is, in effect, a restatement of Needham's point. Where Morris turns into a foil is not in these mechanisms but in the frame he wraps around them. The frame is reductive at its base — "like all life-forms, we survive because we extract energy from our environment"[^morris-energy] — and it shrinks "domestication" to genetic engineering, organisms "genetically modif[ied]… eventually reaching the point that the genetically modified organisms could survive only in symbiosis with humans,"[^morris-dom] which misses the phenotypic, developmental, and behavioural register where Chapter II located the deeper story of human self-domestication. It reads human motive through a thin and recognizably modern anthropology of "sloth, fear, and greed,"[^morris-human] and it arranges the whole on a single ascending ladder of "social development" whose top rung is, unsurprisingly, the modern West. One can keep Morris's mechanisms — the paradox, the two-way street, the horsemen — and discard his ladder; and once the ladder is gone, his most striking prediction, that the East will overtake the West again,[^m] loses the meaning he assigns it and points somewhere he does not go. That divergence is the cleanest break between this thesis and his, and I take it up once the ecological argument is in place.

Needham is worth following further than that single borrowed point, because his life's comparison supplies the concrete history beneath a claim this chapter has so far only asserted — that the winning mode was *particular*. For all that Western modernity has reigned over the planet, its mode of artificiality was a historical exception, not the human norm; most of the worlds humans have built ran on quite different lines. Joseph Needham closed his *Science and Civilisation in China* on exactly this comparison, and his argument is that a civilisation's dominant mode of production and its deepest stance toward nature *co-emerge* — each shaping and reflecting the other — and are then legible alike in its science, its technology, and its institutions. Where the Mediterranean economies rested on animal husbandry, seafaring, and slavery, the governing posture was command: "the cowboy or shepherd drives his animals about, the sea-captain gives orders to his crew," occupations that "encourage excessive command and obedience."[^ndhm-occ] Where the Sinosphere rested on settled agriculture, the governing posture was patience: "the peasant-farmer, once he has done all that is necessary for the crops, must wait for them to come up."[^ndhm-occ] Needham gave the contrast its sharpest form in the Chinese pair *wei* and *wu wei*: *wei*, "the application of the force of will-power, the determination that things, animals, or even other men, should do what they were ordered to do," against *wu wei*, "leaving things alone, letting Nature take her course… and knowing how not to interfere."[^ndhm-weiwuwei] He named the two poles in just the terms this argument needs — "characteristically occidental 'interventionism', so natural to a people of shepherds and sea-farers," against a "non-interventionist conception of human activity"[^ndhm-intervention] — and read them through into the metaphysics: a Mediterranean nature to be "subdued by will-power and brute force," a Chinese nature understood as "the greatest of all living organisms," to be worked with rather than commanded.[^ndhm-coemerge] The same split runs through the institutions — the mechanical clock and the autonomous merchant city-state on one side, the hydraulic bureaucracy and its mandarinate of "minimum intervention" on the other (Table III.1).

**Table III.1 — Two co-emergent modes of artificiality (after Needham, *SCC* VII.2, §50), read non-teleologically.**

| Dimension | Mediterranean → Western modernity (*interventionist*) | Sinosphere (*non-interventionist*) |
|---|---|---|
| Dominant mode of production | animal husbandry, seafaring, slavery | settled (esp. wet-rice) agriculture |
| Geo-ecological base | archipelago of seas; pastoral–maritime | continental land-mass; great river-valleys |

| Axiology / stance to nature | *wei*: command; the "domination of Nature" | *wu wei*: non-interference; "go along with her" |
| Characteristic technoscience | experiment-as-intervention; mechanical; capital- & energy-intensive | patient observation; "action at a distance"; state-organic |
| Characteristic institutions | autonomous merchant city-states → competitive capital | hydraulic bureaucracy; mandarinate ("minimum intervention") |

*Rows drawn from Needham §50 pp.1–2, 5, 8, 11–12, 16–18, 91 (verbatim in the notes). "Interventionist / non-interventionist" are Needham's own terms (p.17); the table is read against — not with — his single-science teleology, on which see the next paragraph.*

One qualification must be entered at once, because Needham draws from this a conclusion the present argument rejects. For him these were so many "ethnic cultural rivers" destined to flow "into the ocean of modern science"[^ndhm-ocean] — a single, universal science that Europe reached first and that everyone else approaches late. That teleology is exactly what Yuk Hui's reframing of the "Needham Question" refuses. To ask why China "failed" to produce modern science is, on Hui's account, to mistake one cosmotechnics for the destination of all the others; the question's proper object "is not to show who is more advanced than the other, but rather to elaborate on the different systems of technological thought."[^hui-needham] What this chapter keeps from Needham, then, is the *co-emergence* — mode of production, worldview, and technoscience arising together; what it discards is his assumption that one of them was the finish line. There is no single ocean, only a plurality of technical worlds, of which the interventionist Western mode is one — neither the telos nor the measure of the rest.

Read without the teleology, the global triumph of the interventionist mode looks less like the victory of the fittest than like a contingent escape. Its take-off at the Renaissance was driven not by a finer intellect but by a particular social pressure — intensified military and commercial competition among feudal states, financed by the intergenerationally accumulated private wealth of an aristocracy — which rewarded a mechanical, capital-intensive technoscience built to *intervene*.[^renaissance] But intervention on a planetary scale needed something the mode could not generate from within, and here the economic historians press the point past Needham. Kenneth Pomeranz's revision of the whole "rise of the West" cuts straight against the superiority reading. As late as the eighteenth century, he shows, western Europe was "a none-too-unusual economy," one of several densely settled Old-World cores all "headed for a common 'proto-industrial' cul de sac" in which production "was just barely staying ahead of population growth."[^pom-cul] What set Europe apart was not capital — "capital was not a particularly scarce factor of production" — nor superior institutions, whose record, when they had to compete without force, Pomeranz calls "mediocre": "Only in overseas colonization and armed trading did Europe's financial institutions… give it a crucial edge."[^pom-inst] The binding constraint everyone shared was ecological — "land and energy," the "shrinking forests of core areas throughout Eurasia"[^pom-land] — and Europe broke it not by ingenuity but by two windfalls: the underground (English coal) and the colonies, whose New-World acreage "relieved the strain on Europe's supply of what was truly scarce: land and energy."[^pom-atlantic] Europe, in his summary phrase, "became a fortunate freak only when unexpected and significant discontinuities… enabled it to break through the fundamental constraints of energy use and resource availability that had previously limited everyone's horizons."[^pom-cul]

Two conclusions follow, and both feed the argument of this chapter. The first is that the interventionist mode and its mechanistic metaphysics turned in a self-reinforcing loop: the worldview licensed the intervention, and the spoils of intervention — the coal, the colonies, the costs displaced onto both — funded and confirmed the worldview. Drawing on the underground and the colonies released the West from the organic limits of soil-fertility and human labour that had bounded every prior mode, and bought it the room to elaborate an artificiality running on externalised energy and externalised cost.[^miura] The second conclusion is decisive for the question this chapter asks. If the West's ascendancy rested on a contingent ecological windfall and on the displacement of its costs onto colonised peoples and the deep carbon past, then its universalization is no evidence of fitness. It is evidence that one mode, for a historical moment, found somewhere else to put the bill. A mode that can spread only by externalising its costs has not learned to live within a world's means; it has postponed that reckoning and exported it — which is precisely the condition the ecological argument now turns to describe.

The third error concerns the word "fitness" itself, and here ecology corrects a common intuition rather than merely qualifying it. In evolutionary biology, fitness is not a measure of intrinsic worth or of long-term viability; it is the relative reproductive success of a trait *in a particular environment*, at a particular time. A trait can spread rapidly and still be self-undermining: a pathogen so virulent that it kills its host before transmitting, a population that overshoots its resource base and crashes. Present dominance, in other words, is no guarantee of future survival, and may be its opposite. More tellingly still, the dominance of a single form is not read, in ecology, as a sign of health. It is read as a sign of fragility. A field planted to a single genetic variety yields more efficiently than a diverse one, and is destroyed by a single pathogen against which it has no variation to fall back on; the Irish potato famine, where a uniform clone met *Phytophthora infestans*, and the loss of the Gros Michel banana to a single soil fungus, are the textbook cases.[^mono] The general principle is that diversity buys resilience — not because diverse systems are more productive in calm conditions, but because they retain the variety of responses that lets some part of the system absorb a shock that would level a uniform one.[^resil] The closest ecological analogue to a universalizing mode is therefore not a champion but an invasive species: a form that spreads, outcompetes, and homogenizes, reducing the diversity of the system it enters. No ecologist mistakes the spread of an invasive for a verdict of its superiority; the spread is a symptom of the system's degradation, not a measure of the invader's merit. The historian of biological expansion Alfred Crosby, who traced one biota's conquest of whole continents, reached exactly this conclusion: "There is little or nothing intrinsically superior about Old World organisms compared with those of the Neo-Europes. 'Superior,' in truth, is a term that has no meaning in this context, except as one organism fits into a given ecosystem and another does not."[^crosby-superior] Read this way, the planet-wide convergence on a single mode is not the system passing a fitness test. It is the system losing the variety of responses on which its capacity to survive a shock depends — and doing so at exactly the moment when its shocks have become planetary in scale.

It is important not to over-correct here, because the temptation is to answer the historians by simply reversing their verdict — to declare the Enlightenment mode not the fittest but the worst. That move would re-commit the original error with the sign flipped, and the argument of this thesis forbids it: if there is no common metric across incommensurable worlds, there is none for failure any more than for success. The ecological objection is stronger than a counter-verdict, and it does not require one. What universalization threatens is not this or that parochial good but the *precondition* of any mode's fitness whatsoever — the diversity and resilience of the planetary system that every mode, the Enlightenment mode included, depends upon in order to persist at all. A monoculture of crops is brittle; a monoculture of *modes* is planetary brittleness. And survival is the one stake that is not internal to any single mode's values, because a collapsed mode optimizes nothing: whatever a world is for, it must first continue in order to be for anything. The universalizing mode, by eliminating the alternatives that were the planet's reserve of responses, undermines the ground on which it too must stand. This is the precise sense in which the chapter's later argument about collapse is not a moral complaint but a structural one.[^collapse]

This also tells us how to read the collapse of ancient worlds — the evidence with which we began. The historians read collapse as the unfitness of the collapsed: the world that ended was the world that failed the test, and the survivor is its passing grade. Three corrections are needed. First, collapse is very often the *footprint of domination*, not the outcome of a fair contest. Many of the worlds that ended did not lose a competition on the merits; they were destroyed — by conquest, by extraction, and above all by the biological and ecological imperialism that carried the diseases, the livestock, and the weeds of the expanding mode into worlds with no defenses against them.[^crosby] Their collapse is evidence of the expanding mode's *disposition to domination*, which is the very thing this chapter set out to describe, and not a verdict on the worlds it overran. Second, collapse is evidence of *plurality*, not of failure. That so many modes existed, many of them for spans far longer than modernity has yet run, and that they were *displaced* rather than *out-competed*, argues against the claim that only the Enlightenment mode was viable; it argues, on the contrary, for the plurality of actual worlds that universalization erased.[^plurality] Third, and finally, the collapse of prior worlds is the warning the survivor declines to read. If monoculture is the condition that precedes collapse, then a mode that has made the whole earth into a single mode has made the whole earth into that condition.

Two recent bodies of thought converge on this reading from inside the philosophy of technology, and they are worth naming because they supply the apparatus the ecological analogy only gestures at. Bernard Stiegler reads the homogenizing drive of industrial modernity not as order but as *entropy*. "The destruction of social diversity," he writes, "results from short-circuits of the processes of transindividuation imposed by industrial standardization";[^st1] what is being destroyed is "the burgeoning and proliferation of differences — as the bio-diversity, sociodiversity… and psychodiversity"[^st2] on which living and noetic systems alike depend, so that the proper science of the present, he suggests after Lévi-Strauss, would be an "entropology."[^st3] In Stiegler's vocabulary, negentropy is the production and maintenance of difference, and industrial standardization is its entropic undoing; universalization, on this account, is not the perfection of a mode but the running-down of a planet's differences toward a uniform and fragile state. Yuk Hui makes the corresponding argument for technology itself. Against the assumption that there is one technology, becoming planetary as it matures, Hui argues for *technodiversity* — a plurality of "cosmotechnics," each embedding technical activity within a different cosmological and moral order — and reads the global convergence onto a single technological mode as a danger of exactly the ecological kind: a monoculture of technics, impoverishing the very diversity from which alternative futures might be grown.[^hui] Technodiversity is to monotechnology, on Hui's account, as biodiversity is to monoculture. Between them, Stiegler and Hui give the thesis a principled way to say what the ecological analogy says intuitively: that the spread of a single mode is the loss of a system's variety, and the loss of variety is the loss of resilience.

Hui's argument also answers the one question this section has so far left hanging: what to make of Morris's forecast that the East will overtake the West again. It marks the sharpest place where this thesis parts from him. Morris frames the whole of history as a race between regions — a Western team and an Eastern team, with geography deciding which leads — and on that framing the East's return is simply a change of champion. But this thesis is not about which region rules. It is about which *mode* of world-making rules, and a mode is indifferent to the longitude of its host. An Eastern-dominant future would not, by itself, be the defeat of the Enlightenment mode; it may well be that mode wearing an Eastern face. Hui states the point plainly: the rivalry between China and the West "is situated on the ground of Western technology," so that "the antagonism between the West and the East, seen from the perspective of technology, is only one form of homogenization."[^hui-mode] What looks from a distance like a contest between civilizations is, at the level that matters here, two competitors running one operating system. China's own modernization, on his account, has gone so far that "China ceases to be China, and becomes instead capitalism with Chinese characteristics," its inherited culture serving "only to inflect an otherwise triumphant westernisation."[^hui-face] The diagnosis is not new: Heidegger already saw the last century's rival superpowers as "metaphysically … the same," gripped by "the same hopeless frenzy of unchained technology,"[^heid] and Chakrabarty has shown how completely the categories of European modernity became "the sovereign, theoretical subject of all histories," the Indian and Chinese and Kenyan included.[^chak] None of this denies that these worlds still differ; they plainly do, and the defenders of an Asian or "Confucian" modernity are right that ways of life persist and diverge.[^counter] The claim is narrower and harder to evade. A civilization-state that runs the same technosphere, optimizes the same metrics, and keeps time by the same developmental clock has adopted the mode even where it has kept its culture. This is what turns the rise of the rest from a reassurance into the gravest version of the worry: a world of many powers all running one mode is not a diverse world; it is a monoculture of mode, the geopolitical face of the agricultural monoculture this chapter has been tracking. Hui names the convergence in just those terms — "monotechnology, which recklessly views itself as the only option," the technological form of what Shiva called the "monoculture of the mind."[^hui-mono] Multipolarity, on this reading, can disguise the very homogenization it appears to relieve.

This is not a prophecy that the East is condemned to be Western, and it should not be read as one. Hui diagnoses the convergence precisely in order to keep open the possibility of *technodiversity*, a genuine plurality of technical modes; his warning that "China cannot continue with this blind modernisation" is a call rather than a verdict.[^technodiv] The argument here is only that the contest as it is *now* constituted universalizes one mode, and that this is exactly why the recovery of real alternatives is what is at stake. The danger is the foreclosure of other modes, not the longitude of the winner.

The chapter therefore continues to use the collapse of prior worlds, but with its inference now made explicit and guarded. Collapse is never, in this thesis, a verdict of fitness on the collapsed. It is the footprint of a domination, the trace of a plurality erased, and a warning about what becomes of a system that has lost its alternatives. The universalization of the Enlightenment mode is not the sign of its fitness. It is the sign of a planet shedding the diversity on which any mode's fitness — including the Enlightenment mode's own — finally depends.
