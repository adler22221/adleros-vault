## 8. Chapter abstract  *(≈380 words; citations as numbered footnotes, excluded from count)*

Education is irreducible to instruction or schooling: it is the total process of forming our ways of coexisting with the world. Yet this commonplace reduction flattens planetary crisis into something to "teach about," obscuring how the crises are bound up in the very structures education operates upon.

Among the mechanisms at work is Allocation Dependence.[1] Whenever an agent depends on externally allocated resources — material or not — it assimilates to the allocation measure and trims what does not fit; its horizon narrows to redeeming resources, regeneration drops from view, and fragility spreads as any failure in the allocation pipeline cascades through the lives dependent on it. Credentialism and meritocracy are only its measure-specific manifestations. This mechanism runs across domains — Global South entities fit themselves to Global North schemes of trade-for-aid, protection, or technology; "domesticatable" animals are bred by ever-finer selection while the rest are driven toward extinction — and it is, I argue, among the forces driving the polycrisis at large.[2]

Education cannot solve planetary crises directly: teaching "sustainability literacy" only scratches the surface if these mechanisms remain intact. But as the foundational formation process, education can "embody regeneration" rather than "instruct about" it — what I call Education AS Sustainable Development.[3] Learning by Caring (德中學)[4] is one such embodiment. Beyond Learning by Reasoning[5] and Learning by Doing,[6] it enacts a different relation to the world, grounded in 德[7] — a concept that runs through Confucian, Daoist and Buddhist thought, of regenerative intrinsic goodness — and converses with the Western care literature.[8] Care cannot be coerced, standardized, or outsourced to AI, but can be enacted with whatever is at hand, so the rural poor are not "unequalized by their positionality" — because each learner's measure is their own caring act rather than a common standard, no one is disqualified by their circumstances. Where learning under allocation dependence is measure-based and redemptive, Learning by Caring is agent-based and regenerative: resources flow to learners as others reciprocate their care.

The approach is critical realist retroduction, drawn from my own deschooling social action and from action research with sixteen then-teenagers in Taiwan — half from disadvantaged backgrounds, many now leading social-mission careers or building passion-led paths without a degree. However, a significant limit remains: care yields social and cultural capital but not assured financial security, with essential workers' financial precarity as widely accepted evidence[9] — this is beyond what Learning by Caring alone can resolve. For planetary futures, that same dependence must loosen where we survive, not only where we learn.

## 9. Biographical note  *(~150 words)*

Adler Yang is a scholar-practitioner who works to uncover the common mechanisms behind planetary harm and transform systems from within through research and praxis as education. He is founding director of the Awakening Cooperative Lab, an inaugural Fellow of The Institutional Architecture Lab, a member of the Ecoversities Alliance, and sits on the council of the International Association for Critical Realism and the board of the Japan Association of Synthetic Anthropology; his 2014 TEDxTaipei talk was named one of the year's top ten. At Awakening, he empowered marginalized youth to enter selective universities without examinations and win honors including the Presidential Education Award, shifting scarcity-based competition into prosocial regeneration. As Director of Research at ZA Share, he led a TWD$10M proposal to reimagine post-pandemic education with the National Development Council. His award-winning documentary *If There Is a Reason to Study* and its campaign (2009–2016) were a key voice in the movement that replaced Taiwan's Basic Competency Test.
