Before the chapter can say what a world is made of, it must settle the name of the stuff. The argument that follows runs on a single analytical unit — *artificiality* — and a reader coming from the philosophy of technology, or simply from common usage, will reasonably ask why the more familiar word *technology* is not doing this work instead. The metaverse is, after all, routinely described as a technology; the whole apparatus of tools, platforms, and code that gives the term its urgency is, on any ordinary reckoning, technological. Why not, then, make *technology* the unit and have done with it? The answer is not a quibble over labels. The word one chooses here decides what kind of claim the rest of the thesis is taken to be making, and *technology* — in the sense the word has hardened into — would commit the argument to a position it does not hold and does not need.

## Why not *technology*

In its dominant modern sense, *technology* names a means to an end. The standard reference works state this plainly. The *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy* records, as the widespread view, that "technology is a neutral instrument that can be put to good or bad use by its users," and notes the further reduction by which "technology is 'merely' applied science" — the device, the instrument, the application of theoretical knowledge to practical purposes.[^t1] This is the instrumental-and-anthropological picture: technology is a human activity, and it is a means to an end.[^t2] Whatever its limitations as a complete account — and the same literature spends most of its effort contesting it — this is the gravitational centre of the word. Say "technology" without qualification and the hearer supplies a tool, a machine, a contrivance built to accomplish something.

[^t1]: [VERIFY: confirmed verbatim | SEP, "Philosophy of Technology" (plato.stanford.edu/entries/technology/), §3.1 — "technology is a neutral instrument that can be put to good or bad use by its users"; §2.2 — the view that "technology is 'merely' applied science." Fetched 2026-06-02.]

[^t2]: [VERIFY: Heidegger's "instrumental and anthropological definition of technology" — *The Question Concerning Technology* (Heidegger 1977, p.4–5): technology as "a means to an end" and "a human activity." That gravitational pull is exactly the problem. If *technology* is the analytical unit, then everything the thesis wants to gather under one head — not only tools and platforms but institutions, laws, language, money, *and* science, ethics, and religion — must be called a technology. And that move imports the means-to-end presumption wholesale. To call religion a technology is to presume that religion is a human-made instrument for achieving some end; but the revelatory traditions present themselves precisely as *revealers* or *paths of truth*, not as devices contrived by their adherents, and the claim "religion is technology" reads to them — correctly — as a hostile reduction of revelation to engineering.[^t3] To call science a technology inverts the commonsensical ordering, on which science is the truth-seeking enterprise and technology its applied downstream; the scientific realist hears "science is technology" as a collapse of truth-claims into function-claims. Neither move is unthinkable — there is a respectable lineage, from Foucault's technologies of the self to Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics, on which such inversions are principled.[^t4] But each is *contestable*, each is agreeable only to some, and each would oblige the thesis to fight, on the side, a philosophical battle over the created-versus-revealed status of cultural forms that is no part of its argument. The philosophical baggage of that battle is heavy, and the thesis gains nothing by carrying it.

[^t4]: [VERIFY: Foucault, "technologies of the self" (Foucault 1988, *Technologies of the Self*); Hui, cosmotechnics (huiQuestionTechnologyChina2016, Zotero CWX5W69K). Cite only to acknowledge the inversion is *defensible to some*, not to endorse it. Resolve Foucault citekey.]

The decisive point is that even the tradition most committed to a broad, non-instrumental reading of *technology* concedes that the narrow instrumental sense is the word's centre of gravity. Bernard Stiegler, whose whole project recovers an expansive *technē*, opens his account by registering the modern restriction in order to reject it: "in general, technics designates in human life today the restricted and specified domain of tools, of instruments, if not only machines."[^t5] He calls this received understanding what it is — "an inadequate understanding, one that is already restrictive and derived."[^t6] The lesson cuts both ways. It shows that a broader reading is available; but it also shows that one must *fight the word* to reach it, because the word's default is the narrow instrumental one. A term that has to be wrested from its own dominant meaning every time it is used is a poor candidate for the load-bearing unit of an argument whose central worry is precisely that the metaverse will be misread as "just more advanced technology." The misreading the thesis most needs to foreclose is the one the word *technology* most invites.

## Why *artificiality*

The umbrella the thesis adopts instead is *artificiality*: the whole class of humanly-produced organizing principles, material and nonmaterial alike — tools, built environments, institutions, laws, language, money, art, and, with no change of kind, science, ethics, and religion. The crucial difference from *technology* is what *artificiality* does *not* say. It names a thing's *origin and mode of being* — that it is humanly made, humanly instituted, humanly maintained — and it says nothing whatever about whether that thing is an instrument for some further end. To call religion an artificiality is to say only that religion is humanly instituted and transmitted, not that it is a mere device; to call science an artificiality is to say only that the institutions, theories, notations, and norms of science are humanly produced, not that science is reducible to applied function. *Artificiality* picks out the made, where *technology* picks out the *useful-made-for-an-end*. The means-to-end presumption that made "religion is technology" and "science is technology" intolerable simply never arises, because it was never part of the term. Science, ethics, and religion are not *technologies*; they are *kinds*, or *realms*, *within* artificiality — the same humanly-instituted ordering at work in law, in money, in language.

That this broad unit is not an idiosyncratic coinage is warranted by a convergence of two traditions arriving at it from opposite directions. Stiegler's recovered *technē* is the broad one: technics "designates… first and foremost all the domains of skill," so that politeness, cooking, rhetoric, poetry, medicine, the architecture of the *polis*, and "something of poetry and rhetoric in all language" all fall within it — and "all human action has something to do with tekhne, is after a fashion tekhne."[^a1] From an entirely different starting point, Obara Hideo's 「モノ」 — the katakana form deliberately distinguished from the hiragana 「もの」 of concrete things — names everything humans have made, abstract language included, with religion, ideology, and music explicitly inside the category.[^a2] A late-modern French philosophy of technics and a contemporary Japanese ethological anthropology reach the same unit from opposite ends. What both traditions reach is exactly *artificiality* in the sense used here; and what the present argument does is take that found category under a name — *artificiality* — that carries the breadth without dragging in the instrumental baggage that "technology," even in Stiegler's hands, must constantly be defended against.[^a3]

[^a1]: [VERIFY: confirmed verbatim | Stiegler 1998, *Technics and Time 1* (Zotero API752Q9), p.83 — "Technics (tekhne) designates, however, first and foremost all the domains of skill. And there is something of poetry and rhetoric in all language" (cached text line 996, 1003); "All human action has something to do with tekhne, is after a fashion tekhne" (line 1004).]

A third tradition, older than either, supplies the name in almost its exact classical form. In the *Xunzi* (荀子), the term 偽 picks out precisely the humanly-made: not nature's spontaneous endowment but what is deliberately learned, worked at, and accomplished by human beings. "What is good in people," Xunzi writes, "is their 偽" — "人之性惡，其善者偽也" — and he fixes the term by its mode of acquisition: "what can be learned and accomplished by effort, and lies in the person — this is called 偽" (可學而能、可事而成之在人者謂之偽).[^x1] The Tang commentator Yang Liang (楊倞) glosses the character by decomposing it: 偽 is a compound graph, 人 ("human") set beside 為 ("doing/making") — 「『偽』字『人』傍『為』，亦會意字也」 — so that 偽 just *is* 人為, the human-done.[^x2] This is about the most exact classical counterpart to *artificiality* the record offers, and it is in one respect more serviceable than the Western word: where English *artificial* descends from Latin *ars* ("skill, art") + *facere* ("to make") and has since drifted into the pejorative "fictitious… not genuine" and "insincere,"[^x3] Xunzi's technical 偽 names the made without that taint of the false. Two cautions govern the borrowing. The name is taken; the doctrine is not. Xunzi sets 偽 against 性 (inborn nature) as one term of a dualism — "the nature is the original raw material; 偽 is its refinement and adornment" (性者，本始材朴也；偽者，文理隆盛也)[^x4] — and that nature/artifice split is exactly the kind of divide Chapter I has already dismantled. The thesis adopts the neutral *name* 偽=人為 for the humanly-made; it does **not** re-import the 性/偽 opposition, and the reader should not read it back in.[^x5]

The cross-linguistic detail is not incidental ornament; it sharpens the very point the chapter is making about *technology*. For the pejorative drift was not Europe's alone. In ordinary classical usage 偽 already meant *deceit*: the *Shuowen Jiezi* defines it flatly as "偽，詐也" — 偽 is to deceive.[^x6] Two civilizations, independently, took a word for *the made* and let it curdle into a word for *the false* — the Latin *artificialis* into "fake," the Chinese 偽 into "deception." Xunzi's technical usage *recovers* the neutral sense against that slide, holding 偽 to its plain meaning of the deliberately human-wrought; and that recovery is the very operation this thesis performs on "artificial." Naming the prejudice in two traditions at once is the strongest evidence that it *is* a prejudice — a recurrent reflex of language, not a discovery about the made — and that the neutral category the argument needs is the prior and more basic one. This 偽=人為 is, finally, complementary to but distinct from the other Chinese angle on the artificial the thesis draws on: Yuk Hui's 器 (*qi*), the instrument or vessel within a cosmotechnics. 偽/人為 names the general field of the humanly-done; 器 names the made thing as instrument. Two distinct Chinese articulations of the artificial, not one — to be coordinated, not conflated.[^x7]

## The residual objection, and why it dissolves

One objection presses exactly here, at the moment "science is artificiality" is asserted, and it must be met where it arises rather than deferred. The objection is that science, ethics, and religion are not human artefacts at all but *domains of truth that represent or track a reality independent of us* — a scientific theory answers to nature, a moral law to the good, a revelation to the divine; to file them under "the humanly-made" looks like a category mistake that confuses the *made* with the *false*, the *constructed* with the *merely conventional*. If artificiality means "set over against nature," then calling a true theory an artificiality seems to deny its truth.

But this objection presupposes precisely the nature/culture divide that Chapter I has already dismantled, and once that divide goes, the objection goes with it.[^o1] The thesis does not hold that artificiality stands *against* nature; that opposition is the very dualism Chapter I rejected, on ecological grounds and through Obara's duality of human existence, where there is no pre-cultural nature for the human to be measured against or to return to. Being *made*, on this account, does not make a thing unnatural, and it certainly does not make it false. A scientific theory can be at once an artificiality — humanly produced, instituted, revisable — *and* world-tracking, answerable to the universe it models. The two are not in competition: that a theory is built by human hands and minds is no more an argument against its tracking the universe than that an eye is built by evolution is an argument against its seeing. The objection draws all its force from the assumption that "artificial" means "unreal" or "anti-natural"; remove that assumption — as Chapter I already has — and "science is an artificiality" says nothing more alarming than "science is humanly instituted," which is true and innocuous.[^o2]

[^o1]: [VERIFY: confirmed verbatim | DOCX [P0109] (Ch1) — "the distinction between the world and the universe in this thesis is not a rebranding of the two discrete metaphysical categories in the culture/nature dualism… Obara's duality of human existence also shows that there is no pre-culture nature that can be returned to… Artificiality – my analytical unit that replaces culture – is what makes human human, and a world world." Sources Haila 2000; Jones 2009; Obara. `.research/cache/docx-extract-20260530.txt` line 119.]

[^o2]: See the fuller elaboration of this point in the pure-footnote alternative drafted in the Scaffold notes — the author may prefer to relegate the entire "not against nature" passage to a footnote if scope discipline (Liu) favours economy in the body.

With the unit fixed, the chapter can proceed. *Artificiality* is the umbrella: the humanly-made organizing principle, in every register, material and nonmaterial, instrumental or not. What such artificiality *does* in the forming of a world — its impetus, its nonmaterial dimension, and the irreducibility of its laws to the universe's — is the business of the sections that follow.

### Pure-footnote alternative for the "not against nature" point (HYBRID — author chooses)
The body above uses the **hybrid** form recommended in the brief: a main-text passage that resolves the objection + cross-references Ch1, with the option (via [^o2]) to relegate the elaboration to a footnote. The **pure-footnote alternative** keeps only a single clause in the body and moves the whole resolution into a note. Drop-in version:

> **Body (pure-footnote variant):** "…Science, ethics, and religion are not *technologies*; they are kinds within artificiality — the same humanly-instituted ordering at work in law, money, and language.[^FN]"
>
> [^FN]: It may be objected that science, ethics, and religion are domains of *truth* that represent a mind-independent reality, and so cannot be human artefacts. The objection presupposes the nature/culture divide rejected in Chapter I [DOCX P0109; Haila 2000; Jones 2009; Obara]. On the account given there, being *made* entails neither falsity nor opposition to nature: there is no pre-cultural nature against which the made could be set, and a scientific theory may be at once an artificiality — humanly produced and revisable — and world-tracking, answerable to the universe it models. "Science is an artificiality" therefore says only "science is humanly instituted," which is true and innocuous; it does not impugn science's truth. Once the divide is gone, the objection has no purchase.

Rationale for preferring the hybrid in the body (kept, per the brief): the objection *arises exactly where* "science is artificiality" is asserted, so a reader needs to see it answered in the body, not hunt for a footnote; but Ch1 did the heavy lifting, so the body stays brief and the elaboration/evidence sits in the note. If the author (or Liu's scope-discipline concern) prefers maximal economy in the body, swap in the pure-footnote variant above.
