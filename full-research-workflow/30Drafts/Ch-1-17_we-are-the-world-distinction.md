## The Two Claims and Why They Appear to Conflict

**"We are the world"**: Humans don't merely inhabit a world; they *constitute* it. The world does not pre-exist human practice; it is continuously made and remade through the collective activity of human communities. In this sense, we *are* the world — we are the world-making activity through which a world exists. (This is the Castoriadis sense: the world as "social-historical self-creation"; the Goodman sense: "ways of worldmaking"; the Harari-inflected sense: the world as collectively maintained shared fiction.)

**"Different worlds"**: Different communities live in genuinely different worlds — not just different cultures within one shared world, but different configurations of meaning-space that are incommensurable at the level of basic organizing principles. The Chinese world, the Andean world, the medieval European world are not different rooms in one shared house; they are different houses built according to different architectures.

**The apparent conflict**: If "we are the world" (we constitute it), then all humans participate in the same world-making activity and the world is, at bottom, one. But if "different worlds" (genuine plurality), then there is no single world that all humans inhabit, and the unity of "we are the world" is an abstraction that obscures real differences.

This is the tension Mi identified. The thesis needs to show that both claims are true simultaneously — and that their apparent conflict rests on a confusion of logical levels.

## The Resolution: Two Different Senses of "World"

### Level 1: World as Type (= "we are the world")

At the level of the generic type, all humans participate in the same world-making activity: the constitution of a meaning-space that mediates their relationship to the universe. This is what is universal about the world — not its content but its form: the structure of being a world-making animal, of inhabiting the universe through a humanly constructed meaning-space. At this level, "we are the world" is correct: all humans share the condition of being world-makers, all human communities produce a world as the condition of their biological survival. This is the claim that grounds the thesis's comparative breadth: what Chinese, Islamic, indigenous, and Western communities share is the world-making activity itself.

This level is what Castoriadis means by "the social imaginary": every community operates through a "magma of imaginary significations" that constitutes the shared meaning-space through which social life is organized. The specific content differs; the form (organized social meaning-space) is universal. We are all the world in this sense: world-making is the human universal.

### Level 2: World as Token / Instance (= "different worlds")

At the level of specific instances — the particular world that a particular community has constructed — worlds are genuinely different. The Chinese world's governing principles (relational ethics, cosmological resonance, seasonal attunement) are not the same as the Western world's governing principles (individual rights, mechanistic mastery, linear progress). These differences are not merely cultural preferences within a shared framework; they constitute genuinely different architectures of meaning-space — different "ways of worldmaking" in Goodman's precise sense.

At this level, "different worlds" is correct: the Chinese world and the Western world are not different regions of one world but different instantiations of the world-making type, built according to different organizing principles. The content of the world is not universal; only the form (world-making itself) is universal.

### The Logical Levels: Why Both Are True

| Level | Claim | True of |
|-------|-------|---------|
| Type | "We are the world" — all humans are world-makers | The universal human condition |
| Token | "Different worlds" — Chinese, Western, indigenous worlds are different | The specific instances of world-making |

The apparent conflict dissolves once these levels are distinguished:
- "We are the world" refers to the *type*: all humans share the world-making condition
- "Different worlds" refers to *tokens*: specific communities produce specific worlds with specific content

This is exactly the structure Ch-1-11 established for the singular/plural distinction: "the world" (singular, generic type) and "worlds" (plural, specific instances).

## The Critical Third Claim: The Metaverse Threatens the Type, Not Just the Tokens

The thesis's distinctive move — which is what makes it more than a defense of cultural pluralism — is to argue that the metaverse threatens not only specific world-tokens (individual cultural worlds) but the *type* itself: the conditions under which world-making of any kind is possible.

Prior threats to worlds were threats at the level of the token: colonialism threatened specific worlds (indigenous worlds, the worlds of colonized communities) by destroying the conditions of their specific meaning-space. This is a profound harm, but it leaves the type intact: colonialism did not make world-making itself impossible; it destroyed specific worlds while the world-making capacity survived (sometimes in the colonized communities themselves, sometimes carried forward by other communities).

The metaverse threatens at the level of the type: by progressively displacing the world as the mediating space between human and universe — by installing itself as the second background environment that administers the conditions of existence — the metaverse threatens the conditions under which world-making activity of any kind can operate. If the world's tertiary retention is colonized by platform-managed memory [Ch-2-5], if the world's governing principles are replaced by algorithmic governance [Ch-5-3], if the world's livelihood structures are replaced by platform radical monopoly [Ch-6-8], then the capacity for world-making itself — not any particular world but the human world-making activity — is structurally threatened.

This is the thesis's most fundamental claim, and it depends on holding both levels simultaneously:
- "We are the world" (the type is universal): the threat to world-making is a threat to something universal, not merely to one cultural tradition
- "Different worlds" (the tokens are genuinely plural): the metaverse's threat is specifically totalizing — it threatens to replace the genuine plurality of world-tokens with a single algorithmically administered environment

## Recommended Passage for §I

> A distinction of levels clarifies the relationship between two claims this thesis makes simultaneously: that humans *are* the world — that world-making is constitutive of human existence as such — and that there are genuinely *different worlds* — that the Chinese world and the Andean world are not different regions of one shared global meaning-space but different architectures of world-making that are incommensurable at the level of their organizing principles.
>
> The two claims operate at different logical levels. At the level of the type — the generic human condition — all communities share the world-making activity: the constitution of a meaning-space that mediates their relationship to the universe and makes biological survival possible for a species that, as Stiegler following Epimetheus observes, compensates for its constitutive lack through technical and cultural inheritance. "We are the world" is true at this level: world-making is the human universal, and the world is what all humans share as the condition of their existence.
>
> At the level of the token — the specific world that a specific community has constructed — worlds are genuinely different. Goodman's "ways of worldmaking" are plural: the principles by which one community organizes its meaning-space are not the same as the principles by which another does, and these differences are not merely stylistic but structural — different answers to the fundamental question of how the universe shall be made inhabitable. "Different worlds" is true at this level.
>
> The thesis's argument about the metaverse operates across both levels. Prior cultural threats to worlds have operated at the level of the token: colonialism destroyed specific worlds without eliminating the world-making capacity itself. The metaverse's threat operates at the level of the type: by progressively installing itself as a second background environment that administers the conditions under which existence — and therefore world-making — can occur, the metaverse threatens not any particular world but the conditions under which world-making of any kind remains possible. This is what makes the squeeze this thesis describes different from all prior squeezes between different cultural worlds: it is a squeeze that threatens the world-making type itself, not merely any particular token.
