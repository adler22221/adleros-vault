## Prose Draft — Full Version

Approximately 2.4 billion years ago, the dominant life-form on Earth was the cyanobacterium — a class of single-celled photosynthetic organisms commonly called blue-green algae [NOTE: "blue-green algae" is a common name; scientific precision = cyanobacteria; the thesis uses "proto-blue-green algae" in DOCX — retain if author prefers]. Like all organisms, they survived by exploiting available energy sources and excreting the metabolic byproducts. Their energy source was sunlight; their byproduct was oxygen. For hundreds of millions of years, this byproduct was absorbed into iron-rich oceans and soils, leaving the atmosphere largely undisturbed. Then the sinks saturated. Oxygen began to accumulate in the atmosphere.

The consequences were catastrophic. Oxygen was, for the anaerobic organisms that had previously dominated the biosphere, a lethal toxin. The cyanobacteria's collective metabolic activity — not directed, not intended, not even perceptible at the level of any individual organism — had fundamentally altered the chemical composition of the planetary environment. The Great Oxidation Event, as geologists now call it, triggered what may have been the largest mass extinction in Earth's history. Species that had flourished for billions of years in the oxygen-poor world were pushed to refugia — the margins, the deep sediments, the anaerobic pockets where the new atmosphere could not reach — or eliminated altogether. The world the anaerobic biosphere had inhabited ceased to exist, replaced by an oxygen-rich environment built not from outside intervention but from the organisms' own collective activity [VERIFY: scientific characterization — standard geological consensus; if specific figures cited, check against sources such as Lyons et al. 2014 *Nature* or equivalent].

The cyanobacteria did not survive unchanged either. The oxygen-rich atmosphere they had produced posed new selective pressures on all life, including their own descendants. But the metabolic innovation that oxygen enabled — aerobic respiration, orders of magnitude more energetically efficient than anaerobic alternatives — opened evolutionary pathways that led, eventually, to all complex multicellular life on Earth. The atmospheric transformation that began as a metabolic byproduct became the substrate of everything that followed. The organisms that adapted to the new environment inherited its possibilities; those that could not were confined to its margins.

---

The Great Oxidation Event is not a metaphor. It is a documented episode of geobiological transformation in which the dominant life-form of an era collectively produced an environmental crisis through the ordinary operation of its characteristic activities. What the cyanobacteria "did" was survive and reproduce; what they produced was a planetary catastrophe for the world they inhabited, followed by the emergence of a new world that their activity had made possible.

The analogy this thesis draws is structural, not superficial. The proto-blue-green algae mirrors us as humans today; the Great Oxidation Event mirrors the emerging metaverse. Human civilization, like the cyanobacterial biosphere, has pursued its characteristic activities — world-making, artifice, the construction and maintenance of humanly organized environments — without any collective intention to transform the conditions of its own existence. The byproduct of this world-making activity is not oxygen but a qualitatively new layer of artificial environment: the metaverse. Just as the cyanobacteria's oxygen production was invisible at the level of any individual organism and catastrophic only in aggregate, the metaverse's emergence is the cumulative byproduct of billions of individually rational acts of tool use, platform adoption, digital participation, and institutional adaptation. No individual agent has produced the metaverse; no individual agent has chosen to initiate the civilizational transformation it represents. It has emerged from collective activity, as oxygen emerged from collective metabolism.

The question the GOE poses — the question this thesis inherits — is whether the organisms producing the new environment can adapt to what they have made. Some anaerobic organisms adapted, acquiring oxygen tolerance and eventually exploiting the new energetic possibilities aerobic respiration provided. Others retreated to refugia. Many did not survive. The squeeze this thesis describes — the progressive narrowing of the lived world between the given universe and the emerging metaverse — is the experiential correlate of this adaptational pressure: the pressure that any organism faces when the environment it inhabits is being replaced by an environment its evolved capacities were not designed for.

The difference, and it is crucial, is that the human case admits of reflexivity. Cyanobacteria could not observe the GOE or deliberate about its consequences. Human beings — the organisms producing the metaverse — can. The thesis's argument is not that the squeeze is inevitable or that the metaverse's emergence is unstoppable, but that the pressure it exerts is real and already operative, and that understanding it requires a framework adequate to its scale: not a political framework (which asks who controls the metaverse) or an economic one (which asks who benefits from it) but an environmental and evolutionary one — which asks what kind of organism the human is, what kind of environment the human has been building, and what follows when the environment the human has built begins to differ from the environment the human evolved to inhabit.

## Condensed Version (if full version is too long for §I opening)

Approximately 2.4 billion years ago, cyanobacteria — the dominant life-form of the early Earth — began accumulating oxygen in the atmosphere as a byproduct of their collective photosynthetic activity. The Great Oxidation Event that followed was not a catastrophe visited on the biosphere from outside; it was self-generated, emerging from the ordinary operation of the organisms' own characteristic activities. Its consequences were nonetheless catastrophic: an environment that had sustained life for billions of years was replaced, through collective metabolic activity without any collective intention, by a chemically new environment to which existing organisms were not adapted. The squeeze this thesis describes follows the same logic at the civilizational scale. The metaverse is not a technology imposed on civilization from without; it is the cumulative byproduct of civilization's own characteristic activity — world-making, tool use, the construction of artificial environments. Like the GOE, it replaces, gradually and then suddenly, the environment in which the organisms producing it have evolved to live. The critical difference: the organisms producing the metaverse can recognize what they are doing.
