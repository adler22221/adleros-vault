## Key Passages

### Passage 1 — The Social Imaginary as Creative Ground (p. 3 area)

> "The imaginary of which I am speaking is not an image of. It is the unceasing and essentially undetermined (social-historical and psychical) creation of figures/forms/images, on the basis of which alone there can ever be a question of 'something'. What we call 'reality' and 'rationality' are its works."

— Castoriadis, *The Imaginary Institution of Society*, p. 3 [NOTE: page number from position in extracted text — verify against print edition; Polity 1987]

**Relevance**: Castoriadis's social imaginary is not a collection of false beliefs or stories (the Harari framing of "myths" and "fictions"). It is the *creative ground* from which any reality can appear as such — including what counts as "rational" or "real." This is a much stronger philosophical claim than Harari's: it is not that humans happen to believe shared fictions; it is that the social-imaginary is the condition of possibility for any shared world at all.

### Passage 3 — Society as Self-Creation; Values as Social Creation (p. 787–790 area)

> "[T]he types of motivation (and the corresponding values that polarize and direct human lives) are social creations, that each culture establishes its own values and rears individuals in relation to these. This training is practically all-powerful for there is no 'human nature' that could offer resistance to it; in other words, man is not born with the sense of his life already laid out."

— Castoriadis, *The Imaginary Institution of Society* [NOTE: verify page]

**Relevance**: Castoriadis argues against both naturalism (there is no human nature that fixes values) and economic determinism (values are not reducible to production relations). Each society is a *self-institution* — it creates the values, meanings, and reality-structures through which it organizes life. This is the argument that can replace Harari's "myths" framing with a philosophically rigorous account of how shared meaning-making constitutes the world.

## How to Use Castoriadis in §IA

The thesis's §IA argument (world-making as human existential necessity) uses the shared-fictions claim to show that the human "world" is not given by nature but constituted through collective meaning-making. Castoriadis provides the philosophically rigorous version of this claim:

1. The "world" in the thesis's sense is what Castoriadis calls the *social-historical*: the always-already instituted totality of meanings, symbols, and practices through which a collective constitutes itself and its reality.
2. The world is not a fiction (false belief) but an *imaginary institution* — a creative act of self-constitution that is ontologically prior to any individual consciousness.
3. The metaverse, in Castoriadis's terms, would be a radical rupture in the self-instituting process: it imposes an externally designed, algorithmically managed imaginary over the collectively self-created imaginary of lived social worlds.
