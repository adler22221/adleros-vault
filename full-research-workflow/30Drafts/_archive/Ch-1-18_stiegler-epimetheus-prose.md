## Prose Draft — Full Version

The necessity of world-making — the human imperative to construct an inhabitable order that the universe does not provide ready-made — finds its most precise philosophical articulation not in any modern theory but in the oldest stratum of Greek mythology. In Plato's *Protagoras*, the gods charge Prometheus and his brother Epimetheus with equipping all mortal creatures before releasing them into the world. Epimetheus takes the task upon himself and distributes natural capacities generously and with apparent care: speed to some, strength to others, wings to the birds, shells to the slow, venom to the small. He balances predator against prey, endurance against swiftness, size against fecundity — a living economy of compensated differences, each species equipped with precisely what its survival in a given environment requires. When Prometheus comes to inspect the work, he finds every animal well provisioned — and the human race "naked, unshod, unbedded, and unarmed" (Stiegler 1998, pp. 188–189, citing Plato *Protagoras* 320d–323a). Epimetheus has forgotten the human. He has used up all the available powers on the brute beasts and is left with nothing to give.

Bernard Stiegler's reading of this myth, developed in *Technics and Time, 1: The Fault of Epimetheus* (1998), refuses the temptation to treat this omission as a narrative accident. The fault is structural. "Humans are the forgotten ones," Stiegler writes. "Humans only occur through their being forgotten; they only appear in disappearing" (p. 188). There is no pre-technical human whom Epimetheus forgot and Prometheus subsequently rescued; there is only the human-constituted-by-the-fault, the being whose defining characteristic is the absence of any defining characteristic. Every other creature receives its *dunamis* — its power, its mode of being — from the natural distribution. The human receives nothing: no fixed instinct, no ecological niche, no built-in behavioral repertoire adequate to any particular environment. It is precisely this originary lack that makes technics not supplementary but constitutive. Prometheus's gift of fire and skill-in-the-arts is not an afterthought that fills a gap in an otherwise complete creature; it is the condition without which the human would not exist as a kind of being at all.

Stiegler makes this point with characteristic precision: "There will have been nothing at the origin but the fault, a fault that is nothing but the *de-fault of origin* or the *origin as de-fault* [*le défaut d'origine ou l'origine comme défaut*]. There will have been no appearance except through disappearance" (p. 188). The human does not *have* a world in the way a mole has a burrow or a bird has a nest — structures furnished by evolved instinct and anatomical form. The human must *make* a world, because it is the one animal for whom the universe has provided no ready-made dwelling. The prosthesis — technics, language, culture, the built environment — "is not a mere extension of the human body; it is the constitution of this body *qua* 'human'" (pp. 152–153). The world, understood as the structured human-made habitat nested within the universe, is not an achievement layered on top of a prior human existence: it is what the human *is*, insofar as the human is anything at all.

This insight finds wide corroboration across cultural traditions, each of which encodes the same structural recognition through different narrative means. The Hebrew and Christian mythology of Eden frames the originary moment as expulsion: the human is cast out of a state of sufficiency into a condition of labor and mortality, compelled to till the ground — to produce, by effort and artifice, the sustenance that had previously been given. The direction of the myth is the same: there is a before (the garden, the given world) and an after (the fallen world, the made world), and the human condition is defined by dwelling in the after. What Stiegler's Epimetheus reading makes explicit is that there was never a before: the garden was always already the fault, the forgetting that makes fabrication necessary.

Chinese cosmogonic mythology reaches the same conclusion from the opposite angle. Where the Abrahamic tradition narrates the *loss* of sufficiency, the Chinese tradition narrates the *incompleteness* of the natural order from the outset and the human vocation to complete it. 女媧補天 — the myth of Nüwa mending the sky — begins not with a fall but with a rupture in the firmament, a natural disorder that threatens the cosmos, and proceeds to the act of repair: the goddess melts five-colored stones to seal the broken sky, cuts the legs of a cosmic tortoise to prop up the four cardinal points. 后羿射日 — Hou Yi shooting down the surplus suns — begins with natural excess: ten suns rise together, scorching the earth into uninhabitability, and the human-divine archer must reduce them to the one that is sufficient. 大禹治水 — Yu the Great taming the floods — begins with an overwhelming natural substrate, water that covers the land without order or form, and proceeds through thirteen years of hydraulic engineering, the carving of drainage channels and river courses, the literal construction of a livable territory from formless excess. In each case, the world does not arrive from nature; it must be produced against, from, or within nature, by a compensatory act that transforms the uninhabitable into the habitable.

The pattern is not coincidental. The cross-cultural convergence of these mythologies reflects a structural feature of human existence that the Epimetheus myth names most precisely: the human is the animal that must build its world because the universe does not provide one. This is not a historical contingency — a particular culture's way of narrating a particular social transition. It is the phenomenological condition that all these traditions, independently, have registered and encoded in narrative form. The Promethean theft, the Edenic expulsion, Nüwa's mending, Yu's hydraulic engineering — all are figurations of the same ontological fact: the human being is constituted by the necessity of world-making, and world-making is the compensatory activity of a being whose nature is to have no fixed nature.

It is this structural condition — the originary lack that necessitates the construction and maintenance of a human-made order within the universe — that this thesis takes as its starting point.

## Condensed Version (if full version is too long)

The necessity of world-making finds its sharpest philosophical articulation in the myth Plato's *Protagoras* hands down. In distributing natural powers to all creatures — speed, strength, wings, venom — Epimetheus forgets the human, leaving it "naked, unshod, unbedded, and unarmed" (Stiegler 1998, pp. 188–189). Prometheus steals fire and technical skill as a last resort. Bernard Stiegler reads this not as narrative accident but as structural truth: "Humans are the forgotten ones. Humans only occur through their being forgotten" (p. 188). There is no pre-technical human whom technics later supplements; the human is constituted by its originary lack. The prosthesis — tool, language, the built environment — "is not a mere extension of the human body; it is the constitution of this body *qua* 'human'" (pp. 152–153). The world is not humanity's achievement; it is humanity's condition of existence.

The same recognition appears across cultural traditions, each encoding the structural insight through different narrative means. The Abrahamic expulsion from Eden narrates the onset of necessity: cast out of the given garden, the human must produce by labor what was previously given. Chinese cosmogonic mythology — Nüwa mending the broken sky, Hou Yi shooting down the surplus suns, Yu the Great carving drainage channels through the flood — narrates the incompleteness of the natural substrate from the outset: the cosmos arrives disordered or excessive, and the human vocation is to complete, repair, and render it habitable. In each tradition, the world does not arrive ready-made from the universe; it must be constructed against natural excess or deficiency.

The cross-cultural convergence is not coincidental. It registers a structural feature of human existence: the human is the animal whose biological niche is always already the built environment — the made world that compensates for the lack of any instinct-given one. It is this structural condition — the originary lack that makes world-making ontologically necessary — that grounds the argument of this thesis.
