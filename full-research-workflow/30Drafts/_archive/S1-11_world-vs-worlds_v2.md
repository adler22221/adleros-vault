## I. The Formal Definition: World as Operating System

S1-10 and S1-11 v1 define "the world" as a human-made meaning-space nested within the universe. S1-11 v2 sharpens this into an explicit structural definition that can anchor the rest of the thesis's argument.

**Working definition:** A world is a human-made *operating system* — a structured platform built on a specific mode of artificiality that coordinates the basic conditions of collective survival, meaning-making, and intergenerational transmission within the universe.

Like a digital operating system, a world has three functional levels:

| Level | OS analogue | World analogue |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| Kernel | Foundational logic: process management, memory model, security | Ontological/cosmological foundations: what counts as real, what counts as a being, what the cosmos is; Hui's cosmotechnics; Descola's modes of relating interiority and physicality |
| System calls / APIs | Interfaces between kernel and applications | Institutions, laws, social forms, ritual structures, markets |
| Applications | Programs that run on the OS | Cultural practices, social roles, individual projects, artistic and intellectual forms |

The kernel is the layer that determines what is *possible* within that world — what questions can be asked, what problems can be posed, what solutions can count as valid. It is not normally visible to participants; like an OS kernel, it runs silently beneath the surface of everyday life, becoming apparent only when it fails or when it encounters a different kernel.

This is an *ideal-typical* definition in the Weberian sense. No actual historical society fully instantiates a pure world-kernel. Real human communities are always transitional, hybrid, and internally contested. The Chinese world, the medieval Islamic world, the Western Modern world are ideal types: deliberate one-sided accentuations of structural tendencies, not empirical labels for pure instances. The ideal type captures what is *causally dominant* in a particular configuration without claiming purity. ## II. Critical Realist Re-Reading of Goodman

Nelson Goodman's *Ways of Worldmaking* (1978) [goodmanWaysWorldmaking1978] provides the strongest philosophical warrant for the thesis's claim that worlds are genuinely multiple: "Worldmaking as we know it always starts from worlds already on hand; the making is a remaking." Goodman's account establishes that there is no neutral, un-made "given" behind all the versions — each world is a constructed product.

But Goodman's position is ultimately constructivist and irrealist: for Goodman, the worlds just *are* the versions; there is no world-independent fact that one version gets right and another gets wrong. Each symbol system makes a world, and the plurality of symbol systems yields a genuine plurality of worlds — not descriptions of one world but genuinely different worlds.

The thesis requires a critical realist correction to Goodman. The human-made worlds that this thesis analyzes are not merely symbol systems or conceptual versions: they are *material-institutional-technological structures* with causal efficacy that operates independently of whether any particular participant correctly represents them. A feudal world is not merely a conceptual version; it is a real configuration of property relations, coercive institutions, agricultural practices, and cosmological commitments that imposes structural pressures on its inhabitants regardless of what they believe about it. A world can be misrepresented by those who live within it (ideology, false consciousness, cosmological error) — a point that Goodman's irrealism cannot accommodate.

The thesis therefore adopts Goodman's insight that worlds are genuinely produced and genuinely multiple, while replacing his irrealism with a critical realist ontology: the made worlds are real structures, not merely versions. This matters for the thesis's argument because it means the displacement of the world by the metaverse is not merely a change in conceptual scheme — it is a change in the real causal structures that govern the conditions of collective survival.

## III. Critical Realist Re-Reading of Schutz

Alfred Schutz's "finite provinces of meaning" (*Collected Papers I*, 1962, pp. 207–259 "On Multiple Realities") [schutzCollectedPapersProblem1962] analyzes the internal plurality of the individual's experience: the everyday world of working, the world of dreams, the world of play, the world of scientific theorizing each constitute a distinct "province" with its own "cognitive style, its specific tension of consciousness, its specific épochè, its prevalent form of spontaneity, its specific form of self-experience, and its specific form of sociality and of time" (Schutz, p.230). Each province is shut off from the others by a "leap" (Kierkegaard's term, adopted by Schutz): you cannot gradually slide from the world of daily work into the world of dreams; the transition requires a discontinuous shift.

The critical realist re-reading notes that Schutz's provinces are not merely phenomenological descriptions — they are, at the individual level, *real structures of meaning* that differentially organize attention, motivation, and embodied practice. The "leap" between provinces is not merely a cognitive event but a genuine ontological transition between different real domains of causal influence.

The thesis extends Schutz's insight from the individual to the civilizational level: what Schutz calls "finite provinces of meaning" at the individual level, the thesis calls "worlds" at the civilizational level. Just as an individual moves between provinces with different cognitive styles, human communities historically inhabited different worlds with incommensurable kernel-level structures. And just as Schutz's provinces are real (not merely descriptive), the worlds of this thesis are real — they are not cultural flavor applied to a universal substrate but causally efficacious structures that differentially shape what survival requires, what counts as a good life, and what technologies can and cannot mean.

The Schutzian leap — the discontinuous transition between provinces — is the individual-phenomenological analogue of what the thesis identifies at the civilizational level as kernel-level incommensurability.

## IV. Incommensurability and Component Portability

The kernel-level structure of each world is *incommensurable* with others: you cannot run the applications of one world's kernel on another's. But this does not mean worlds are hermetically sealed. Components circulate across kernels; the difference is that they undergo transformation in the process.

**MacOS / Windows analogy:** The macOS kernel (Darwin/BSD) and the Windows NT kernel are architecturally incommensurable: they have different process models, memory architectures, security models, and system-call interfaces. You cannot run a native macOS application on Windows without a compatibility layer (Wine, Rosetta, etc.) — and compatibility layers are never transparent; they impose constraints, performance costs, and translation artifacts. Yet MacOS and Windows share many components at higher levels of the stack: the PDF format, the MP4 container, the TCP/IP protocol, the HTTP standard. File-level and protocol-level portability coexists with kernel-level incommensurability.

The same structure applies to human worlds:

- **Gunpowder** travels from Song China to medieval Europe. As a material component (a composition with explosive properties), it is portable. But it arrives without its embedding in Chinese cosmological-administrative context (its kernel) — in Europe, it is reorganized under an entirely different kernel (Christian cosmology + Aristotelian natural philosophy + feudal-mercantile political economy), producing a very different trajectory of weapons development.
- **Buddhism** travels from India to China. But the process of transmission is not kernel-neutral transport: it is a kernel-level transformation. Chinese Buddhism (Chan/Zen, Pure Land) is not Indian Buddhism with Chinese decoration; it is a genuinely different articulation of Buddhist categories under a different cosmotechnical kernel (Daoist-Confucian relational ontology). The traveler arrives, but is changed in the crossing.
- **Arabic numerals and algebra** travel from the Islamic world to medieval Europe. The mathematical content is portable; the cosmological embedding (the relation of mathematics to divine unity in Islamic thought vs. to natural-rational order in Christian Scholastic thought) is not transferred — it is re-embedded under a different kernel.

The theoretical point: components can be disaggregated from their kernel and re-embedded in a different one. But re-embedding is not neutral adoption — it is active transformation. This has implications for the thesis's claim about the metaverse.

## V. Corroboration: Hui's Cosmotechnics and Descola's Ontological Plurality

Two major theoretical traditions provide independent corroboration of kernel-level incommensurability.

**Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics** [CWX5W69K — citationKey VERIFY]: Hui argues that different civilizations have different *cosmotechnics* — different ways of relating the cosmological (the moral-cosmological order) to the technical (the material transformation of the world). The Greek cosmotechnics relates *logos* to *techne*; the Chinese cosmotechnics relates *Dao* to *Qi*. These are not different applications of a universal conception of technology — they are incommensurable at the kernel level, because what "technique" means, what it is for, and what order it stands in relation to, differs fundamentally between them.

Hui's account is directly applicable: his "cosmotechnics" is what the thesis calls the kernel of a world's mode of artificiality. Different cosmotechnics → different worlds → kernel-level incommensurability.

**Philippe Descola's four ontologies** [VERIFY: Descola, *Beyond Nature and Culture* (2013, University of Chicago Press) — KWW4S9Q5 is an orphan PDF attachment in Zotero; recommend creating a proper Zotero book entry]: Descola identifies four structural modes of relating interiority (soul, mind, intentionality) and physicality (body, substance, material form):
1. *Naturalism* (Western Modernity): universal nature, plural cultures — the physical substrate is shared, the meaning-configurations differ
2. *Animism*: universal culture (intentional beings share a similar inner life), plural natures (they differ in their physical envelopes)
3. *Totemism*: correlated differences between interior and physical series
4. *Analogism*: all differences — maximum proliferation of singular entities

These are not merely cultural preferences or symbolic differences: they are different structural frameworks for what *kinds of beings* there are and what *kinds of relations* are possible between them. They are, in the thesis's vocabulary, kernel-level differences — and they are genuine, causally efficacious structures that differentially organize what technologies can mean, what the environment is, and what world-making requires.

Descola's four ontologies demonstrate that kernel-level incommensurability is empirically well-documented across human civilizations, not merely a theoretical possibility.

## VI. Implications for the Squeeze

The kernel-level analysis has a direct implication for the thesis's central argument.

The metaverse is not a kernel-neutral technology that can be component-adopted while leaving each world's kernel intact. The metaverse is embedded in — and expresses the culmination of — the Western Modern cosmotechnics: the relation of logos to techne, of formal abstraction to material transformation, of optimization to purpose. Its foundational logic is Descola's naturalism: universal nature (a shared computational substrate), managed by plural but formally commensurable applications.

When communities that operate under different kernels adopt metaverse technologies, they are not adopting a neutral component: they are initiating a process of kernel-level transformation. The compatibility layer — the translation interface between their world's kernel and the metaverse's kernel — imposes structural constraints. Each instance of metaverse adoption reorganizes the conditions under which their kernel's questions can be asked, their world's modes of being can be enacted, their community's survival can be organized.

This is what the thesis means by the squeeze: not that the metaverse is one powerful force among others, but that it operates at the kernel level — the level at which the very conditions of world-making are determined. It is not competing *within* the world but restructuring the substrate *on which* any world runs.
