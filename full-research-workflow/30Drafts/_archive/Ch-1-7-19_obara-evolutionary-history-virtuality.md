## Part 1: Ch-1-7 — Obara's Evolutionary History

### Full Version (Recommended for §I)

> The emergence of world-making as a distinctively human capacity did not occur as a single cognitive threshold but across a series of evolutionary and civilizational transitions — each one both enabled and constrained by transformations of the environment in which human ancestors found themselves. Obara's evolutionary analysis traces this trajectory through four decisive junctures [VERIFY: confirm framing, sequence, and characterization against Obara's text].
>
> The first juncture is geological. The progressive formation of the East African Rift altered the regional climate, converting dense equatorial forest into open savanna — an environment that placed new adaptive pressures on the hominid lineage: pressures toward bipedalism, toward the liberation of forelimbs for manipulation, and toward expanded social coordination across open and unenclosed terrain [VERIFY: Obara's use of the Rift narrative; cross-reference paleoanthropological consensus — Potts and Faith 2015 on African Rift climate change]. The genus Homo that emerged was not simply an organism newly adapted to a changed habitat; it was a lineage whose adaptive responses to environmental instability were already proto-world-making in character. The world did not yet exist; but the selection pressures and adaptive capacities that would eventually constitute it were being organized by geological time.
>
> The second juncture is technical. The systematic production and transmission of tools — from the Oldowan percussion flakes of 2.6 million years ago through the Acheulean hand axes, the coordination of collective hunting, and eventually the mastery of fire — represents not merely cognitive development but the first systematic externalization of adaptive competence into technically transmissible objects [VERIFY: Obara's characterization of tool production as the origin of world-making; compare Stiegler's account of the same transition in Ch-1-18]. Where earlier organisms modified their environments through the activities of their bodies alone, the tool-producing hominid began to modify the environment through technical objects that could persist beyond any individual's lifespan, be transmitted to subsequent generations, and be progressively refined by accumulated collective intelligence. This is the first stirring of what Stiegler calls tertiary retention in the biological record [cross-reference Ch-2-5]: the capacity not merely to act upon the environment but to store the capacity for action in objects that outlast the acting organism. The technical object is the world's first crystallization.
>
> The third juncture is climatic. The termination of the African Humid Period — the desiccation of the Green Sahara that had sustained large populations across what is now desert (approximately 6000–5000 BCE) — forced mass migrations toward river valleys, coastal zones, and the ecological margins where displaced populations converged [VERIFY: date range; Obara's use of this episode; deMenocal 2004 is the primary paleoanthropological source for Green Sahara termination]. Population displacement at this scale is not merely hardship; it is a world-making pressure. Populations forced to reconstitute social life in unfamiliar terrain, to negotiate coexistence with other displaced groups under conditions of resource scarcity, and to develop institutional forms adequate to unprecedented demographic density — these are populations for whom world-making has become an urgent project rather than an unreflective background condition. The Saharan displacement, in Obara's account [VERIFY], is among the proximate causes of the social complexity that would crystallize in the first river-valley civilizations.
>
> The fourth juncture is the most consequential: the agricultural revolution. Agriculture is not merely a new subsistence strategy; it is the first deliberately sustained construction of an artificial environment at civilizational scale [VERIFY: Obara's framing of agriculture as world-construction]. The domestication of cereals and legumes, the selective breeding of livestock, the irrigation of floodplains, the spatial reorganization of settlement around cultivated land — each of these constitutes a layer of the human world: an artificial environment whose conditions of persistence are the product of accumulated human decision and whose breakdown would be catastrophic for the populations that depend on it. For the first time, the world that humans inhabited was in significant part a world that humans had made. And because this made world was constitutively dependent on the human labor and knowledge that sustained it — on the agricultural techniques, the seasonal calendar, the social institutions of field allocation and harvest coordination — it demanded a new kind of human being: one whose survival required active participation in the reproduction of the made world.
>
> The significance of this four-juncture trajectory for the thesis is structural: each transition is simultaneously an environmental pressure and a world-making response. The human is not a being that makes worlds because it has always enjoyed the freedom to do so; it is a being that makes worlds because it has repeatedly encountered environments that outpace its biological adaptive capacity, and has repeatedly developed world-making as a compensatory and survival-oriented response. The world is not a luxury; it is a prosthesis — a technically constituted extension of biological adaptation into the medium of collectively maintained meaning-space. To be human is already to be in a world; and to be in a world is already to be engaged in the project of world-maintenance that the agricultural revolution made visible as a permanent civilizational condition.

Each transition is both environmental pressure and world-making response: the human is not a being free to make worlds, but a being for whom world-making has repeatedly proven necessary for survival.

## Part 2: Ch-1-19 — Ethological Virtuality

### Full Version (Recommended for §IIA)

> Virtuality does not begin with digital technology, or with the screen, or with computation. It is a structural feature of biological organization that precedes the human lineage by tens of millions of years — present in the behavioral systems of animals far simpler than Homo sapiens, though in forms far less complex than those through which the human world is constituted. Obara's analysis of ethological proto-virtualities identifies in several animal behavioral repertoires the structural precursors of what becomes, in the human case, the virtual layer of the world [VERIFY: Obara's specific framing of these examples as "proto-virtual"; verify which examples are Obara's own].
>
> The gorilla's chest-beating display is the paradigm case. When a silverback responds to a perceived threat or social challenge by beating its chest, the display substitutes a symbolic performance for actual combat: it represents the gorilla's fighting capacity, social dominance, and readiness for confrontation — without the costs and risks of physical engagement [VERIFY: Obara's use of this example; compare de Waal on primate display repertoires]. The display is virtual in the precise structural sense: it produces real effects — deterrence, social ordering, conflict avoidance — through a representational medium that refers to capacities rather than enacting them directly. The chest-beat stands for fighting; it is a sign, not the thing itself. This is virtuality's minimal structure: the interposition of a representational layer between biological capacity and social effect.
>
> The crane's courtship dance operates by the same structural logic in a different social register. The ritualized sequence of postures, calls, and movements functions as a representation of reproductive fitness: it signals genetic quality, behavioral coordination capacity, and species-membership to a potential mate without directly demonstrating the biological capacity it represents [VERIFY: Obara's use of crane courtship; compare ethological literature on ritual displays in Gruidae]. The dance is a virtual system of species-identification — a representational layer through which biological compatibility is assessed before biological union. It precedes and makes possible the coupling it anticipates; and what precedes it is itself a complex, choreographed, socially legible representational act.
>
> Collective excitement management — the coordinated vocalizations, postural displays, and movement patterns through which many social species regulate group responses to threat, prey, or social crisis — completes the ethological picture [VERIFY: Obara's specific examples; which species?]. The synchronized alarm call, the coordinated sentinel behavior, the ritualized submission and dominance gestures that manage social tension in primate groups: each of these constitutes a representational medium through which individual physiological states are communicated, integrated, and responded to at the group level. The group's behavioral coherence is achieved through a virtual layer — through representations that mediate between individual biological states and collective social action.
>
> What unites these three cases is the structural presence of a representational layer interposed between organism and environment, between biological capacity and social effect: a layer that refers, signifies, and stands-for in ways that produce real consequences without directly enacting the capacity they represent. This is what the thesis means by "virtuality" in its most general sense — not digital technology or computational simulation, but the structural feature of any behavioral system in which a representational layer mediates between organism and world. In the human case, this virtual layer achieves a constitutive complexity and civilizational reach that has no equivalent in any other species: it becomes the world itself — the shared meaning-space within which human existence is organized. But its evolutionary roots extend into the behavioral repertoires of animals that preceded the hominid lineage by millions of years [VERIFY: evolutionary timeline for display behaviors]. This depth is the most decisive response to any suggestion that human world-making is a contingent cultural invention rather than a deep biological necessity grounded in the evolutionary trajectory that connects gorilla chest-beats to human civilizations.

> Virtuality predates human technology. In Obara's ethological analysis [VERIFY], the gorilla's chest-beating display (conflict avoidance through symbolic combat), the crane's courtship dance (species-identification through representational fitness display), and the coordinated excitement management of social species (group behavioral regulation through representational media) constitute proto-virtual systems: behavioral structures in which a representational layer mediates between biological capacity and social effect. These are virtuality's evolutionary precursors — the same structural logic that, in the human case, develops into the world. The human world is not a discontinuous invention; it is the apex of an evolutionary trajectory that begins wherever representation mediates between organism and environment.
