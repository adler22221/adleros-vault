## Diagnosis: The Problem to Solve

The thesis uses "world" as a technical concept, but three different uses of "world" or "worlds" appear in the thesis and in the committee's comments, and they need to be clearly distinguished:

1. **The world** (singular, definite article) — the thesis's central concept: the human-made meaning-space that mediates between the human and the universe. When the thesis says "the world is being displaced by the metaverse," it means this.

2. **Worlds** (plural) — used in at least two senses:
   a. *Different cultural worlds*: the Sinosphere world, the Western world, indigenous worlds — distinct human-made meaning-spaces produced by different civilizations. "There are many worlds" in this sense.
   b. *Possible worlds* — the technical term in modal logic and analytic philosophy (Leibniz, Kripke, David Lewis): the full set of logically possible states of affairs. This is a completely different use and the thesis must not be confused with it.

3. **The metaverse as a world (or not)** — Mi asks: "Is the metaverse a new world? Is it just one of the worlds?" The thesis must explain why the metaverse is categorically different from (a) — not a cultural world in the Goodman/Schutz sense — and why conflating it with (b) would be a philosophical error.

Mi's concern: by using "world" without carefully distinguishing these uses, the thesis risks appearing philosophically sloppy. The intervention must be terminologically precise.

## The Terminological Argument

### Step 1: "Possible worlds" is a term of art — not the thesis's vocabulary

In modal logic and possible worlds semantics (Leibniz, Kripke, David Lewis), a "possible world" is a logically consistent state of affairs: a complete description of how reality might have been. Possible worlds semantics is a tool for analyzing modal claims ("necessarily," "possibly," "contingently") and does not have the phenomenological or cultural content that the thesis's concept of "world" carries.

The thesis's concept of "world" is explicitly *not* this: it is not a logical construct but a *phenomenological and ontological* concept — the human-made meaning-space that is lived in, maintained through practice, and transmitted across generations. The two concepts share a word but not a content.

This needs to be stated explicitly in a footnote or brief passage to preempt confusion. **Recommended footnote text:**

> *Note on terminology: "World" in this thesis designates the human-made meaning-space through which a community organizes its inhabitation of the universe — a concept in phenomenological and civilizational analysis (Husserl, Heidegger, Schutz, Goodman, Brague). This use must not be confused with "possible worlds" in modal logic and possible worlds semantics (Leibniz, Kripke, David Lewis), where "world" designates a logically consistent state of affairs. The two vocabularies are distinct: the thesis's "world" is lived and culturally constructed; modal logic's "possible worlds" are abstract logical constructs.*

### Step 2: Why "the world" (singular) rather than "worlds" (plural)?

The thesis uses both the singular and the plural, but in different senses:

**The world (singular)** = the thesis's analytical concept: the category of human-made meaning-space as such. When the thesis says "the world is being displaced," it means the *type* of environment that human communities have always constructed — the world as the mediated space between human and universe. This is a generic singular: "the world" in the way "the cell" is a generic singular in biology — referring to the type, not to any particular instance.

**Worlds (plural)** = the multiple instances of this type: the Chinese world, the Andean world, the medieval European world, the contemporary Japanese world. These are all different instantiations of the same type (human-made meaning-space), differing in their particular content but sharing the same formal structure.

The thesis's claim is about the *type* being displaced — about the structural possibility of world-making itself being threatened — not about any particular cultural world. This is why the singular "the world" is the correct usage for the thesis's central claim, even though the thesis fully acknowledges the plurality of cultural worlds.

**Analogy**: "The habitat is being destroyed" (generic singular — the type of environment that organisms inhabit) vs. "the Amazon rainforest is being destroyed" (one particular habitat). The thesis's claim about the world's displacement is at the level of the generic singular: the conditions under which world-making of any kind is possible are being threatened.

### Step 3: Goodman's "worldmaking" and the plural of the singular

Nelson Goodman's *Ways of Worldmaking* (1978) [WZEEWC4D] is the most precise philosophical reference for the relationship between the singular and the plural. Goodman's title says "ways of worldmaking" (plural) because he recognizes that there are many different processes and principles through which worlds are made. But his concept of a "world" — a symbol system that organizes experience according to its own internal logic — is a singular type with plural instances.

Key passage from Goodman (verified in S1-13 lookup): "Worldmaking as we know it always starts from worlds already on hand; the making is a remaking." This "remaking" is always remaking of *a* world (one) from worlds (many previously existing ones) — Goodman treats worldmaking as culturally and historically particular (many versions) while the *type* "world" remains a coherent singular concept.

The thesis follows Goodman's structure: "the world" (singular concept, the thesis's analytical unit) / "worlds" (plural instances, the comparative breadth that §III requires and that Ma asks for).

### Step 4: Schutz's "finite provinces of meaning" and why they are not "possible worlds"

Alfred Schutz's concept of "finite provinces of meaning" (S1-14 lookup, QSKTHW3A — Collected Papers I, p.230) names what the thesis calls the sub-worlds or registers of world-experience: the world of daily life, the world of scientific theory, the world of dreams, the world of play. These are multiple regions *within* the world, not multiple worlds in the plural sense.

Schutz's finite provinces of meaning are explicitly distinguished from the Leibnizian possible worlds: Schutz's provinces are *real, lived* regions of experience, not logical constructs. The thesis draws on Schutz to support the claim that the world contains internal plurality (different provinces, different registers, different contexts of meaning) without the world itself being simply one of many co-equal possible worlds.

The metaverse is not another finite province of meaning within the world (in the Schutz sense). It is a *second background environment* — not a region within the world but a competing environment that threatens the world's coherence as a mediated space between human and universe. This is the V-2 distinction [cross-reference V-2_metaverse-ontological-status.md]: the metaverse is meta-world, not one-world-among-many.

## Recommended Passage for §I (S1-11 Insertion)

> A terminological clarification is required before proceeding. "World" in this thesis designates a human-made meaning-space: the structured environment of significance that a community constructs, maintains, and transmits within the universe. This is a technical use in the phenomenological and civilizational tradition (Husserl, Heidegger, Schutz, Goodman, Brague); it must not be confused with "possible worlds" in modal logic and analytic metaphysics (Leibniz, Kripke, Lewis), where "possible world" designates a logically consistent state of affairs — an abstract formal construct without the cultural and historical content the thesis's concept carries.
>
> The thesis uses both the singular and the plural. *The world* (singular) designates the analytical concept itself: the type of environment that human communities have always constructed to mediate their relationship to the universe. Following Goodman (1978), the singular is generic — "the world" functions as "the cell" does in biology, naming a type that is instantiated in multiple cases. *Worlds* (plural) designates the instances of this type: the Chinese world, the Andean world, the medieval European world, the contemporary Japanese world — each a different realization of the same formal structure, differing in content while sharing the same constitutive relation to the universe as their substrate.
>
> The thesis's claim about the metaverse is not that it is another world alongside these others. Multiple cultural worlds are incommensurable in content while commensurable in type: they are all human-made meaning-spaces nested within the universe, all constitutively dependent on the universe as their substrate. The metaverse is categorically different: it is not a new content within the type but an emerging *second background environment* — a system with governing principles that do not derive from the universe's laws and that tend, in their totalizing logic, to substitute for the universe as the ground of existence. To call the metaverse "a world" or "one of the worlds" would be to misidentify its ontological character: it is not a world but a *meta-world*, not a meaning-space but an environment that threatens the conditions under which meaning-spaces of any kind remain possible.
