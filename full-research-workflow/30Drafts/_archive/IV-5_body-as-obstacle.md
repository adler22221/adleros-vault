## The Body as the Universe's First Claim on the Self

The thesis's argument about "life-as-willed" requires a concrete account of what it is that the will encounters as resistant: the body. The body is not an instrument of the will but its condition — and a condition that imposes its own temporality, materiality, and finitude in ways that the self-finishing tendency experiences as obstacles, failures, or inadequacies.

The body-as-obstacle is not a modern discovery; it is the perennial human experience of the gap between the ideal self (the self as the will conceives it) and the material self (the self as the universe has given it). What is historically specific to the contemporary moment is the emergence of technical environments — culminating in the metaverse — that promise to close this gap: to give the will more complete control over the conditions of its expression by moving existence into environments where the body's material limitations are suspended or overridden.

## Concrete Examples

### 1. The Body's Basic Rhythms as Interruption

Sleep, hunger, fatigue, and pain are the body's most basic assertions of its own agenda against the will's projects. The will wants to continue working; the body demands sleep. The will wants to maintain focus; the body imposes hunger. The will wants to perform; the body breaks down.

Contemporary culture's relationship to these assertions has become systematically antagonistic: stimulants to override fatigue, appetite suppressants to manage hunger, continuous-glucose monitors to optimize metabolic performance, sleep-tracking devices to rationalize sleep. Each technology constitutes an attempt to manage the body's rhythms according to the will's priorities rather than the universe's patterns to which the body is evolutionarily calibrated. The body's assertion of its universe-time against the self's willed-time is experienced as a malfunction rather than a given.

### 2. Aging — The Widening Gap Between Willed and Biological Self

Aging makes the gap between the experienced self (the self the will inhabits) and the biological body increasingly visible and increasingly resistant to closure. The self that the will maintains — its sense of competence, its energy, its social identity — does not age at the rate at which the biological body ages. The result is a form of daily dispossession: the discovery, in the mirror, in the physio's office, in the social situation that privileges youth, that the body is not following the will's project.

The cosmetic industries, the longevity research complex (Calico, Altos Labs), the anti-aging supplement market, and the broader gerontological anxiety of contemporary culture are all responses to this dispossession — all attempts to manage the body's aging according to the will's preference for non-aging. The metaverse offers a more radical solution: in the virtual environment, the avatar need not age; the body's temporal claims on the self are suspended. The Drifter (§IV, Uegaki) who inhabits the metaverse's controlled environments finds there a self-image unconstrained by biological time.

### 3. Illness and Chronic Conditions

Chronic illness and chronic pain impose the body's agenda with particular force: they are not temporary interruptions but structural features of lived experience that make the gap between the willed self and the biological self permanent and unresolvable through individual effort. The person with chronic fatigue syndrome, chronic pain, or a progressive degenerative condition encounters the body as the universe's constant claim — a claim that does not yield to the will's projects, planning, or optimization.

For the thesis: chronic illness constitutes one of the clearest cases in which the body functions as the universe's assertion against the self-finishing tendency. The digital environments that allow individuals with mobility limitations or chronic conditions to maintain social and economic participation — which is genuinely valuable — simultaneously instantiate the universe-metaverse squeeze at the body level: what makes the metaverse livable for these individuals is its suspension of exactly the universe-time constraints (mobility, physical presence, embodied presence) that the body imposes.

### 5. Connection to Self-Petification

Obara's concept of self-petification [VERIFY: Obara; NOT in Zotero] — the tendency to enclose oneself in a totally controlled environment, analogous to the domesticated animal that depends entirely on the controlling system for its conditions of existence — has the body-as-obstacle dynamic as one of its drivers.

The self that experiences its biological body as a persistent obstacle to its willed existence is precisely the self for whom the metaverse's offer of controlled environments is most attractive: environments where the body's material limits (spatial, temporal, physiological) are suspended or overridden, where the avatar presents the unimpeded will rather than the resistant biology. The logic of self-petification is: if the body is the universe's obstacle to the willed life, then the solution is to retreat into environments where the body's claims are minimized.

But self-petification does not resolve the body-as-obstacle; it relocates it. The biological organism continues to require the universe's metabolic inputs (food, oxygen, warmth, movement) even while the psychological self inhabits the metaverse. The self-petified individual becomes simultaneously more metaverse-adapted (psychologically, socially) and more vulnerable to the universe's claims on the body — more dependent on the metaverse's controlled environments precisely as those environments reduce the organism's capacity to manage the universe's demands.
