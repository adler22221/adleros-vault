# I-6 GENERATIVE v2: Methodological Statement — Tightened Synthesis & Corrected Stratification

## What's Wrong with v1 (the document this draft replaces)

The previous I-6 draft committed two errors that this revision corrects:

1. **The Bhaskar mapping was isomorphic.** v1 wrote: "the universe's laws operate at the level of the real… the world is the domain of the actual… human experience is at the empirical level." This is a category mistake. The three domains of Bhaskar's stratified ontology (real / actual / empirical) are not levels of being like *universe / world / human experience*; they are differential modes of access to *each* level. Universe, world, and human experience each have their own real, actual, and empirical aspects. The error obscures rather than clarifies the thesis's central argumentative move.

2. **The synthesis was too lightly named.** v1 invoked Bhaskar by name but did not develop the link to Continental philosophy of technology (Stiegler, Heidegger, Baudrillard) or East Asian environmental philosophy (Obara, Uegaki, Miura) — the three traditions whose synthesis actually constitutes the thesis's theoretical position.

This draft fixes both, and adds what v1 did not provide: two presentations of the methodology — an *explicit* critical-realist version (for readers who follow Bhaskar) and an *analytical-friendly* version (for Mi and other readers in the analytic tradition who do not).

## Section 1: The Theoretical Synthesis (Three Traditions)

This thesis develops its argument through a synthesis of three theoretical traditions whose convergence has not previously been articulated as a unified framework:

**(i) Continental philosophy of technology** — principally Bernard Stiegler's account of technicity as the originary condition of the human (the "fault of Epimetheus," tertiary retention, exosomatization), with selective use of Martin Heidegger on the question of technology as a mode of revealing-and-concealing (*Gestell*) and Jean Baudrillard on the order of simulacra and the autonomization of the sign. From this tradition the thesis takes its account of *how artificiality constitutes the human world* rather than supplementing a pre-given human nature.

**(ii) Critical realism** — Roy Bhaskar's stratified ontology, retroductive method, and dialectical critical realism. From this tradition the thesis takes its account of *how to argue from observable phenomena to underlying generative mechanisms*, and its commitment to a realist ontology compatible with epistemic relativity. Bhaskar's framework is what permits the thesis to claim that the metaverse is real (in the sense that it has causal efficacy on human existence) without claiming that the thesis's own description of it is the final word.

**(iii) East Asian environmental philosophy** — principally Obara Hideo's evolutionary-ecological account of human self-domestication (`小原秀雄`, MWBGTIFF, 2Q8KYMHC, 6L278LXZ), Uegaki Takahide's analysis of self-completing society and the historical typology of post-war Japanese subjectivity (`上柿崇英`, VESTLJGF, CYAAA3IK), and Miura Nagamitsu's intellectual-historical reconstruction of the relationship between environmental thought and society (`三浦永光`, NUF7WCJD). From this tradition the thesis takes its account of *what specifically Modern Western artificiality has done to the human-environment relationship* and *what alternative civilizational trajectories have looked like*.

These three traditions are not merely additively combined. They are used because each addresses a different layer of the same problem: Continental philosophy of technology articulates the *mechanism* (how artificiality constitutes worlds), critical realism provides the *logic of inference* (how to argue from squeeze-symptoms back to squeeze-mechanisms), and East Asian environmental philosophy supplies the *historical and evolutionary specificity* (what particular trajectory is being analyzed and what alternatives have existed).

## Section 2: Theoretical Orientations (What Kind of Project This Is)

This synthesis instantiates four methodological orientations:

**Environmental** — the unit of analysis is the relationship between organism and environment, not the organism alone. The metaverse is analyzed not as a technology used by individuals but as a *background environment* that produces selection pressures on the populations that come to inhabit it.

**Ecological** — this environmental relationship is mediated by structural feedback loops between organism and environment, in which each shapes the other across multiple temporal scales. Niche construction theory (Odling-Smee, Laland, Feldman) is the immediate biological analogue; Obara's self-domestication thesis is its civilizational extension.

**Evolutionary** — the human being is treated as an evolved organism whose evolutionary history did not stop at the emergence of *Homo sapiens* but continues through the cultural-technical-prosthetic processes that Stiegler calls *exosomatic evolution* (after Lotka). The metaverse imposes new selection pressures whose evolutionary consequences are the thesis's ultimate object.

**Emergentist** — higher-level structures (worlds, modes of artificiality, the metaverse) have their own causal powers irreducible to the lower levels they depend on (universe, biology, individual cognition). This is what makes the squeeze argument intelligible: the metaverse can impose its own laws on human existence without violating the laws of the universe, because the relationship between the two is one of emergent dependence (Polanyi's dual-control structure), not reduction.

These four orientations intersect with three established disciplinary fields: **philosophy of technology** (the Continental side), **civilizational studies / Big History** (Obara, Uegaki, Needham, the comparative-civilizational dimension), and **critical theory** (the Frankfurt School heritage in the analysis of Western Modernity's self-totalization, accessed primarily through Bhaskar's reading of the Platonic-Aristotelian fault line in *Plato Etc.*).

## Section 3: Methodological Tools

**Retroduction.** Following Bhaskar (CDY2DJWM, pp. — `[VERIFY: Bhaskar Dialectic, retroduction passages]`), this thesis proceeds by retroductive inference: from observable phenomena (the empirical) to a hypothesis about the generative mechanisms (the real) that would, *if they existed*, produce phenomena of the kind observed. This is distinct from induction (generalization from observed regularities) and from hypothetico-deductive testing (deriving observable predictions from a postulated theory). It is closer to what analytic philosophy of science calls *inference to the best explanation* — but with an explicit ontological commitment that the explanation, if successful, refers to real structures of the world.

**Stratified ontology.** Bhaskar distinguishes three domains of the world: the **real** (entities and structures that exist whether or not anything happens because of them), the **actual** (events that happen, whether or not anyone observes them), and the **empirical** (events as observed). These are nested: empirical ⊂ actual ⊂ real. Crucially — and this is what v1 of this draft got wrong — these three domains are *not* a stratification of being (universe → world → human experience) but a differential structure of access *internal to each level of being*. Section 5 below works out the consequences for the thesis.

**Laminated systems.** A laminated system is one whose causal structure cannot be reduced to any single level but requires the coordinated operation of mechanisms at multiple, irreducible levels (physical, biological, psychological, social, cultural). The squeeze, in this thesis, is laminated: it cannot be explained by physics alone, biology alone, psychology alone, or sociology alone. It is what happens *between* these levels — at the interface between biology (the universe in us) and the algorithmically governed artificial environment (the metaverse). Bhaskar's seven-level stratification (`physical-chemical → biological → psychological-cognitive → socio-economic → cultural-discursive → normative-ethical → spiritual-existential`) is the formal scaffold; this thesis uses a simplified version centered on the universe / world / metaverse triad.

**POSWID** (the principle "the purpose of a system is what it does," Stafford Beer) — invoked as a heuristic against intentionalist explanations of the metaverse. Whatever any individual designer intends, the metaverse-as-system *does* what it does; its functional output is the analysandum, not its programmers' stated purposes. This is the methodological corollary of treating the metaverse as a background environment rather than a tool.

## Section 4: The Corrected Stratification Mapping

The thesis's central conceptual triad — **universe / world / metaverse** — is a stratification *of being*: distinct kinds of background environment that humans inhabit. Bhaskar's triad — **real / actual / empirical** — is a stratification *of access*: distinct kinds of relationship one can have to whatever level of being is in question. The two stratifications cut across each other; they are not isomorphic.

The correct picture has three columns and three rows:

| | **Universe** | **World** | **Metaverse** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Real** (generative mechanisms) | Physical and biological laws operating regardless of observation | Structural mechanisms of human cultural-technical existence (Western Modernity's disposition to domination, the structural tendency toward self-completion) | The metaverse as second background environment imposing adaptational pressures incommensurable with those of the universe |
| **Actual** (events) | Geological, evolutionary, climatological events | Historical events, institutional formations, technological developments | The unfolding cybernetic loop (AI decisions made, algorithmic codes executed, IoT signals exchanged) |
| **Empirical** (observed) | Scientific observation, lived weather, perceived ecology | Observed politics, news headlines, individual social experience | The metaverse as XR (Stephenson's coinage; Meta's Horizon Worlds; consumer VR) |

The thesis's central argumentative move — the move that makes the squeeze hypothesis non-trivial — is to insist on the distinction between **the metaverse at the empirical level** (XR, headsets, virtual worlds — the surface phenomena that *Reality+*-style philosophy and consumer-tech discourse take to *be* the metaverse) and **the metaverse at the real level** (the underlying generative mechanism: a self-completing artificiality that has become, or is becoming, a second background environment). The April 2026 collapse of consumer XR (Meta's $73B Reality Labs losses; the Horizon Worlds shutdown) is *empirically* the death of the metaverse; *really*, it is no such thing — the underlying mechanism continues to operate through industrial digital twins, AI-spatial platforms, and algorithmic governance regardless of whether anyone wears a headset.

Other mappings the thesis makes use of are deliberately *less specific* than v1 suggested. The world is not "the actual"; it has a real level (its mechanisms), an actual level (its events), and an empirical level (what is observed of it). The universe is not "the real"; it has a real level (laws), an actual level (events), and an empirical level (observed phenomena). The squeeze itself — the central mechanism the thesis postulates — is at the **real** level: it produces actual events (psychosomatic symptoms, livelihood failures, governance breakdowns) that are then observed empirically. The thesis's task is to retroduce from the empirical signs back to the real mechanism.

## Section 5: Two Presentations

The methodology described above can be presented in two ways. Mi's intellectual orientation — virtue epistemology in the analytic tradition — does not include critical realism as a familiar reference point. Either of the following two presentations is therefore appropriate; the choice depends on rhetorical strategy and reader context.

### Version A: Explicit Critical Realism (~250 words)

This thesis is grounded methodologically in critical realism (Bhaskar, 2008; 2009). It takes a stratified view of being: at any level — the physical universe, the human world, the emerging metaverse — there are structures and mechanisms that exist whether or not they happen to be activated, events that occur whether or not they are observed, and observations that are filtered through the conceptual and perceptual apparatus of the observer. The thesis proceeds *retroductively*: from empirical phenomena (the signs of squeeze cataloged in §VI — psychosomatic malfunctions, singularization of livelihood, lives governed by opaque laws) back to the generative mechanism that would, if it existed, produce phenomena of this kind. The mechanism the thesis postulates — the squeeze as an evolutionary double-bind between two incommensurable background environments — is real in Bhaskar's sense: it has causal powers exercised whether or not anyone recognizes it. The methodological corollary is that a critique of the metaverse should not be confined to the empirical domain (the XR products that are now collapsing as a market category) but should reach for the real mechanism (the self-completing artificiality whose operation continues regardless of any specific technical instantiation). Theoretically, the thesis synthesizes three traditions: Continental philosophy of technology (Stiegler, Heidegger, Baudrillard) for the account of technicity; critical realism (Bhaskar) for the logic of multi-level inference; and East Asian environmental philosophy (Obara, Uegaki, Miura) for the historical and civilizational specificity of what Modern Western artificiality has done. Methodologically, the thesis works through retroduction, stratified ontology, the analysis of laminated systems, and the heuristic that the purpose of a system is what it does (POSWID).

### Version B: Analytical-Friendly Common-Sense Continental (~250 words)

This thesis takes a multi-level approach to its object. It distinguishes — at every layer of analysis — between the *underlying structures* that produce phenomena (whether or not anyone observes them), the *events* those structures generate (whether or not those events are recorded), and the *observations* through which we come to know that any of this is happening. This is a familiar distinction in the philosophy of science: it underwrites the difference between, say, the laws of thermodynamics (which obtain regardless of observation), the heat events those laws produce, and the temperature readings by which we come to know about them. The thesis's argumentative strategy is to argue from observed phenomena (the cataloged signs of human malaise under late artificiality) to a hypothesis about the underlying causal structure that best explains them. This is what philosophers of science call inference to the best explanation; the thesis claims that the best explanation for the empirical phenomena is what it calls "the squeeze" — a structural double-bind between two background environments imposing incommensurable adaptational pressures. The methodology integrates three theoretical resources: the Continental philosophy of technology of Stiegler, Heidegger, and Baudrillard for the analysis of how technical artifacts and systems *constitute* (rather than merely supplement) the human form of life; the analysis of multi-level emergent causation for the logic of inferring across the physical, biological, social, and existential layers; and East Asian environmental thought (Obara on self-domestication; Uegaki on the structure of post-war Japanese society; Miura on the intellectual history of the human-environment relation) for the specific civilizational diagnosis the thesis makes.
