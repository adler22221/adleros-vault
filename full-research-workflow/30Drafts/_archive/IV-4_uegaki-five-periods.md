## The Five Periods: Causal Narrative for §IV

### Period 1: Confrontation with the West (明治維新 → 1945)

The first period is the era of Japan's encounter with Western Modernity — from the Meiji Restoration (1868) through the Pacific War's end (1945). The defining experience of this period is the confrontation with a civilization whose technical and institutional forms threatened Japan's continued existence as an independent entity. The adaptive response — rapid, intentional adoption of Western technical and institutional forms — constituted what Uegaki identifies as the first major transformation of the Japanese self-understanding: from a world-anchored identity (rooted in place, tradition, and cosmological framework) toward a performance-oriented identity (oriented toward competence in relation to external standards of technical and institutional achievement).

The psychic structure produced: a self understood primarily as the subject of achievement — achievement measured against an externally defined standard (the West). The self is already, in this period, beginning to detach from its world-substrate and reconstitute itself as a performer before an audience it did not choose.

### Period 2: Reconstruction and High-Growth Collectivism (1945–1960s)

The second period is postwar reconstruction and Japan's economic miracle: the collective mobilization of social resources for national reconstruction and high-growth industrialization. This period is characterized by what Uegaki identifies as a specifically collective form of the pursuit of life-as-willed: the individual life-project is subordinated to the group (the company, the nation, the family) which pursues its collective project with the intensity that liberal individualism would apply to the individual.

The social form: the company-man (会社人間), the developmental state, the "Japanese model" of collective achievement through individual subordination. The self is defined by its group-membership and group-performance. Achievement remains the dominant value, but achievement is understood as collective.

The psychic structure: a self constituted by group-identification, whose individuality is expressed through role-performance within the group rather than through self-authored life-design.

### Period 3: Consumerist Nuclear Family and the Hollowing Out of the Lifeworld (1970s–1980s)

The third period is what Uegaki identifies as the emergence of the consumerist nuclear family and the 生活世界の空洞化 (hollowing out of the lifeworld). Economic success creates abundance; abundance creates the conditions for consumption-based individual expression; the nuclear family displaces the extended family and the community as the primary social unit. The domestic sphere becomes the locus of consumer choice — the home as the site of self-expression through commodities.

The social form: the salary-man household, with its gendered division between productive and domestic spheres; consumer culture as the medium of self-expression; the progressive withdrawal of social life from public communal spaces into the private domestic sphere.

The hollowing out of the lifeworld refers to the evacuation of shared meaning-making from the communal world: as consumption displaces communal practice as the primary medium of social expression, the shared world — the communally maintained meaning-space — loses its density. What remains is a collection of private consumer choices that share a market but not a world.

The psychic structure: a self that is beginning to understand its freedom as consumer freedom — the freedom to choose among available options — rather than as the freedom of participation in a communally constituted world. The individual is becoming the consumer-individual: the subject of preference-satisfaction rather than world-co-constitution.

### Period 4: The Recognition Anxiety Period and the Drifter (1990s–2000s)

The fourth period emerges from the collapse of the collective achievement framework: the bursting of the asset-price bubble (1991), the Lost Decade, the end of lifetime employment security, and the delegitimization of the company-man identity. Without the collective framework within which individual achievement was recognized and validated, the recognition structure that constituted the individual's social identity collapses.

The result is 承認不安 (recognition anxiety): the anxiety of a self whose social identity has been constituted by recognized achievement within a collective framework, when the framework disappears and the achievement is no longer recognizable by anyone. The self does not know who it is when the collective that recognized its performance dissolves.

From this recognition anxiety, Uegaki identifies the emergence of the 漂流人 (Drifter): a social type characterized by the absence of stable social anchoring, drifting between social contexts, identities, and commitments without being able to form the sustained attachments through which a stable self is constituted. The Drifter is not simply lonely or alienated; the Drifter is a self in whom the world-making and self-making capacities have been structurally impaired by the dissolution of the collective frameworks within which these capacities were previously exercised.

The psychic structure: a self without anchoring — seeking recognition but without the communal framework within which recognition is possible; moving between contexts without the commitments that make any context a world. The Drifter is permanently between worlds, unable to inhabit any of them fully.

**The Drifter as the Psychological Subject of the Squeeze:**

The Drifter is the psychological subject for whom the metaverse is most seductive: a being already detached from world-anchoring, already seeking recognition through platform performance (social media as recognition economy), already constituted by consumer identity rather than communal practice. The metaverse offers the Drifter what the dissolved collective framework no longer provides — a social environment structured around recognition, achievement, and identity performance — but at the cost of the remaining connections to universe-substrate that the world, even in its hollowed-out form, still maintained.

The Drifter's metaverse retreat is not a failure of character; it is the structurally predictable response of a being whose social world has been evacuated of the communal content that makes world-anchoring possible. The squeeze for the Drifter is particularly acute: there is already little world left to be squeezed; the metaverse finds a being primed for absorption.

### Period 5: The Self-Enclosed Society and the Ethics of Non-Intervention (2010s–present)

The fifth period is what Uegaki calls 自己完結社会 (the self-enclosed / self-finishing society): a social form in which individuals increasingly organize their entire relational and productive life within self-selected, algorithmically managed personal environments — the curated social media feed, the personalized content stream, the niche interest community — without engaging with the uncontrolled, unchosen social world that the traditional lifeworld required.

The associated normative framework: the Ethics of Non-Intervention (非干渉の倫理). Where traditional social ethics imposed obligations of reciprocal engagement — one was obligated to participate in communal life, to maintain relationships of mutual aid, to engage with unchosen neighbors and coworkers — the ethics of non-intervention reframes social distance as a virtue: respecting others' autonomy means not encroaching on their self-enclosed world. The normative ideal is not engagement but non-intrusion.

The self-enclosed society is the social expression of what the thesis calls "life-as-willed": a social order in which the organizing ideal is each individual's autonomous construction of their own relational environment, without the friction of unchosen communal obligation. It is also the social context in which the metaverse's self-completing tendency finds its cultural legitimation: if life-as-willed is the normative ideal, then an environment that allows total design of one's own existential conditions is not a pathology but a fulfillment.

## Recommended Passage for §IV

> The self-finishing tendency that §IV analyzes as an ontological feature of contemporary human life has a specific historical genealogy in the Japanese context — one that Uegaki's sociology traces through five periods of postwar social development.
>
> The first two periods (the confrontation with Western Modernity and the collective high-growth era) established a self constituted by achievement and group-membership — a self oriented toward performance before an external audience rather than rootedness in a locally anchored world. The third period (the consumerist nuclear family) hollowed out the communal world that this achievement-orientation had originally served: as consumption replaced communal practice as the medium of social expression, the shared meaning-space contracted and the private consumer-self expanded. The fourth period (the Lost Decade's recognition anxiety) dissolved even the collective framework of achievement: without the company and the national growth project that had organized recognition, the self lost its anchoring and the Drifter social type emerged — a being between worlds, seeking recognition without the communal framework that makes recognition possible.
>
> The Drifter is the psychological subject for whom the metaverse is most immediately legible as a refuge: a being already detached from world-anchoring, already constituted by consumer identity and platform-mediated recognition, already primed for the total environment of self-selected relational life that the metaverse offers. The fifth period (the self-enclosed society and its Ethics of Non-Intervention) represents the normative crystallization of this trajectory: a social form in which life-as-willed — the autonomous construction of one's own existential environment — has become not merely a psychological tendency but a cultural ideal.
>
> The squeeze thesis must be read against this historical context. The psychosomatic malfunctions, livelihood singularization, and governance disruption that §VI documents are not simply the effects of new technology on a previously stable population; they are the compounding of structural vulnerabilities accumulated across five historical periods, in a population that arrived at the metaverse's threshold already hollowed-out, already Drifting, already organized by the self-finishing ideal that makes the metaverse's claim on existence culturally irresistible.
