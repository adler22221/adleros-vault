## Key Passages — Extracted from PDF

### Passage 1 — The Central Problem (Section 50a, p. 1)
> "When I first formed the idea, about 1938, of writing a systematic, objective and authoritative treatise on the history of science, scientific thought, technology and medicine in the Chinese culture-area, I regarded the essential problem as that of why modern science had not developed in Chinese civilisation (or Indian or Islamic) but only in that of Europe. Nevertheless... As the years went by... I came to realise that there is a second question at least equally important, namely, why, between the -1st century and the 15th century, was Chinese civilisation much more efficient than occidental in gaining natural knowledge and in applying it to practical human needs?"

— Needham, Joseph. "Science and Society in East and West." In *Science and Civilisation in China*, Vol. 7, Pt. 2. Cambridge UP, 2004. §50(a), p. 1.

**Relevance:** This is Needham's own formulation of the double question that structures his lifework. The first question (why didn't modern science arise in China?) is well known. The second (why was Chinese civilization *more* successful scientifically before the 15th century?) is what Needham develops in Vol. 7 — and it is this second question that is directly relevant to the thesis's §III argument about Western modernity as an *exception* rather than the norm.

### Passage 2 — Geography as Social Destiny (Section 50a, pp. 1–2)
> "Europe, as I have often said, is like an archipelago. There is the Baltic, the North Sea, the Irish Channel, the Mediterranean, the Aegean, the Black Sea and then the great Atlantic outside the Pillars of Hercules — all inviting maritime commerce and the activities of sea-captains. In contrast to all this, China is a vast land-mass, well suited for the activities of peasant-farmers in their millions. It is hardly surprising that the civilisations turned out to be so different."

— Needham, §50(a), pp. 1–2.

**Relevance:** Needham here gives the geographical basis for his civilizational comparison. The Mediterranean's fragmented, sea-facing geography produced maritime, commercial, and eventually military-interventionist cultures. China's continental land-mass produced agrarian, administrative, and harmony-oriented social structures. This is the empirical anchor for the thesis's claim that "the Mediterranean mode of artificiality" is rooted in particular modes of production.

### Passage 3 — The Non-Interventionist vs. Interventionist Divide (Section 50a, p. 17)
> "It might thus be said that this non-interventionist conception of human activity was, to begin with, propitious for the development of the natural sciences. [...] However, if the non-interventionist character of the 'villages-prince' relationship engendered a certain conception of the world which was propitious to the progress of science, it had certain natural limitations. It was not congruent with characteristically occidental 'interventionism', so natural to a people of shepherds and sea-farers."

— Needham, §50(a), p. 17.

**Relevance:** This is the key passage. Needham distinguishes a *non-interventionist* Chinese cosmological disposition (rooted in Taoist "action at a distance," hydraulic agriculture, and the bureaucratic-civil tradition) from a characteristically *occidental interventionism* "natural to a people of shepherds and sea-farers." Note that Needham does not simply say one is better than the other — the non-interventionist disposition was propitious for early science but ultimately could not produce the breakthrough of modern science, because it was incompatible with the fusing of mathematics and active experimental intervention. This nuance is important for the thesis's §III argument: Western modernity's "disposition to domination" is both an enabling condition for its global reach *and* a structural pathology.

### Passage 4 — The Mechanistic Imperative as Pathology (Section 50, p. 75)
> "They feel too that science encourages a 'mechanistic imperative', that is to say, an urge to apply every piece of knowledge, in every possible way, whether or not its application is health-giving for human beings, or preservative of the non-human world in which they have to live. The scientific world-view is thus accused of a cerebral and ego-centric mode of consciousness, completely heartless in its activity. [...] In a word, technocracy is rampant, and the more complete the domination of Nature, the more fully does it become possible for ruling elites to increase their control of individual human behaviour."

— Needham, §50, p. 75.

**Relevance:** Needham here (summarizing the anti-science movement's critique, to which he is partly sympathetic) articulates the "mechanistic imperative" as a structural feature of Western scientism — the compulsion to apply knowledge regardless of consequences. This directly maps onto the thesis's "disposition to domination" in §III. That Needham — himself the foremost historian of Chinese science, and a biochemist — identifies this as a specifically Western pathology strengthens the thesis's comparative argument.

## Nuance and Interpretation for the Thesis

**What the committee is asking for:** 馬's concern is that Needham's argument is reduced to a summary claim without showing the reader *what Needham actually saw* in the historical evidence. The following points need to be made explicit:

1. **Needham's method is comparative and empirical.** He spent fifty years studying Chinese scientific history to reach these conclusions. The comparison is not speculative but based on extensive primary-source work across Chinese astronomy, chemistry, medicine, engineering, and social history.

2. **The "non-interventionist vs. interventionist" distinction is not Needham's value judgment** but his characterization of two civilizational orientations, each with enabling conditions and limitations. The non-interventionist orientation enabled China's pre-Renaissance scientific lead; the interventionist orientation enabled the Western breakthrough to modern science but at the cost of what Needham calls the "mechanistic imperative."

3. **The relevant axis for the thesis is not "who did more science"** but "what kind of relationship to nature each civilization's worldview encodes." Needham's contribution is to root these worldviews in material conditions (geography, modes of production, social structure) rather than in cultural essence or racial character.

4. **Note:** Needham uses "interventionism" in a descriptive-structural sense. The thesis uses "disposition to domination" (following the Frankfurt School and Brague). These are compatible but not identical — the thesis should acknowledge this terminological difference in a footnote.

## Recommended Citation Form for §II/§III

> Joseph Needham — biochemist, Sinologist, and lead author of the monumental *Science and Civilisation in China* — identifies two civilizational dispositions rooted in divergent material conditions. European culture, organized around sea-facing geographies "inviting maritime commerce and the activities of sea-captains," developed what Needham calls "characteristically occidental 'interventionism'" — an active, experimental, and ultimately domination-oriented relationship to nature (2004, pp. 1–2, 17). Chinese civilization, by contrast, structured around the activities of "peasant-farmers in their millions" across a continental land-mass, cultivated a "non-interventionist conception of human activity" whose cosmological expression was the Taoist principle of action-at-a-distance (p. 17). Both orientations were propitious for early scientific development, but it was the interventionist tradition that produced the qualitative leap of modern science — and, with it, what Needham identifies as the "mechanistic imperative": the urge to "apply every piece of knowledge, in every possible way, whether or not its application is health-giving for human beings" (p. 75). The thesis's §III borrows Needham's civilizational comparison as empirical grounding for the claim that Western modernity's "disposition to domination" is a historically specific formation, not a universal human tendency.
