## Key Passages — Epimetheus and World-Making as Structural Necessity

### Passage 1 — The Protagoras Version of the Myth (pp. 188–189)

The myth as Stiegler reads it from Plato's *Protagoras* (320d–323a):

> "Once upon a time, there existed gods but no mortal creatures. When the appointed time came for these also to be born, the gods formed them within the earth out of a mixture of earth and fire and the substances which are compounded from earth and fire. And when they were ready to bring them to the light, they charged Prometheus and Epimetheus with the task of equipping them and allotting suitable powers [*dunameis*] to each kind. Now Epimetheus begged Prometheus to allow him to do the distribution himself... In his allotment he gave to some creatures strength without speed, and equipped the weaker kinds with speed. Some he armed with weapons... Thus he made his whole distribution on a principle of compensation, being careful by these devices that no species should be destroyed... Now Epimetheus was not a particularly clever person, and before he realized it he had used up all the available powers on the brute beasts, and being left with the human race on his hands unprovided for, did not know what to do with them. While he was puzzling about this, Prometheus came to inspect the work, and found the other animals well off for everything, but man naked, unshod, unbedded, and unarmed... Prometheus therefore, being at a loss to provide any means of salvation for man, stole from Hephaestus and Athena the gift of skill in the arts [*ten enteknen sophian*], together with fire — for without fire there was no means for anyone to possess or use this skill — and bestowed it on man. In this way man acquired sufficient resources to keep himself alive..."

— Stiegler, *Technics and Time, 1*, pp. 188–189 (citing Plato, *Protagoras*, 320d–323a).

**Relevance for §I mythology section:** The myth encodes the structural claim at the heart of the thesis's Section I: *the human has no fixed natural equipment*. Where every other animal receives specific powers (speed, strength, claws, wings), the human is *forgotten* — left without a defined biological niche. This originary lack is not a fall from a prior fullness; it is the condition of the human as such. Technics (fire, skill in the arts) is not humanity's supplement but its constitution: the human *is* the technical being, not a pre-technical being who later acquires tools.

### Passage 2 — "Humans Are the Forgotten Ones" (p. 188)

> "It is immediately by deviating from the equilibrium of animals, from tranquillity — a departure engendered by the fault of Epimetheus — that mortals occur. Before the deviation, there is nothing. Then the accidental event happens, the fault of Epimetheus: to have forgotten humans. Humans are the forgotten ones. Humans only occur through their being forgotten; they only appear in disappearing."

— Stiegler, *Technics and Time, 1*, p. 188.

**Relevance:** This is the philosophical core. Stiegler reads Epimetheus's "fault" not as a moral failure but as the *originary structure of the human*: humanity is constituted by a default, a lack-at-the-origin (*le défaut d'origine*). This means world-making — the construction of artificial environments to compensate for what the human does not receive from nature — is not contingent or historical but *ontologically necessary*. The human must build worlds because it is the being who was not built-for a world.

### Passage 3 — The Default of Origin (p. 188)

> "There will have been nothing at the origin but the fault, a fault that is nothing but the *de-fault of origin* or the *origin as de-fault* [*le défaut d'origine ou l'origine comme défaut*]. There will have been no appearance except through disappearance. Everything will have taken place at the same time, in the same step."

— Stiegler, *Technics and Time, 1*, p. 188.

**Relevance:** "Origin as de-fault" — the origin of the human *is* the lack, the forgetting. There is no pre-technical human who then falls into technics. Technics is originary. This is directly relevant to the thesis's argument that the world is not a supplement to the universe but a structural necessity of human existence: the universe presents no pre-built human niche; the world must be made.

### Passage 4 — The Prosthesis as Constitution (pp. 152–153)
*(For use in S1-18 and the §I prosthesis argument — flagging here for cross-reference)*

> "A 'prosthesis' does not supplement something, does not replace what would have been there before it and would have been lost: it is added. [...] The prosthesis is not a mere extension of the human body; it is the constitution of this body *qua* 'human'... It is not a 'means' for the human but its end."

— Stiegler, *Technics and Time, 1*, pp. 152–153.

**Relevance:** The prosthesis-as-constitution claim grounds world-making as an existential rather than instrumental activity. The human does not *use* the world as a tool; the world *constitutes* the human as human. This applies directly to the Epimetheus frame: the "fault" is not a problem to be solved by technics but the condition that makes human existence possible through technics.

## Disambiguation: Where Is This in Stiegler's Argument?

The Epimetheus passage appears in **Part II, Chapter 1: "Prometheus's Liver"** of *Technics and Time, 1*. The structure of Stiegler's argument:

1. Part I: Leroi-Gourhan's paleontological account of technical evolution (pp. 49–182)
2. Part II, Ch. 1: Reading the Prometheus/Epimetheus myth against Heidegger's existential analytic (pp. 183–219) — **this is the section of interest**
3. Part II, Ch. 2–3: Heidegger and the technical structure of who/what (pp. 220+)

The Epimetheus reading is Stiegler's own philosophical intervention: he reads the myth as a "mythology of the origin of technics" that encodes a temporal structure of anticipation-and-delay (*promêtheia* / *epimêtheia*) that Heidegger's analysis of finitude requires but suppresses.

**Key claim for thesis:** Stiegler argues that the Prometheus/Epimetheus myth is *thanatological*: it is a mythology of mortality, not of progress. The theft of fire is not triumphant — it is the ambiguous gift that makes mortals what they are (dying beings who must work, fabricate, build worlds). This applies to the thesis's §I argument: the world is not humanity's achievement but its compensation for what nature did not provide.

## Integration Note for §I

The Stiegler/Epimetheus passage should follow or precede the "Expulsion from Eden" and Chinese mythology references (女媧補天, 后羿射日, 大禹治水). The sequence:

1. **Eden** — expulsion as loss of original sufficiency; now must work to survive
2. **Epimetheus** (Stiegler) — philosophical reading: the fault is originary, not a fall; technics is constitutive
3. **Chinese cosmogonic myths** (女媧補天: Nüwa mending the sky; 后羿射日: Hou Yi shooting down extra suns; 大禹治水: Yu taming the floods) — all are world-*repair* myths: the natural substrate is broken or excessive, and human action constructs a livable order within/upon it

The pattern across traditions: the world does not arrive ready-made; it must be built, repaired, and maintained against natural excess or deficiency. The "fault of Epimetheus" gives this pattern its sharpest philosophical articulation.

**Note on 大禹治水:** This myth is particularly apt — Yu's flood control is literally world-making as hydraulic engineering, transforming an uninhabitable cosmos into a livable territory. Cross-reference with the Polanyi boundary-conditions argument (S1-4): the world's "laws" emerge through the human imposition of form on formless natural process.
