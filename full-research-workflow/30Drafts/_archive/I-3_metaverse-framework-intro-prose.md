## Recommended Introduction §B Passage (~350 words)

### Version A — Full, for insertion into Introduction §B after the XR critique

The popular discourse defines the metaverse by reference to specific technologies: virtual reality headsets, persistent online environments, digital avatars, blockchain-based digital ownership. This thesis does not begin from that definition. The reason is not arbitrary: a definition anchored to specific current technologies would bind the concept to the historical moment in which those technologies are dominant — and would be falsified by the moment those technologies are superseded or abandoned, as the 2022–2026 collapse of the consumer XR market has already demonstrated.

Instead, this thesis develops the concept of the metaverse through a process of conceptual derivation. The argument requires, in sequence: an account of what a *world* is (Section I); an account of how *artificiality* constitutes and maintains a world (Section II); an account of how a specific mode of artificiality — Western Modernity — acquired its distinctive disposition to domination and self-completion (Section III); an account of how the self-finishing tendency of human life under this mode of artificiality generates conditions favorable to the emergence of a new kind of environment (Section IV); and, finally, an account of how self-completing artificiality crosses a threshold at which it can no longer be treated as a component of the world but must instead be recognized as a second background environment comparable to the universe itself (Section V).

The metaverse, as this thesis defines it, is the name for that second background environment: not a specific platform, headset, or protocol, but an emergent condition in which artificiality has sufficiently decoupled from human oversight to impose its own structural pressures on human survival — pressures that are not reducible to, and may be incommensurable with, the pressures of the universe that humans have always inhabited and adapted to.

This definition will be formally stated at the end of Section V. The reader is asked to hold the popular concept lightly in the interim: what the preceding sections establish is not the metaverse's technological specifications but the ontological conditions under which any system — regardless of its specific technical form — qualifies as a metaverse in the thesis's sense.

### Version B — Condensed (~150 words, for a brief placeholder in Introduction §B)

This thesis does not define the metaverse at the outset. The definition that will eventually be offered — the metaverse as an emerging artificial background environment that operates on laws of its own, irreducible to those of the universe — requires prior conceptual work that is the purpose of Sections I through V. Specifically: a theory of what a human world is (§I), of how artificiality constitutes it (§II), of why Western Modernity's mode of artificiality has a structural disposition to self-completion (§III–IV), and of how self-completing artificiality crosses the threshold from a component of the world to a second background environment (§V). The metaverse concept is therefore deferred — not because it is unimportant, but because a definition offered before this conceptual work is done would necessarily remain at the level of current technological forms, and the thesis's argument is precisely that the metaverse is more — and more threatening — than its current technological instantiations suggest.
