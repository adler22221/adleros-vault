## ¶4 — Disambiguation 2: against Chalmers (REPLACES existing line 200)

### ¶4a — Steel-manning Chalmers

A more serious engagement with Chalmers's position is needed than the gloss "virtual as computer-based" allowed in earlier drafts. Chalmers (2022) holds that "a virtual reality environment is an immersive, interactive, and computer-generated space" [VERIFY: confirmed | chalmersRealityVirtualWorlds2022 | UUID d672826d | nlm-excerpt: "A virtual reality environment is an immersive, interactive, and computer-generated space."], and that "at least to a first approximation, a virtual object is a structure of bits inside a computer" [VERIFY: confirmed | chalmersRealityVirtualWorlds2022 | UUID d672826d | nlm-excerpt: "At least to a first approximation, a virtual object is a structure of bits inside a computer."]. Read carelessly, this position invites the obvious rebuttal — "virtuality is older than the computer." Read carefully, the rebuttal fails: Chalmers's "computer" is deliberately substrate-neutral. "Physical information is substrate-neutral. The same sequence of bits can be encoded in all sorts of substrates — punched cards, mechanical switches, transistors, or patterns of beer cans" [VERIFY: confirmed | chalmersRealityVirtualWorlds2022 | UUID d672826d | nlm-excerpt: "Physical information is substrate-neutral… The same sequence of bits can be encoded in all sorts of substrates — punched cards, mechanical switches, transistors, or patterns of beer cans."]. He grounds this in a *causal structuralism*: "a physical system implements a mathematical computation when the causal structure of the system mirrors the formal structure of the computation" [VERIFY: confirmed | chalmersRealityVirtualWorlds2022 | UUID d672826d | nlm-excerpt: "According to my causal structuralism, a physical system implements a mathematical computation when the causal structure of the system mirrors the formal structure of the computation."]. His project is, importantly, *anti-simulationist*: he grounds virtuality in *some* computational substrate precisely so as to defend its reality against the "fake reality" intuition. The thesis shares that anti-simulationist commitment; the disagreement lies elsewhere.

### ¶4b — Locating the disagreement at computational form, not substrate

The thesis's quarrel with Chalmers is not about substrate. DNA, quantum systems, neuromorphic chemistry, and even (in Chalmers's own image) beer-can arrays all count as computational substrates and may, in principle, host virtuality. The disagreement is about whether *computational form itself* is necessary to virtuality. Chalmers's causal structuralism requires that anything counting as a virtual object be implemented by a system whose causal structure mirrors a formal computational structure. This requirement excludes a vast class of causally-efficacious nonmaterial structures recognised since Durkheim as real but not material — institutional facts, status functions, ritual structures, collective representations, customary norm, sovereignty, kinship. These are not computations on any non-vacuous reading of the term; if "computation" is widened until it absorbs them, the concept loses analytic purchase. The thesis claims, accordingly, that *virtuality is the broader genus of which Chalmersian computational virtuality is one species* — historically a late and increasingly dominant species, but a species nonetheless.

Chalmers himself feels the pull of cases that do not fit the causal-structuralist mould: "we can think of dreams, illusions, and shadows as ancient counterparts of virtual worlds — minus the computer, which would not be invented for two millennia" [VERIFY: confirmed | chalmersRealityVirtualWorlds2022 | UUID d672826d | nlm-excerpt: "We can think of dreams, illusions, and shadows as ancient counterparts of virtual worlds — minus the computer, which would not be invented for two millennia."], and concedes that "a dream world is a sort of virtual world without a computer" [VERIFY: confirmed | chalmersRealityVirtualWorlds2022 | UUID d672826d | nlm-excerpt: "A dream world is a sort of virtual world without a computer."]. He treats these as degraded cases ("limited," meeting only two of five reality-checklist criteria), but the concession is suggestive. The thesis takes the door he leaves cracked open and walks through it: the broader genus is not a degraded version of computational virtuality but its precondition.

### ¶4c — Non-computational virtuality: the money paradigm case (REVISES existing line 202)

> [KEEP existing line 202 prose on shells/grain/salt/metal/paper/digits as temporal mediums; APPEND the warrant sentence at the close of the paragraph]

Money is the cleanest test of the broader genus. Money is causally efficacious, intersubjectively binding, substrate-promiscuous, and predates the computer by millennia. Money is still money, and money has always been virtual; shells, grain, salt, metal, paper, or digits are merely temporal mediums to operationalise money's virtuality. The point reaches back through the history of social ontology. Searle puts the institutional structure of money plainly: "it is only given the institution of money that I now have a five dollar bill in my hand. Take away the institution and all I have is a piece of paper with various gray and green markings" [VERIFY: confirmed | searleSpeechActs1969 | p.51 | nlm-excerpt: "it is only given the institution of money that I now have a five dollar bill in my hand. Take away the institution and all I have is a piece of paper with various gray and green markings."]. In Searle's mature formulation, money is the textbook *status function* generated by collective acceptance of a *constitutive rule* of the form *X counts as Y in context C*: "such and such counts as a $20 bill in our society" [VERIFY: confirmed | searlePhilosophyNewCentury2008 | p.33 | nlm-excerpt: "I have tried to state the logical form of the assignment of a status function when it becomes regular, and thus a matter of a rule, as that of the constitutive rule of the form, X counts as Y, or more commonly, X counts as Y in context C. Thus, such and such counts as a $20 bill in our society."]. Electronic money tightens the point further: the bank, by issuing a loan, performs a *Status Function Declaration* — "We, the Bank of America, make it the case by Declaration that Jones has $1,000 in his account… But there need be no physical reality to the $1,000, other than the representation" [VERIFY: confirmed | searleLanguageSocialOntology2008 | p.457 | nlm-excerpt: "Suppose the Bank of America loans Jones $1,000. The Declaration has this form: We, the Bank of America, make it the case by Declaration that Jones has $1,000 in his account (in return for which he promises to pay the Bank $1,000 plus interest)… there need be no physical reality to the $1,000, other than the representation."].

The same structural treatment extends, with the appropriate substitutions, to law, sovereignty, marriage, citizenship, ritual obligation, customary norm. Each is causally efficacious. None is computational in any sense that does not vacate the term. This treatment of money as virtual is not idiosyncratic; it sits within a well-established social-ontological tradition that recognises money, law, sovereignty, and ritual obligation as causally-efficacious nonmaterial structures arising from collective intentionality (Searle 1995) and operating as real-stratum mechanisms with their own emergent powers irreducible to events or experiences (Bhaskar 2008) [VERIFY: confirmed | searleConstructionSocialReality1995 | NLM UUID 8186ca58][VERIFY: confirmed | bhaskarRealistTheoryScience2008 | A-Sources UUID 15591783].

### ¶5a — Trans-historical continuity (KEEP)

As Durante observes, humans have always dealt with the virtual, ever since humans lived by the stories they collectively invented [VERIFY: durante2022 | Zotero key SBXRDXBB — "Technology and the Ontology of the Virtual"]. The claim is structural, not chronological: virtuality is not a recent invention that arrived with the computer, but a feature of the meaning-spaces humans have always inhabited.

### ¶5b — Worked example, with a light parenthetical (KEEP existing prose, ADD parenthetical)

Initially, virtuality manifested as stories of gods and as the ceremonies that bound them to communities — providing the nonmaterial yet causally efficacious basis on which worlds could form. Over time, gods were displaced by the science(s) and ceremonies by laws, each transition involving a *change of substrate* (oral to written to computational) operating on the *same underlying virtuality*. Today, AI guidance and algorithmic regulation, mediated by computer-based interfaces, are the dominant operating layer. The substrate has shifted repeatedly; the virtuality has persisted.

### ¶5c — Future trajectory: embedded computational virtuality (REPLACES the deleted "biologically programmed and powered" sentences)

Some commentators imagine that the future of virtuality will move beyond computational substrates altogether — that, for example, biologically programmed and powered interfaces will mark the end of the computer-based. This expectation rests on a category mistake. As Chalmers himself recognises, computation is substrate-neutral: biological, quantum, neuromorphic, and not-yet-imagined substrates all qualify as computational provided they implement the requisite causal-computational structure. The actual trajectory is not the supersession of computational virtuality but its *embedding*. Brain-computer interfaces, ambient intelligence, augmented sensory prostheses, always-on agents — these dissolve the interface into the body schema and the everyday environment, making computational virtuality phenomenologically transparent rather than absent (Stiegler 1998 on tertiary retention thickening; on the body schema's absorption of the prosthesis, see Merleau-Ponty 1945 [VERIFY: merleauPonty1945 — not in Zotero snapshot 2026-05-16; LOOKUP needed before final draft]). The squeeze the thesis diagnoses, accordingly, is not a squeeze between virtual and non-virtual. It is a squeeze between an emerging *embedded computational virtuality* and the older virtualities — ritual, institutional fact, customary norm, narrative — that the embedded computational layer is increasingly displacing or absorbing.

## ¶6 — Closing: substrate-independence at the real stratum (NEW — author to decide whether to retain in §IIB.B or defer to §IIIB)

> [OPTIONAL: include here or defer to §IIIB. See Open Decision (i) below.]

Virtuality, on the definition adopted in this thesis, lives at the *real* stratum of the stratified ontology developed in §III: it is a generative structure with causal powers, not an event or an experience. Substrate-independence at the real stratum follows directly from this stratum-placement — a real-stratum mechanism is identified by its causal-structural profile, not by any particular actual-stratum substrate that may instantiate it. Particular instantiations (a chant, a coin, a verdict, a rendered screen) live at the actual stratum; experiences of them, at the empirical. This stratification is what allows the trans-historical continuity claim of §IIB.B to be philosophically rigorous rather than merely intuitive: it is not a metaphor of "essence persisting through changing forms," but a real-stratum identification under actual-stratum re-instantiation [VERIFY: bhaskarRealistTheoryScience2008 | A-Sources UUID 15591783].
