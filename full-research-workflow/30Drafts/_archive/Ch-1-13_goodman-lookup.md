## Key Passages

### Passage 1 — The Foreword (most citable summary statement)
> "The movement is from unique truth and a world fixed and found to a diversity of right and even conflicting versions or worlds in the making."

— Goodman, Nelson. *Ways of Worldmaking*. Indianapolis: Hackett, 1978. Foreword, p. ix.

**Relevance:** Goodman's central thesis: worlds are not discovered but made. Supports the thesis argument that the "world" is a human construction (idealist layer) nested within the universe (realist given). The phrase "worlds in the making" directly parallels the thesis's framing of human world-construction.

### Passage 2 — Worldmaking as remaking (Chapter I §4, p. 7)
> "Worldmaking as we know it always starts from worlds already on hand; the making is a remaking."

— Goodman, *Ways of Worldmaking*, Ch. I §4, p. 7.

**Relevance:** Worldmaking is not creation ex nihilo but transformation of inherited worlds — resonates with the thesis's argument that worlds are nested, historically sedimented human constructions. Also supports the claim that the emerging metaverse is a remaking of the existing world.

### Passage 3 — Distinguishing from "possible worlds" (Chapter I §2, pp. 2–3)
> "Let it be clear that the question here is not of the possible worlds that many of my contemporaries, especially those near Disneyland, are busy making and manipulating. We are not speaking in terms of multiple possible alternatives to a single actual world but of multiple [actual worlds]."

— Goodman, *Ways of Worldmaking*, Ch. I §2, pp. 2–3.

**Relevance:** Critical for Ch-1-14 context (Schutz): Goodman explicitly distinguishes his "many worlds" from the technical philosophical notion of possible worlds (Leibniz/modal logic). This is precisely the disambiguation 米 called for ("possible worlds是專有名詞"). Goodman's "worlds" are actual, constructed, symbol-system-relative — not modal alternatives.

### Passage 4 — Versions as worlds (Chapter I §2)
> "Right world-descriptions and world-depictions and world-perceptions, the ways-the-world-is, or just versions, can be treated as our worlds."

— Goodman, *Ways of Worldmaking*, Ch. I §2.

**Relevance:** Goodman's "irrealism" — there is no single underlying world independent of our symbol systems. Worlds just *are* the right versions we construct. Supports the thesis's claim that the world is the totality of human meaning-making, not an ontologically independent substrate.

---

### Passage 5 — Cassirer framing (Chapter I §1, p. 1)
> "Countless worlds made from nothing by use of symbols — so might a satirist summarize some major themes in the work of Ernst Cassirer. These themes — the multiplicity of worlds, the speciousness of 'the given', the creative power of the understanding, the variety and formative function of symbols — are also integral to my own thinking."

— Goodman, *Ways of Worldmaking*, Ch. I §1, p. 1.

**Relevance:** Connects Goodman to the broader neo-Kantian tradition (Cassirer) that the thesis draws on. The "speciousness of 'the given'" is directly relevant to the universe/world distinction: what is "given" (universe) becomes the substrate that worlds are made upon, but is never directly accessible without symbolic mediation.
