## Recommended Conclusion (Tightened Derivation — ~600 words)

### Version A — Full revised Conclusion

The analysis conducted in this thesis can be summarized as a retroductive argument: from the observable phenomena catalogued in Section VI — the rise of psychosomatic malfunctions, the singularization of livelihood, the emergence of lives governed by opaque and changing algorithmic laws — backward to the underlying mechanism that generates them, and forward to the structural condition that mechanism implies.

The mechanism is what this thesis has called the squeeze: human existence is progressively caught between two background environments — the universe, which imposes the evolutionary pressures of biological life, and the emerging metaverse, which imposes the adaptational pressures of self-completing artificiality — whose respective demands are not mutually reducible and may be structurally incommensurable. To adapt to one is increasingly to maladapt to the other. This is not a contingent feature of current technology but a structural consequence of the trajectory identified in Sections I through V: the world, as a human-made environment nested within the universe, has progressively generated a layer of artificiality that is slipping beyond the boundaries of world-making and forming its own background order.

This analysis entails two categorical conclusions about what the squeeze is *not*.

It is not, first, a Montesquieuan problem — a problem of the check and balance of power. The regulatory efforts that have followed this diagnosis — the European Union's General Data Protection Regulation, the Digital Markets Act — address the concentration of executive power in platform corporations. They are necessary but insufficient, because the squeeze operates through structural adaptational pressure, not primarily through political domination. An algorithmically governed environment whose power is perfectly distributed among democratic institutions would still impose incommensurable survival demands on a biological organism evolved for a different substrate. Power balancing does not resolve the evolutionary mismatch.

It is not, second, a Marxist problem — a problem of the ownership of the means of production. Proposals to reclaim data ownership through "data cooperatives" address the distribution of the benefits and risks of platform economies. They too are necessary but insufficient, because the singularization of livelihood that Section VI identifies is a consequence of the radical monopoly that self-completing artificiality imposes on the conditions of survival — not merely on the distribution of its proceeds. A platform economy in common ownership would still eliminate the plural livelihood pathways that Illich's "tools for conviviality" sought to protect. Redistribution does not resolve the structural foreclosure.

What the squeeze requires, therefore, is not a political or economic response alone but a civilizational one: a reconstitution of the relationship between artificiality and the human world-making capacity it both enables and threatens. This is what Stiegler means by the pharmakon — the medicine that is simultaneously a poison. Every extension of human capability through artificiality is simultaneously a reduction of the biological and social capacities that give that capability meaning. The thesis does not resolve this tension; it insists on its depth. The resolution — if there is one — lies not in the optimization of existing artificiality but in the kind of reflexive redesign of artificial environments that has always been the telos of world-making as a human practice.

Section I established that there have always been multiple, incommensurable worlds — different modes of artificiality for collective survival within the universe. Western Modernity achieved dominance not because its mode of artificiality was superior but because, as Section III argued, it possessed a structural disposition to self-complete and totalize. That dominance is not permanent; it is a contingent trajectory with contingent alternatives. The relativity of Western Modernity is therefore the productive remainder of this analysis: the squeeze may be the condition that finally makes visible the need for modes of artificiality that are not totalizing, not self-completing, not disposed to the exclusion of incommensurable others. The research questions that follow from this — concerning appropriate technology, tools for conviviality, cosmotechnics of co-becoming — exceed this thesis. They are the beginning of the next one.

### Version B — Condensed (~300 words, if full version exceeds scope)

This thesis has argued, retroductively, from the observable signs of the squeeze — psychosomatic malfunctions, singularization of livelihood, lives under opaque algorithmic governance — to their underlying mechanism: the emergence of the metaverse as a second background environment whose adaptational demands are structurally incommensurable with those of the universe that biological life evolved within.

Two conclusions follow from this mechanism that are categorical, not merely empirical. The squeeze is not resolvable through power-balancing (Montesquieu): regulatory checks on platform power do not eliminate the evolutionary mismatch between organisms and environments. It is not resolvable through redistribution (Marx): shared ownership of digital infrastructure does not restore the plural livelihood pathways that artificial monopoly has foreclosed. What is at stake is not a political or economic imbalance but a civilizational one — the progressive decoupling of artificiality from the world-making purposes it was created to serve.

The pharmakon structure (Stiegler) — artificiality as simultaneously medicine and poison — is the most honest account of where the thesis leaves the reader. There is no resolution; there is only the insistence on the depth and structural character of the tension. The productive remainder is the relativity of Western Modernity itself: other modes of artificiality have existed and continue to exist. The squeeze may be the condition that makes their recovery or reinvention not a nostalgic aspiration but a survival necessity. That recovery is the work this thesis opens, not the work it completes.
