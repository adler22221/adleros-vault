### 2. Is the Metaverse a "World" in the Thesis's Sense?

**First answer: No — not in the full sense.**

<!-- ANNOT: this misses the angle of this thesis.| priority: high -->

The world in the thesis's sense is constituted by the *vernacular* process of meaning-making: the accumulated, collectively maintained, embodied practices through which communities organize experience, value, and orientation within the given substrate of the universe. It is not designed; it is grown. It is not programmed; it is lived. It is not an artifact; it is the medium of all artifice.

The metaverse, by contrast, is *designed*. It is an engineered artifact — a system of platforms, protocols, governance structures, and affordances that has been deliberately constructed to certain specifications. It is not emerged from the communal inhabitation of a shared universe-substrate; it has been built by corporations according to commercial logics, technical constraints, and investor mandates.

This designed character is what distinguishes the metaverse from any world that has ever existed. Even heavily artificial worlds — the Chinese imperial court, the medieval city, the modern bureaucratic state — emerge through centuries of practice, negotiation, and cumulative inhabitation. They have historical depth, sedimentary complexity, and a relationship to the universe-substrate (land, climate, material affordances) that is constitutively uncontrolled. The metaverse is shallow in this sense: it was designed to specification, and can be redesigned, scaled, discontinued, or sold.

**Second answer: Partly yes — but with a qualification that changes everything.**

In one register, the metaverse does constitute a "world" in the functional sense: it provides a structured meaning-space in which human beings orient themselves, form identities, build relationships, exercise capacities, and pursue ends. Those who inhabit it substantially do experience it as a world — as the background structure against which their activities make sense.

The qualification: this functional world-character of the metaverse is achieved *at the cost of the substrate*. The vernacular world was anchored in the universe — its meanings referred, however obliquely, to the material conditions of embodied existence in a given environment (the seasons, the landscape, the needs of the body, the constraints of natural process). The metaverse's meaning-space is decoupled from this substrate: it is engineered rather than grown, algorithmically managed rather than communally maintained, and designed to be functionally independent of any particular material environment.

This decoupling is what the thesis calls *neo-artificiality* — a new register of artificiality that exceeds all prior artificial worlds in its degree of substrate-independence. The metaverse is not more artificial than the city; it is artificial in a *qualitatively different* way, because its organizing logic does not refer to the universe-substrate as its constitutive ground.

### 3. The "One of the Worlds" Question

Mi asks: "Might the metaverse be just one of the worlds?" — implying that if the metaverse is one world among others (rather than a replacement for all worlds), the squeeze thesis might be overstated.

**The answer is two-tiered:**

<!-- ANNOT: tier 1 doesn't hold in this thesis, as we decided to omit Schultz's sense of a world entirely.| priority: high -->

**Tier 1 — At the individual level, yes: for any given person, the metaverse may be one world among several they inhabit.** People shuttle between work-world, family-world, digital-world, ritual-world. The metaverse, for most current users, is one domain among others.

**Tier 2 — At the structural level, no: the metaverse's logic is totalizing, not complementary.** The thesis's central claim is not that all individuals will live entirely in the metaverse but that the *structural logic* of the metaverse tends toward self-completion — toward becoming the organizing framework for all domains of livelihood, identity, and world-access. This is the radical monopoly argument (Illich): the metaverse does not compete with other worlds as one option among equals; it restructures the conditions under which any world can be inhabited. Once platform-mediated identity, credentials, and social access become prerequisites for economic and civic participation, the metaverse is no longer "one of the worlds" but the meta-world — the organizing framework within which all other worlds are administered and accessed.

The prefix "meta-" is therefore philosophically load-bearing in the thesis's use: it does not mean "beyond the world" in a spatial sense but "organizing framework for world-access" in a structural sense. The metaverse is not a world alongside the world; it is the environment that determines the conditions under which any world-inhabitation is possible.

### 4. "If Humans Disappear, Is There Still a World?"

Mi's final question presses the logical extreme: if the metaverse fully replaces the world, and if the world is constituted by human inhabitation, does the world cease to exist?

This is a precise ontological question. The answer, within the thesis's framework:

**If humans fully migrate into the metaverse:** The "world" — understood as the idealist, human-constituted meaning-space nested in the universe — would not simply relocate to the metaverse. It would be transformed into something that lacks the key feature that makes a world a world: the *constitutive relationship* with the universe-substrate.

The universe does not disappear when humans inhabit the metaverse. It continues, indifferently, as before. What would disappear is the *mediated relationship* between human life and the universe that the world has always provided — the organized, meaning-laden, humanly maintained orientation toward the given substrate of existence.

What would remain would be *a metaverse* — a fully artificial world — and *a universe* — the given substrate — but the *world* as the humanly made meaning-space that mediates between them would be gone. Humans would exist in an engineered environment decoupled from the universe; they would not have ceased to exist, but they would have ceased to *have a world* in the thesis's sense.

This is the ontological core of the squeeze: the squeeze describes the process of this decoupling. Not the extermination of humans but the progressive elimination of the *world* — the organized human-made meaning-space that mediates between human existence and the universe — as the metaverse substitutes itself for the mediating function the world used to perform.

### 5. What the Metaverse Is: A Proposed Ontological Definition

Drawing on the above, the thesis can offer a precise ontological characterization:

**The metaverse is a neo-artificial environment that:**

1. **Functions as a world** (provides structured meaning-space, supports identity, livelihood, and orientation) — world-like in its functional register

2. **Is not grounded in the universe** in the way vernacular worlds are — it is substrate-independent, designed rather than grown, decoupled from the material affordances and constraints of embodied existence in the given natural environment

3. **Tends toward self-completion** — its logic drives toward becoming the universal organizing framework for world-access, rather than remaining one domain among others (the radical monopoly tendency)

4. **Displaces rather than supplements** the vernacular world — not by replacing it in a single act but by progressively eliminating the competencies, practices, and orientations through which the vernacular world was maintained

5. **Is therefore ontologically novel** — it is neither a world (lacks the constitutive universe-anchorage) nor simply a tool (exceeds tool-scale to become a total environment) nor simply a medium (it does not merely facilitate world-inhabitation but restructures its conditions)

The "verse" in "metaverse" (from Stephenson's 1992 coinage, itself from "universe") is apt in a sense Stephenson did not intend: the metaverse is a *verse* — a partial, structured, humanly organized sub-totality — that attempts to become a *uni-verse* — a totalizing environment that encompasses all others. The squeeze is the experiential correlate of this attempted totalization.

## Recommended Passage for §V

> The question of the metaverse's ontological status must be faced directly. Is it a world — a new variety of the same category as the humanly made meaning-spaces this thesis has described? Or is it something categorically different?
>
> The metaverse functions as a world: it provides a structured meaning-space in which human beings orient themselves, form identities, exercise competencies, and pursue ends. In this functional register, those who inhabit it substantially do experience it as a world. But this functional world-character is achieved at the cost of the feature that distinguishes all prior worlds from mere artifacts: the constitutive anchorage in the universe-substrate. Prior worlds — however artificial, however elaborately maintained — referred, through their practices, competencies, and material structures, to the given conditions of embodied existence in a particular environment. The metaverse's organizing logic does not. It is substrate-independent by design: engineered to be functionally equivalent across any physical hardware, any geographic location, any material environment. This is neo-artificiality in the precise sense: not greater artificiality but a qualitatively different mode of artificiality — one that no longer takes the universe as its constitutive ground.
>
> The metaverse is therefore not "one of the worlds" in the sense Mi's question implies. It does not exist alongside vernacular worlds as one domain among others. Its structural logic — the radical monopoly of the digital mode of production over the conditions of world-access — tends toward becoming the organizing framework within which all world-inhabitation is administered, accessed, and authorized. This is the ontological novelty the thesis identifies with the prefix "meta-": not a world beyond the world, but a meta-world, a framework that organizes the conditions under which any world can be had.
>
> And if the metaverse were to fully complete itself — if human existence were entirely administered through its infrastructure — what would remain would not be a world but a verse: a humanly organized sub-totality decoupled from its constitutive ground. The universe would continue, indifferent. The metaverse would persist, engineered. But the world — the human-made meaning-space through which existence was oriented toward its given material substrate — would have been displaced. The squeeze, in its terminal form, is this displacement.
