# I-6 GENERATIVE v3: Methodological Statement — Diachronic Critical Realism Oriented to Complex Civilizational Emergence

**v2** corrected the stratification mapping (real / actual / empirical apply *across* every level — universe, world, metaverse) but framed the methodology as textbook-retroduction (synchronic inference from phenomenon to mechanism). This understates what the thesis actually does.

## Section 1: The Theoretical Synthesis (Three Traditions)

This thesis develops its argument through a synthesis of three theoretical traditions whose convergence has not previously been articulated as a unified framework:

**(i) Continental philosophy of technology** — principally Bernard Stiegler (the fault of Epimetheus, exosomatization, tertiary retention, the technical constitution of the human form of life), with selective use of Martin Heidegger (technology as a mode of revealing-and-concealing; *Gestell*) and Jean Baudrillard (the autonomization of the sign; the order of simulacra). From this tradition the thesis takes its account of *how artificiality constitutes the human world* rather than supplementing a pre-given human nature.

**(ii) Critical realism** — Roy Bhaskar's stratified ontology and his account of transfactual structures, mechanisms, and tendencies. From this tradition the thesis takes its commitment to a depth-realist ontology (there are real structures whose causal powers operate whether or not they are activated or observed) and its license to argue from observable phenomena to underlying generative dispositions.

**(iii) East Asian environmental philosophy** — principally Obara Hideo's evolutionary-ecological account of human self-domestication (`小原秀雄`, MWBGTIFF, 2Q8KYMHC, 6L278LXZ), Uegaki Takahide's analysis of self-completing society and the historical typology of post-war Japanese subjectivity (`上柿崇英`, VESTLJGF, CYAAA3IK), and Miura Nagamitsu's intellectual-historical reconstruction of the relationship between environmental thought and society (`三浦永光`, NUF7WCJD). From this tradition the thesis takes its specific civilizational diagnosis: *what Modern Western artificiality has done to the human-environment relationship*, and *what alternative trajectories exist or have existed*.

These traditions are not additively combined. Each addresses a different layer of the same problem: Continental philosophy of technology articulates the *mechanism* of world-making (how artificiality constitutes worlds); critical realism provides the *ontological warrant* (what kind of thing the squeeze and the metaverse *are*, such that they can be analyzed at all); and East Asian environmental philosophy supplies the *historical-civilizational specificity* (what particular trajectory is being analyzed and against what alternatives).

## Section 2: Theoretical Orientations

Four orientations characterize the inquiry:

**Environmental** — the unit of analysis is the relationship between organism and environment, not the organism alone. The metaverse is analyzed not as a technology used by individuals but as a *background environment* that produces selection pressures on the populations that come to inhabit it.

**Ecological** — this organism-environment relationship is mediated by structural feedback loops in which each pole shapes the other across multiple temporal scales. Niche construction theory is the immediate biological analogue; Obara's self-domestication thesis is its civilizational extension.

**Evolutionary** — the human is treated as an evolved organism whose evolutionary history did not stop with the emergence of *Homo sapiens* but continues through cultural-technical-prosthetic processes (Stiegler's "exosomatic evolution," after Lotka). The metaverse imposes new selection pressures whose evolutionary consequences are the thesis's ultimate object.

**Emergentist** — higher-level structures (worlds, modes of artificiality, the metaverse) have their own causal powers irreducible to the lower levels they depend on (universe, biology, individual cognition). The squeeze is intelligible only on this assumption.

These intersect with three established disciplinary fields: **philosophy of technology** (the Continental side), **civilizational studies / Big History** (the East Asian side and the comparative-civilizational dimension), and **critical theory** (the Frankfurt School heritage, accessed primarily through Bhaskar's reading of the Platonic-Aristotelian fault line).

## Section 3: Methodological Commitments — Diachronic Critical Realism

The thesis's *methodological* posture is what distinguishes it from a synchronic structural analysis on the one hand and a purely historical narrative on the other. Three commitments organize the inquiry:

### 3.1 Critical-realist ontology (transfactual depth)

What is real is not exhausted by what happens, and what happens is not exhausted by what is observed. Following Bhaskar (2008; 2009), the inquiry distinguishes the **real** (entities, structures, dispositions whose powers operate transfactually — whether or not they happen to be activated), the **actual** (events that occur, whether or not they are observed), and the **empirical** (events as observed). The empirical is a subset of the actual, and the actual a subset of the real. This stratification of *access* is to be distinguished from any stratification of *being* (universe / world / metaverse): every level of being has its own real, actual, and empirical aspects (see Section 5 for the corrected mapping).

### 3.2 Morphogenetic (developmental-processual) frame

Drawing on Margaret Archer's morphogenetic approach (Archer, 1995; 2003 [VERIFY Zotero]) and complementary resources in systems thinking, the inquiry treats its objects — worlds, modes of artificiality, the metaverse — as *processes*, not standing structures. Structures *condition* action; action *reproduces or transforms* structures; the resulting **morphogenesis** (or **morphostasis**) constitutes the next round of structural conditioning. The squeeze, on this account, is not a static double-bind but a *morphogenetic outcome* — a developmental result of how human dispositions to self-organize, iterated through their own technical and institutional products over civilizational time, reshape the environment that subsequently selects on those dispositions. This is what permits the thesis to argue diachronically: from observed contemporary phenomena, backward through deep time to the dispositional pattern, and forward (with appropriate hedging) to the trajectory the pattern continues to generate.

### 3.3 Complex-adaptive-systems framing

The objects analyzed (organisms, worlds, civilizations, the metaverse) are treated as **complex adaptive systems** exhibiting self-organization, emergence, and scale-recurrent patterning (fractal regularities across organism, social, and civilizational scales). This framing draws methodologically on the broader systems-thinking tradition — including Stafford Beer's principle that the purpose of a system is what it does (POSWID), the agent-based / systems-relational orientation in Cabrera & Cabrera's DSRP framework (Distinctions, Systems, Relationships, Perspectives) [VERIFY Zotero], and Yuk Hui's distinction between the *organized inorganic* and the *organizing inorganic* (Hui, 2016). The metaverse, in these terms, is the contemporary appearance of an *organizing inorganic* that has acquired its own self-organizational dynamics and is now exerting selection pressure on the *organized organic* (the human being).

### 3.4 Inferential pattern: retroduction, extended diachronically

The inferential pattern is **retroductive** in Bhaskar's sense — from observable phenomena to a hypothesis about the underlying mechanism that, *if real*, would produce phenomena of the kind observed (Bhaskar, 2008). What the thesis adds to textbook retroduction is *temporal depth*: the "mechanism" inferred is itself a developmental process — a disposition to self-organize — whose character is reconstructed through the history of its operation. The analysis is not "given these symptoms, what static structure produces them?" but "given these symptoms, what *developmental trajectory of self-organization* produces them, and how did that trajectory get to this point?" The inferential range is therefore deep-temporal: the dispositions inferred are not local to the contemporary metaverse but reach back through prehistorical, evolutionary, and civilizational time — the territory that Big History approaches and Obara's self-domestication framework recover.

### 3.5 Heuristic resources

Three further resources are used as heuristics, not as substantive methodologies:

- **Ideal-typical analysis** (Weber): the thesis's central concepts — "world," "mode of artificiality," "the squeeze," "Western Modernity," "metaverse" — are to be read as *ideal types* in Weber's sense: simplified analytical models that highlight characteristic features of the phenomenon, not empirical-descriptive labels. No actual world is purely one mode of artificiality; no actual civilization perfectly instantiates "Western Modernity"; the squeeze is everywhere a matter of degree, never a binary state. The boundaries between ideal types are never empirically clean. (See S1-11 for the elaborated definition of "world" as an ideal-typical category based on a specific mode of artificiality.)

- **POSWID** (Stafford Beer, "the purpose of a system is what it does"): used as a methodological prophylactic against intentionalist explanations of the metaverse. Whatever any individual designer intends, the metaverse-as-system *does* what it does; its functional output is the analysandum, not its programmers' stated purposes.

- **Transdisciplinarity / antidisciplinarity**: the thesis's object — the universe-world-metaverse relation as it produces the squeeze — is not the object of any inherited single discipline. The methodology is transdisciplinary by necessity, not by stylistic preference (cf. Montuori, 2013).

## Section 4: The Corrected Stratification Mapping

The thesis's central conceptual triad — **universe / world / metaverse** — is a stratification *of being*: distinct kinds of background environment that humans inhabit. Bhaskar's triad — **real / actual / empirical** — is a stratification *of access*: distinct kinds of relationship one can have to whatever level of being is in question. The two stratifications cut across each other; they are not isomorphic.

The correct picture has three columns and three rows:

| | **Universe** | **World** | **Metaverse** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Real** (generative dispositions, structures, mechanisms) | Physical and biological dispositions and laws operating regardless of observation | Structural mechanisms of human cultural-technical existence (Western Modernity's disposition to dominate, the structural tendency toward self-completion) | The metaverse as second background environment — a self-completing artificiality whose own organizational dynamics impose adaptational pressures incommensurable with those of the universe |
| **Actual** (events) | Geological, evolutionary, climatological events | Historical events, institutional formations, technological developments | The unfolding cybernetic loop: AI decisions made, algorithmic codes executed, IoT signals exchanged |
| **Empirical** (observed) | Scientific observation, lived weather, perceived ecology | Observed politics, news headlines, individual social experience | The metaverse as XR (Stephenson's coinage; Meta's Horizon Worlds; consumer VR — *what is empirically observable as "the metaverse"*) |

The thesis's central argumentative move — the move that makes the squeeze hypothesis non-trivial — is the distinction between **the metaverse at the empirical level** (XR, headsets, virtual worlds — the surface phenomena that consumer-tech discourse and the *Reality+* style of philosophy take to *be* the metaverse) and **the metaverse at the real level** (the underlying generative disposition: a self-completing artificiality that has become, or is becoming, a second background environment). The April 2026 collapse of consumer XR (Meta's $73B Reality Labs losses; the Horizon Worlds shutdown) is *empirically* the death of the metaverse; it is in no sense its real death — the underlying disposition continues to operate through industrial digital twins, AI-spatial platforms, and algorithmic governance regardless of whether anyone wears a headset.

The other mappings — universe-real, universe-actual, universe-empirical, world-real, world-actual, world-empirical — are deliberately *not* asserted as one-to-one correspondences. The world is not "the actual"; it has a real level (its mechanisms), an actual level (its events), and an empirical level (what is observed of it). The universe is not "the real"; it has a real level (its laws and dispositions), an actual level (its events), and an empirical level (its observed phenomena). The squeeze itself — the central mechanism the thesis postulates — is at the **real** level: a transfactually operating disposition that produces actual events (psychosomatic symptoms, livelihood failures, governance breakdowns), which are then accessed empirically. The thesis's task is to retroduce from the empirical signs through the actual events back to the real disposition — and then forward, by tracing the disposition's developmental trajectory, to the structural condition the disposition continues to generate.

## Section 5: Two Presentations

The methodology described above admits of two presentations. Mi's intellectual orientation — virtue epistemology in the analytic tradition — does not include critical realism, the morphogenetic approach, or complex-adaptive-systems theory as familiar reference points. Either of the following two presentations is therefore appropriate; the choice depends on rhetorical strategy and reader context. The thesis can use either or both.

### Version A: Explicit Critical Realism (recommended ~300–400 words)

This thesis is grounded methodologically in **diachronic critical realism oriented to complex civilizational emergence**. Three commitments define this position. *Ontologically*, the inquiry adopts critical realism (Bhaskar, 2008; 2009): there are real structures, mechanisms, and dispositions whose causal powers operate transfactually — whether or not they are activated, and whether or not they are observed. At any level of being — the physical universe, the human world, the emerging metaverse — three domains of access can be distinguished: the *real* (structures and dispositions), the *actual* (events that occur), and the *empirical* (events as observed). *Methodologically*, the inquiry follows the morphogenetic approach (Archer): structures and the actions they condition are analyzed as a developmental process across time, in which structural conditioning generates action, action reproduces or transforms structure, and the resulting morphogenesis constitutes the next round of structural conditioning. *Inferentially*, the thesis proceeds by retroduction (Bhaskar) extended diachronically — from observable phenomena to the underlying disposition that, if real, would produce them, and from the disposition's reconstructed history to the structural condition the disposition continues to generate. The objects analyzed (worlds, modes of artificiality, the metaverse) are treated as **complex adaptive systems** exhibiting self-organization, emergence, and scale-recurrent patterning. Heuristics including ideal-typical analysis (Weber), the principle that the purpose of a system is what it does (POSWID, Beer), and the systems-relational orientation of recent systems thinking (Cabrera & Cabrera) are used as needed. Theoretically, the thesis synthesizes three traditions: Continental philosophy of technology (Stiegler, Heidegger, Baudrillard) for the account of how artificiality constitutes the human world; critical realism (Bhaskar) for the depth-realist ontology and the logic of multi-level inference; and East Asian environmental philosophy (Obara on self-domestication; Uegaki on the structure of post-war Japanese society; Miura on the intellectual history of the human-environment relation) for the historical and civilizational specificity of the diagnosis. The synthesis is unapologetically transdisciplinary: the squeeze cannot be analyzed within any single inherited discipline, because no inherited discipline takes the universe-world-metaverse relation as its object. The methodology is therefore not the application of a single named procedure but the construction of an integrative mode of inquiry adequate to its object.

### Version B: Analytical-Friendly Common-Sense Continental (recommended ~300–400 words)

This thesis adopts a **multi-level, developmental, systems-oriented approach** to its object. It distinguishes — at every layer of analysis — between the *underlying structures and dispositions* that produce phenomena (whether or not anyone observes them), the *events* those structures generate (whether or not those events are recorded), and the *observations* through which we come to know about any of this. This is a familiar distinction in the philosophy of science: it underwrites the difference between, say, the laws of thermodynamics (which obtain regardless of observation), the heat events those laws produce, and the temperature readings by which we come to know about them. The thesis's argumentative strategy is to argue from observed phenomena (the cataloged signs of human malaise under late artificiality) to a hypothesis about the underlying structure that best explains them — what philosophers of science call inference to the best explanation. The hypothesis advanced in this thesis is what it calls "the squeeze": a structural double-bind between two background environments imposing incommensurable adaptational pressures. Because the hypothesized structure is itself a *developmental process* — a disposition that has unfolded across deep historical time, iteratively reshaping the environments to which it then has to adapt — the analysis is necessarily diachronic. It is not enough to identify a static causal mechanism behind contemporary symptoms; one must also reconstruct the historical trajectory by which that mechanism has come to operate, since it is the *direction* of that trajectory (toward further self-completion) that grounds the thesis's diagnostic claims about where the trajectory is heading. The objects of analysis (organisms, worlds, civilizations, the metaverse) are treated as complex adaptive systems whose characteristic dynamics — self-organization, emergence, the appearance of scale-recurrent patterns — are not artifacts of description but features of the phenomena themselves. Three theoretical traditions are integrated to make this analysis possible: Continental philosophy of technology (Stiegler, Heidegger, Baudrillard) for the analysis of how technical artifacts and systems *constitute* — rather than merely supplement — the human form of life; multi-level emergentist analysis for the logic of inferring across the physical, biological, social, and existential layers; and East Asian environmental thought (Obara on self-domestication; Uegaki on the structure of post-war Japanese society; Miura on the intellectual history of the human-environment relation) for the specific civilizational diagnosis the thesis makes.

## Section 6: Strategic Notes on Presentation

**Why "diachronic critical realism oriented to complex civilizational emergence" rather than "retroduction"?** Because the latter is a technical term in critical realism that names a specific synchronic inferential operation, and what the thesis actually does goes beyond it. The thesis's mode of inquiry combines retroduction *with* morphogenetic analysis (Archer) *with* complex-adaptive-systems framing — and the integration is the methodology, not any single component.

**Why omit "dialectics"?** Because invoking dialectics in any form (Hegelian, Marxist, or Bhaskar's specifically realist version) invites a jurisdictional dispute about which dialectical tradition is being used, when the analytical work of the thesis can be done with weaker and clearer terms — *structural double-bind*, *incommensurability*, *adaptational contradiction*, *real tension*. The squeeze has dialectical *content* (it is a real contradiction that does not resolve through any necessary mechanism) but the thesis does not use dialectical *form* (no triadic synthesis, no necessary supersession, no Aufhebung). Avoiding the term saves rhetorical energy.

**The Mi strategy.** Mi will press on the realist commitment ("how do you know the disposition is real if you only observe its effects?"). The retroductive answer — that *all* causal explanation in the empirical sciences proceeds this way — is most readily made in Version B's analytic vocabulary. If Mi is willing to engage critical realism, Version A is the stronger statement; if Mi is not, Version B accomplishes the same work without inviting an unproductive jurisdictional dispute. The two versions are intellectually equivalent — the difference is rhetorical, not substantive.

**The Liu strategy.** Liu's scope discipline is best served by Version B, which keeps the methodological apparatus light and reserves the heavy machinery (Bhaskar, Archer, Cabrera) for footnotes if needed.

**The Ma strategy.** Ma will respond to the explicit comparative-civilizational dimension of the methodology — the East Asian (Obara, Uegaki, Miura) tradition is a substantive theoretical resource, not a token gesture toward "non-Western perspectives." Either version makes this clear.
