# V-3 GENERATIVE v2: The Laws of the Metaverse — What Are They?

## §0 — Author's framing (preserved verbatim from v1 [!VERIFY] block)

The honest answer (per author's [!VERIFY] annotation, v1 line 31–35): **we cannot know what exactly the real-level laws of the metaverse are**. As the thesis itself argues — and unlike the laws of the universe (or at least our current majority consensus of them) — the laws of the metaverse may be opaque and ever-changing, deliberately so when controlled by a few without transparent auditing. Whenever inhabitants are close to grasping a law, the law changes to evade comprehension and control. The predicament resembles that of the Santi civilization in Cixin Liu's *Three Body Problem*: the rules of the cosmos are not stable enough to be learned by those subject to them. This is observable in the rapid changes in algorithms across major platforms: best practices are rendered obsolete at an increasingly accelerating pace.

What the thesis can do, however, is articulate **candidate meta-principles** that characterize the metaverse's real-level disposition without claiming to enumerate the laws themselves. The candidate meta-principles articulate what the laws *do* (their dispositional signature) without committing to what the laws *are* at any moment.

This restructure recasts v1's "five laws" accordingly:
- **Real-level disposition (candidate meta-principles)** — what the laws do, not what they are at any moment. **Partially queued for [LOOKUP]** pending latest theoretical and empirical research.
- **Actual-level signatures** — observed events and processes that index the real-level disposition.
- **Empirical-level instantiations** — specific platforms, algorithms, mechanisms.

Liu's question — "metaverse的own laws是什麼?" — is then answered honestly: at the real level, we know the *structural disposition* (opacity, mutability, asymmetric law-change, radical-monopoly tendency) but not the specific contents; at the actual and empirical levels, we know the recurring patterns through which the disposition manifests, drawing on Lessig (`RCYT4KFH`) and the existing literature on platform governance.

## §1 — Real-level disposition: meta-principles (candidate)

The real-level law of the metaverse is not a list of specific governance rules but a *meta-law*: the structural disposition that the metaverse's operative governance is **opaque to its inhabitants, deliberately mutable, and asymmetrically tilted in time**. This connects directly to F3 of the squeeze ontology (law-change asymmetry): F3 names the operational feature that the metaverse's laws change faster than human biological and cultural adaptation; §1 here names the underlying real-level disposition that *makes F3 operational*.

### 1.1 Meta-principle of unknowability-by-the-governed

The real-level disposition is for the governing logic to be inaccessible to those subject to it during the time they are subject to it. This is not (only) a contingent failure of transparency; it is a structural feature of how the metaverse's governance works as governance. The governed cannot read the law before they are governed by it — and by the time they have inferred enough of it to navigate, the law has shifted.

**[!VERIFY: 2026-currency | LOOKUP-1 | DOM-excerpt: pending]** This claim needs verification against the latest theoretical and empirical research on:
- Algorithmic transparency efforts (Audrey Tang's plurality / Taiwan governance experiments; EU AI Act 2024–2025 transparency provisions for high-risk and GPAI systems)
- Whether opacity is an *inherent* disposition of large-scale algorithmic systems or a contingent design choice that transparency advocacy can overcome
- Recent technical work on interpretability, explainability, and algorithmic auditing

The honest meta-principle is therefore conditional: *insofar as* the metaverse's operative governance remains opaque to the governed, the meta-law of unknowability-by-the-governed obtains. Whether the various transparency efforts (above) succeed in overturning this disposition or merely complicate it is an empirical question for [LOOKUP] follow-up.

### 1.2 Meta-principle of designed mutability

The real-level disposition is for the governing logic to be deliberately changeable by those who control it, on timescales much shorter than human adaptation. Where the universe's laws are stable on civilizational timescales (thermodynamics, biology, ecology), the metaverse's laws are by design subject to revision — terms of service updated without negotiation, algorithms retrained continuously, access policies shifted with corporate priorities.

This is the dispositional analogue of F3: F3 says the laws change faster than adaptation can track; meta-principle 1.2 says this is *not accidental* but a real-level feature of how the metaverse's governance is structured. Whether mutability serves the governed (responsive, adaptive) or the governors (extractive, controlling) depends on the empirical-level distribution of control — but the dispositional mutability itself is independent of which way it is used.

### 1.3 Meta-principle of structural compulsion

The real-level disposition is for participation in the metaverse's environment to become structurally non-optional — not by prohibition of alternatives but by progressive elimination of the conditions under which alternatives can sustain themselves. This is Illich's *radical monopoly* (`Tools for Conviviality`, 1973) at the level of the governing logic itself: the metaverse's laws make non-participation increasingly impossible by altering the substrate on which all life-options depend. F5 of the squeeze ontology names this operational feature; meta-principle 1.3 names the real-level disposition that makes F5 operational.

- Whether decentralized alternatives genuinely escape the radical-monopoly disposition or merely reproduce it at a different scale

The meta-principle is again conditional: *insofar as* alternative pathways for civic, economic, and social participation remain structurally precarious, the meta-law of structural compulsion obtains. The fediverse and similar projects test this empirically; the test is ongoing.

## §2 — Actual-level signatures of the real-level disposition

What the real-level meta-principles look like in operation. Five recurring patterns observed in the empirical-level metaverse (the level Lessig analyzes; the level industry surveys document; the level Liu was pointing to in viva). Each pattern is an *actual-level signature* of the real-level meta-principles in §1.

### 2.1 Code-as-architectural-governance (after Lessig)

Lawrence Lessig's "code is law" thesis (*Code: And Other Laws of Cyberspace*, 2006 — citekey `RCYT4KFH`) establishes the actual-level baseline: in digital environments, the technical architecture *is* governance. What the code permits is permitted; what the code prohibits is prohibited — not by a visible legal rule but by the invisible constraints of the technical infrastructure.

**Stratification:** code-as-law is an actual-level pattern, not a real-level disposition. It identifies what the metaverse *does* through its architecture, not what its governing disposition *is*. The real-level disposition that drives architectural governance is meta-principle 1.1 (unknowability-by-the-governed) plus 1.2 (designed mutability): code can govern unobtrusively because the governed cannot read it, and code-as-governance is amenable to continuous revision in a way that statutory law is not.

The critical extension beyond Lessig: Lessig analyzed code-as-law in terms of regulatory power (who controls code controls behavior). The thesis's analysis goes further by stratifying: regulatory power at the actual level operates through the meta-principles at the real level. Lessig is correct about what is happening; the thesis identifies the dispositional structure that makes what is happening operate as governance at all.

### 2.2 Extraction as actual-level selection pattern

[!VERIFY: stratification level for "extraction imperative"] — **Author asked**: is this imperative empirical, actual, or real?

**Phase-1 stratification answer:** Extraction operates across all three levels but the load-bearing claim is at the **real** level.
- *Empirical level*: specific extractions — Facebook's tracking pixels, Google's ad targeting, Apple's privacy-preserving telemetry, Vision Pro's eye-tracking data flows. These are observable instances.
- *Actual level*: the events of extraction-occurring — extraction processes running continuously across the platform infrastructure. These are the recurring patterns that empirical instances aggregate into.
- *Real level*: the disposition of the platform-economy structure toward extraction. Platforms exist as platforms because their economic viability *requires* extractable behavioral data; the imperative is dispositional, not contingent. This is Zuboff's claim about surveillance capitalism reframed in critical-realist vocabulary.

Has the EU AI Act / Digital Services Act materially altered the dispositional structure or only the empirical-level practices?

The real-level extraction imperative — *insofar as* it persists as a structural feature of the platform economy — functions as an environmental law in the squeeze-ontology sense: it differentiates between viable (data-generative) and non-viable (data-non-generative) modes of life within the metaverse. Forms of life that leave no digital trace are architecturally marginal; this is a selection pressure incommensurable with the universe's selection pressures (which do not differentiate between data-generative and non-data-generative activities).

### 2.3 Optimization-driven governance — actual-level mechanism

The metaverse's behavioral regulation occurs primarily through recommendation algorithms, content moderation systems, and feed curation — systems that do not apply pre-stated rules to individual cases but optimize toward specified objectives (engagement, retention, conversion) across billions of interactions simultaneously.

**Stratification — corrected per author [!REVISION]**: Recommendation algorithms, content moderation, and feed curation are *empirical-level mechanisms* and *actual-level governance patterns*, not real-level dispositions. v1's framing called this "the model of the metaverse" at the load-bearing level, which the author flagged as misclassification: the *model* is what is *real*; what we observe is the actual-level signature of the model.

What is real-level here is the **disposition toward governance-by-feedback rather than governance-by-rule** — the structural tendency for governance to occur through cybernetic optimization of objective functions rather than through articulated rules-and-sanctions. Recommendation algorithms etc. are the empirical/actual-level instances of this disposition.

The distinction matters for the squeeze argument:
- **Governance by rule** (the model of the legal system at the real level): a rule is in principle articulable; a violation is identifiable; the ruled party can in principle read the rule and know what is required.
- **Governance by feedback / optimization** (the disposition the metaverse instantiates): an objective function is operative; the system adjusts its outputs continuously to maximize the function; the ruled party experiences the effects (amplification, suppression, recommendation, denial) without necessary access to the objective function or its current parameters.

- Recent technical work on mechanistic interpretability of large neural networks (Anthropic, DeepMind, OpenAI internal research; Apollo Research)

This is the central LOOKUP-driven question. Pending resolution, the thesis's claim is conditional: *insofar as* large-scale optimization systems remain operationally opaque to the governed, the squeeze's law-change asymmetry (F3) operates through this dispositional opacity.

### 2.4 Network-effect compulsion — actual-level pattern

The metaverse exerts its governing logic through network effects: the value of the network increases with each additional participant, creating a compulsion toward inclusion. Non-membership becomes a form of social and economic exclusion; exit carries costs (loss of social connections, professional visibility, economic access) that are not voluntarily assumed but structurally imposed.

**Stratification:** Network-effect compulsion is an actual-level pattern that signatures meta-principle 1.3 (structural compulsion). It is empirically visible in the dynamics of dominant platforms (LinkedIn for professional networking, WhatsApp for social messaging in many regions, etc.) and operates as an actual-level mechanism of the radical-monopoly disposition.

### 2.5 Temporal acceleration — actual-level mechanism of F3

The laws of the universe are stable on human timescales. The actual-level pattern of the metaverse's governance is that policies, algorithms, and access conditions change faster than human cultural and biological adaptation can track. Platform terms of service are updated without negotiation; algorithms are retrained continuously; what is amplified today is suppressed tomorrow.

**Stratification:** Temporal acceleration is the actual-level signature of meta-principle 1.2 (designed mutability) and the operational expression of F3 (law-change asymmetry, per `squeeze-ontology.md` §2). Empirically, this is visible in how rapidly dominant platforms revise their operational parameters; structurally, it is a feature of the platform-architecture, not a contingent feature of any specific platform.

This temporal acceleration is itself a governing mechanism: by changing rules faster than human adaptation can respond, the metaverse creates a permanent condition of adaptive lag. Inhabitants are always already behind; always adapting to a previous version of the law; always vulnerable to the next update. This is the structural condition from which psychosomatic disruption, existential anxiety, and livelihood precarity follow at the level of human experience (which the squeeze argument analyzes downstream).

## §3 — Synthesis: incommensurability, restated

The five actual-level signatures (§2.1–§2.5) and the three real-level meta-principles (§1.1–§1.3) jointly answer Liu's question without overclaiming.

**The honest claim:** at the real level, the metaverse's governing disposition is structurally opaque to the governed, designed for mutability, and tending toward structural compulsion. We cannot enumerate its specific real-level laws; we can identify the dispositional signatures and recurring actual-level patterns through which the disposition manifests.

**The squeeze claim, restated:**

The universe's laws are *stable, in-principle legible, and indifferent to data-generativity or platform-participation* — they operate transfactually on all physical processes and admit of long-observation learning. The metaverse's laws (insofar as they can be characterized at all at the real level) are *unstable, opaque to the governed, and selecting for specific modes of being* (data-generative, algorithmically legible, network-participant). The two sets are incommensurable in the sense F2 of the squeeze ontology specifies: there is no common measure that would allow a single trade-off calculation between adapting to one and adapting to the other.

An organism that adapts fully to the metaverse's dispositional signature — becoming maximally data-generative, algorithmically legible, network-included — simultaneously becomes less adapted to the universe's laws: more dependent on mediated infrastructure, more attuned to algorithmic visibility, more fragile under conditions in which the metaverse's empirical-level infrastructure fails. The organism is caught between two sets of selection pressures whose demands cannot both be optimally satisfied. This is the squeeze.
