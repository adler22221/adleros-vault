**Evidential grounding (from Needham):**

The II-3 Needham lookup provides two directly relevant passages:

2. **Archipelago/land-mass geography (Needham pp.1-2)**: Needham's framing of civilizational comparison as analogous to an archipelago vs. a land-mass — Chinese scientific traditions as an archipelago of achievements that did not converge into a single dominating tradition. [VERIFY: passage confirmed in II-3 lookup]

**Recommended evidential passage:**

> The contrast with the Chinese tradition provides the clearest evidence that the disposition to domination is a specific cultural configuration rather than a universal feature of advanced civilizations. Needham's comparative analysis identifies the "non-interventionist ideal of classical Chinese science" as its governing orientation — a disposition toward resonance with natural processes rather than their mastery and rearrangement (Needham et al. 2004, p.17). The Chinese scientific tradition produced technical achievements in many domains comparable to or exceeding those of Europe; what it did not produce — and what Needham identifies as requiring explanation — is the systematic disposition to frame the natural world as an object to be dismantled and reconstructed. This difference is not a deficit; it reflects a different governing relationship to nature, one in which human activity seeks accommodation with natural processes rather than their subordination. The Western disposition to domination is visible in relief against this contrast: a historically specific configuration that, by the time of Western Modernity, had achieved a degree of systematic totalization — extending instrumental mastery to all domains, including social, biological, and cognitive life — that the Chinese tradition's governance of its technical development did not replicate.
