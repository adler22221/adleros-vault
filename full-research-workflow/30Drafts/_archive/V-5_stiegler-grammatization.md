## The Grammatization Concept

### What Grammatization Is

Stiegler introduces "grammatization" as his term for the general process by which continuous human flows — of speech, gesture, perception, emotion, and thought — are discretized into calculable and reproducible elements (grammes). The term is derived from the Greek *gramma* (letter, written mark): writing is the paradigmatic grammatization of speech — the continuous, situated, embodied flow of spoken language is broken into discrete, context-independent, reproducible marks that can be stored, transmitted, and reproduced without the presence of the speaking body.

Grammatization is therefore the unified logic of what Stiegler also calls *hypomnemata* (technically externalized memory supports, from *Phaedrus*) and *tertiary retention* (from the II-5 passage, already written): it is the process by which the continuous flow of lived experience is discretized into storable, transmissible technical objects. The alphabet grammatizes speech; musical notation grammatizes musical performance; double-entry bookkeeping grammatizes commercial exchange; the scientific method grammatizes experimental observation; digital code grammatizes all of the above into a single computational substrate.

### Grammatization as the Unified Logic of the Cybernetic Loop

The metaverse's "self-completing tendency" (§V) operates through grammatization: the progressive discretization of all human flows — behavioral, social, emotional, cognitive — into the data substrate that algorithmic systems can process, store, analyze, and feed back into the governance of human behavior. The cybernetic loop (behavior → data → algorithm → behavioral modification → behavior) is the grammatization loop: every human action within the metaverse is a gramme — a discrete, recordable, analyzable unit of behavior.

The significance: grammatization is not merely surveillance. It is a *constitutive transformation* of what the grammatized flow is. Written language is not merely recorded speech; it transforms the possibilities of linguistic thought — enabling kinds of reasoning (legal argument, mathematical proof, complex historical narrative) that spoken language cannot sustain. Digital grammatization of human behavior is similarly constitutive: it does not merely record what humans do; it transforms what it means to do anything within the metaverse, because every action is simultaneously a piece of behavior AND a data object with commercial and algorithmic value. The two are inseparable within the metaverse's infrastructure.

This constitutive transformation is the mechanism of the self-completing tendency: as more of human activity is drawn into the grammatization loop, the loop's capacity to govern activity expands — creating the feedback dynamic that drives the metaverse's progressive extension over more domains of existence.

## Three-Stage Generalized Proletarianization

Stiegler's most important political consequence of grammatization is the thesis of *generalized proletarianization*: the loss of savoir (know-how) that accompanies the industrial appropriation of each stage of human competence. The argument runs through three historical stages:

### Stage 1: Loss of Savoir-Faire (Manual Deskilling, 19th–20th century)

Industrial production grammatizes manual craft knowledge: the specific, embodied, accumulated competence of the craftsperson is discretized into a sequence of standardized operations that can be performed by a machine or by an unskilled laborer following machine-defined procedures. The result: the craftsperson's savoir-faire — the embodied knowledge of how to work with specific materials in specific conditions — is transferred from the worker's body into the machine's design. The worker retains the capacity to perform the discrete operations the machine requires; the integrated know-how that constituted the craft is lost.

Marx's analysis of the deskilling of the industrial worker is the classical account of Stage 1. Stiegler's contribution is to embed this in the grammatization framework: the machine is a grammatized form of craft knowledge — knowledge that has been discretized, stored, and externalized in the technical object.

### Stage 2: Loss of Savoir-Vivre (Cultural Standardization, 20th century)

The 20th-century culture industries extend grammatization to savoir-vivre: the know-how of how to live — how to cook, how to dress, how to celebrate, how to mourn, how to organize a household, how to conduct social relations. These forms of practical cultural competence are grammatized through the standardizing mechanisms of broadcast media, commodity food production, fashion systems, and lifestyle marketing. The result: the culturally specific, locally calibrated, communally transmitted know-how of living is replaced by algorithmically optimized (in the broad sense: market-researched, audience-tested, commercially managed) content that is produced outside the community and consumed rather than practiced.

The loss is not of the activities themselves — people still eat, dress, and celebrate — but of the active competence that constituted these as forms of cultural *practice* rather than consumption. Watching a cooking show is not the same as knowing how to cook; following a fashion algorithm is not the same as knowing how to dress for one's specific body, climate, and social context. The savoir-vivre is lost even as its surface forms are preserved.

### Stage 3: Loss of Savoir-Théoriser (AI Automation of Intellectual Labor, 21st century)

The third stage — the one the metaverse most fully represents — is the grammatization of intellectual and cognitive labor: savoir-théoriser, the know-how of thinking. Large language models, algorithmic recommendation systems, and AI-assisted decision-making systems grammatize the processes of reasoning, judgment, and intellectual synthesis that previously constituted the irreducible contribution of the human thinker. The AI is not merely a tool for executing human decisions; it grammatizes the decision process itself — breaking it into discrete, pattern-matchable elements that can be reproduced without the human judgment that originally produced the patterns.

The significance for the thesis: the third stage is what makes the metaverse's proletarianization qualitatively different from prior deskilling. The first two stages deskilled manual and cultural competence; intellectual judgment was preserved as the distinctively human domain. The third stage penetrates that domain. If savoir-théoriser is lost, what remains is not a deskilled but fully human subject; it is a being that has lost the competence of self-governance — the capacity to form, evaluate, and act on its own judgments. This is the deepest form of the squeeze: the squeeze at the level of the cognitive capacities through which the human *is* the world-making animal.

## The Squeeze as Lived Experience of Total Deskilling

Stiegler's three-stage framework provides the mechanism behind the squeeze's phenomenology:

- **Psychosomatic malfunctions** (VI-1): the biological signal that Stage 1 and Stage 2 deskilling have exceeded the body's adaptive capacity — the body can no longer maintain universe-fitness under conditions of increasing metaverse-adaptation
- **Singularization of livelihood** (VI-8): Stage 2 deskilling at the scale of vernacular economic competence — the radical monopoly over livelihood is the economic expression of savoir-vivre loss
- **Disturbed lives under opaque laws** (VI-1): Stage 3 deskilling at the level of civic and cognitive judgment — algorithmic governance replacing the savoir-théoriser of democratic deliberation

The squeeze, in Stiegler's grammatization framework, is not simply the pressure between two environments but the *lived experience of progressive expropriation of competence*. The organism being squeezed is not merely subject to two sets of demands; it is an organism whose capacities for managing those demands are being systematically transferred out of its control into the technical system that generates the demands. This is what makes the squeeze increasingly irresolvable by individual adaptation: the tools for adaptation are being grammatized out of the individual's possession at the same time as the adaptive demands increase.
