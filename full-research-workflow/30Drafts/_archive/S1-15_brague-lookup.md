## Key Passages

### Passage 1 — Arabic root of "world" and its original meaning (core passage)
> "In Hebrew, the medieval and modern word for 'world' is `ōlām. Its root is Semitic, the same as the Arabic word `ālam — which itself came from Aramaic. The word is indeed found in the Bible; but there, it has only the sense of 'unlimited time, eternity,' and not yet that of 'world,' which it would only assume in the Talmudic period through the intermediary of the meaning of 'era'."

— Brague, Rémi. *The Wisdom of the World: The Human Experience of the Universe in Western Thought*. Trans. Teresa Lavender Fagan. Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 2003. p. 12.

**Relevance:** The Arabic word for "world" (`ālam) etymologically meant *time/eternity*, not world-as-totality. The concept of world as a spatial-physical totality was a later, non-original development — mediated through Greek influence (the aion parallel is explicitly noted). This directly supports 馬's point: the Arabic linguistic tradition did not natively generate the concept of "world" as a unified totality.

### Passage 2 — The conceptual precondition for "world" (immediately following)
> "For there to have been a word meaning 'world,' the idea that it expresses would have had to have reached human consciousness. And this assumes that people envisioned a concept in its totality, a category grasped in its two moments, that is, as a synthesis of the first two categories of quantity, plurality and unity. It is necessary, therefore, on the one hand, that the parts that make up the whole be dealt with exhaustively, without anything being excluded, and, on the other hand, that such totality be considered unified."

— Brague, *Wisdom of the World*, p. 12.

**Relevance:** Brague specifies the conceptual achievement required to have a "world" concept: grasping plurality-as-unity as a totality. This is the Greek innovation. Pre-Greek civilizations (including those with Semitic-language roots) approached totality differently — through list-making or binary opposition (heaven and earth) rather than a unified "cosmos." This passage justifies the thesis's emphasis on the Western origins of the "world" concept.

### Passage 3 — Ancient civilizations' alternative approach (no word for "world")
> "The earliest civilizations certainly did not formulate the concept of the totality of things in this way. The ancient Egyptians did not have a word for 'world,' any more than did the languages of Mesopotamia."

**Relevance:** Broader comparative frame: the concept of world-as-totality is not universal. ### Passage 4 — World concept as specifically Greek achievement
> "This was only to be the case in Greece. It was there, and there alone, that that 'distanced' position would appear, that 'Archimedes point' from which man would be able, 'conscious of being a subject (subjektbewußt), submit nature to objective research'."

— Brague, *Wisdom of the World*, p. 15.

**Relevance:** Brague argues that the concept of "world" as a neutral, unified, objective totality is a specifically Greek invention — enabled by the Greek move to place the observer at an Archimedean distance from nature. This is directly relevant to the thesis's §III argument about Western modernity's "disposition to domination" as rooted in a specific cosmological tradition.

## Nuance for the Thesis

**What 馬 said vs. what Brague actually argues:**

馬's summary — "Arabs don't have the concept of world" — is a simplification of Brague's more nuanced historical argument:

1. The *original* Arabic/Semitic word for world (`ālam) meant *time*, not totality
2. The *concept* of world-as-totality is a Greek achievement, spread westward
3. Non-Greek civilizations (Egypt, Mesopotamia, early Semitic cultures) expressed totality via lists and binary pairs, not unified "cosmos" concepts
4. Medieval Arabic philosophy (Avicenna, Averroes) *did* develop cosmological thought — but this was through the reception of Greek philosophy, not an indigenous development

**Recommended thesis formulation:** Brague's argument should be presented carefully — not as "Arabs have no world concept" (which overstates and invites objection) but as "the concept of world as a unified, observer-independent totality is a specific intellectual inheritance from Greek cosmology, not a universal given across traditions" (which is more defensible and is what Brague actually argues).
