## Searle's Distinction — Summary

John Searle distinguishes between *brute facts* and *institutional facts* [VERIFY: Searle *The Construction of Social Reality* 1995; also *Making the Social World* 2010 — check Zotero for either]:

- **Brute facts**: facts that obtain independently of human opinion, convention, or collective recognition. The mountain is 8,000 meters high; the rock is composed of granite; the wavelength of light at 540nm. These facts are not constituted by human agreement — they would obtain even if no humans existed.

- **Institutional facts**: facts that obtain only within the context of human institutional practices and collective recognition. This piece of paper is money; this person is the President; this action is a foul. These facts require a background of human collective acceptance of *status functions* — the collective agreement that X counts as Y in context C. Institutional facts impose *deontic powers*: obligations, rights, prohibitions that have normative force within the institutional context.

The Searlean framework maps onto the thesis's universe/world distinction to a significant degree: the universe corresponds (roughly) to the domain of brute facts, and the world corresponds to the domain of institutional facts. The world is constituted by status functions and deontic powers — by the collectively accepted "X counts as Y" structures that organize human social life.

## The Author's Critique — Why Searle's Framework Is Too Anthropocentric

The author's DOCX note identifies the crucial limitation: "Searle's dichotomy still too anthropocentric — wouldn't say that rituals in animals have deontic powers."

The ethological proto-virtualities identified in Ch-1-19 (gorilla chest-beating, crane courtship dance, collective excitement management) exhibit a structural feature that Searle's framework cannot accommodate: they function as status functions — they produce real social effects through a representational layer that substitutes for the direct enactment of the represented state — but they do not involve human consciousness, language, or "collective acceptance" in Searle's sense.

The gorilla chest-beat functions as a deontic constraint in the gorilla social world: it establishes claims of dominance that other gorillas respond to as obligatory — stepping back, deferring, modifying their behavior in response to the display. This is functionally equivalent to a deontic power: the display imposes an obligation-structure on other members of the group. But it does not arise from human collective acceptance, language, or institutional practice in Searle's sense. It arises from the evolutionary history of the species and the neurobiological mechanisms that produce and respond to the display.

Searle's framework would deny this the status of institutional fact: for Searle, deontic powers require language (since status functions must be linguistically articulable: "X counts as Y in context C") and human collective recognition. The gorilla chest-beat is a brute natural event — a behavior produced by neurobiological mechanisms — not an institutional fact.

But this seems to miss what is significant about the display: its *functional role* in producing socially structured obligations is identical to what institutional facts do in human social life, even if the mechanism through which it operates (evolutionary neurobiological programming rather than linguistic collective acceptance) is different.

## The Thesis's Position

The thesis's world/universe distinction is broader than Searle's brute/institutional distinction in a specific way:

**Searle's institutional facts** require: human collective recognition, language, status functions ("X counts as Y in C"), deontic powers arising from those functions.

**The thesis's "world"** requires: a representational layer that mediates between organism and environment, producing social effects through reference rather than direct enactment — with the specific human contribution being the *scale* and *constitutive role* of this representational layer (the world, not merely isolated representational acts).

This means the thesis's framework can accommodate:
1. **Human worlds**: institutional facts in Searle's full sense — language, status functions, explicit deontic powers
2. **Animal proto-worlds**: representational systems that produce social obligations without Searlean institutional facts — the gorilla's social world has a structure, even if it is not an institutional structure in Searle's sense

And it avoids Searle's anthropocentrism: the world is not the unique possession of language-using species but the apex of an evolutionary trajectory that includes non-human representational systems at lower levels of complexity. Searle draws the line at human language; the thesis draws the line at world-making (which human language enables in its full form, but which has evolutionary precursors in animal representational behavior).
