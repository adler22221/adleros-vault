**Phantomization** — Fuchs uses this term for the process by which mediated interaction becomes disembodied: the other is reduced to a "phantom" — a presence that registers at the level of perception and affect but lacks the full corporeal weight of co-presence.

**Virtual contemporaneity** — Fuchs contrasts *synchrony* (real-time shared embodied presence) with *virtual contemporaneity* (simultaneous connection that simulates co-presence without achieving it). The distinction turns on whether two bodies actually share a *lived moment* or merely exchange information in real time.

## Content of *Ecology of the Brain* (2018) — From Secondary Sources

Based on reviews, citations in secondary literature, and knowledge of Fuchs's phenomenological project, the 2018 book develops the following framework relevant to the thesis:

### Core Argument

The brain is not a self-contained processing unit but an *ecological organ* — it is constituted in and through the organism's ongoing intercorporeal engagement with its environment. Mind is not "in" the brain but in the *loop* between brain, body, and world (the "extended" or "enactive" mind position, but in Husserlian-Merleau-Pontyean idiom).

### The Leib / Körper Distinction

Fuchs maintains Husserl's distinction (confirmed in a PMC review consulted in Cycle 1):

- **Leib** (lived body): the body as experienced from within — proprioceptive, affective, temporally extended in lived time
- **Körper** (object body): the body as seen from without — anatomical, mechanically measurable, in clock time

Digital/virtual environments engage predominantly with the *Körper*-register of the other (their image, their data-trace) while suppressing the *Leib*-register (their felt presence, their embodied rhythms).

### Temporal Phenomenology — "Body Time" vs. Abstract/Virtual Time

Fuchs argues (following Husserl's *Internal Time-Consciousness* and Merleau-Ponty's account of motility) that the lived body has its *own* temporal structure:

- **Body time** (*Leibzeit* / lived time): the rhythmic, pulsed, affectively colored temporality of embodied existence — heartbeat, breathing, hunger, fatigue, anticipation, grief. This time is not measurable by clock; it is the felt duration of a subject for whom the past has weight and the future is protended.
- **Abstract/virtual time**: the standardized, reversible, compressible time of digital environments — time that can be paused, rewound, accelerated, synchronized across hemispheres. It is a *representation* of time, not its lived enactment.

The thesis's V-1 placeholder likely refers to a claim Fuchs makes — possibly in Chapter 5 or 6 of the 2018 book, on "Temporality and Intersubjectivity" or "The Virtual World" — that media saturation imposes *abstract time* on subjects accustomed to *body time*, with pathological effects: the "always-on" digital subject loses access to the natural rhythms through which embodied identity is maintained (sleep-wake cycles, meal rhythms, seasonal attunement).

### The "Ecology" Frame — Relevance to Thesis

The title "ecology of the brain" is directly cognate with the thesis's environmental/ecological framing. Fuchs treats the brain-body-world loop as an *ecological relationship* — not a machine running software but an organism embedded in and constituted by its environment. This aligns with the thesis's claim that the metaverse functions as a new *environment* that imposes adaptational pressures on organisms evolved for a different one.

Specifically, if the brain is ecologically constituted by its environment, then the replacement of the vernacular world-environment with the neo-artificial metaverse-environment represents not merely a change in stimuli but a restructuring of the conditions under which embodied subjectivity is possible. The "squeeze" is not metaphorical; it is a literal ecological pressure on the organism.
