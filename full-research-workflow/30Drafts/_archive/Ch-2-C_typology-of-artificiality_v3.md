# §IIC — A Typology of Artificiality

## §IIC.1 — Recap and the question §IIC asks

§IIA introduced **artificiality** as the broad category: humanly-produced organizing principles — material and abstract alike — including not only tools but institutions, laws, symbols, language, customs, religion, ideology, and music. The category is triangulated by two independent traditions. Stiegler's broad reading of *technē* (*Technics and Time 1*) holds that "technics (tekhne) designates… first and foremost all the domains of skill" — politeness, dance, rhetoric, poetry, language, philosophy, medicine, the polis, written law — such that "all human action has something to do with tekhne." Obara Hideo's concept of 「モノ」 stipulates that the katakana form (as distinct from the hiragana 「もの」 of concrete material artefacts) names "everything humans have created, including abstract language," with religion, ideology, and music explicitly included (*ペット化する現代人*, 1995). The convergence of these two traditions — late-modern French philosophy of technics and contemporary Japanese ethological anthropology — supplies the warrant for treating artificiality as the umbrella concept rather than as a coined term.

§IIA's disclaimer paragraph stipulates the thesis's deliberate vocabulary discipline: the term "technology" is *not* deployed as a major thesis concept. Stiegler himself diagnoses the narrow modern reading of technics as "an inadequate understanding, one that is already restrictive and derived" (T&T1). The thesis takes his diagnosis at its word: where authors in the philosophy-of-technology tradition (Stiegler, Heidegger, Hui, Foucault, Feenberg, Ellul) write of "technology," this thesis relabels their concept as *artificiality* in the broad sense, with the specific narrowing or broadening noted in each engagement. This vocabulary discipline avoids the otherwise-unavoidable slippage between the broad sense of "technology" (which is what most of these authors mean) and the narrow modern engineered-instrumental sense (which is what readers tend to import). The slippage is precisely the move that would invite the thesis-defeating misreading "the metaverse is more advanced technology"; the thesis works at the artificiality level to keep that misreading foreclosed.

§IIB introduced **virtuality** as the *causally efficacious nonmaterial* dimension of artificiality — anchored on Giddens (social structures exist virtually) and Bhaskar (the virtual as a sui generis real emergent stratum with its own irreducible causal powers). Against Pierce's "as if" simulationism, against Chalmers' "computer-based" definition, and against Baudrillard's pure-simulation reading, the thesis defines virtuality as that subset of artificiality whose compelling power is given by its meaning rather than by its physical instantiation. Across history, virtuality has populated gods, money, laws-as-institutional-facts, narrative, customary structures, scientific paradigms, animal proto-virtuality (Obara's gorilla chest-beating, crane courtship). Virtuality is *substrate-independent* in implementation: shells, coins, paper, digits, biological circuitry, sound waves are all merely temporal media for the underlying causally-efficacious nonmaterial principle.

Three committee questions converge on §IIC:

- **米 / Ch-2-2**: "rule比law更合適" — is "rule" not more appropriate than "law" when speaking of artificial environments?
- **米 / Ch-2-4**: "laws為何reducible?" — argue, do not assert, that the laws of artificial environments are not reducible to universe-laws.
- **Author / Ch-2-7**: "Rules? Laws? / Argumentation needed."

Per the author's answer to the examiner's "if world is in universe, where is virtuality?" (recorded in `claude-snippets.md` line 634): the nested structure is `universe [world [material artificiality + virtual artificiality]]`. World is composed of *both* material artificiality (paradigmatically technical artefacts) *and* virtual artificiality (the §IIB virtuality field). Both dimensions together constitute world; neither is reducible to universe-laws.

§IIC asks: **how are artificialities — including the cases that virtuality picks out as a specific subset — structurally related to one another, and what makes the contemporary moment structurally distinctive?** The answer is a typology that classifies artificialities by two axes (substrate and register) yielding four cells, with one cell that was effectively empty across most of human history but has been newly populated at scale by the metaverse-stage.

## §IIC.2 — Virtuality as analytical lens on artificiality

Before the typology proper, the relation between *artificiality* (the umbrella) and *virtuality* (§IIB's specific concept) must be made explicit, because the latter is easily mistaken for an axis-value or for a parallel category.

Artificiality and virtuality are NOT parallel concepts. They are *hierarchically related*: virtuality is the §IIB-specific *analytical lens* that the thesis applies to the nonmaterial-causally-efficacious subset of artificiality. Every virtuality is an artificiality (in the broad Stiegler-technē / Obara-モノ sense); not every artificiality is paradigmatically virtual (walls, locks, infrastructure are artificialities whose compelling power is given by their material instantiation, not by their meaning).

The §IIB definition of virtuality stipulates: virtuality is the causally efficacious nonmaterial dimension of artificiality, real-and-causal-but-not-material, substrate-independent in implementation. The typology that follows uses the substrate-axis to mark the artificialities the virtuality lens illuminates — those whose substrate is nonmaterial, where the principle's compelling power is given by meaning with the physical substrate merely the temporal medium.

A defensive sentence: in the typology that follows, "nonmaterial artificiality" (the substrate-axis label) is co-extensive with "virtuality" in the §IIB sense. The two terms are deployed in different rhetorical contexts: "virtuality" in §IIB, §V, §VI, and the introduction — where the conceptual lineage, the redefinition against Pierce / Chalmers / Baudrillard, the historical continuum from animal proto-virtuality to algorithmic systems, and the metaverse-argument-bridge all require the term; "nonmaterial artificiality" in §IIC's typology — where the analytical work is substrate × register classification, not the philosophical-cultural concept of the virtual.

## §IIC.3 — Two axes for classifying artificialities

### Axis 1 — Substrate: material / nonmaterial

Does the artificiality operate *as a material thing* (its compelling power is given by its physical instantiation) or *as a nonmaterial thing* (its compelling power is given by its meaning, with the physical instantiation merely the temporal medium)?

- **Material**: the artificiality's compelling power is given by its physical-material properties operating under universe-laws. A wall stops you because of mass and structural integrity. A door opens because of hinge geometry. A locked door compels by the physical properties of lock and frame. The substrate is constitutive of the principle.

- **Nonmaterial**: the artificiality's compelling power is given by its *meaning* — the rule, the value, the institutional fact — and the physical-material substrate is merely the temporal medium of operation. Money compels by what it means, not by the metal or paper or digits it inhabits. A god compels by faith and community, not by any specific material relic. An algorithm compels by what it computes, not by the specific server farm running it. The substrate is incidental; the principle is *substrate-independent* (per §IIB; per the author's #40 DNA-programming elaboration).

This is the same axis as substrate-coupling (embedded in universe-substrate vs. decoupled from universe-substrate) but named in §IIB's vocabulary. Material = embedded; nonmaterial = decoupled. **The substrate axis does NOT carry the technology/virtuality distinction** (v2's framing, now superseded). Nonmaterial artificiality IS the virtuality field per §IIB; "technology" is not a typological term within the thesis (per §IIA's vocabulary discipline).

### Axis 2 — Register: conventional / structural `[TERMINOLOGY-PENDING]`

Does the artificiality compel by ongoing human enactment (conventional register) or by the structure of the situation itself (structural register)?

- **Conventional register**: the artificiality compels because and to the extent that humans continue to enact it. A statute is enforced because police, prosecutors, judges, and prisons exist and act. A norm holds because the community sanctions violators. A market clears because participants honor contracts. A religious commandment compels because the faithful believe and the community enforces. **When practice ceases, the artificiality ceases to operate.** The Lessig modalities of *law*, *norms*, and *market* (`Code 2.0`) are paradigmatic.

- **Structural register** `[TERMINOLOGY-PENDING]`: the artificiality compels by the structure of the situation itself — the architecture, affordances, and operational dynamics of the artificiality — rather than by social agreement. A locked door does not require an enforcer to remain locked. An algorithm executes regardless of which user encounters it or whether the user believes in it. A blockchain smart contract performs its conditional regardless of subsequent regret. Lessig's fourth modality — *architecture* — is paradigmatic. **The violation is foreclosed as an action-possibility within the field**, not merely deterred by sanction.

> **Defensive note on "structural"** `[TERMINOLOGY-PENDING]` *(working signpost; cross-reference: `20Wiki/squeeze-ontology.md` §1.3 Aspect A)*. By "structural" we mean: compliance secured by the structure of the situation — the architecture, affordances, and operational dynamics of the artificiality — rather than by social agreement. The contrast with "conventional" is *modal*: a conventional rule remains in force when violated (contestable in principle); a structural rule forecloses the violation as an action-possibility within the field. The structural register is NOT Humean constant-conjunction causality, and it is NOT merely the generic critical-realist claim that real-level structures have causal powers (every real-level structure does). What "structural" names is the specific case in which an artificiality's architecture / affordance-field structurally circumscribes action-possibilities (Gibson affordances / Lessig architecture-as-regulation / Foucault dispositif / Hui productive-ontologies-via-associated-milieus). The term accommodates both deterministic mechanisms (a lock; gravity) and probabilistic mechanisms (LLM next-token distributions; recommender rankings) — in both cases compliance is enforced by the structure of the situation rather than by ongoing social enactment. The register applies symmetrically to cell 1 (physical-structural: universe-laws operating on substrate) and cell 4 (engineered-structural: algorithmic architecture, platform affordances).

The register is a **dominant** claim, not absolute. Pre-metaverse conventional artificiality has *structural margins* (legal coercion enforced by state monopoly on violence; infrastructural lock-in; market compulsion that approaches structural inescapability). Metaverse-stage structural artificiality has *conventional margins* (platform policies are revisable; community guidelines are contested). What `squeeze-ontology.md` §1.3 names is the *progressive shrinking* of the conventional margin through 自足完成化 — initial decisions become harder to revise once the system reaches operational maturity; algorithmic systems become entrenched; auditability decreases.

## §IIC.4 — The four cells

The two axes cross to yield four cells of artificialities.

| | **Material (embedded in universe-substrate)** | **Nonmaterial (substrate-independent; the virtuality field per §IIB)** |
|---|---|---|
| **Structural** `[TERMINOLOGY-PENDING]` | **Cell 1** — Material artificialities whose compelling power is given by universe-laws: walls, locks, infrastructure, the built environment as Lessig-style architectural constraint | **Cell 4** — Nonmaterial artificialities with structural compulsion: code-as-law, algorithmic content moderation, recommendation systems, ML decision systems, blockchain smart contracts |
| **Conventional** | **Cell 2** — Material artificialities with conventional use-norms: tools whose use is governed by norms (a fork in dining etiquette; a ceremonial object); physical infrastructure subject to political revision (zoning, traffic conventions) | **Cell 3** — Nonmaterial artificialities with conventional enforcement: gods, money-as-institutional-fact, customary law, narrative, social structures, religious commandments, scientific paradigms, etiquette |

Cells 1–4 are populated by *artificialities* — no cell is labelled "technology" or "virtuality." The virtuality lens (per §IIB) applies to cells 3 and 4 (the nonmaterial column). The technology lens of the broader philosophy-of-technology tradition, when imported, applies most naturally to cells 1, 2, and 4 (the cases where exteriorization of organs and memory is structurally pronounced); the thesis does not adopt this lens as a load-bearing classificatory device (per §IIA's vocabulary discipline) but acknowledges it when engaging interlocutors.

### Cell 1 — Material + Structural: physical artificiality with structural compulsion

A locked door compels by the physical properties of lock, frame, and material. A wall stops you because of mass and structural integrity. Infrastructure shapes movement because of physical geography. These artificialities are *substrate-embedded* (compelling power given by universe-laws acting on the material substrate) and *structural* (compelling power does not require ongoing human enactment; the lock stays locked when nobody is watching).

Cell 1 includes both naturally-given features (gravity, terrain, weather — borderline cases on the artificiality / non-artificiality boundary) and humanly-engineered features (walls, doors, plumbing, electrical grids). What matters for the classification is that compelling power is given by universe-laws operating on the substrate.

### Cell 2 — Material + Conventional: material artificiality with conventional use-norms

A fork is a physical object whose proper use is governed by dining-etiquette norms. A ceremonial mask is a physical object whose meaning is enforced by community convention. A road exists physically, but its "right-of-way" rules require ongoing enactment. A border is materially-instantiated (border posts, fences) but its function as a border is conventionally enforced (passports, border guards, treaties).

Cell 2 is the cell of *artificiality subject to norm at the use-level*. The artefact is material; the rule governing its use, ownership, or meaning is conventional. This cell is heavily populated by historical artifacts of culture: tools-with-use-norms, ceremonial objects, vehicles-with-traffic-rules, weapons-with-warrior-codes.

### Cell 3 — Nonmaterial + Conventional: pre-metaverse virtuality (the vast historical field)

This is the cell of §IIB's virtuality in its dominant historical form. **The vast majority of human civilizational virtuality, across all of recorded history until very recently, has occupied this cell.**

Examples per §IIB and per Harari's cognitive-revolution argument:

- **Gods and religious systems**: causally efficacious (faith elicits emotions, rituals shape behavior, religious law constrains action) but nonmaterial; enforced by community sanction and the believer's ongoing belief
- **Money-as-institutional-fact** (the underlying virtuality of which shells, coins, paper, and digits are merely temporal media)
- **Laws-as-institutional-fact** (statutes are written on parchment / paper / digital files, but the *law itself* is the nonmaterial rule; enforced by ongoing apparatus)
- **Narratives and stories** (per Durante: "humans have always dealt with the virtual ever since humans lived by the stories they collectively invented")
- **Customary law and social structures** (per Giddens: social structures exist virtually); etiquette, kinship rules, status hierarchies
- **Scientific paradigms** (Kuhn): the paradigm itself is nonmaterial-causally-efficacious; enforced by ongoing scientific practice and community
- **Cognitive-revolution shared fictions** (per Harari): gods, nations, laws, money — "the nonmaterial scaffolding of the first human worlds" that "broke through Dunbar's number" by enabling cooperation among strangers
- **Animal proto-virtuality** (per Obara, per §IIB): gorilla chest-beating, crane courtship — pre-recursive virtual-like systems, showing virtuality is not uniquely human and predates dedicated technical mediation (the human/animal threshold is recursive cumulation, per the Stiegler-Obara convergence specified in §IIA)

Cell 3 has been the operative virtuality-cell for all of human civilization since the cognitive revolution and possibly before (in animal proto-virtuality). It is *vast*; it is the *default* form of virtuality across history.

### Cell 4 — Nonmaterial + Structural: metaverse-stage virtuality

This is the cell of artificiality that compels by the structure of the situation itself rather than by ongoing human enactment.

Examples per the metaverse-laws specification of §V (Ch-5-3 v3 §1–§5):

- **Code-as-law** (Lessig): an algorithm's output is the rule, and the rule executes regardless of belief or consent
- **Algorithmic content moderation**: a post is removed by automated decision; the user's appeal is itself processed algorithmically
- **Recommendation systems**: the user cannot ask to see different content; the algorithm's probabilistic distribution is what determines what is seen
- **ML inference systems**: the model's decision is the decision; the user has no recourse within the system
- **Blockchain smart contracts**: once instantiated, the contract executes its conditional regardless of subsequent regret or social agreement
- **Platform affordances**: what the user *can* do is structurally circumscribed by the platform's design; what is not afforded is not negotiable within the system

Cell 4 has the same *structural-compelling character* as cell 1 (in cell 1, the wall stops you because of physics; in cell 4, the algorithm decides because of code) but operates in the nonmaterial register that virtuality has always occupied (in cell 4, the rule is engineered-nonmaterial — code's meaning, an algorithm's logic — not given by universe-laws).

## §IIC.5 — The structural-novelty claim: cell 4 was empty, and what cell 4's population does

Cells 1, 2, and 3 have all been occupied for most of human history.

- **Cell 1** has been occupied for as long as humans have built walls, roads, and tools — from the late Pleistocene niche-construction transitions onward; naturally-given cell-1 features (gravity, terrain) precede humanity entirely.
- **Cell 2** has been occupied since the emergence of material culture with associated use-norms — i.e., as soon as humans produced artefacts and developed conventions about them, perhaps from the Paleolithic.
- **Cell 3** has been occupied since the cognitive revolution (per Harari, ~70,000 years ago), and animal proto-virtuality (per Obara) extends even further back. The cell-3 population — gods, money, narrative, customary law, social structures — is the entire vast field of human civilizational virtuality across all of recorded history.

**Cell 4 was effectively empty until very recently.** Across most of human history, causally efficacious nonmaterial artificiality compelled by *conventional enforcement* (cell 3), not by structural operation. A statute became operational because police and courts existed; a religious commandment became operational because the faithful enforced it; money became operational because all participants accepted it. The principle was nonmaterial and causally efficacious, but its compulsion was conventional — it required ongoing human enactment.

The metaverse-stage's structural distinctness is that **cell 4 has been newly populated at scale**. Code-as-law is virtuality (nonmaterial: the rule is the program's logic, not the silicon) that compels structurally (the program executes regardless of consent or belief). This is *new in human history* — not as a quantitative expansion of cell 3 (more virtuality), but as the *qualitative populating of a previously-empty cell* (virtuality acquiring structural compulsion).

**The diagnostic stakes of cell 4's novelty.** Cell 4's typological novelty is not merely classificatory; it carries the load of the thesis's diagnostic argument. Across four traditions developed in pre-metaverse settings — Stiegler's pharmakon (writing as both remedy and poison; cell-3 case), Illich's two watersheds (cars and schools and hospitals as cell-1/2 cases), Adorno and Horkheimer's *Dialectic of Enlightenment* (the administered world as cell-2/3 case), and the Confucian 君子不器 dictum against the reduction of human-being-ness to specialized function — independent diagnoses converge on a single structural fact: artificialities at scale tend to generate the conditions for the harm they were instituted to relieve, absent active counter-work. Cell 4 is the typological position from which this tendency-toward-inversion reaches its limit case, because cell 4's structural register forecloses the counter-work that each tradition named (Stiegler's care; Illich's threshold-vigilance; Frankfurt's dialectical critique; Confucian 修身) at the rate that artificiality-mutation now requires. §IID develops this argument and segues into §V (cell 4's internal specification) and §VI (cell-4-stage squeeze deployment).

This is the structural fact that the squeeze ontology's §1.3 names as the "stratified two-environment configuration": the metaverse is a new real-level background environment because cell 4 is now occupied at scale, and cell-4-occupied virtuality has the structural-compelling character that makes it function as a *real-level background environment* (parallel to cell 1's universe-laws) rather than as a derivative cultural-institutional layer (cell 3 occupies this position).

**Putting it precisely**: pre-metaverse virtuality was nonmaterial but conventional (cell 3); metaverse-stage virtuality is nonmaterial AND structural (cell 4). This is what Ch-5-3 v3 §1.4 specifies as *Yang's 自足完成化*: artificiality acquiring the disposition to operate self-sufficiently, without continuous human direction. The cell-4 entry is the structural-cell-name for this disposition.

## §IIC.6 — Answering the committee questions via the typology

### On rule vs. law (Ch-2-2, Ch-2-7)

The terminological question "rule vs. law" admits a structural answer: the two terms have been doing double-duty for *different cells* of the typology.

- **"Law" in cell 1's context** names the physical-architectural laws governing universe-substrate (gravity, thermodynamics, biology). These are *universe-laws* operating on substrate-embedded artificiality. They are structural by nature.
- **"Rule" in cell 2's context** names use-norms attached to material artificiality — etiquette, traffic conventions, ceremonial protocols. These are conventional.
- **"Rule" or "norm" in cell 3's context** names the vast conventional virtuality field — religious commandments, customary law, narrative structures, social roles, paradigms. These are virtuality-with-conventional-enforcement.
- **"Law" in cell 4's context** names engineered-structural artificiality — code-as-law (Lessig), algorithmic decisions, smart contracts. These are virtuality-with-structural-compulsion.

A defensible terminological convention:
- Use **"law"** for *structural* artificialities whose violation is structurally prevented rather than externally punished (cells 1 and 4).
- Use **"rule"** or **"norm"** for *conventional* artificialities whose violation is externally punished but not structurally prevented (cells 2 and 3).

Under this convention, the metaverse genuinely has *laws of its own* — not a rhetorical extension of "law" but a structural homology with cell 1's universe-laws. The difference between cell 1's laws and cell 4's laws is that cell-1 laws are *naturally given* (operating by universe-substrate) and cell-4 laws are *humanly engineered* (operating by code-as-substrate). The structural character — they compel without enforcer-intervention — is shared between the two cells.

Conventional and structural are *registers*, not absolute classifications, so the convention has edge cases. Juridical statutes (cell 3 in dominant register, but with cell-1-like structural-coercion margins via the state's monopoly on violence) sit awkwardly between cells; this is a real fact about how juridical law has always tried to acquire structural-compelling power despite its essentially conventional nature. The metaverse's cell-4 character is therefore the *successful structural-compelling power* that conventional law has long aspired to and never fully achieved.

### On irreducibility (Ch-2-4, Ch-2-7)

Cell 4's laws are not reducible to cell 1's laws for the same structural reason that *a piece of software's semantics are not reducible to the physics of the transistors implementing it*: the substrate-embedded layer (the physical hardware) is necessary (the code must run on physical hardware; the algorithm needs servers and electricity) but *not semantically determinative* (the code's meaning is given by the program, not by the transistor physics). This is the **multiple-realizability** argument from philosophy of mind, formalized for artificialities.

The viva-passed §IIC's screwdriver/scissors example precisely illustrates this irreducibility. A screwdriver in cell 1 works because of:
- physical fastener (a screw exists materially with helical threading)
- physical rotational mechanics (torque applied through grip → screwdriver tip → screw)
- gravity (objects to be tightened stay in place)
- soreness of legs (which makes chairs needed for prolonged use)

A screwdriver *simulated* in a digital environment fails to work *not* because the simulation is functionally limited but because the *conditions for screwdriver functionality are absent*: there are no physical fasteners, no gravity, no soreness. The digital environment has its own mechanisms for "tightening up" — algorithms, references, dependencies — none of which a simulated screwdriver could operate on. The screwdriver is *use-less* in the digital environment, not for technical reasons but for structural ones: cell 4's organizing principles are different from cell 1's.

The "physically fittest" example from §IIC pushes the same point. In a *purely biological* environment (cell 1), the physically fittest person has the best survival odds. In a *world* — an artificiality field with cell-3 or cell-4 organizing principles — the physically fit person may have a *higher* mortality rate (designated to hunting, war, heavy labor) while a physically unfit person uses charisma or wit to concentrate power and resources. The fitness function operative is different: cell 1 selects for embodied resilience; cells 3 and 4 select for capacities aligned with the artificiality's organizing principles (rhetoric, social-network position, algorithmic-platform legibility).

The irreducibility is *structural*, not merely epistemological. Universe-laws (cell 1) constrain cells 2, 3, and 4 only at the substrate-implementation boundary (servers need power; bodies need food; institutions need humans) — not at the semantic-operation level (algorithms decide; institutions confer status; narratives organize meaning — none of these are derivable from universe-laws even though they are universe-laws-implemented).

This irreducibility is what the squeeze ontology's §1.3 names as the "two real-level background environments" configuration: universe (cell 1's laws + cell 1's manifestations) and metaverse (cell 4's laws + cell-4-mediated manifestations) each have their own real-level generative structures, neither reducible to the other. Cells 2 and 3 are *world-internal* organizing principles (still part of the *world* per the author's nested structure: `universe [world [material artificiality + virtual artificiality]]`); cell 4 is what makes the *metaverse* operationally separable from the world as a parallel background environment.

## §IIC.7 — Integration with the squeeze ontology

### Cells, Level 1, and Level 2

The typology classifies *artificialities* (by substrate and register). The squeeze ontology's two-level squeeze (`20Wiki/squeeze-ontology.md` §1.3) classifies *subjects of the squeeze* (organisms at Level 1; worlds-as-systems-of-means at Level 2). The two classifications are orthogonal and combine cleanly:

- A Level-1 organism (human or other creature) navigates artificialities from multiple cells: cell-1 universe-laws (gravity, climate, biology — borderline cases on the artificiality boundary), cell-2 material-artificiality with use-norms (the tools of one's daily life), cell-3 conventional virtuality (the social structures, narratives, religious or paradigmatic frames one inhabits), cell-4 metaverse-stage structural virtuality (algorithmic recommendations, platform-mediated access, ML decisions encountered).

- A Level-2 world-as-OS is constituted by a configuration of means drawn from cells 1–3 (pre-metaverse) and increasingly from cell 4 (metaverse-stage), with the cell-4 means progressively gaining apparatus-side independence from the world that produced them (the 自己完結化 ↔ 自足完成化 bidirectional coupling per §V §1.2; the Level-2 squeeze names the world's diminishment as its means migrate from internal-to-the-world cells 2 and 3 into background-environment cell 4).

### Aspect A and cell 4

## §IIC.10 — Handoff to §IID

The typology establishes *what kinds of artificiality there are* — substrate × register, four cells, one cell newly populated at scale. The handoff to §IID is the *diagnostic-stakes* question that the typology motivates but does not itself answer: *given that cell 4 has been newly populated at scale, what follows for the artificiality field as a whole?* §IID provides the answer through four canonical diagnoses (pharmakon, Illich's two watersheds, Dialectic of Enlightenment, 君子不器), each treating cell 4's novelty as the limit case of a tendency-toward-inversion that pre-metaverse artificiality had already exhibited at smaller scale, slower rate, or in different cells. The convergence becomes the analytical warrant for §V's framing of the metaverse-stage as the limit case rather than as one more instance of artificiality-at-scale.
