#### ¶4a — Steel-manned Chalmers (NEW, replaces line 200)

**Load-bearing claims**:
- Chalmers (2022) defines virtual reality as "an immersive, interactive, and computer-generated space"
- His "virtual digitalism" holds that "a virtual object is a structure of bits inside a computer"

#### ¶4b — Locate the disagreement at computational form (NEW)

**Function**: Identify the *actual* disagreement now that Chalmers is steel-manned.

**Load-bearing claim**: The thesis's disagreement with Chalmers is **not** about substrate (we agree with Chalmers that computation is substrate-neutral — DNA computing, quantum computing, beer cans, etc., all count). The disagreement is about whether **computational form itself** is necessary to virtuality.

**Argumentative move**:
- Chalmers requires virtuality to be implemented by a system satisfying his causal structuralism — i.e., a system whose causal structure mirrors a formal computational structure
- This excludes a vast class of causally-efficacious nonmaterial structures that have been recognised since Durkheim as "real but not material" — institutional facts, ritual structures, collective representations, customary norms, kinship, sovereignty
- These are not "computational" on any sensible reading of "computation" (and if Chalmers extends "computation" to absorb them, his concept becomes vacuous)
- The thesis claims virtuality is the **broader genus** of which Chalmersian computational virtuality is one species

**Note on Chalmers's dream concession**: Chalmers himself notes "a dream world is a sort of virtual world without a computer" but downgrades it as "limited" (meeting only "two of the five criteria of the reality checklist"). This concession is suggestive evidence that Chalmers feels the pull of cases that don't fit cleanly into causal structuralism. The thesis takes the door he leaves cracked open and pushes through it: the broader genus is not a degraded version of computational virtuality but its precondition.

#### ¶5c — Future trajectory: embedded computational virtuality (HEAVY REVISION — replaces "biologically programmed and powered" sentence)

**Function**: Anticipate the future without committing the substrate/computational-form strawman.

**DELETE the existing sentence**:
> ~~"Some day in the future, what we now call virtual, augmented, or mixed reality might no longer be computer-based but biologically programmed and powered. By then, the biologically programmed and powered applications that a person interacts with directly in their/eyes will cease to be virtual if we follow Chalmer's definition, but still relevant if we follow mine."~~

**Why delete**: per 2026-05-15 audit and `feedback_t0_before_attack.md`, this sentence assumes "biologically programmed" = non-computer-based, which fails on Chalmers's substrate-neutral view. Biological programming is *exactly* the kind of non-silicon computational substrate Chalmers welcomes. The intended gotcha doesn't land; it commits the strawman the thesis must avoid.

**REPLACE WITH** (load-bearing claims):
- The metaverse-stage trajectory is not the emergence of non-computer-based virtuality, but the **embedding of computer-based virtuality into the body and the everyday environment**
- This embedding makes computational virtuality phenomenologically transparent: BCIs (Neuralink and successors), AR contact lenses, ambient AI, always-on agents — the user no longer experiences "using a device" because the interface dissolves into the body schema (Stiegler's tertiary retention thickens; Merleau-Ponty's body schema absorbs the prosthesis)
- This is *more* squeeze, not less — the prior virtualities (ritual, place-based meaning, embodied skill, institutional trust) are increasingly mediated by an embedded computational substrate that withdraws from explicit notice
- The defensive task is therefore **not** to find non-computer-based virtuality at the technological frontier (the frontier is pervasively computational), but to defend the *non-metaverse virtualities* (money-as-institutional-fact, law-as-tradition, ritual, narrative, kinship) and the *non-virtual material zones of life* (body, food, rest, environment, manual labor, sleep) that the embedded computational virtuality threatens to displace or capture

**Citations to add**:
- **Stiegler** (already in §IIB.A as `stieglerTechnicsTime1998`): tertiary retention as the framework for embedded mnemotechnics; the body schema absorbing the prosthesis
- Optional: Andy Clark *Natural-Born Cyborgs* (2003) for the cognitive-extension framing; Don Ihde for postphenomenology of embodiment relations. Defer if not in Zotero.

**Suggested replacement prose** (to be polished in v3):
> "Some commentators imagine that the future of virtuality will move beyond computational substrates altogether — for example, that biologically programmed and powered interfaces will mark the end of the computer-based. This expectation rests on a category mistake. As Chalmers himself recognises, computation is substrate-neutral: biological, quantum, neuromorphic, and not-yet-imagined substrates all qualify as 'computer-based' provided they implement the requisite causal-computational structure. The actual trajectory is not the supersession of computational virtuality but its **embedding**: brain-computer interfaces, ambient intelligence, augmented sensory prostheses, always-on agents — these dissolve the interface into the body schema and the everyday environment, making computational virtuality phenomenologically transparent rather than absent (Stiegler 1998 on tertiary retention; on the body schema's absorption of the prosthesis, see Merleau-Ponty 1945). The squeeze the thesis diagnoses is therefore not a squeeze between virtual and non-virtual, but between an emerging embedded computational virtuality and the older virtualities — ritual, institutional fact, customary norm, narrative — that it is increasingly displacing or absorbing."

**Function**: Position the §IIB.B definition within the thesis's CR-stratified framing (per `thesis_squeeze_ontology_lock_2026_05_12.md`).

**Load-bearing claims**:
