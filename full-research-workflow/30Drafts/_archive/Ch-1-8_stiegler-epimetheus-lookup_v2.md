## Methodological framing — comparative cosmogony as evidence

Mircea Eliade's *The Myth of the Eternal Return* (1954) and David Adams Leeming's *Creation Myths of the World: An Encyclopedia* (2010, 2 vols.) supply the comparative-religion frame that justifies citing multiple traditions side by side without reducing them to a single template. Both works argue that cosmogonic narrative is a near-universal feature of human cultures and that *world-as-construction* is a recurrent — though not invariant — motif. Leeming distinguishes five basic types of cosmogonic story (creation from nothing, from chaos, from a cosmic egg, from earth-divers, from a primordial parent's body); the world-as-construction motif appears across all five with culture-specific inflections.

This framing avoids the methodological objection that the thesis cherry-picks Western, Chinese, and Judeo-Christian sources to fit a predetermined argument. The pattern *across* traditions is what the §I passage cites; specific differences matter and are flagged in footnotes.

## B. Judeo-Christian: Genesis, Eden, the Flood, Babel — sharpened from v1

The Judeo-Christian Bible contains *several distinct* world-making and world-repair narratives, not just the Eden expulsion. v1 elided this. The §I passage should distinguish:

### B1. Genesis 1 — *creatio ex chaos* (priestly source, ~6th c. BCE)

The Hebrew text of Genesis 1:1–2 does not describe creation *ex nihilo* but creation out of *tōhu wā-bōhu* ("formless void") and *təhōm* ("the deep") — a primordial chaos cognate with the Mesopotamian *Tiamat*. God's creative act is *separation*: light from darkness, waters above from waters below, dry land from sea. The world, in this account, is what emerges when ordering operations are imposed on prior chaos.

> "And the earth was without form, and void; and darkness was upon the face of the deep [təhōm]. And the Spirit of God moved upon the face of the waters." (Gen. 1:2, KJV)

**Relevance:** This is *world as ordered construction against chaos* — the same structure as the Babylonian Enuma Elish (§C below), with which Genesis 1 has documented intertextual relations (cf. Dalley 2000).

### B2. Genesis 3 — Eden expulsion (Yahwist source)

> "Cursed is the ground for thy sake; in sorrow shalt thou eat of it all the days of thy life... In the sweat of thy face shalt thou eat bread, till thou return unto the ground." (Gen. 3:17–19)

**Relevance:** The expulsion is the loss of a niche of pre-given sufficiency. After the expulsion, humans must labor to extract subsistence from a ground that no longer freely provides — the existential condition that mythologically grounds world-making as compensatory work. This is the version v1 cited.

### B3. Genesis 6–9 — the Flood

> "And, behold, I, even I, do bring a flood of waters upon the earth, to destroy all flesh." (Gen. 6:17)

**Relevance:** The flood story is a *world-repair* narrative analogous to Yu's flood-taming in Chinese mythology (§D below): an excess of natural process threatens livable order; human action (Noah's ark) preserves the conditions of inhabitation. Documented intertextual relation to Mesopotamian flood narratives (Atrahasis, Gilgamesh tablet XI).

### B4. Genesis 11 — Babel

> "Go to, let us build us a city and a tower, whose top may reach unto heaven... let us go down, and there confound their language, that they may not understand one another's speech." (Gen. 11:4, 7)

**Relevance:** Babel is the *limit* of world-construction — a self-completing tendency of artificiality (cf. thesis §V) checked by divine intervention. The myth registers awareness that world-making has thresholds beyond which it overreaches. Could be footnoted to anticipate §V.

**Source for translation/scholarship:** Robert Alter, *The Five Books of Moses* (Norton, 2004) is the standard contemporary scholarly translation; if the author wants a single citation, this is it. Alternatively, the *Jewish Study Bible* (Oxford, 2014). Neither is currently in Zotero — flagged as optional acquisition.

## C. Mesopotamian: Enuma Elish (Tiamat → Marduk → world from her body)

The Babylonian creation epic *Enuma Elish* (~12th c. BCE in surviving form, with older oral roots) is the foundational text. The plot:

1. Apsu (fresh water) and Tiamat (salt water / chaos-sea) commingle; younger gods are born in their mingled waters.
2. The young gods' noise disturbs Apsu; he plans to destroy them; Ea kills him first.
3. Tiamat raises monstrous offspring to avenge Apsu.
4. Marduk, son of Ea, is granted supreme authority by the assembly of gods on condition he defeat Tiamat.
5. Marduk slays Tiamat and *splits her body to make the world*: half becomes the heavens, half becomes the earth; her eyes become the Tigris and Euphrates; her tail becomes the Milky Way.
6. Marduk creates humans from the blood of the rebel god Kingu, "to bear the burden of the gods" — i.e., humans exist to perform the labor that maintains cosmic order.

**Standard scholarly translation:** Stephanie Dalley, *Myths from Mesopotamia: Creation, the Flood, Gilgamesh, and Others* (Oxford, rev. 2000) [Zotero: VCZTTCPG]. Dalley's translation supersedes the older Heidel and Speiser versions for current scholarship.

**Relevance for §I:** Three points the Mesopotamian myth contributes that Eden does not:
- *World-substance from divine body*: the world is literally constructed from Tiamat's dismembered chaos-flesh — the substrate is given but not yet world; world-making is the imposition of order on substrate.
- *Humans as world-maintenance*: humans are explicitly created to perform *the work that holds the cosmos together*. Maintenance is constitutive, not optional.
- *World as agonistic order*: world is what wins against chaos. This contrasts with the Greek kosmos as static elegance (cf. Brague Ch-1-15) and complements the dynamic-construction motif.

> "He split her in two, like a fish for drying. / Half of her he set up and made as a cover, heaven. / He stretched the hide and assigned watchmen, / And ordered them not to let her waters escape." (*Enuma Elish*, Tablet IV, lines 137–140; Dalley 2000, p. 255 area; verify exact page in print edition)

## D. Sinic: 女媧補天, 后羿射日, 大禹治水 — sharpened from v1

v1 listed the three Chinese myths but did not specify their primary textual sources. The §I passage should cite:

### D1. 女媧補天 (Nüwa mends the sky)

Primary source: *淮南子·覽冥訓* (*Huainanzi*, "Surveying Obscurities," 2nd c. BCE):

> "往古之時，四極廢，九州裂，天不兼覆，地不周載，火爁炎而不滅，水浩洋而不息，猛獸食顓民，鷙鳥攫老弱。於是女媧鍊五色石以補蒼天，斷鼇足以立四極，殺黑龍以濟冀州，積蘆灰以止淫水。"

(In antiquity, the four pillars were broken, the nine provinces split, heaven did not cover all, earth did not bear all, fire raged unquenched, water swelled without abating, fierce beasts devoured the people, raptors seized the weak. Then Nüwa smelted five-colored stones to mend the azure sky, cut off the legs of the great turtle to set up the four pillars, killed the black dragon to save Jizhou, and piled reed-ash to stop the wild waters.)

**Relevance:** The sky/cosmos *was already broken* and humans (or quasi-divine human-progenitors) had to *repair* it. World as patch-job, not as prior gift.

### D2. 后羿射日 (Hou Yi shoots down the suns)

Primary source: *淮南子·本經訓* (Huainanzi, "Fundamental Norms"); also *山海經* (Classic of Mountains and Seas):

> "逮至堯之時，十日並出，焦禾稼，殺草木，而民無所食... 堯乃使羿... 上射十日。"

(In the time of Yao, ten suns rose together, scorching crops, killing plants, leaving people no food... Yao sent Yi... to shoot down the suns.)

**Relevance:** The natural order is *excessive*, hostile to inhabitation. World-making requires reducing/regulating natural excess to make space for human life. The bow as primordial technology.

### D3. 大禹治水 (Yu tames the flood)

Primary source: *尚書·禹貢* (Book of Documents, "Tribute of Yu") and *史記·夏本紀* (Records of the Grand Historian):

> "禹乃遂與益、后稷奉帝命，命諸侯百姓興人徒以傅土，行山表木，定高山大川。"

(Yu, following the emperor's command with Yi and Hou Ji, commanded the lords and people to mobilize laborers to spread earth, traverse mountains and mark trees, fix the high mountains and great rivers.)

**Relevance:** This is *world-construction as hydraulic engineering*. The *Yu gong* tract reads as a literal cosmography: Yu does not "create" the world — he organizes a livable order out of an uninhabitable substrate of floods and unmarked terrain. Cross-reference with Polanyi (Ch-1-4) on boundary conditions.

**Scholarly note:** The Chinese flood-taming tradition diverges from Mesopotamian/Hebrew flood traditions in a structurally important way: in Genesis and Atrahasis, divine wrath sends the flood and humans survive; in 大禹治水, the flood is a feature of an unfinished world and humans (Yu) finish the world by *engineering* it. This contrast is footnote-worthy and supports the thesis's emphasis on world-making as constructive labor rather than survival of divine punishment.

## E. Vedic / Hindu: Purusha Sukta, Hiranyagarbha, Manu's flood

### E1. Purusha Sukta (Rig Veda 10.90)

The "Hymn of the Cosmic Person" — the universe is created from the dismemberment of the primal Purusha through ritual sacrifice:

> "When they divided Purusha, into how many parts did they apportion him? What was his mouth? What were his arms? What were his thighs and his feet called? / The Brahmin was his mouth; of his arms was made the warrior. His thighs became the Vaishya, of his feet the Shudra was born." (Rig Veda 10.90.11–12)

**Relevance:** The world (including the social order) is *built from the body of a primordial being via sacrifice*. Structural parallel to Norse Ymir (§F below). The *world is a sacrificial construction* — order from primordial dismemberment.

### E2. Hiranyagarbha (Rig Veda 10.121)

The "Golden Embryo" — primordial cosmic egg from which the universe hatches.

**Relevance:** Cosmic-egg motif (cf. Leeming 2010, Type 3 cosmogony). The egg-as-substrate must be *broken* for world to emerge.

### E3. Nasadiya Sukta (Rig Veda 10.129)

Often called "the most agnostic hymn of the Rig Veda":

> "Then was not non-existent nor existent... whence all creation had its origin... Whether He fashioned it or whether He did not... only He knows — or perhaps He does not know."

**Relevance:** Vedic tradition contains *self-aware skepticism* about the construction story — a counterpoint to assuming all cosmogonies are dogmatic. Optional but powerful for the §I argument that world-making is a question to be addressed, not a settled assumption.

### E4. Manu and Matsya (the Hindu flood myth)

Source: *Shatapatha Brahmana* 1.8.1; later in *Matsya Purana* and *Mahabharata*. Vishnu in fish-avatar warns Manu of an impending flood; Manu builds a boat; humanity is preserved through Manu, who is then the progenitor of the surviving human race.

**Relevance:** Independent flood-survival tradition; world-repair through preservation of a remnant. Comparable structure to Mesopotamian Atrahasis and Hebrew Noah but with a distinctive *piscine* mediator.

**Scholarly source:** Wendy Doniger, *Hindu Myths: A Sourcebook Translated from the Sanskrit* (Penguin, 1976) [Zotero: MG6ZUN8T]. Doniger is the standard English-language source.

## F. Norse: Ginnungagap, Ymir, the world from his body

Source: *Prose Edda* (Snorri Sturluson, ~1220 CE) and *Poetic Edda* (compiled 13th c. from earlier oral material). Already in Zotero (Snorri Edda).

The Norse cosmogony:
1. Before creation: Ginnungagap (the yawning void) between Niflheim (ice) and Muspelheim (fire).
2. Where ice and fire meet: drops form Ymir, the primordial frost-giant.
3. The gods (Odin, Vili, Vé — sons of Bor) kill Ymir.
4. *They make the world from his body*: blood → seas, flesh → earth, hair → trees, bones → mountains, skull → sky, brains → clouds.
5. Humans (Ask and Embla) are then made from two pieces of driftwood encountered on a beach.

**Relevance for §I:** Two distinctive contributions:
- *World-from-divine-body*: structural parallel to Vedic Purusha and Mesopotamian Tiamat. The world is *constructed substance*, not pre-existent.
- *Cyclic destructibility (Ragnarök)*: the Norse cosmos is mortal in a way the Greek kosmos largely is not. World-making is also world-decline. Anticipates the empirical record §B of the v3 Brague-corrected Ch-1-11 mobilizes (worlds rise and fall).

## G. Mesoamerican: Popol Vuh — multiple failed creations

Source: *Popol Vuh* (K'iche' Maya, compiled c. 1554–1558 from earlier oral tradition). Standard English: Dennis Tedlock, *Popol Vuh: The Definitive Edition* (1996) [Zotero: QBSGR2PJ].

The K'iche' creation narrative:

1. The gods (Heart of Sky, Plumed Serpent, et al.) create earth and animals.
2. Animals cannot praise the gods — first failure.
3. The gods make humans of *mud*; the mud humans dissolve — second failure.
4. The gods make humans of *wood*; the wooden humans have no minds, do not remember the gods, are destroyed by a flood — third failure.
5. The gods finally make humans of *maize*; these humans see and speak well — fourth attempt succeeds, but the gods then *fog their vision* so that humans cannot see too clearly.

**Relevance for §I:** This is the most explicit *world-as-iterative-construction* myth in the comparative record. The world is built, fails, rebuilt, fails again, built again — and even the final "successful" version is a partial achievement, deliberately limited. Three contributions:

- *Construction is iterative and includes failure as constitutive*: a sharper version of the world-making motif than any single-shot creation.
- *Multiple worlds in succession*: each failed creation was a world, now extinct. Anticipates the rise-and-fall empirical record (cf. Ch-1-11 v3 §B).
- *Limits on world-completion*: the gods' fogging of human sight is structurally analogous to the Babel motif (§B4) — world-making is bounded.

## H. Egyptian: creation from Nun and ma'at as world-order

Source: scattered references across the *Pyramid Texts*, *Coffin Texts*, and *Memphite Theology*. No single canonical creation narrative in the way Genesis or Enuma Elish is canonical.

Key elements:
- *Nun*: primordial waters, the chaos-substrate.
- *Atum* (or Ptah, Re — the active creator god varies by tradition): emerges from Nun and creates the world by speech, by sneezing/ejaculating other gods, or by thinking-and-speaking.
- *Ma'at*: the principle of cosmic-and-social order. The pharaoh's duty is *to maintain ma'at* against the perpetual encroachment of *isfet* (chaos/disorder). World is what is maintained by ongoing ritual and political labor.

**Relevance for §I:** Brague (2003, p. 14–15) discusses ma'at extensively. Egyptian cosmology presents *the most explicit version of world-as-ongoing-maintenance*: the world does not stay made; the king and the priesthood must ritually re-make it daily. This is the mythological precursor to the §I argument that the world is not simply built and then permanent but must be continuously re-instituted.

## I. Polynesian and Indigenous Australian (briefly noted)

**Polynesian (Maori) Te Pō → Te Aō:** the cosmos emerges through the separation of Rangi (sky-father) and Papa (earth-mother) by their children. Structural parallel to Egyptian Geb-Nut and Greek Ouranos-Gaia. Source: George Grey, *Polynesian Mythology* (1855); standard scholarly: Margaret Orbell, *The Illustrated Encyclopedia of Maori Myth and Legend* (1995). Neither in Zotero.

**Australian Dreamtime:** Ancestral beings travel across an undifferentiated landscape and *sing it into being* — landforms, watercourses, species, social rules all come into existence through the ancestors' acts. The world *is* the path of the singing. Source for English-language reader: W. E. H. Stanner, *White Man Got No Dreaming* (1979); also Bruce Chatwin, *The Songlines* (1987), though Chatwin is impressionistic. Not in Zotero.

**Relevance:** Both add weight to the cross-cultural pattern but are not necessary for the §I passage if space is constrained. They could appear in a footnote.

## Recommended §I prose passage (draft)

> Across cultures, founding mythologies converge on a structural insight: the world does not arrive ready-made. In the Hebrew Bible, creation in Genesis 1 is the imposition of *separation* on a primordial *tōhu wā-bōhu* — formless void; the expulsion in Genesis 3 stages the loss of an Edenic niche of pre-given sufficiency, after which humans must labor to extract subsistence from a now-resistant ground (Gen. 3:17–19). The Babylonian *Enuma Elish* recounts world-construction in a sharper agonistic register: the world-substance is made from the dismembered body of the chaos-goddess Tiamat, and humans are created precisely to perform "the burden of the gods" — the work that maintains cosmic order (Dalley 2000). Chinese cosmogonic tradition foregrounds repair and engineering rather than ex nihilo origin: Nüwa mends a broken sky with smelted stones (女媧補天), Hou Yi shoots down nine of ten suns to make the surface inhabitable (后羿射日), and Yu fixes a flooded substrate by moving earth and channeling rivers (大禹治水) — world-making as hydraulic engineering. The Vedic Purusha Sukta and the Norse cosmogony of Ymir converge structurally though they share no historical contact: in both, the world is constructed from the dismembered body of a primordial being, social order included (Rig Veda 10.90; Snorri Sturluson's *Edda*). The K'iche' Maya *Popol Vuh* sharpens the construction motif into iteration with failure: the gods make humans of mud, then wood, then maize, with prior creations destroyed because they were inadequate (Tedlock 1996). Egyptian cosmology emphasizes that what is constructed must be *maintained*: the pharaoh's first duty is to uphold *ma'at* against the perpetual encroachment of *isfet* (Brague 2003; Assmann 1990).
>
> The Greek myth of Prometheus and Epimetheus encodes the same structure with the sharpest philosophical articulation. In Plato's *Protagoras*, Epimetheus distributes specific powers to all creatures — speed, claws, wings — but forgets the human, leaving it "naked, unshod, unbedded, and unarmed"; Prometheus then steals fire and *technē* from the gods to compensate. Bernard Stiegler's reading is precise: the "fault of Epimetheus" is not a contingent mishap but the *originary structure* of the human — "humans are the forgotten ones; they only occur through their being forgotten" (Stiegler 1998, p. 188). There is no pre-technical human who is then equipped; the default — *le défaut d'origine* — is constitutive. Across this comparative record, the recurrent pattern is the same: the world is what humans must build, repair, or maintain because the universe presents no pre-built human niche. Eliade and Leeming (2010) have shown that this motif appears, with cultural inflections, across the global archaic record — a corroboration that the pattern is not parochial.
