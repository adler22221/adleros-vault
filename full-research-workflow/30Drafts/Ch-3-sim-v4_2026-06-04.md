# Chapter III — The Universalization of the Enlightenment Mode

> **Sim v4.** Clean prose-forward draft on the locked flat 7-section structure. Verbatim grounding and citekey-resolution status are recorded in **Scaffold-notes** at the foot of the file; in-text superscripts key to the footnotes. `status: ai-draft` — not for citation as a source; for the author to read, revise, and promote.

Artificiality, the preceding chapters have argued, is not a thing humans possess but the mode in which humans exist: we live in *worlds* — made meaning-spaces — nested within, and built upon, the *universe* that is given independently of us. This chapter concerns a claim about one such world. I argue that a single mode of artificiality, which I will call **the Enlightenment mode**, has *universalized*: it has come to be projected as though it were the universe itself — the given measure of all worlds — displacing the plurality of worlds and, in the limit, threatening the conditions under which any world can be remade at all.

To establish this I proceed in seven steps. §1 recovers the plurality of worlds and shows that a world can be *squeezed* against its universe — a recurring figure we have already met at smaller scales. §2 reads collapse as the extreme case of that squeeze, and isolates the *regeneration base* that has, until now, always survived it. §3 defends the name "Enlightenment mode" against "Western" and "modern." §4 and §5 give, respectively, the *material* and the *nonmaterial* conditions of the mode's rise and universalization. §6 rebuts the reading that universalization is evidence of fitness. §7 turns toward Chapter IV.

## §1 — The plurality of worlds and the universe↔world squeeze

Begin not with a theory of worlds but with a fact about them: *personhood* is composed incommensurably across worlds. As Chapter I argued, who or what counts as a person — an ancestor, a mountain, a kami, a lineage, a corporation, a foetus, an AI — differs between worlds not by degree but in kind, so that the question "but who is *really* a person?" has no world-neutral answer.[^1] Personhood is not read off the universe; it is *made*, and made differently. The incommensurability is the evidence; the plurality of worlds is the inference.

That worlds are made and many is Goodman's thesis, and I take it up — with one correction. Worldmaking, Goodman writes, "always starts from worlds already on hand; the making is a remaking"; there is no single "ready-made world" waiting to be described.[^2] [ADLER: the quote here only says that a world is (re)made, but doesn't say it's plural. Goodman's plurality of worlds must be further elaborated.] I keep the *plurality* and decline the *irrealism*. Worlds are indeed made and many, but they are made *upon* a real, structured universe — not spun out of nothing, and not a sign that "there is no world." This is the recurring signature of the thesis: **plurality without irrealism**, a realism about the universe that nonetheless refuses to collapse the many worlds into one.[^3] 

Descola's four ontologies — animism, totemism, analogism, and *naturalism* — give one vivid illustration of the plurality, with the modern Western world figuring as just one mode [ADLER: description too thin. each ontologies should be briefly unpacked. these are Descola's categorization (not the only one) of the ontologies upon which the different modes of artificiality constituting different worlds as AOSs are built], "naturalism," which posits "a single unifying nature and a multiplicity of cultures."[^4] I use Descola illustratively and decline to treat his four as *the* exhaustive scheme: to fix the plurality into one master typology would be to re-totalize it, the very move this chapter is against. The plurality exceeds any catalogue of it.

Worlds, then, are made; and what is made against a given can come into *strain* with that given. We have already met this strain. At the biospheric scale [ADLER: do not use this label], the Great Oxidation Event was a squeeze: life remade its own atmospheric substrate and, in doing so, extinguished much of the life that depended on the old one (Chapter I). At the evolutionary scale [ADLER: do not use this label], the hominin lineage was squeezed into artificiality itself by an under-specialized body in a shifting habitat (Chapter II). [ADLER: these squeezes were diachronic and with both sides at the actual level] The squeeze is thus not a single event but **a recurring figure that returns at escalating scales**, and this chapter takes it to the next scale up — the *civilizational* [ADLER: do not use this label. the point is that the squeeze expands from simply diachronically actual, to being inclusive of synchronic fracture between not only the actual of the universe and world, but also between the actual of the universe and the real of the world]. In comparative history, Morris describes the same dynamic in his more general register — "as social development rises, the resources it demands change," and societies "push through" bottlenecks by adopting successful innovations — and I borrow that description à la carte, without his picture of history as a race between homogeneous regional teams.[^7]

From an ecological view, Obara's ... "lag? *ズレ*"[^6] . Uegaki, who builds upon Obara and is central to the next following chapters, elaborates upon Obara's ズレ as *fracture* (亀裂 / 切断)[^5] ... [ADLER: your engagement with Obara and Uegaki here is superficial. there words are loadbearing for this type of squeeze so must be expanded/elaborated upon] In the terms of this thesis, a world emerges as a response to its local universe, to the real and actual conditions [ADLER: you forgot to de-jargon CR and use my terminology] it finds; but as that world's own demands grow and change, the conditions it now requires may outstrip what its background universe can supply. The fit that founded the world becomes a misfit. This is what I mean by the **universe↔world squeeze**.

But here is the decisive qualification, and it governs the whole chapter: a world *can* be squeezed; it is not *fated* to be. The squeeze is a risk a world runs, not a law it obeys. And it is precisely because the risk is real that so many traditions have made the *avoidance* of it their highest wisdom. The Confucius injunction to *harmony between heaven and humanity* (天人合一), the Daoist practice of *wu wei* — "refraining from activity contrary to Nature," "production without possession, action without self-assertion, development without domination"[^8] — indigenous protocols of reciprocity and restraint: these are not romantic ornaments but **squeeze-prevention**, the deliberate keeping of a world's real & actual aligned with that of the universe. [ADLER: de-jargon the real & actual here] A world that cultivates harmony is a world that has organized itself *not* to outstrip its given. It follows that a mode which abandons harmony for intervention — which takes the relation to the universe to be one of mastery rather than fit — is a mode that does not merely risk the squeeze but *courts* it. That mode is the subject of the rest of this chapter.

## §2 — Collapse and the regeneration base

Collapse is the universe↔world squeeze in its extreme case: the point at which a world can no longer satisfy the conditions it has come to require, and the order it sustained gives way. I want to read collapse carefully, because the reading matters for §6: collapse is **mismatch, not unfitness** — a morphing universe and an over-reaching world, meeting, rather than a verdict that the fallen world "deserved" to fall.

The fall of Ming China is the case, and Timothy Brook has treated it twice over. *The Price of Collapse* makes the squeeze **quantitative**: the index of a world failing against its universe is the price of grain. As the Little Ice Age deepened — plunging, late in the 1630s, toward its coldest phase, the Maunder Minimum — harvests failed and famine grain prices, "the longest series of prices in China before the eighteenth century," climbed past what people could pay; contemporaries reached for "the price of rice to quantify the disaster and track its course."[^9] Brook's causal claim is pointed — "global climate, not global trade, drove Ming grain prices to crippling levels" — and the grain price is, he writes, "the core phenomenon around which I have written this book: the prices to which grain rose in times of dearth."[^10] The mechanism is the squeeze exactly: a universe-side morph (the cold) met a world that had organized its subsistence around a warmer mean and a silver-monetized market, until the *price regime* and the *political regime* fell together — "as he was witnessing the collapse of the Ming price regime, he was also witnessing the collapse of the Ming political regime"; Beijing fell to rebels in April 1644.[^11] Two things matter for what follows. The Ming was not a world without squeeze-prevention: it "maintained a system of grain storage explicitly to adjust the price of grain in times of dearth" — an ever-normal-granary apparatus built precisely to keep a world within what its universe could give (cf. §1) — yet a downturn of sufficient depth overwhelmed it. And Brook is careful in the way §6 will need: the dynasty "was not singlehandedly destroyed by climate, but its collapse cannot be explained in the absence of climate and human responses to climate."[^12] *The Troubled Empire* adds the longer rhythm — the "sloughs," nine extended disasters across the Yuan and Ming, the Chongzhen Slough at the end the worst on record, no single cause sufficient: "fiscal insolvency? rebellions? Manchu military might? the weather? … their combination was what brought down the house of the Ming."[^13] Not unfitness; mismatch — a morphing universe meeting a world that could no longer meet its own demands. [ADLER: summarize the case with the universe-actual vis a vis world-actual&real squeeze structure]

Two further things follow from the Ming case. First, what came after was *a different world*, not the absence of one: the population "came out the other side of the Chongzhen Slough to find themselves the subjects of a new dynasty," submitting to Manchu authority and marking that submission in their very bodies (the imposed hairstyle); the Qing was a substantially different adaptational order — a different AOS — ... [ADLER: e.g., a new system that integrates 蒙古, 吐蕃, 新疆 with 華夏, or nomads with agrarians, in unprecedented operational logic; imposed outfit and hairstyle as the wiping out & reinstallation of identity(?); new systems of 文字獄 as control protocols for OS version integrity(?)] [^13] Collapse, that is, is itself *evidence of the plurality of worlds*: worlds succeed worlds. Second, and most importantly for what follows, the collapse was ... [ADLER: we should perhaps recontextualize with the stratified ontology and typology of artificiality here] and it left a floor. [ADLER: if you do not split this paragraph, empirical accounts of the Ming collapse relevant to Tainter & Schwartz&Nichols should be added] Tainter teaches that collapse is the loss of complexity when the marginal returns on complexity turn negative — "an economizing process," not a moral failure;[^14] and Schwartz and Nichols show that what survives the loss is a **regeneration base**: "collapse is almost never total," the rural and vernacular substrate persists, and the societies that regrow "did not have to reinvent the wheel."[^15] The vernacular floor is the thing from which new worlds are remade.

This sets up the hinge of the whole chapter. Every collapse considered so far has been the collapse of *one world among many*, and each has left a regeneration base from which other worlds could grow. The danger the Enlightenment mode poses is that it **breaks this pattern**. By universalizing — by projecting itself as the universe and drawing all worlds into its single mode (§§3–6) — it makes the squeeze *planetary* and consumes the regeneration base itself. A monoculture-of-mode leaves no vernacular floor outside it to regrow from. That is why the terminal squeeze of the later chapters is categorically different from the Ming's: not "a collapse" among others, but the foreclosure of the very capacity to regenerate. Plurality, on this reading, is not a luxury; it is the regeneration base of the species.

## §3 — Why "the Enlightenment mode"

If something is universalizing, we must say *what*. I call it the Enlightenment mode, and the name is doing work that "Western" and "modern" cannot.

The mode has a specific lineage — a confluence of Greek-rational and Judeo-Christian inheritances, crystallized in the European Enlightenment, materially consolidated in the Great Divergence (§4), and now extended into the digital era — and a specific disposition [ADLER: do not fabricate. use authoritative, non-temporally-tied definitions to triangulate]: interventionist rather than harmony-seeking, certainty-seeking rather than fit-seeking, treating nature as standing reserve and the relation to the universe as one of mastery. It is, in Descola's terms, the ontology of "naturalism" — one of four, not the frame of frames.[^16]

Why not call it "Western"? Two reasons, the second decisive. First, "Western" essentializes a region and a people, smuggling in exactly the homogeneity the thesis denies. Second — and this is the argument that fixes the name — **the mode outlives Western regional dominance**. Suppose the coming century is East-dominant rather than West-dominant; this would not touch my thesis at all, *provided the dominant order still runs the interventionist, certainty-seeking, dominating mode*. A geographically Eastern future that operates the Enlightenment mode is the Enlightenment mode *universalized*, not refuted. This is precisely where my claim parts from accounts like Morris's: Morris narrates history as a race between regional teams and asks which *region* will lead; I am indifferent to the locale and ask which *mode of artificiality* rules. The two questions are orthogonal — an Eastern-dominant future is fully compatible with the metaphysics grounding that dominance being an extension of the Enlightenment mode. Hui makes the corresponding point on the side of technology: the threat is *monotechnology* and the homogenization of technical worlds — "epistemicide leads to homogeneity" — and the remedy he names, *cosmotechnics* / technodiversity, is a name for the plurality the universalized mode erases, on whichever continent it is run.[^17] "Western" points at a map; "the Enlightenment mode" points at the thing that travels off the map.

Why not "modern"? Because "modern" is an empty temporal index — the ever-receding now — that names *when* and not *what*, and so leaves the post-/post-, neo-/neo- regress unsolved: every "after the modern" is still dated rather than characterized. "Enlightenment mode" names the *what* — a lineage and a disposition — and so can survive the calendar.

## §4 — Material conditions of the mode's rise

The mode rose; the question is *why*, and the answer is not "because it was superior." It is, rather, that a particular set of contingent material conditions allowed an interventionist world to break a constraint that had bounded all worlds before it. Two bodies of work anchor this — Needham on the comparison, Pomeranz on the contingency.

The interventionist mode is *one* mode that universalized — not the sea every river was always seeking.

Pomeranz supplies the contingency [ADLER: too thin. should at least be 2-3 paragraphs to fully elaborate on the argumentation, evidence & nuances]. Europe, on his account, was "a none-too-unusual economy," and the Great Divergence was "late and contingent … not wholly produced within Europe."[^19] It was not capital or institutions that decided it: the Lower Yangzi delta was an equally developed core — it "may have produced as much cloth per capita in 1750 as Britons did in 1800," so that the real question reverses into "why didn't England become the Yangzi Delta?"[^20] The binding constraint everywhere was *land and energy*, and Europe escaped it through "two crucial discontinuities — one created by coal and one by colonies."[^21] English coal was "forest-saving coal in particularly fortunate places," near water transport in a fuel-starved society — an advantage resting "as much on geographic accident" as on ingenuity, "fortuitous, and specifically English."[^22] And the New World supplied *ghost acreage*: by Pomeranz's reckoning some 25–30 million "ghost acres," a land-equivalent "exceeding even the contribution of coal," in the form of sugar (each acre of which spared nine to twelve acres of European wheat-equivalent), cotton, timber, and silver — an "ecological windfall," an "ecological relief" that Europe's own soil could never have yielded.[^23] The rise of the mode was thus a *fortunate freak* compounded by a vast *externalization*: its costs were displaced onto colonized land and labour and onto the deep carbon past. 

Miura's contribution completes the picture as a *feedback loop* [ADLER: too thin. should at least be 1-2 paragraphs to fully elaborate on the argumentation, evidence & nuances] — once the windfalls fed the interventionist mode, the mode reorganized to demand still more, ratcheting the intervention upward.[^24]

The upshot, which §6 will need: the mode rose by accident and by cost-dumping, not by an inner virtue. Contingency at the origin is the first nail in the coffin of the fitness reading.

## §5 — Nonmaterial conditions of the mode's universalization

The windfalls of §4 explain how the Enlightenment mode *rose*; they do not explain how it came to be *universalized* — projected as the universe, installed as the measure to which every other world must answer. Rise is material; universalization is, at its root, a matter of *how the mode knows*. The nonmaterial driver is a particular structure of knowing, and I locate it — as the **spine** of this section, not as one option among equals — in Roy Bhaskar's diagnosis of the **primal squeeze**. The neighbouring critiques of Enlightenment reason each illuminate a real facet; Bhaskar is the spine because the thesis needs what only he supplies: a *realist*, *ontological*, *nature-relating* account of what the mode does to being.

**The mechanism.** Bhaskar diagnoses a founding fault in the Western theory of knowledge: the squeezing-out of a real, knowable *middle* between two jaws. The middle is *natural necessity* — the commonsense of the working scientist, that things have real underlying structures and powers (water boils because of what water is), grasped fallibly through theory tested by experiment, never with certainty but really. [ADLER: too weak. nobody nowadays seems to disagree with this, making this claim like a weak strawman] [^25] The first jaw is pure Reason: on the Platonic/rationalist side, thought alone is held to reach necessary truth, which renders empirical science redundant — "empirically controlled theory is rendered redundant by metaphysical a priorism and rationalism." The second jaw is pure Experience: on the Aristotelian-into-Humean side, all we are granted is sense-data and the regular conjunctions we notice, so that real necessity simply vanishes — "natural necessity disappears in the empiricist problem of induction."[^26] What lets the two jaws close on the middle is a shared, hidden premise: that the real is exhausted by what actually happens (*actualism*) and that to be is to be knowable (*the epistemic fallacy*). Grant that premise and, as Bhaskar says, "rationalism and empiricism … seem to exhaust the alternatives"; there is no room left for structures that are neither self-evident to reason nor visible to the senses, and the middle is crushed from both sides at once.[^27] Rationalism and empiricism, apparent enemies, turn out to be "necessary conditions of each other" — which is why the tradition swings endlessly between them rather than escaping the problem.

**The motive, and the cost.** What drives the squeeze is the *demand for certainty* — a "fundamentalism" Bhaskar reads as a fear of change and of error: "the quest for certain foundations is expressive of fear of change."[^28] But certainty can be had only from pure reason or pure given fact, and both require discarding the fallible real. The squeeze is the price of the craving for certainty. And its cost is exactly what universalization needs. Once the structured real is gone, the *human* becomes the measure: either the cosmos is read in the image of mind (*anthropomorphism*) or man is installed as the centre and the real is equated with the perceivable (*anthropocentrism*). Bhaskar's umbrella term is the **anthropic fallacy** — "the exegesis of being in terms of human being."[^29] Crucially, the certainty-seeking stance does not merely shrink the world; it *divides* it: the quest for an indubitable criterion "inevitably splits reality into two (viz. that which conforms to its criterion and that which does not)."[^30] That split is the intellectual engine of the mode's drive to totalize and to exclude — the logical form of universalization itself.

**My extension — and its warrant, contribution, and tensions.** I extend Bhaskar's transhistorical anthropic fallacy to name the Enlightenment mode as its *extreme, universalizing form* — what I call **enlightenment-morphism / enlightenment-centrism**: the projection of being only in the terms of the Enlightenment mode, which in this thesis's vocabulary *is* universalization — the projection of the made *world* as the given *universe*. The warrant is in Bhaskar himself: he treats "any regional geo-historical variant of anthropocentrism (e.g. Eurocentrism)" as an ethnocentrism, one that assumes "an extreme form … in post/modernity."[^31] Enlightenment-centrism is then a geo-historical variant — the extreme form — of the anthropic fallacy, and "projecting the made world as the given universe" is the epistemic fallacy *fused* with the anthropic fallacy and recast ontologically [ADLER: or, since Bhaskar's diagnosis is only applied to the problems of western philosophy, not to the other streams of non-Western philosophy, the "anthropic" here is essentially the "Enlightenment man" -- it would be absurd to say the platonic-aristotelian fault line is universal even to the maoris or amazonians. the anthropocentrism/morphism in this particular Bhaskarian diagnosis (not claiming that other cultures don't commit) is inherently one of the Enlightenment mode]. The *contribution* I claim is fourfold: I historicize Bhaskar's perennial fallacy into one *mode* and its *planetary* universalization (he gives the genus; I name the species); I coin the mode-specific term; I recast the error ontologically as world-projected-as-universe, tying it to the squeeze-ontology; and I yoke the nonmaterial driver to the material one (Bhaskar ⟷ Needham–Pomeranz), where Bhaskar's account is purely philosophical. Four *tensions* must be flagged honestly. (i) The anthropic fallacy is *transhistorical*, common to all irrealism — so my claim is that the Enlightenment mode *universalizes* it to a planetary extreme, not that it is the sole locus; non-Western thought can commit it too. (ii) Bhaskar projects *reason*, a faculty; I project a whole *mode*, a sociohistorical formation — a defensible extension from faculty-level to mode-level, since the mode embeds a particular reason. (iii) The realism must hold: "world projected as universe" means the mode *mistakes its made world for the given universe* (the epistemic fallacy), not that there is no real universe — the universe is real; the error is the projection. (iv) Epistemic plurality is *not* ontological relativism: "other ways of knowing" are different ways of knowing the same intransitive universe — not a flat relativism that would dissolve the thesis's realism along with the mode's.[^32]

**Why Bhaskar is the spine and the neighbours are facets.** [ADLER: too shallow/superficial. the argumentation, evidence & nuances of each should at least be 1 paragraph to avoid names dropping] Derrida names the *form* of the split — logocentrism, the metaphysics of presence and its privileged term — but dissolves the referent; he gives the binary's *shape* and not the positively-real thing excluded, and the thesis, being realist about the universe, needs the latter.[^33] Adorno and Horkheimer give the *pathos* and the self-undoing — enlightenment reverting to myth, identity-thinking liquidating the non-identical — and they alone keep the *nature-domination* in view; but negative dialectics is ontology-shy, diagnosing the drive without grounding what is flattened in a stratified real.[^34] Habermas gives the *social* form — system imperatives colonizing the lifeworld — but it is social rather than ontological or nature-relating, and he frames the encroachment as *resistible* through communicative reason, which is too local and too hopeful for a planetary, ontological squeeze.[^35] Bhaskar alone (a) names the positively-real excluded middle — a stratified, mind-independent world; (b) supplies a *genealogy and a mechanism* rather than a pathos or a periodization; (c) explains the totalizing drive *structurally*, as built into certainty-seeking epistemics; and (d) integrates epistemics, ontology, and the nature-relation in a single figure — matching the thesis's own triple structure (epistemic squeeze ↔ ontological squeeze ↔ artificiality's domination of nature). Hence: spine, not option.

That the diagnosis is not Bhaskar's idiosyncrasy is shown by independent witnesses who reach the same projection from other directions. Husserl, decades earlier, described the *mathematization of nature*: Galileo "substitutes the mathematically substructed world of idealities for the only real world, the world actually given … our everyday life-world," so that a "garb of ideas" (*Ideenkleid*) is taken *for* true being.[^36] That is precisely the projection of the made world as the given universe, named in 1936. Heidegger's *mathematical projection* and "age of the world picture" describe modern science projecting nature in advance as a calculable nexus of forces, and the world becoming a *picture* set before a representing subject — the same move in another idiom.[^37] And Dewey's *quest for certainty* — the flight from the precarious to the fixed, the spectator theory of knowledge — corroborates Bhaskar's *motive* from the pragmatist side.[^38] Four thinkers, four vocabularies, one structure.

Finally, the projection does not merely *err*; it *excludes* — and here the triangulation turns from epistemology to its political and technical edge. Mignolo names the *colonial matrix of power*: the "rhetoric of modernity" is inseparable from "the logic of coloniality," so that the universal is constructed by disqualifying other knowledges — modernity's darker side.[^39] Santos names the result *epistemicide*, governed by an *abyssal line* that renders the knowledges on its far side not false but *non-existent*, beyond the realm of the sayable.[^40] Hui names the technical endpoint — *monotechnology* and the homogenization of technical worlds, "epistemicide leads to homogeneity" — and the plurality it destroys, *technodiversity*.[^41] Mignolo → Santos → Hui give the colonial, the epistemic, and the technical faces of the single "split" the anthropic fallacy performs; and because each reaches "Eurocentrism" *without* Bhaskar, they independently corroborate the very extension §5 proposes.

**The link.** Bhaskar's primal squeeze is *epistemic*: the real middle — natural necessity — is squeezed *out of knowledge*, between pure reason and pure experience. The thesis's squeeze is *ontological*: the *world*, the made meaning-space, is squeezed *out of being*, between the universe and the metaverse. Same figure — the elimination of the middle — driven by the same disposition, a totalizing certainty that admits only what conforms to it and flattens the plural middle. **Bhaskar's primal squeeze is the nonmaterial, epistemic ancestor of the thesis's ontological squeeze**: the founding intellectual move which, once materially realized (Needham–Pomeranz: mechanistic interventionism plus externalization), becomes the planetary squeeze the rest of the thesis traces.

## §6 — Universalization is not fitness

A natural objection now arises. If the Enlightenment mode has universalized — outcompeting, absorbing, or displacing every rival world — is that not just what it *looks like* for a mode to be *fitter*: superior, selected, the winner? I reject the inference on three grounds.

First, **survivor is not fittest, because the winner writes the metric.** [ADLER: the argument as it stands now is only decorative and conflates fitness to the world with fitness to the universe. this doesn't answer why survivor isn't fitter in the universe] "Fitness" is never mode-neutral; it is always fitness *to* a standard, and the mode that prevails is the mode that gets to set the standard — GDP, technological power, energy throughput, rate of growth. "The Enlightenment mode is the fittest, by the Enlightenment mode's own metrics" is a circle, not a finding. This is the incommensurability the thesis marks as F2: there is no neutral scoreboard on which modes compete. A dominance secured by accident and deferred cost is not evidence of intrinsic fitness; it is evidence of contingent advantage drawing down an unpaid account. Universalization is the elimination of precisely the plurality that long-run fitness requires [ADLER: re-integrate an earlier note on colonization as the replacement of artificiality, especially replacement at the real & actual levels, not just the empirical level; and how such colonization is related to ecological entropy/fragility]: it consumes the regeneration base (§2) on which any future remaking depends. So far from being the proof of fitness, universalization is, in the deep sense, its opposite — short-run dominance bought with the destruction of the conditions of renewal.

And this coheres with §2: collapse is mismatch, not unfitness — so neither is dominance fitness. The Ming's fall did not prove the Ming "unfit," and the Enlightenment mode's present reach does not prove it "fit." Dominance and collapse are alike *squeeze-phenomena*, not verdicts in a competition that was never neutrally scored.

## §7 — Bridge to Chapter IV: life-as-willed

What, in the end, is the *content* the Enlightenment mode universalizes? Not only a method, but a telos: the universal pursuit of *life-as-willed* — Uegaki's 意のままになる生 — existence remade to answer to the will, the world bent to want.[^43] This pursuit, I will argue, is largely an Enlightenment invention now mistaken for the human condition as such. And it is here that this chapter's squeeze passes into the next: as life-as-willed is *fully actualized*, it raises a new layer of neo-artificiality — the metaverse — into which the world is drawn, and existence is squeezed anew, no longer merely between a world and its universe, but between the **universe and the metaverse**. Chapter IV takes up that terminal squeeze.
