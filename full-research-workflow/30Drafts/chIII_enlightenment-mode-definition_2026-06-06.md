# The Enlightenment mode of artificiality, and its principle of controllability
> Ch III mode-definition prose (ai-draft, voice-matched). Verification + integration notes at foot.

The mode of artificiality whose expansion this chapter follows I will call the **Enlightenment mode** — and I must say at once in what sense, because the word, left unglossed, will mislead. I do not mean the eighteenth century: not the *Aufklärung* as a period, not Kant's essay and the *philosophes*, not a chapter in the history of European ideas. I mean what Adorno and Horkheimer meant when they spoke of enlightenment "in the widest sense as the advance of thought" — a metaphysical disposition rather than a date, one that "has always aimed at liberating human beings from fear and installing them as masters" of a disenchanted world (Horkheimer and Adorno, Jephcott trans. [1947] 2002: 1). Enlightenment, in this sense, is not the property of a century but of a *stance*: the positing of the human subject as standing prior to, and over against, a given stripped of any meaning of its own — the world made into mere object, there to be known, calculated, and turned to use. The eighteenth century is where the stance reaches self-consciousness and gives itself its proudest name; it is the crystallisation, not the origin, and not the whole.

If "Enlightenment" names the stance, "controllability" names its operative principle — and here too the term is borrowed precisely. Hartmut Rosa has argued that what distinguishes the modern relationship to the world is not a particular belief but a *relationship*: the drive to render the world *verfügbar*, which his translator, having weighed and rejected "availability," renders as **controllable** (Rosa, 2020: viii–ix). The world is to be made controllable along four dimensions — made *visible*, and so knowable; made *reachable*, and so accessible; made *manageable*; and made *useful*, "pressed into service" (Rosa, 2020: 14–17). This is not yet a reproach; it is the description of a comportment. To meet the given as that which is to be brought under control is *one* way of relating to it, and — as Rosa is at pains to say — not the only one: there are relationships to the world built on attunement and responsiveness, on living within the given rather than mastering it. The Enlightenment mode is the mode for which controllability has become the *whole* of the relation, so that the given appears to it as nothing but a field of what is, or is not yet, under control.

Beneath the principle lies a single root, which the next chapter follows inward but which must be named here. The controllability-relation becomes possible only once the subject has been set apart from and above the given — what Uegaki calls the human's 時空間的自立性 (*jikūkanteki jiritsusei*, its spatio-temporal self-subsistence: its standing as something that exists prior to and independent of the world around it). From that one root grow two drives, and the mode is their marriage. One is a drive of *value*: the conviction that the human bears within it a true nature owed to it and not yet realised, to be brought about by remaking the given until it matches the ideal. The other is a drive of *instrument*: the reduction of the given to what can be measured, predicted, and worked. I develop these two engines, and the necessity of their convergence, in Chapter IV; here it is enough to mark that controllability is what they jointly yield — the value-drive supplying the *ought*, the instrument-drive the *can*, and the world remade between them.

What makes the present chapter's concern the mode's *universalising* is that the controllability-relation does not stay within the world that bred it. A mode of artificiality is, in the thesis's terms, one actual world among many — one way of constituting a meaning-space within the given universe — and most such worlds have been content to be local, knowing themselves as one way among others of inhabiting the given. The Enlightenment mode is not. It carries its own made world as though that world were the given universe itself; and wherever it meets another world — another way of relating to the given, often one built on attunement rather than control — it does not encounter it as a peer but overwrites its organising conditions, recoding them in the terms of controllability. That is the outward face of the mode, and it is what this chapter tracks: not the mode's internal dynamics, which wait for Chapter IV, but its comportment toward the worlds it displaces.

That this universalising should have issued from the West, and not elsewhere, is the question the literature on the "great divergence" has long worried, and the thesis takes from it a deliberately modest lesson. Needham asked why the systematic, controlling science that remade the world arose in Europe and not in a China that had long been its technical equal; Pomeranz answered, for the economic divergence, that much was contingent — coal where it was needed, and the windfall of New World land and labour — rather than written into any civilisation's essence. To this material story the thesis adds a *metaphysical* layer: that what the West universalised was, before any particular technology, the controllability-relation itself — and that the universalising was rarely gentle. Miura's reading of Locke shows the mechanism in miniature: the labour that "mixes" the self into the world converts the given into property; the earth is to be "subdued" and made over to "the industrious and rational"; and on that ground the lands of others — judged unimproved, merely *natural* — become appropriable without their consent, so that a theory of property does the work of a justification of conquest, "the ownership of nature… bound up with domination over… alien peoples" (三浦 / Miura, 2006: 95–99). The overwriting of worlds is not a figure of speech; it has a documented history, and the controllability-relation is its principle.

A discipline must travel with the name, the same one the thesis imposes wherever it speaks of "the West." To call this the Enlightenment mode is *not* to say that the West *is* this mode, or that a civilisational essence drives some peoples toward control and spares others. The mode is a *strand* — one tendency, selected and radicalised out of a far more various inheritance. Its eponymous ancestors are conventionally named "Platonic" (the idea set over the given) and "Aristotelian" (the given known and ordered), and I will use those names — but as eponyms, not histories. Greek thought was never reducible to Plato and Aristotle, still less to idealism and empiricism; the sophists, the mystics, the atomists, the Stoics and the Skeptics all stand off that axis, and neither Plato nor Aristotle was the modern idealist or empiricist the labels evoke. What the Enlightenment mode did was not to inherit "the Greek mind" but to *select* two strands from it, sharpen them, and universalise them, while the others — the traditions of withdrawal, of attunement, of dwelling within the given — were subordinated or forgotten. The mode is a distillation, then, and not a destiny: which is exactly why it might have been otherwise, and why to name it is to identify a tendency, not to convict a civilisation.

None of this is yet a verdict. To say that the Enlightenment mode meets the given as a thing to be controlled, and that it universalises that relation over the worlds it meets, is to describe a comportment, not to indict it — the freedoms and the masteries this mode has won are real, as the chapters to come will insist as firmly as they insist on the cost. What the description fixes is only the shape of the thing whose expansion this chapter follows; its eventual collision with the very given it declared itself independent of — the return, in Rosa's word, of an "uncontrollability" the mode can neither absorb nor abide — is what the later chapters will name the squeeze.
