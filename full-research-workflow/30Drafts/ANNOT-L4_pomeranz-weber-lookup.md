## 4. Synthesis Point for Ch-3-4 PROSE

The Ch-3-4 PROSE cycle should hold all four positions explicitly without adjudicating between them â€” the thesis's argument operates *underneath* the historiographical debate, not inside it.

**Proposed structure for the Ch-3-4 paragraph(s):**

1. **One sentence each acknowledging the four standing positions:**
   - **Material/ecological (Pomeranz, Frank):** the Western breakthrough was contingent on coal endowment and Atlantic windfall; comparable Asian cores could have made similar transitions under different resource configurations.
   - **Institutional (Acemoglu/Robinson):** what mattered was the formation of inclusive economic and political institutions that made productive innovation profitable.
   - **Cultural-values (Weber, Landes, Mokyr's earlier work):** distinctive religious-ethical and "useful knowledge" cultures of early-modern Europe shifted incentives toward systematic technical improvement.
   - **Cultural-rhetorical (McCloskey, Mokyr 2017):** the change was less about institutions or values per se than about a dignity-revolution in how innovators and merchants were socially talked about.
   - **Reframing pole (Needham):** the very *non-occurrence* of analogous takeoff in pre-modern China â€” a civilization with greater cumulative technological achievement up to ~1500 â€” shows that none of the above is a sufficient condition.

2. **The thesis's own positioning** (the load-bearing move):
   The "disposition to domination" is a claim at the **real level** in the critical-realist sense â€” a tendency built into the metaphysical infrastructure of the Modern Western mode of artificiality, not a prediction about which actual historical configuration would activate it first or most fully. The disposition is *underdetermined* by any single empirical/material configuration; it is consistent with multiple actual histories â€” including counterfactual ones in which coal, Atlantic windfall, or inclusive institutions had been distributed differently. What the disposition explains is not *that* the takeoff happened in northwest Europe between 1750 and 1850, but the *character* of what was unleashed once any sufficient material configuration came online: an artificiality oriented toward the displacement and re-engineering of the universe rather than toward dwelling-within. The Great Divergence debate is, on this reading, a debate about *triggers and timings*; the thesis's claim is about *what got triggered*.

3. **Why this avoids cherrypicking:**
   The thesis does not need any one of Pomeranz / Mokyr / Acemoglu / McCloskey to be right. It only needs the conjunction: *some* material/institutional/cultural configuration sufficed to actualize a real-level disposition that, on the thesis's argument, was already metaphysically prepared in the Modern Western tradition. The historiographical debate is preserved in its full openness; the thesis's claim is shown to be compatible with any of its outcomes.
