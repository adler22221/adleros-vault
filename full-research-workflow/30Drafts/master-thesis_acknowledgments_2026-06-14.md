# Acknowledgments

This thesis exists because many people widened what I could imagine, and it is a pleasure to record my debts to them here.

My first and deepest thanks go to my supervisor, Chienkuo Mi (米建國), who invited and encouraged me into the master's program without a bachelor's degree, and who from the beginning treated me less as a student to be instructed than as an intrapreneur to be entrusted — drawing me into department- and school-wide innovations, and including me, almost without exception, whenever an international workshop or symposium came through, down to the dinners where I found myself seated among leading philosophers from around the world. That apprenticeship incubated in a far wider spectrum of philosophical approaches, themes, and ways of practicing philosophy than any syllabus could have offered.

Our intellectual temperaments are very different — he is an analytic virtue epistemologist, while I have been shaped by social theory, continental philosophy, and systems thinking — and I am all the more grateful for the space and flexibility he gave me to find my own path. In truth, had he not probed and challenged me with the theme of the metaverse, as I recount in the Introduction, I would never have imagined that a master's thesis could be this radical. I had proposed something smaller and safer, close to my professional background in education and social change and drawing on East Asian philosophy toward those ends; he challenged me instead to take on the metaverse, despite my complete lack of a computer-science background. Without his trust in my industry experience, my transdisciplinary interests, and my capacity for self-directed learning, I cannot see how work at this scope would have been possible.

His open-mindedness is the reason the thesis is as authentic as it is. By the time I had thrown myself into Taiwan's technology communities, surveyed the field, and produced a proposal, the work had already diverged sharply from what he had envisioned — his own questions were anchored in analytic philosophy, in the problems of mind and body, mind and world, and mental representation — and yet he urged me onward. That openness extended to the examination board he assembled: by inviting the German philosopher and Sinologist Kai Marchal (馬愷之) and the critical sociologist of technology Yucheng Liu (劉育成), he convened a genuinely interdisciplinary committee able to weigh the thesis on its own transdisciplinary terms — as faithfully and as supportively as possible — rather than by the standards of analytic philosophy alone. Kai and Yucheng's supportive reminders, along with Chienkuo's, were central to the substantial expansion of this post-viva final manuscript.

## A note on AI and the lived squeeze behind this thesis

Honesty about how this thesis was made is, I think, part of its argument.

The proposal submitted in June 2024 and the manuscript for viva submitted in December 2024, passed in January 2025, were conceived, structured, and written by me alone, only with Grammarly and Paperpal as grammar checkers. After the viva, I used Gemini Pro and Perplexity Pro not to write but to _see_: to benchmark the manuscript against my examiners' comments and against publication-grade standards, to push myself from known unknowns toward unknown unknowns, and to solicit critique of my own biases. I used NotebookLM to digest a large literature under a deliberate hallucination-guard — requiring verbatim excerpts and exact sources for every claim. In the final two months before the graduation deadline, I worked with Claude as my principal research assistant, to reduce the physical strain on my health, which is still recovering from a major health crisis in the summer of 2025 that escalated from chronic overwork, while ensuring I graduate by the final deadline (I have already exhausted my permissible extensions due to full-time work and the health crisis).

Concretely, Claude served as (1) a **source-grounded verification layer** — locating exact passages and verbatim quotations, resolving citations against my Zotero library and the primary texts, and catching and correcting misattributions, mis-datings, and altered quotations; (2) a **research-digestion and retrieval aid**, alongside NotebookLM under a strict verbatim-excerpt discipline so every claim could be traced to its source; (3) a **sparring partner** for stress-testing and sharpening arguments; (4) **a de-blocker for my writing** — working from the argumentative structure I supplied, it wove together raw materials and proposed tentative prose for me to assemble and substantially rewrite (materials auditable on request); (5) **a bounded drafting collaborator**, elaborating and tightening particular passages, and de-jargoning my draft that would otherwise be too abstruse and inaccessible, each of which I reviewed, verified, and revised before adopting; (6) an **editorial collaborator** — house-style consistency, copyediting, and a full pre-submission review. The entire conceptual architecture of the thesis — the universe/world/metaverse ontology, the squeeze, the forged Dao, the account of artificiality — and the responsibility for every claim in it remain mine; the machine was an instrument I directed, not an author.

I disclose this not only for honesty but because the writing of this thesis became, against my intention, one of its own cases. I wrote it under a squeeze — between my biological and temporal limits, a demanding job, and the standard I hold myself to, having worked at the edge of burnout. To keep going within those limits I offloaded much of my work, intended and anti-intended alike, onto the artificial: researching by phone in transit, listening to digests and excerpts while showering or exercising, debating with AI tools when I was too ill to sit at a desk. The reality I live fits none of the thesis's clean ideal types — neither the entrusting that actualizes the as-intended, nor the offloading of the anti-intended, nor the innocent bearer crushed by a squeeze that others' entrusting and offloading set in motion. It is a little of each at once. And its deepest irony is the one the thesis predicts: much of my offloading was done in the _hope of re-embedding_, of lightening a body at its limit — yet leaning harder on a self-completing apparatus is acceleration, not re-embedding; it deepens the very dependence it means to relieve. 

Even with these tools, I slept only three to five hours a night through much of the final month before submission, letting my AI assistant extract and organize verbatim excerpts and execute other technical housekeeping tasks while I slept; so even with AI, I went on trespassing the boundaries of my own body, the universe inside me. And yet, without artificiality, I might well not be here to write this at all. That is the pharmakon exactly — the remedy that is also the poison — and for me there was no clean escape from it.

But a diagnosis is also a beginning. The very account that names the squeeze also shows where relief might lie, and setting it down has been, for me, a first step toward it. I do not claim to have escaped the squeeze — only to have learned to see it, which is itself the start of re-embedding rather than flight. My hope from here is modest and concrete: to let artificiality serve an embedded life rather than supplant it, to keep the body — the universe within me — in view, and to recompose my dependence with more care than I once spent it. The squeeze admits more than one ending; I mean to help write a better one, beginning with my own.
