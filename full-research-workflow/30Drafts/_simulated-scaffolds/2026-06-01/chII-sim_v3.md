## Opener — Artificiality as the umbrella

Chapter I argued that a world is an adaptational operating system: a bounded, humanly-made sub-environment, nested within the universe, through which a population organizes its collective survival. The closing claim of that chapter was that artificiality — not culture — is the analytical unit that makes a human human and a world a world.[^op1] This chapter asks what such a world is *made of*, and answers with a single category. By artificiality I mean the whole class of humanly-produced organizing principles, material and nonmaterial alike, by which a world is constituted, transmitted, and adapted: not only tools and built environments but institutions, laws, symbols, language, customs, religion, ideology, and music. Artificiality is the umbrella under which the rest of the thesis proceeds.

[^op1]: [VERIFY: DOCX [P0109] — "Artificiality — my analytical unit that replaces culture — is what makes human human, and a world world."]

That this broad category is the right unit, rather than some narrower one, is warranted by a convergence of two traditions that arrive at it from opposite directions. Bernard Stiegler's reading of *technē* is the broad one: technics designates, first of all, the whole field of skill — rhetoric, medicine, written law, the arts of the polis — so that all human action has something to do with *technē*, and the narrow modern sense of "technics" is a late and derived restriction of it.[^op2] From an entirely different starting point, Obara Hideo's 「モノ」 — the katakana form, deliberately distinguished from the hiragana 「もの」 of concrete things — names everything humans have made, abstract language included, with religion, ideology, and music explicitly inside the category.[^op3] A late-modern French philosophy of technics and a contemporary Japanese ethological anthropology reach the same unit from opposite ends; that independent convergence is what licenses treating artificiality as a found category rather than a coined one.

[^op2]: [VERIFY: Stiegler 1998, *Technics and Time 1* (Zotero YPJ94B8W) — broad-technē reading; narrow modern reading as "already restrictive and derived"; cf. Heidegger 1977 p.13, CONSOLIDATED §B.2.]

[^op3]: [VERIFY: Obara — モノ as "everything humans have created, including abstract language." Obara NOT yet in Zotero per Ch-2-9; acquire/resolve citekey before citing.]

A small discipline of vocabulary follows, and it is worth stating once. The thesis does not deploy "technology" as a major concept. Where the philosophy-of-technology tradition writes "technology," this thesis reads *artificiality* in the broad sense. This is not pedantry. It forecloses a misreading that would otherwise defeat the whole argument in advance — the misreading that "the metaverse is just more advanced *technology*." Working at the level of artificiality keeps that door shut, because it makes the metaverse continuous with everything from oral ritual to written law rather than a sudden technical novelty. So where the running text below would have written "technology," it writes artificiality; the inherited word is named only where a particular author's term is being relabelled.

### One civilization's two categories of the artificial

There is an old vocabulary that sharpens what artificiality is, and the chapter can enter through it — provided one discipline is observed. The ancient Greeks divided what there is, at one fundamental seam, between the grown and the made. On the grown side stood *phusis* — from the root \*bʰeh₂u-, "to grow, arise, be," the same root that gives φύω, "to grow, bring forth, be born"; *phusis* is the self-arising, that whose principle of motion lies within itself.[^op4] On the made side stood two terms, not one. The first was *technē* — from the root \*tek-, "to build," the very root of τέκτων, "the carpenter"; *technē* is craftsmanship, the systematic method of making, whose source of motion lies not in the thing made but in the maker.[^op5] The second was *nomos* — built on νέμω, "to distribute, to allot" — that which is in habitual practice, custom and usage, hardening into statute and ordinance made by authority.[^op6] Custom-becoming-law and craft-becoming-artefact: two categories, both on the made side of the seam.

[^op4]: [VERIFY: confirmed verbatim | Beekes 2010 s.v. φύομαι — "<IE *bʰeh₂u- 'grow, arise, be'>"; LSJ s.v. φύσις, "(φύω)"; CONSOLIDATED §A.1. Aristotle's internal-principle gloss: *Physics* II.1 192b8–193a, CONSOLIDATED §B.1.]

[^op5]: [VERIFY: confirmed verbatim | Beekes 2010 s.v. τέχνη — "<IE *tek- 'produce', *te-tk- 'build, timber'>"; root of τέκτων; LSJ τέχνη sense III "a set of rules, system, method of making"; Cassin 2014 "ART" *teks- "construct"; CONSOLIDATED §A.3.]

[^op6]: [VERIFY: confirmed verbatim | LSJ s.v. νόμος — "that which is in habitual practice, use... usage, custom... statute, ordinance made by authority"; built on νέμω; Beekes 2010 s.v. νέμω <IE *nem- "distribute">; CONSOLIDATED §A.2 / §B.1.]

Here is the move, and here is the discipline. The reframe the chapter makes is that *nomos* and *technē* are the two major categories the ancient Greeks treated as **artificial** — as the made, the humanly-instituted — set together against the grown. This is one civilization's metaphysics of the artificial, and the point of saying "one civilization's" is that other civilizations carved the made differently. Where the Greeks had *technē* and *nomos*, ancient China articulated the artificial through 器 (*qi*), the instrument-or-vessel, within a cosmotechnical order in which the made is subordinate to and resonant with the cosmic-moral way (*dao*) rather than standing over against a separate "nature." Yuk Hui states the asymmetry flatly: the Chinese have no equivalents of the categories the Greeks called *technē* and *physis*.[^op7] The grouping is therefore not a universal grammar of "the artificial" but a description of how *the Greeks* drew the line — which is precisely why the thesis can treat artificiality as its own analytical unit without inheriting the Greek partition as if it were the only one.

[^op7]: [VERIFY: confirmed verbatim | Hui, *The Question Concerning Technology in China* (huiQuestionTechnologyChina2016, Zotero CWX5W69K), p.52 — "the Chinese have no equivalents of the categories that the Greeks called techne and physis"; CONSOLIDATED §G.2. Hui supplies cosmotechnics + non-universality + the 器/道 scheme; the wording "the two categories the Greeks treated as artificial" is the author's synthesis extending Hui — see Scaffold notes.]

This reframe is deliberately a grouping, not an identity. Rémi Brague, in his comparative history, groups *technē* and *nomos* together as precisely this class: the idea of "nature," he writes, is itself derived, the result of a separation between "that which has its principle in the human activity of fabrication or estimation, i.e., the artificial (*tekhnē*) and the conventional (*nomos*), and that which grows by itself, spontaneously, the natural."[^op8] Brague pairs the made-by-craft with the made-by-convention against the grown — which is exactly the grouping the chapter needs, and no more than that. The reframe makes no claim that *nomos is* a species of *technē*; it claims only that a community's law, even when it presents itself as eternal and divinely sanctioned, belongs on the made side alongside its tools. That is what it means to say artificiality is the umbrella: a world's nonmaterial ordering is not a separate ontological kind from its instruments. Both are artificiality.

[^op8]: [VERIFY: confirmed verbatim | Brague 2004, *The Wisdom of the World*, p.14 (bragueWisdomWorldHuman2004); CONSOLIDATED §A.Brague / §B.4. Brague GROUPS technē + nomos as the human-made class; he does not assert an identity. The "two categories the Greeks treated as artificial" formulation is the author's synthesis, now Brague-aligned by the grouping reframe.]

There is a companion provocation that uses the same vocabulary, and it must be located carefully so that it is not mistaken for an argument made here. The later chapters will say that the metaverse is, in a sense, *technē becoming phusis* — the made coming to comport itself as the given. That phrase is **not** a literal claim that the made acquires an internal principle of motion of the kind Aristotle reserved for natural things. It is a deliberate provocation in the tradition's own vocabulary, aimed at exposing that the inherited seam between the grown and the made — the very seam between *phusis* and *technē*/*nomos*, which maps onto the nature/culture/technology divide the thesis already rejected in Chapter I — has stopped doing reliable work. The provocation belongs to Chapters V and VI, where the metaverse-stage argument is made; it is mentioned here only to mark the trajectory this chapter opens. No source — Aristotle, Heidegger, Brague, Hui — asserts it, and it is not argued in Chapter II. What the chapter establishes here is only the umbrella and its two registers; the migration between them is later business.

With the umbrella set, the chapter develops artificiality's role in forming a world through three aspects, in the order the main text already follows: its **impetus** (exosomatization, §IIA), its **nonmaterial dimension** (virtuality, §IIB), and the **irreducibility** of the laws of artificial environments to those of the universe (§IIC). It closes on the **double-edgedness** that runs through all three.

## §IIA — Exosomatization

The first aspect is the impetus to develop artificiality as extensions or enhancements of our functions, compensating for biological weakness. Since the dawn of history, tools and the processes of writing have extended memory; weapons have extended a blunt body and weak biological force. The biophysicist Alfred Lotka named this adaptation through artificial aids *exosomatic evolution* — adaptation *outside* the body, in tools, techniques, records, and institutions — and held that it is what distinguishes *Homo sapiens* from the animals that evolve *endosomatically*, by genetic mutation.[^a1] The distinction is one of kind, not degree: exosomatic adaptation accumulates through non-genetic inheritance, and at a rate biological evolution alone could never deliver.

[^a1]: [VERIFY: Lotka 1945; cf. DOCX [P0113]. Lotka NOT yet confirmed in Zotero — resolve citekey.]

The aids act back on us. Mastering and living with artificial tools becomes itself a new adaptational pressure, oriented toward the ever-emerging artificial environment — the self-domestication Obara identifies as the watershed of human existence.[^a2] The affordances of these aids — their range of possible usages and ways of being interacted with — direct emotion, thought, behaviour, and interaction; and with new thoughts and behaviours come new needs, which call for new aids. The loop closes on itself: artificiality extends what we can do and reshapes who we become in doing it.

[^a2]: [VERIFY: Obara 2000, self-domestication (自己家畜化) / auto-artificial selection; cf. DOCX [P0075], [P0114]. Citekey to resolve.]

Stiegler reads this ever-exteriorization of organs and memories as the very character of artificiality — or, on the other side of the same coin, artificiality as our indispensable organ.[^a3] Exosomatization cannot alter the laws of the universe, but it *localizes* them: it "locally trans-forms the cosmic order in unpredictable ways."[^a4] This tends to form *techno-geographical milieus*, kin to Obara's artificial ecosystems — environments dependent on, but not reducible to, the universe. That last phrase is the hinge to §IIC, and the chapter holds it for now.

[^a4]: [VERIFY: confirmed | Stiegler 2018: 42 | per DOCX [P0115]. Coordinate one Stiegler edition across the chapter per Ch-2-5 note.]

### The fault of Epimetheus

The exosomatic compensation is constitutive, not merely supplementary, and the oldest stratum of Greek myth already encodes it. In Plato's *Protagoras*, Epimetheus distributes natural powers to every creature — speed, claws, wings, shells — but forgets the human, who is left naked, unshod, unbedded, and unarmed, so that Prometheus must steal fire and *technē* to make good the lack.[^a5] Stiegler reads this as structure rather than narrative accident: the human is the forgotten one, occurring only through its being forgotten, so that there is "nothing at the origin but the fault," a *de-fault of origin*.[^a6] The myth figures what developmental anthropology states as secondary altriciality: *Homo sapiens* is born biologically unfinished, without an instinct-complete niche, and must be finished from outside itself.[^a7] Artificiality is therefore not an instrument added to an already-complete creature; it is the constitution of this body *as* human. The human does not *have* a world the way a mole has its burrow; lacking a given niche, it must *make* one — because it has no other way to be at all.

[^a5]: [VERIFY: *Protagoras* 321c–322a (Jowett); via Stiegler 1998 pp.188–189; page-confirmed in Ch-1-8 lookup, re-confirm against print; CONSOLIDATED §C.5 Prometheus correlate.]

[^a6]: [VERIFY: Stiegler 1998, p.188 — "de-fault of origin" / *défaut d'origine*; secondary altriciality. Keep compact per the Ch-1-8 decision memo.]

[^a7]: [VERIFY: secondary altriciality / neoteny — standard developmental anthropology (Portmann's "extra-uterine year"); uncited here — add a source if a citation is wanted.]

This is the strongest form of the §IIA claim, and the anti-instrumentalist point the umbrella frame needs: there is no pre-technical human to be supplemented. It also discharges, in the tradition's own terms, the premature-birth intuition the author has carried independently. The single myth earns its place by being the originary scene from which the very *de-fault of origin* is derived; the wider comparative survey of cosmogonies is left aside.

### Tertiary retention

What is exteriorized is not only labour but *memory*. Beyond the primary retention that is the lingering "comet's tail" of the living present — the just-heard still sounding within what is now heard — and the secondary memory of individual recollection, Stiegler names a third: tertiary memory, the technically externalized record. Writing, documents, recordings, the inherited archive — the supports of the already-there, through which what has passed remains available to the living.[^a8] Tertiary memory is the *hypomnemata*, the written memory-aids, that precede any individual and into which each of us is born; it is the material condition of a world's historical depth, the reason world-making is cumulative and trans-generational rather than Sisyphean.

[^a8]: [VERIFY: Stiegler 1998, pp.247, 251 (primary/secondary/tertiary retention; *hypomnemata*); pages confirmed against extracted text, attachment QS6TCGH4.]

The forward-looking payoff can stay brief here, and Chapter V will carry its weight: the metaverse figures later as the *industrialization of tertiary retention*, when the social archive and the historical deposit come to be administered by algorithmically managed platforms that decide what is amplified, what is buried, and how fast history scrolls — imposing a single, commercially optimized temporality on collective memory. That is the toxic edge, and it belongs to Chapters V and VI, and to the close of this one. In §IIA it is enough to register that memory itself is among the things exosomatized, and that this is what gives a world its inheritable depth.

## §IIB — Virtuality

Material artificiality is the easy half to picture — from stone tools to AI-powered "smart cities." But artificiality is not always manifested materially, and its nonmaterial forms are not a lesser appendix to the material ones; they are indispensable to them, and dialectically bound up with them. As Harari reminds us, artificially created nonmaterial things — gods, money, laws — have the power to direct our thoughts and behaviours and even to elicit emotions or biological reflexes.[^b1] Without such nonmaterial coordinating principles a world is impossible: material things remain mere aggregates, never integrated into functioning systems.

[^b1]: [VERIFY: Harari 2015; cf. DOCX [P0117]. Resolve citekey.]

I call this causally efficacious nonmaterial aspect of artificiality *virtuality*.[^b2] The phrasing is deliberate: virtuality is an *aspect* of artificial things — their nonmaterial-and-causally-efficacious side — not a separate ontological category set alongside artificiality. A coin has a material aspect (the metal) and a nonmaterial aspect (its being-money); the virtuality is the latter. Virtuality is thus the §IIB-specific lens on the nonmaterial subset of the umbrella, not a parallel to it. Every virtuality is an artificiality; not every artificiality is paradigmatically virtual — a wall compels by mass, not by meaning.

[^b2]: [VERIFY: locked definition, DOCX [P0118]: "I call this causally efficacious nonmaterial aspect of artificiality virtuality." KEEP VERBATIM.]

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

Three popular conceptions of "virtual" are declined, each for a precise reason, and each declining keeps something. The first is the simulationist view, which sees the virtual as a not-quite-real replica — a view traceable to Peirce's definition of a virtual *x* as "something, not an *x*, which has the efficiency (*virtus*) of an *x*."[^b3] The thesis keeps the *virtus* — the power, the efficacy — and rejects the "not an *x*," the "as if," because many virtual things have no original to imitate. Baudrillard supplies exactly that ground: many artificial things have no original or real counterpart to represent at all, and a virtual *x* may be original in its own right.[^b4] The second is Chalmers's reassertion of the colloquial "virtual = computer-based," advanced to defend the realness of digital objects against the as-if view.[^b5] The thesis adopts Chalmers's substrate-neutrality and his simulation-realism without modification, but parts from him on the analytical unit: where Chalmers asks whether the *x* inside a virtual environment is real, the thesis's unit is the *trans-historical layer of human-constructed nonmaterial structure* — stories, gods, ceremonies, laws, scientific theories, mathematical models, algorithms — that has always partly constituted human worlds. What Chalmers calls computer-based is, on this reading, no more than a temporal medium of a long historical continuum.

[^b3]: [VERIFY: Peirce 1902, per DOCX [P0119]. Peirce NOT in Zotero per Ch-2-6 v4 — LOOKUP or paraphrase-with-secondary-attribution before final.]

[^b4]: [VERIFY: confirmed | Baudrillard 1994 (Zotero 5495YCYV) | per DOCX [P0120]. [^b5]: [VERIFY: confirmed | Chalmers 2022 | NLM A-Sources UUID d672826d | per DOCX [P0121]. NB: the DOCX [P0122] "biologically programmed and powered" sentences are flagged in Ch-2-6 v4 ¶5c for replacement — highest-value remaining surgical edit; flag for author.]

The historical continuum is the third thing declined, and it is the positive claim the first two clear room for. As Durante observes, humans have always dealt with the virtual, ever since they lived by the stories they collectively invented.[^b6] At first virtuality appeared as gods and ceremonies — the nonmaterial yet causally efficacious basis on which worlds could form; over time, in many places, gods were displaced by sciences and ceremonies by laws, each transition a *change of substrate* — oral to written to printed to computational — operating on the *same underlying virtuality*. Money is the cleanest case: causally efficacious, intersubjectively binding, indifferent to its substrate, and older than the computer by millennia. As Brey notes, while "rocks and trees" or "screwdrivers and chairs" can only be *simulated*, "money can literally exist" in a digital environment.[^b7] Money is still money; money has always been virtual; shells, grain, salt, metal, paper, or digits are merely the temporal mediums through which money's virtuality is operationalized.

The claim that nonmaterial artificiality is genuinely causal finds independent support in Searle's social ontology, and the support belongs here at roughly footnote weight — a corroborating interlocutor, not load-bearing apparatus. Searle distinguishes *brute facts*, which obtain independently of human opinion (a mountain's height, light's wavelength — in this thesis's vocabulary, the universe), from *institutional facts*, which obtain only through collectively accepted *status functions* of the form "*X counts as Y in context C*," and which carry *deontic powers* — obligations and entitlements with real normative force.[^b8] This furnishes the social-ontological mechanism by which money's virtuality binds: money is not a simulation of some prior "real" money but is constituted by, and exhausted in, collective acceptance — which is exactly the as-if view's denial that §IIB needs. But Searle is, for the thesis's purposes, too anthropocentric: by gating deontic powers behind human language, he cannot recognize the proto-virtual phenomena that lie below the human line.[^b9] The thesis draws the line elsewhere — at the presence of a causally-efficacious nonmaterial layer, language-gated or not. Every Searlean institutional fact is therefore a virtuality (a paradigm virtuality, in the human case), but not every virtuality is a Searlean institutional fact.

[^b8]: [VERIFY: Searle, *The Construction of Social Reality* 1995 / *Making the Social World* 2010 — NOT in Zotero per Ch-2-8. The "$20 bill counts as..." money formulation is NLM-confirmed via searlePhilosophyNewCentury2008 p.33; electronic-money "no physical reality... other than the representation" via searleLanguageSocialOntology2008 p.457.]

[^b9]: [VERIFY: author's DOCX note — "Searle's dichotomy still too anthropocentric — wouldn't say that rituals in animals have deontic powers." Footnote weight per Liu's scope discipline.]

This de-anthropocentrizing has a concrete payoff: virtuality has an evolutionary floor below the human line, just as exosomatization does. The gorilla's chest-beating display does real causal work — it produces deference, social ordering, the avoidance of costly combat — through a nonmaterial principle whose compelling power is given by what the display *means* within the group, not by the physical force of the beat; the sound and the posture are merely its temporal medium.[^b10] The crane's courtship dance follows the same logic in the reproductive register, and the coordinated management of collective excitement — synchronized alarm, sentinel behaviour, ritualized dominance and submission — completes the picture. The discipline carried from Ch-2-9 is that these are *proto*-virtual, not fully virtual: the same nonmaterial-causal structure at incomparably lower complexity, lacking the linguistic-conventional and deontic articulation of the human case (which is exactly why Searle excludes them). They are the evolutionary precursors of virtuality, not virtualities *tout court*. The payoff is decisive against any suggestion that the virtual is recent or merely technological: long before Chalmers's computer-based virtual, and even before Durante's storytelling humans, the nonmaterial-and-causal had already organized animal life. Virtuality is not what computation added to the world; it is what the world has run on since long before there were humans to call it virtual.

## §IIC — The irreducibility of artificial-environment laws

The use-lessness of material objects in digital environments exemplifies a key feature of artificial environments: they are dependent on and constrained by the materiality and laws of the universe, yet *not reducible* to them. They have laws of their own. A screwdriver simulated in a digital environment is use-*less* — not from any functional limit of the environment, but because the conditions for its functioning are absent: there are no physical fasteners, no gravity, no soreness of legs desiring a chair to rest on. "Tightening up" there requires an algorithm, not a screwing object.[^c1] So too the "physically fittest" person: best survival odds in the wilderness, which is a purely biological environment, but in a *world* — an artificiality field — possibly a *higher* mortality rate, assigned to hunting, war, or heavy labour, while a physically unfit person uses charisma or wit to concentrate power and resources to her own advantage.[^c2] The fitness function differs because the laws differ.

[^c1]: [VERIFY: DOCX [P0125].]

[^c2]: [VERIFY: DOCX [P0126].]

The committee's challenge was that this irreducibility is asserted rather than argued — that the main text shows it by example without saying *why* the laws are not reducible. The argument runs first through Polanyi, then through three formal moves.

### Polanyi: dual control and boundary conditions

The author's own framework already states the load-bearing point, in his own voice: an artificial environment is a special kind of *boundary condition*. Following Polanyi, a set of conditions and principles at a higher ontological level restricts the behaviour of lower-level things, so that the latter, which on their own have higher indeterminacy, can be harnessed into specific functions; and while higher-level things depend on lower-level ones for their existence, they are neither determined nor explained by them — they are irreducible to them.[^c3] The physical-chemical features of raw materials neither determine nor suffice to explain their uses; it is only through integration into a system — metabolism in an organism, assembly into a machine — that their features are harnessed. An artificial environment introduces a *new level* of this kind: its "laws" are boundary conditions imposed on activity within it, harnessing what the universe makes available without being dictated by it.

### Three arguments for the irreducibility

Three formal moves make explicit what the main text argues only by example; they can be offered as an optional strengthening, likely a footnote or short addition at [P0126]. *First, multiple realizability.* The same artificial-environment law — "a posted message cannot exceed 280 characters" — can be implemented in indefinitely many physical ways, across languages, hardware, and data centres. A multiply-realizable property is not identical to any one physical configuration, and so is not reducible to a physical description. *Second, the semantic-normative gap.* Artificial-environment laws are normative: they distinguish correct from incorrect, permitted from prohibited. An electron cannot "violate" a physical law; a user can violate terms of service. The permitted/prohibited distinction is not derivable from any physical description — it requires a level at which meanings and social significance operate (which is, incidentally, an argument for *virtuality's* irreducibility too, so it strengthens §IIB). *Third, downward causation.* If these laws were wholly fixed by their substrate they could exert no independent causal influence — yet a corporation's legal structure demonstrably causes physical outcomes, factories built and forests cleared, by organizing the behaviour of agents who exist at both levels at once. Downward causation is incompatible with full upward reduction.

The irreducibility, then, is structural rather than merely epistemological: universe-laws constrain artificial environments only at the substrate-implementation boundary — servers need power, bodies need food, institutions need humans — and not at the level of semantic operation, where algorithms decide, institutions confer status, and narratives organize meaning.

### The two registers of artificiality

Here the opener's Greek framing earns its keep, in what is the chapter's most consequential synthesis. The laws governing artificial environments fall into two registers.

The first is the **conventional register**, which I gloss as *nomos*-like. Here the artificiality compels because, and to the extent that, humans continue to enact it. A statute is enforced because police, courts, and prisons act; a norm holds because the community sanctions those who break it; a religious commandment compels because the faithful believe and enforce it. When the practice ceases, the artificiality ceases to operate. This is the register of *nomos* in the strict sense the lexicon gives it — that which is in habitual practice, custom, instituted by authority — the made-and-maintained.

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

The second is the **structural register**, which I gloss not as bare *phusis* but as *phusis-in-operation* — built, but operating as though given. Here the artificiality compels by the structure of the situation itself — its architecture, its affordances, its operational dynamics — rather than by social agreement. A locked door stays locked with no enforcer watching; an algorithm executes regardless of belief; a smart contract performs its conditional regardless of anyone's regret. The violation is not merely deterred by the threat of sanction; it is *foreclosed as an action-possibility*. The crucial qualification is the hyphenated one: this register is not *phusis*, because nothing here is self-arising — every structural law was engineered, instituted, built. It is *phusis-in-operation*: humanly made in its origin, yet given-like in the manner of its operating, confronting its subjects with the indifference of a substrate even though a designer stands behind it. That is why the reserved Chapters-V-and-VI provocation — "*technē* becoming *phusis*" — names, when it comes, the metaverse-stage *migration* of artificiality from the conventional register toward the structural one: not a literal acquisition of nature's internal principle, but the made coming to operate with the given's authority.

The registers name a *dominant* tendency, not an absolute partition. Conventional artificiality has structural margins (legal coercion ultimately rests on the state's monopoly on force), and structural artificiality has conventional margins (a platform's policies are, in principle, revisable). What the later squeeze argument identifies is the *progressive shrinking* of the conventional margin — the way initial decisions become ever harder to revise as a system reaches operational maturity. This register-distinction is also the cleanest answer to the committee's "rule versus law" question. Reserve *law* for structural artificialities whose violation is structurally prevented — the locked door, code-as-law; reserve *rule* or *norm* for conventional artificialities whose violation is externally punished but not structurally prevented — etiquette, customary law. Under this convention the metaverse genuinely has *laws of its own*: a structural homology with the universe's laws, differing only in that the universe's laws are naturally given while the metaverse's are humanly engineered. Juridical statutes sit awkwardly between the two — which is a real fact about them, since conventional law has always *aspired* to structural-compelling power and never fully achieved it. The metaverse's structural register is, in this light, the successful version of an ancient aspiration.[^c4]

## Close — the double-edgedness of artificiality

Whatever register it occupies, artificiality is double-edged, and this is no modern discovery. The structural claim, compressed: *artificialities at sufficient scale or depth tend to generate the very conditions of the harm they were instituted to relieve, absent active counter-work.*

Illich gives the historical shape of this. Every industrial institution crosses two watersheds. At the first it achieves genuine effectiveness — the year 1913, he notes, marks a watershed in modern medicine, the point at which a patient began to have more than a fifty-fifty chance that a trained physician would help rather than harm.[^d1] At the second watershed the effectiveness inverts: the progress demonstrated in a prior achievement becomes the rationale for exploiting society as a whole in the service of a value defined and constantly revised by self-certifying professional élites.[^d2] Past that point the institution enacts what Illich calls a *radical monopoly* — not market dominance but the elimination of the conditions under which alternatives could exist. Cars shape a city into their own image, ruling out locomotion on foot or by bicycle; that motor traffic curtails the very right to walk *is* the radical monopoly.[^d3]

Stiegler names the same ambivalence the *pharmacological* condition of all artificiality. The Greek *pharmakon* is at once remedy and poison; and, after Plato's *Phaedrus* — where Theuth offers writing as a remedy for memory that Thamus warns is also its poison — every exteriorized artificiality, once it has become independent of its user, can return as toxic.[^d4] Writing supplements memory and atrophies it; the platforms that extend sociality also industrialize it. The pharmakon is *undetermined* until a regime of *care* settles its disposition — Stiegler's *otium* against an exploitative *negotium* — and the metaverse-stage danger is that the pharmakon now mutates at platform speed while care still operates at biographical and intergenerational speed, so that the mismatch becomes structural rather than contingent.
