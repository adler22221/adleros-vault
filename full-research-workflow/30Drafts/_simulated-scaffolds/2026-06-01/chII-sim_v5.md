## §0 — Opening: from the Oxidation Event to the under-fitted animal

Chapter I opened with the Great Oxidation Event — the moment, some 2.4 billion years ago, when cyanobacteria flooded the atmosphere with a gas that was, to almost everything then alive, a poison.[^goe] The first and largest of Earth's catastrophes of fit was *self-inflicted*: the organisms that remade the air did not survive the air they had remade. Those that endured did so in one of two ways — by retreating into the anaerobic refuges where the old conditions still held, or by evolving to breathe the very poison, turning catastrophe into the most efficient metabolism life would ever find. Either way the resolution was biological: the living re-fitted, relocated, or died.

The human animal is a creature of fit-gone-wrong in a smaller and stranger key. Set beside the specialists with which it shares a landscape, its body reads as a catalogue of absences: no claws, no fangs, no speed to outrun a predator or run down large prey, no pelt against the cold, a gut that cannot digest what grows most abundantly underfoot. Whatever the precise evolutionary history that produced this body — and that history is, in its particulars, genuinely contested[^contested] — the present fact is not: the human is a biological generalist *under-specialised* for the demanding terrestrial niche it nonetheless occupies. It is, in the oldest description, "naked, unshod, unbedded, and unarmed";[^naked] its strength, an ecologist of our own century writes, "was poor, and it had no weapons" — and "that weakness became the driving force of the development of tools."[^obara-weak] This is the human's version of the Oxidation Event's predicament: a mismatch between an inherited body and an unforgiving world.[^mismatch-def]

What Chapter I did not ask is what such a creature *does* with a mismatch. The biosphere's standard answers are the ones the anaerobes took — adapt, relocate, or perish.[^mismatch-responses] Most lineages, finding the world no longer fits them, re-fit biologically across generations, track their vanishing niche across space, or go extinct. The human did something its under-specialised body made at once necessary and, just barely, possible: it *built*. It met a biological mismatch with an *artificial* answer — and in doing so it eventually stepped out of the ecological food web altogether, into a food chain of its own making: the one Chapter I described as having largely released its members from the predation and scarcity that still press on everything inside the web.[^release]

But other animals engineer their niches too — the beaver dams, the termite raises its mound — and none of them needs, or builds, an artificial food chain; each remains *inside* the web it modifies. So the question this chapter must answer is not *how* the human alters its environment, for every niche-constructor does, but why this one lineage's alteration *ran away* — why it alone compounded into a self-amplifying world of artifice that **exited** the web rather than merely adjusting a place within it. The answer has a robust shape and a set of contested particulars, and everything depends on keeping the two apart.

> [!note] Conceptual hinge — *squeeze* and *evolutionary mismatch* (full reference: [[squeeze-vs-mismatch]])
> The opening's predicament is an **evolutionary mismatch** in the strict sense — and the thesis's **squeeze** *generalises* it rather than renaming it. The canon is *trait*-bound and *change*-bound: mismatch is "a negative consequence that results from a **trait** that evolved in one environment being placed in another environment."[^mismatch-def] So mismatch is **dyadic** (a *trait* against an *environment*), **diachronic** (a lag — a past-tuned trait in a present environment, whether the change is temporal or spatial-by-migration), pitched at the **trait/structures** layer, and in principle **resolvable**. The squeeze keeps all of that as one *limiting case* — **Squeeze₁** — and adds the generalisations the metaverse case requires: **triadic** (caught *between two* sides at once — F1 betweenness, not a one-sided lag), **synchronic** (co-present pressures — and synchronicity *follows* from triadicity), and pitched at the **enduring-principles** layer where the clash is **incommensurable** (F2). So **mismatch is the squeeze minus F1 and F2**: **Squeeze₁ ≈ mismatch; Squeeze₂ (the metaverse) ⊋ mismatch.** Cite the mismatch canon for Squeeze₁; reserve F1/F2 as the thesis's own. (Self-generation — building one's own squeezer — is a shared bridge already in niche-construction theory, not a squeeze-only mark; the GOE shows it is 2.4 Gyr old.)

## §A — Niche-construction and its limit

Begin by clearing away the answer that first suggests itself: that the human is the animal that makes tools, or the animal that remakes its surroundings. Neither is true, or rather neither is distinctive. The beaver fells trees and dams rivers; the termite raises a tower with its own architecture of ventilation; the New Caledonian crow manufactures hooked tools and carries favoured ones from task to task; the octopus, an invertebrate separated from us by some six hundred million years, gathers coconut shells and reassembles them into armour. To engineer a niche — to alter the world so that it suits the alterer — is not a human invention but a deep and convergent fact of life.[^nct][^convergent] If there is a threshold between the human world and the animal's, it does not run between making and not-making.

It runs, instead, through the *structure* of the making. The difference, as the ecologist Obara saw, is not that humans use tools but that human fabrication is **recursive** — what he calls 加工の二重性, a doubleness of working. An otter cracks a shell with a stone it has selected but not altered; this is fabrication of the first order, a found thing put to a purpose. The human takes a second step that almost nothing else takes in any sustained way: it works the material *with another tool*, and then works that tool with a third, so that, in his words, "the working is done with tools, and to make a tool one uses a tool; this begets tool after tool, and tools give birth to a world of tools."[^obara-kakou] The product of the first order is a modified object; the product of the second is a self-extending *system* — a domain of artifice that compounds upon itself and, in compounding, pulls away from the natural world that seeded it.

That pulling-away is the decisive thing, and it is what the opening's image of an artificial food chain names. The beaver's dam changes the beaver's place within its ecosystem; it does not lift the beaver out of the food web. The beaver remains prey; it still forages within the web it has bent. The recursive tool-world does something categorically different: it builds, alongside the ecological web, a second web of the human's own making — cultivation, storage, exchange — and steps into it, until the lineage feeds itself by what Obara calls *seceding from the natural food chain*.[^obara-exit] Niche-construction modifies one's address inside the web; artificiality, past a certain recursive depth, furnishes a different web to live in.

It would be too quick, though, to say that animals merely *have* where the human *makes*, or that they cannot accumulate where the human can — and the chapter's claim must be made with care, because the evidence has grown more interesting than that. Animal culture plainly changes, spreads, and turns over: the song of the humpback whale undergoes "revolutions" that sweep across whole ocean basins, replacing one shared song with another within a few seasons. But through those revolutions, tellingly, "complexity within these song types remained consistent": the song is *replaced*, not improved upon; it cycles rather than climbs.[^whale] Nor is bounded accumulation wholly absent — Savannah sparrows have been observed across generations to "elaborate the 'click train' song form by adding more clicks," and homing pigeons to improve a route "continuously over generations."[^sparrow][^pigeon] So the human difference is not the bare *presence* of cumulative culture, which exists in flickers elsewhere, but its **form**: open-ended where theirs is bounded, a ratchet with no terminal tooth. What is singular is a recursion that never closes — and that, in never closing, leaves the web entirely.

The exception, then, is not a difference of kind dropped into the world from outside but a difference of degree carried to a place no other lineage has reached: niche-construction that lost its ceiling. Which only sharpens the question. Manipulative skill, a capable brain, sociality, even the first rungs of accumulation are each found, in some measure, in animals that never crossed this threshold. Why did *this* lineage's making lose its ceiling, when the crow's and the chimpanzee's did not?

## §B — Why our niche-construction ran away

Three things have to come together for niche-construction to become the open-ended, web-leaving recursion of the last section, and the conjunction is rare. The making must be *obligatory*, so that there is relentless pressure to improve it; the improvements must be *retained*, transmitted faithfully enough that each generation begins where the last left off rather than reinventing from nothing; and the maker must possess a *body and brain plastic enough* to be drawn into the loop. Pressure, retention, substrate — remove any one and the recursion stalls.

The pressure is the lack-of-fit with which the chapter began. For the beaver, the dam is a supplement to an already sufficient body; the beaver would survive, after a fashion, without it. For the under-specialised ape, the tool was not a supplement but a *condition* of survival — there was no claw to fall back on, no turn of speed, no fang. Its weakness, in Obara's blunt formulation, "became the driving force of the development of tools."[^obara-weak] This is the mismatch of the opening read now as a *motive*: where most mismatched lineages re-fit biologically, or relocate, or die, this one met a biological deficit with an artificial compensation — and because the compensation was obligatory, it was under perpetual pressure to get better.

The substrate is what made that compensation possible at all, and here the order of events matters more than intuition expects. It was not that a large brain conceived the tool. The brain came *second*: *Australopithecus*, already upright and already, in all likelihood, handling stones and sticks, had a brain no larger than an ape's. What grew the brain was the tool — "through the interaction of hand and eye the development of tools became possible," Obara writes, "and through the interaction with tools, encephalisation occurred… the tool is the core of hominisation."[^obara-brain] The recursion bootstrapped from equipment the lineage already possessed as an arboreal primate — a dexterous hand, a stereoscopic eye — and fed forward into the very organ we are tempted to credit with starting it.

It is here, at the point where the human is constituted by what it makes rather than the reverse, that this thesis meets Bernard Stiegler — and here too that it must part from him with precision. Stiegler's insight is indispensable and should be kept whole: the prosthesis, for him, "is not a mere extension of the human body; it is the constitution of this body qua 'human.'"[^stiegler-prosthesis] The human does not precede its artifice and then take it up; the two arise together. That much this chapter affirms without reservation. But here precision matters, because Stiegler is more nearly an ally than an opponent, and the one place this thesis parts from him is easily mislocated. It is tempting to read him as making exteriorisation an *exclusively human* affair — the phrase is on his page — but the phrase is Leroi-Gourhan's, and Stiegler pointedly corrects it: the process, he insists, "occurred before the human not only in ape societies but perhaps much earlier in the pre-history of animality," so that "'the human' is precisely this *hegemony*" of the epiphylogenetic — its dominance, not its origin. On the continuum of §A, then, Stiegler stands *with* this thesis, not against it — and further than first appears, for he is no denier of the body. The human, he grants, "is also animal," "a significant part of human activity is instinctual," and the noetic soul remains "unswervingly dependent" on the biological constraints that are "its in-itself"; the human is "an originary psycho-physical complex… first of all a state of the body."[^stiegler-bio] Where the thesis parts from him is subtler than a quarrel over whether biology persists, for on that they agree. It concerns the *weight and direction* the biological is given. For Stiegler, the human's qualities tend to migrate *outward* — once produced, he writes, they "may rather become those of technics" than the human's own — so that the human is, in his recurring phrase, "outside itself," its determinations increasingly lodged in its prostheses.[^stiegler-blank]

The thesis, with Obara, holds the opposite pole steady. The ape thrown upon the demanding ground did not arrive empty; it arrived with an ape's nature — hands, eyes, gut, social brain — that simply did not *suit* the new niche, and that nature did not evaporate when artifice came to compensate for it. It persists as an irreducible *interior*, the biological ヒト, an inner given that does not exteriorise and that the runaway exterior does not absorb but presses against. Read so, Stiegler's "de-fault" is not the absence of a nature but the *misfit* of one — and that shift of weight is load-bearing, because it is the friction of the non-exteriorisable interior against the accelerating exterior that the next chapters will name the squeeze. The human, on this reading, is not a void that culture fills but the *integration* of two persisting poles — of "ヒト," the biological animal, and "モノ," the made; "the human," in Obara's formula, "is the mediator that brings about the integration of *hito* and *mono*."[^obara-hito] Against a determinism that would dissolve the animal into its culture, the animal remains. Stiegler's de-fault is real, but it is the de-fault of an ecological misfit, not of a creature without qualities — and that single correction is what keeps a realist floor beneath the chapters to come.

The biology that underwrites this comes in two stages easy to mistake for rivals and which are not. The deep stage is ancient: the human is born helpless, *secondarily altricial*, finishing outside the womb a development other primates complete within it — an incompleteness that belongs to the genus *Homo* and traces to the old constraints of brain, pelvis, and maternal energy.[^portmann] The recent stage is the *sapiens*-specific intensification Obara calls self-domestication: having built an artificial environment, the human began, unawares, to breed itself to it — "by setting up artificial environmental conditions, it gradually reared and enclosed itself."[^obara-jika] The first stage is the long incompleteness that opened the door to artifice; the second is the runaway that followed once the door was open. They sit on one timeline, not in contradiction.

One temptation must be resisted here, because it is a trap. Having listed the substrate — manipulative hand, executive brain, sociality — it is tempting to say that this is *why* the human crossed the threshold. But the substrate explains only why the human *could*, not why it *did*. The evidence for it is genuine and comparative: across lineages as distant as corvids and octopuses, tool use recurs and is predicted by relative brain size, manipulative capacity, and social life.[^convergent] Yet every one of those better-equipped animals had the door open and did not walk through it. Chimpanzees, given the chance, "use social learning to acquire a skill that they failed to independently innovate" — the capacity is there, the runaway is not.[^chimp] So availability is necessary and not sufficient; it tells us the fork was open to this lineage and a handful of others, not why this one alone took it to the end. What tipped it was the *conjunction* — an obligatory pressure the comfortable substrate-bearers did not face, a fidelity of transmission they lacked, and, once the recursion began, a self-domesticating lock-in that closed the way back — together with a residue of contingency this thesis does not pretend to dissolve. The human took a rare fork; it was not fated to.[^megafauna]

And this answers, at last, the question the chapter opened with — why an artificial *food chain*, and not merely artifice in general. The lack-of-fit was, before it was anything else, **alimentary**. The under-specialised body's most pressing deficit was precisely how to eat, and how not to be eaten, in a web for which it was equipped neither to hunt nor to flee: out-competed for prey, and itself prey. So the obligate artifice was food-directed from its first flake — tool and fire and cooperation turned upon what the body could not reach unaided: the large game no claw could fell, the tuber no hand could unearth, the carcass no lone forager could hold, the plant no gut could digest until it was cooked. Each such reaching loosened the lineage's dependence on the wild web by a degree; and the recursive compounding of these food-directed artifices simply *is* the slow building of a parallel, humanly-governed supply — through provisioning and the sharing it demands, then cultivation, then domestication — until the lineage feeds itself wholly within an artificial ecosystem, and the wild web is left behind. The artificial food chain is therefore not one outcome of the runaway among many; it is its **native object**, because the founding misfit was a hunger. The human came to keep a food chain of its own because it could not feed itself in the one it was born into.

What it found down that fork, having taken it, was a thing without a proper name. We have been describing it — recursive, web-leaving, food-building, born of obligation — without yet calling it anything. It is time to.

## §C — Naming the thing: artificiality, not technology, not technics

The thing we have been describing is most naturally called *technology*, and that is the first name to refuse. "Technology" carries, ineradicably, the sense of means to an end — a tool *for* something, an instrument of use — and the moment that sense is in force, the category fractures. Language is not a means to an end in the way a hammer is; neither is a rite, a law, a god. To call them technologies is either to strain the word past recognition or to smuggle an instrumental picture into domains it distorts.[^why-not-tech] What this chapter is after is broader and more basic than the useful: it is the *made* as such, whatever its end, or lack of one.

The more searching rival is *technics* — the word Bernard Stiegler and, after his fashion, Yuk Hui prefer — and refusing it requires a concession first. "Artificiality" is, etymologically, no purer than "technics": *artificiālis* descends from *artificium*, which is *ars* — the Latin rendering of the Greek *technē* — joined to *facere*, to make. The word is a *technē*-word at the root, and any claim that it escapes the Greek inheritance by etymology would be false. But the inheritance is divided, and the operative half is the second. *Artificium* is built on *facere*: its centre of gravity is the *factum*, the made, not the *ars*, the skill. This is why the living antonym of "artificial" is *natural* and not *unskilled* — the word tracks the line between the made and the given, which is precisely the line this thesis needs, and not the narrower line between craft and clumsiness that "technics" tracks.[^ars-facere] And the warrant for reading it so is not the thesis's alone: Stiegler himself, at the close of his account of the human's prosthetic origin, writes that *technē* "is prosthetic; that is, it is entirely artifice."[^stiegler-artifice] The noun is his too; he merely declines to make it the name of the genus. This thesis makes it so.

Three departures follow, and they are why the choice is not merely verbal. Where "technics" names the thing by the *faculty* that produces it, "artificiality" names it by the *status* of the produced — the made as a kind of being, not a kind of doing — which is what allows law and language and rite to fall under it without strain. Where Stiegler's account runs entirely through the Greek inheritance, this thesis cross-checks the made against a second civilisation's word for it: the Xunzi's *wei*, 偽, glossed by its commentator Yang Liang as *renwei*, 人為 — "that which is done by the human."[^wei] The Chinese term lands, like *facere*, on the *made-by-human* rather than on craft-skill, and without the Greek's metaphysical freight — a convergence that gives the concept a reach Stiegler's word does not seek. And where technics, for Stiegler, is a condition the human is *born into*, this thesis follows artificiality forward to a terminus he does not narrate, where the made ceases to depend on its maker — a thread the final sections take up.

With that, the chapter's unit is fixed. *Artificiality*: the made as against the given, the *factum* as against the *natum*, of which tools, institutions, and meanings are alike species. It is the analytical unit on which the rest of the thesis turns. The remainder of this chapter asks what it is made of, and where it is going.

## §D — The typology and irreducibility of artificiality

### The two aspects: material and virtual

Material artificiality is the easy half to picture — from stone tools to AI-powered "smart cities." But artificiality is not always manifested materially, and its nonmaterial forms are not a lesser appendix to the material ones; they are indispensable to them, and dialectically bound up with them. As Harari reminds us, artificially created nonmaterial things — gods, money, laws — have the power to direct our thoughts and behaviours and even to elicit emotions or biological reflexes.[^b1] Without such nonmaterial coordinating principles a world is impossible: material things remain mere aggregates, never integrated into functioning systems.

[^b1]: [VERIFY: Harari 2015; cf. DOCX [P0117]. Resolve citekey.]

I call this causally efficacious nonmaterial aspect of artificiality *virtuality*.[^b2] The phrasing is deliberate: virtuality is an *aspect* of artificial things — their nonmaterial-and-causally-efficacious side — not a separate ontological category set alongside artificiality. A coin has a material aspect (the metal) and a nonmaterial aspect (its being-money); the virtuality is the latter. Every virtuality is an artificiality; not every artificiality is paradigmatically virtual — a wall compels by mass, not by meaning.

[^b2]: [VERIFY: locked definition, DOCX [P0118]: "I call this causally efficacious nonmaterial aspect of artificiality virtuality." KEEP VERBATIM.]

Three popular conceptions of "virtual" are declined, each for a precise reason, and each declining keeps something. The first is the simulationist view, which sees the virtual as a not-quite-real replica — Peirce's virtual *x* as "something, not an *x*, which has the efficiency (*virtus*) of an *x*."[^b3] The thesis keeps the *virtus* — the power — and rejects the "not an *x*," because many virtual things have no original to imitate (Baudrillard).[^b4] The second is Chalmers's "virtual = computer-based," advanced to defend the realness of digital objects.[^b5] The thesis adopts his substrate-neutrality and simulation-realism but parts from him on the unit: the thesis's unit is the *trans-historical layer of human-constructed nonmaterial structure* — stories, gods, ceremonies, laws, theories, models, algorithms — of which the computer-based is only a temporal medium. The third, the positive claim, is that historical continuum itself: humans have always dealt with the virtual, ever since they lived by the stories they collectively invented (Durante).[^b6] Money is the cleanest case — causally efficacious, intersubjectively binding, indifferent to its substrate, older than the computer by millennia: as Brey notes, while "rocks and trees" can only be *simulated*, "money can literally exist" in a digital environment.[^b7] Shells, grain, metal, paper, or digits are merely the temporal mediums through which money's virtuality is operationalized.

The claim that nonmaterial artificiality is genuinely causal finds independent, footnote-weight support in Searle's social ontology. He distinguishes *brute facts*, which obtain independently of human opinion (in this thesis's vocabulary, the universe), from *institutional facts*, which obtain only through collectively accepted *status functions* of the form "*X counts as Y in context C*," carrying *deontic powers* — obligations and entitlements with real normative force.[^b8] This furnishes the mechanism by which money's virtuality binds: money is constituted by, and exhausted in, collective acceptance. But Searle is too anthropocentric for the thesis: by gating deontic powers behind human language he cannot recognize the proto-virtual phenomena below the human line.[^b9] Every Searlean institutional fact is a virtuality (a paradigm one); not every virtuality is a Searlean institutional fact — the gorilla's chest-beating display does real causal work (deference, the avoidance of costly combat) through a nonmaterial principle given by what the display *means*, not by the force of the beat.[^b10] Virtuality is not what computation added to the world; it is what the world has run on since long before there were humans to call it virtual.

### Irreducible to the universe — artificiality has laws of its own

These two aspects share one decisive property: whichever it occupies, artificiality is dependent on the materiality and laws of the universe, yet *not reducible* to them. It has laws of its own. A screwdriver simulated in a digital environment is use-*less* — not from a limit of the environment, but because the conditions for its functioning are absent: no fasteners, no gravity, no tired legs wanting a chair. "Tightening up" there requires an algorithm, not a screwing object.[^c1] So too the "physically fittest" person: best odds in the wilderness, which is a purely biological environment, but in a *world* — an artificiality field — possibly a *higher* mortality (assigned to hunting, war, heavy labour), while a physically unfit person uses charisma or wit to concentrate power.[^c2] The fitness function differs because the laws differ.

[^c1]: [VERIFY: DOCX [P0125].]
[^c2]: [VERIFY: DOCX [P0126].]

*Why* are the laws not reducible? First, in the author's own Polanyian voice: an artificial environment is a special kind of *boundary condition* — a set of conditions and principles at a higher ontological level restricts the behaviour of lower-level things, harnessing them into specific functions; and while the higher level depends on the lower for its existence, it is neither determined nor explained by it.[^c3] Three formal moves make this explicit. *Multiple realizability*: the same law — "a posted message cannot exceed 280 characters" — is implementable in indefinitely many physical ways, so is identical to none. *The semantic-normative gap*: artificial-environment laws distinguish correct from incorrect, permitted from prohibited; an electron cannot "violate" a physical law, a user can violate terms of service — and the permitted/prohibited distinction is not derivable from any physical description. *Downward causation*: a corporation's legal structure demonstrably causes physical outcomes — factories built, forests cleared — by organizing agents who exist at both levels at once. The irreducibility is therefore structural, not merely epistemological: universe-laws constrain artificial environments only at the substrate boundary (servers need power, bodies need food, institutions need humans), not at the level of semantic operation.

### The two registers: conventional and structural

The laws governing artificial environments fall into two registers. The first is the **conventional register** (*nomos*-like): the artificiality compels because, and to the extent that, humans continue to enact it — a statute holds because courts act, a norm because the community sanctions, a commandment because the faithful enforce; when the practice ceases, the artificiality ceases to operate. The second is the **structural register**, glossed not as bare *phusis* but as *phusis-in-operation* — built, yet operating as though given. Here the artificiality compels by the structure of the situation itself — architecture, affordance, operational dynamics — rather than by agreement: a locked door stays locked with no enforcer watching; an algorithm executes regardless of belief; the violation is not deterred but *foreclosed as an action-possibility*. The hyphen is crucial: this is not *phusis*, for nothing here is self-arising — every structural law was engineered. It is *phusis-in-operation*: humanly made in origin, given-like in operation. That is why the reserved Ch V–VI provocation — "*technē* becoming *phusis*" — names the metaverse-stage *migration* of artificiality from the conventional register toward the structural one: not a literal acquisition of nature's internal principle, but the made coming to operate with the given's authority.[^c4]

### The grid: irreducibility across depth × register × aspect

The registers (conventional / structural) crossed with the two aspects (material / virtual) and with Chapter I's two ontological depths — **enduring conditions/principles** (道, the CR "real") and **structures/dynamics** (理, the CR "actual") — give the full map of *how* the world is irreducible to the universe. Two tables, one per depth. The result: artificiality is irreducible everywhere it occurs, but the *ground* of irreducibility grades from thick to thin, and the gradient is itself diagnostic.

**Table D-1 — Structures/dynamics (理 · the "actual"): how specific artificialities are irreducible**

| Configuration | What it is | Irreducible to the universe by… |
|---|---|---|
| actual · conventional · material | a coin, banknote, passport, title-deed, boundary-stone | its **status**: the matter is universe-stuff, but "money / citizenship / boundary" exists only by collective acceptance, which the mindless universe wholly lacks. Substrate reducible; status irreducible. |
| actual · conventional · virtual | a statute, contract, debt, price, a word's sense, a move's legality | **doubly**: a made norm/meaning (belief-dependent) with no material substrate of its own. The universe has neither the meaning nor any matter that *is* it. |
| actual · structural · material | a wall, locked door, dam, road, cell | **made form only (thinnest)**: matter + physics = universe; once built, the foreclosure runs on universe-physics (a wall blocks as a cliff does). Irreducible solely in its intentional arrangement. ⇒ the home of `tekhne→phusis`. |
| actual · structural · virtual | a paywall, DRM lock, ban-mechanism, algorithmic gate (code-as-law) | **made pattern + authored foreclosure**: a nonmaterial rule (no universe-substrate) that *hard*-forecloses. Binds like a wall yet, being authored rule, does not naturalise. **The metaverse's signature cell.** |

**Table D-2 — Enduring conditions/principles (道 · the "real"): how enduring artificial conditions are irreducible**

| Configuration | What it is | Irreducible to the universe by… |
|---|---|---|
| real · conventional · material | — | **n/a.** An enduring convention is, *qua principle*, nonmaterial (acceptance isn't matter); its material side is only a *bearer*, a particular at the *actual* level — not an enduring principle. Dissolves into row 2 (its principle) + Table D-1 row 1 (its bearers). *(This is exactly why money is **substrate-independent** — shell, coin, paper, bit — and why an institution survives the loss of all its buildings: convention has material bearers, never a material principle.)* |
| real · conventional · virtual | money-as-such, private property, the rule of law, promising, kinship, language-as-system | **thickest + foundational**: durable made-conditions holding wholly by collective intentionality across generations. The universe has no institutions, yet these organise whole civilisations. |
| real · structural · material | the built environment, road/rail/power networks, a dammed river-system, a city's morphology (a standing régime, not one wall) | **made arrangement scaled to a durable régime**: matter/physics = universe; the longer it endures, the more it is taken as the terrain one is born into. ⇒ the strongest historical engine of `tekhne→phusis`: yesterday's artifice = today's landscape. |
| real · structural · virtual | the standing régime of code-as-law / algorithmic governmentality / the **self-completing tendency (自足完成化)** as the structural principle of the metaverse stage | **the deepest cell**: irreducible as made-pattern *and* authored-foreclosure *and* foundational; binds as hard as infrastructure yet never naturalises into physics. **The metaverse's "real" (偽道)** — the squeeze's deepest pole. |

*Reading the grid.* Irreducibility grades along two axes, both running *toward* the universe: **conventional → structural** (convention needs a mind the universe lacks; structure needs only made *form*, and the universe already forecloses by cliff and gravity) and **virtual → material** (the virtual has no universe-substrate; the material rides on one). The **thinnest** corner is *structural-material* — made form on universe-matter, running on universe-physics: the cell where artificiality most nearly *becomes* universe, most acutely when enduring (infrastructure → terrain). That is where `tekhne→phusis` is *located*, not merely invoked. The **thickest** is *conventional-virtual* — pure made-meaning, belief-dependent, no substrate. And the metaverse's distinctive cell is *structural-virtual*: the only place combining *maximal binding* (foreclosure, like the wall) with *maximal made-ness* (authored rule, no substrate, like the meaning) — which is why the metaverse can **foreclose like nature while remaining wholly artificial**, and why its squeeze is uniquely tight: a constraint as hard as a cliff but as made as a meaning, which never even acquires the alibi of looking natural.

### Rule versus law

The grid also settles the "rule versus law" question. Reserve **law** for structural artificialities whose violation is structurally prevented (the locked door, code-as-law); reserve **rule** or **norm** for conventional artificialities whose violation is externally punished but not structurally prevented (etiquette, customary law). Under this convention the metaverse genuinely has *laws of its own* — a structural homology with the universe's laws, differing only in that the universe's are naturally given and the metaverse's are humanly engineered. Juridical statutes sit awkwardly between the two, which is a real fact about them: conventional law has always *aspired* to structural-compelling power and never fully achieved it. The metaverse's structural register is the successful version of that ancient aspiration. The registers name a *dominant* tendency, not an absolute partition — conventional artificiality has structural margins (coercion rests on the state's monopoly on force), structural artificiality conventional ones (a platform's policy is, in principle, revisable) — and the later squeeze argument is precisely about the *progressive shrinking of the conventional margin* as a system reaches operational maturity.[^c5]

## §E — The autonomy of artificiality and the seed of the squeeze

Two properties of that irreducible artificiality remain to be drawn out, and together they carry the chapter into the rest of the thesis. The first is **autonomy**: having laws of its own (§D), the made acquires a momentum of its own. A tool, once made, has a logic of its own; tools beget tools, and the world of tools, once underway, develops by its own momentum, soliciting the next tool and the next along a direction no single maker chooses. The second follows from the first: this self-amplifying made-world turns back upon its maker and remakes him. The human that built the artificial environment began, within it, to be bred by it — and so opened a gap between the biological animal it remains and the artificial world it now inhabits. Obara names that gap a **ズレ**, a dis-synchrony: "the change in the systems and the 'mono' produced, and the change in the biological human, differ in speed and in kind, and a *zure* arises."[^obara-zure] That gap is the first appearance, at the scale of the single human, of the structure this thesis calls the squeeze.

Autonomy has an edge, and it cuts both ways. Because the made runs ahead of its maker, artificiality is **double-edged** — and this is no modern discovery: *artificialities at sufficient scale or depth tend to generate the very conditions of the harm they were instituted to relieve, absent active counter-work.* Illich gives the inversion its historical shape. Every industrial institution crosses two watersheds: at the first it achieves genuine effectiveness; at the second the effectiveness inverts, and the institution enacts a *radical monopoly* — not market dominance, but the elimination of the conditions under which alternatives could exist. The car remakes a city into its own image and so curtails the very right to walk; that curtailment *is* the radical monopoly.[^d1] Stiegler names the same ambivalence the *pharmacological* condition of all artificiality: the Greek *pharmakon* is at once remedy and poison, and every exteriorised artificiality, once it has become independent of its user, can return as toxic — writing supplements memory and atrophies it; the platforms that extend sociality also industrialise it.[^d2] The pharmakon is *undetermined* until a regime of *care* settles its disposition: which edge dominates is decided by counter-work, not by the artificiality itself. Autonomy is precisely what makes that counter-work necessary, and never automatic.

It is worth seeing the whole arc at once, because it bends back upon itself. The human *began* squeezed — caught, as an under-fitted body, between its inherited equipment and an unaccommodating world. Artificiality was its rare answer to that first squeeze, and a successful one: the artificial food chain released it from the predation and scarcity that press on everything still inside the web. But the answer did not end the squeezing; it *relocated* it. The runaway recursion that resolved the first mismatch generated a second — between the biological human and its own accelerating artifice — and this second squeeze, the chapters ahead will argue, is the worse of the two. The creature that built its way out of the first squeeze has been building its way into the last.

The reason the second is worse can be stated in the vocabulary Chapter I established. The first squeeze is a clash of *configurations* — an inherited structure of the body against the structure of a changed world — and beneath both lies a shared order of principles, the same physics and the same selection, within which the clash can in principle be re-fitted: adapt, move, rebuild. That is why mismatches of the first kind are, in the end, resolvable. The second is not a clash of configurations beneath a common order; it is a clash *of the orders themselves* — the enduring principles of the given world against the enduring principle of a made one that has begun to set its own terms — and between two such orders there is no deeper court of appeal in which to re-fit them. A squeeze relocated from the layer of *structures* to the layer of *principles* is a squeeze with nothing underneath it: the made becoming what the thesis will provocatively call a second nature — a *technē* that has begun to stand as a *phusis*.[^provocation] And it is here that the double-edgedness turns fatal. The structural register that forecloses action-possibility forecloses, with it, the very counter-work by which the pharmakon might be kept a remedy — the made now mutating at platform speed while care still moves at biographical and intergenerational speed, so that the inversion becomes *structural* rather than contingent. The metaverse stage is thus the *limit case* of a tendency-toward-inversion that pre-metaverse artificiality already exhibited; the convergence of pharmakon, Illich, the *Dialectic of Enlightenment*, Obara's self-petification, and 君子不器 — developed later in the thesis — reads it as exactly that.[^d3] The chapter need establish only this much: that artificiality, in every register, cuts both ways; that remedy and poison are inseparable; and that which of them dominates is settled by counter-work, not by the made itself. That is the threshold the metaverse will cross, and the threshold the rest of this thesis is written to meet.
