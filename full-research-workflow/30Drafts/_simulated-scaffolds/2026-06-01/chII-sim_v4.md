## Opener — Artificiality as the umbrella

Chapter I argued that a world is an adaptational operating system: a bounded, humanly-made sub-environment, nested within the universe, through which a population organizes its collective survival. The closing claim of that chapter was that artificiality — not culture — is the analytical unit that makes a human human and a world a world.[^op1] This chapter asks what such a world is *made of*, and answers with a single category. By artificiality I mean the whole class of humanly-produced organizing principles, material and nonmaterial alike, by which a world is constituted, transmitted, and adapted: not only tools and built environments but institutions, laws, symbols, language, customs, religion, ideology, and music. Artificiality is the umbrella under which the rest of the thesis proceeds.

[^op1]: [VERIFY: DOCX [P0109] — "Artificiality — my analytical unit that replaces culture — is what makes human human, and a world world."]

The chapter establishes that umbrella in a particular order, and it is worth previewing the order because it is the order of the argument's *grounding*. I begin not with definitions but with origins: §1 shows where artificiality *comes from* — that the human is a biologically unfinished animal which must build the conditions of its own survival, and that the capacity to do so is continuous with, not a clean break from, the rest of animal life. Only then, in §2, do I argue why *artificiality* — rather than the more familiar *technology* — is the right name for the stuff so produced, and locate that name among the ways different civilizations have carved the made off from the grown. With the unit grounded and named, the remaining sections develop what artificiality *does* in forming a world: its impetus (exosomatization, §IIA), its nonmaterial dimension (virtuality, §IIB), and the irreducibility of artificial-environment laws to the universe's (§IIC). The chapter closes on the double-edgedness that runs through all three. Grounds first, then name, then function: that is the route.

## §1 — The evolutionary origins of artificiality

### The radically immature animal

Before artificiality can be shown to form a world, it is worth asking why a creature should need to form one at all. The answer is biological, and it is not flattering. The human is born unfinished. Among mammals the newborn arrives along a spectrum that runs from the *altricial* — born blind, naked, and helpless, like a kitten or a songbird hatchling — to the *precocial* — born furred, sighted, and mobile, like a foal that stands within the hour; and these are not two boxes but a graded series, "a continuum, without distinct gaps between them."[^e1] The human sits at a peculiar place on that series. By the measure of brain and motor development at birth we should, like other large primates, be precocial; instead we are delivered in a state of helplessness more typical of the altricial nest. The zoologist Adolf Portmann named this paradox *secondary altriciality*: the human is born, in effect, about a year too early, requiring an "extra-uterine year" in which the development that other primates complete in the womb is instead completed outside it, in arms, in speech, in the company of others.[^e2] We are the animal whose gestation finishes in the world rather than in the body.

This is the biological fact on which the whole apparatus of artificiality rests. A creature born into an instinct-complete niche needs no world: its environment is given to it from within, in the form of an inherited behavioural repertoire fitted in advance to the conditions of its life. The secondarily altricial human inherits no such fit. Lacking a niche given by instinct, it must have one *made* — and made not once but continuously, from the outside, by the very generation that bore it. What we will go on to call artificiality is, in the first instance, the name for whatever does this making. It is not a tool that an already-complete creature picks up; it is what completes the creature. The human does not *have* a world the way a mole has its burrow, ready-dug by its body; lacking a given niche, it must build one, because it has no other way to be at all.

The biological literature already has two names for what this necessity demands. The biophysicist Alfred Lotka observed that the human species evolves along "an entirely new path": where other organisms adapt by the "slow adaptation of anatomical structure and physiological function in successive generations by selective survival," the human achieves adaptation "by the incomparably more rapid development of 'artificial' aids to our native receptor-effector apparatus, in a process that might be termed *exosomatic evolution*" — adaptation *outside* the body, in tools, records, and institutions, rather than within it.[^e3] Bernard Stiegler gives the same fact a philosophical name. Reading the myth of Epimetheus, who distributes every natural power to the animals but forgets the human and leaves it "naked... without fur and means of defense," Stiegler argues that "there will have been nothing at the origin but the fault, a fault that is nothing but the de-fault of origin."[^e4] Where the animals are "positively endowed with qualities," "humanity is without qualities, without predestination: it must invent, realize, produce qualities."[^e5] Exosomatic evolution and the default-of-origin are a biophysicist's term and a philosopher's term for one and the same condition — that the human is the animal which must be completed from outside itself. Artificiality is therefore constitutive, not supplementary: there is no pre-artificial human waiting to be equipped.

### A spectrum, not a split

It would be easy to convert this into a story of human exceptionalism — the one animal that broke from nature into artifice. That is not the thesis's commitment, and the biology does not support it. Immaturity at birth is graded, not binary; the human is an *extreme* on the altricial–precocial continuum, not an exception to it. And the same gradedness holds for the response to immaturity. The capacity to compensate for what the body does not supply — to extend a function outward, to construct the conditions of one's own survival — does not begin with us. It is distributed, in lesser degrees and narrower forms, across the animal world. The thesis therefore approaches artificiality as Chapter I approached world-building: as "one of the many coexisting evolutionary strategies in a continuum," not "a disconnected split from other animals."[^e6] What the human does at full scale, other animals do in proto-form. The argument of this section is that artificiality has *evolutionary precursors*, and that the difference between the precursor and the full case is one of degree along identifiable dimensions, not a metaphysical break.

### Proto-artificiality: the material side

On its material side, the proto-form of artificiality has already been established in Chapter I under the name *niche construction*: organisms that do not merely occupy an environment but build it — the beaver that engineers a river's hydrology, the ant colony that tunnels a micro-climate, the coral that secretes the reef — and so become, in Lewontin's phrase quoted there, "both the object of natural selection and the creator of the conditions of that selection."[^e7] Animals make material aids and material environments; the dam, the burrow, and the nest are the evolutionary kin of the wall and the city. This much the thesis takes as settled and does not re-argue here. What remains, and what matters for an account of artificiality as such, is the *nonmaterial* side — for it is the nonmaterial that the philosophy-of-technology tradition, fixated on tools, most often misses.

[^e7]: [VERIFY: cross-reference Ch1 §B "Niche Construction," DOCX [P0060]–[P0070]; Odling-Smee, Laland & Feldman 2003, *Niche Construction: The Neglected Process in Evolution* (Zotero TJ7XAN6V); Levins & Lewontin 1985 quoted at [P0064]: organisms "influence [their] own evolution, by being both the object of natural selection and the creator of the conditions of that selection." ### Proto-artificiality: the nonmaterial side, or proto-virtuality

Animals coordinate not only by force but by *meaning*. The ethologist Obara Hideo's studies of animal ritual furnish the clearest cases. When two gorilla groups meet, the leading males drum their chests, and the display "fulfils the function of avoiding a fight": it does real causal work — it produces deference and averts costly combat — yet it works not by the physical force of the beat but by what the beat *signifies* within the group.[^e8] The courtship dance of the crane and the kneeling, horn-flaunting display of the ibex follow the same logic in the reproductive register. A nonmaterial principle — a posture, a sound, a sequence — compels behaviour through its meaning rather than its mass. This is the evolutionary floor of what §IIB will call *virtuality*: the causally efficacious nonmaterial aspect of artificiality. Long before there were humans to live by collectively invented stories, the nonmaterial-and-causal had already organized animal life. The fuller treatment — including the worked gorilla case and the reason these animal displays are *proto*-virtual rather than virtual outright — belongs to §IIB; here they establish only that the nonmaterial dimension of artificiality, too, has a biological origin.[^e9]

The reach of this nonmaterial coordination is wider than ritual. Ethology has documented, across the animal kingdom, traditions transmitted not genetically but socially — "cultural differences (often known as 'traditions' in ethology) are well established phenomena in the animal kingdom and are maintained through a variety of social transmission mechanisms," from the dialects of songbirds to the sweet-potato washing of Japanese macaques.[^e10] These are nonmaterial coordinating principles — learned, shared, inherited outside the genome. They are not yet a world; but they are the raw material from which a world is built.

### Recursivity as the (graded) criterion

If the proto-form and the full form lie on a continuum, the continuum needs a measure. What distinguishes proto-artificiality from artificiality proper is *recursivity*: the degree to which the exteriorized supports feed back upon, accumulate within, and reorganize the very life that produced them. Stiegler names this loop *epiphylogenesis* — "the conservation, accumulation, and sedimentation of successive epigeneses" in the inherited technical object, such that the artificial support "is as much the result as the condition of its production."[^e11] The made thing carries forward the experience that made it, and the next generation is born into and formed by that deposit; in Stiegler's compressed formula, "the *what* invents the *who* just as much as it is invented by it."[^e12] Obara describes the same self-referential loop in biological terms as *self-domestication*: the artificial environment humans build "imposes selective pressure so that one survives within that society," so that "human selection has changed humans themselves" — a feedback he calls *auto-artificial selection* (自己人為淘汰).[^e13] The exteriorized world acts back on its makers and remakes them. Where the loop is rich, cumulative, and self-reorganizing, we have artificiality; where it is thin and non-cumulative, proto-artificiality. That is the criterion.

But — and the thesis must hold this line carefully — recursivity is itself graded, and the boundary it draws is not clean. The same ethology that grounds the proto-forms also supplies evidence that the rudiments of recursivity reach below the human line. Animals manipulate and modify objects: "tool use is quite widespread in the animal kingdom," and "the budding forms [萌芽形態] of the various traits and abilities of humans can be seen within animals," Obara insists.[^e14] Chimpanzee tool-use is not uniform but varies by community as a transmitted *tradition*, the combined repertoire of each group being "itself highly distinctive, a phenomenon characteristic of human cultures... but previously unrecognised in non-human species."[^e15] The reach of such animal culture, on recent surveys, is "burgeoning" — found to "permeate the lives of a diversity of animals, including all major classes of vertebrates," resting on "a suite of social learning processes."[^e16] And recursion in the strict structural sense — long held to be the signature of the human language faculty — has been demonstrated in birdsong: European starlings "accurately recognize acoustic patterns defined by a recursive, self-embedding, context-free grammar," so that "the capacity to classify sequences from recursive, centre-embedded grammars is not uniquely human."[^e17] None of this collapses the proto/full distinction — the *degree* of cumulative self-reorganization in human artificiality is incomparably greater, and some researchers reserve "cumulative culture" for humans altogether.[^e18] But it does mean that recursivity, like immaturity-at-birth, reads as a graded dimension along which the human is an extreme, not a faculty the human alone possesses. The continuity survives at the very criterion that was meant to mark the difference.

### The anthropocentric-bias caveat

One qualification governs this entire section, and it must be stated in the first person, because it concerns the limits of the person stating it. As a human author writing about animals, I cannot fully escape anthropocentric bias; the very categories I reach for — ritual, meaning, recursivity — are drawn from the human case and projected outward. The distinction I have drawn between proto-artificiality and artificiality is an attempt to honour the *continuity* between animal and human life without smuggling in a verdict I have no warrant to give: a verdict on animal *subjectivity*. Whether there is "something that it is like to be" a gorilla performing its display, or a starling parsing its grammar, is a question this thesis deliberately does not answer. Thomas Nagel's formulation marks the boundary precisely — "an organism has conscious mental states if and only if there is something that it is like to be that organism" — and his conclusion marks why I stay on my side of it: even with complete knowledge of the animal's physiology, "it would still be impossible to know what it is like for a bat to be a bat."[^e19] The proto/full distinction is a heuristic for tracing continuity in *function* — in what artificiality and its precursors *do* — not a claim about animal experience.

This restraint cuts in both directions, and Frans de Waal named the opposite error. Against anthropomorphism — projecting human minds onto animals — he set *anthropodenial*: "a blindness to the human-like characteristics of other animals, or the animal-like characteristics of ourselves."[^e20] To deny continuity wholesale, to insist on an unbridgeable break between the human and everything below it, would be its own bias — the very human exceptionalism the spectrum claim rejects. The thesis steers between the two: it affirms continuity of function while bracketing the question of inner experience. Jakob von Uexküll's lesson stands behind this caution — that each creature inhabits its own *Umwelt*, a perceptual-meaningful world built to the measure of its own body, neither reducible to ours nor transparently knowable from outside.[^e21] We can trace the evolutionary lineage of artificiality through the animal world without claiming to occupy any animal's point of view. That the human is the animal squeezed hardest by its own constructions is the thesis of what follows; that it is continuous with the animals it is squeezed apart from is the commitment established here.

## §2 — Why *artificiality*, not *technology*

With artificiality grounded in the animal's biological default, the chapter can settle the name of the stuff. The argument runs on a single analytical unit — *artificiality* — and a reader coming from the philosophy of technology, or simply from common usage, will reasonably ask why the more familiar word *technology* is not doing this work instead. The metaverse is, after all, routinely described as a technology; the whole apparatus of tools, platforms, and code that gives the term its urgency is, on any ordinary reckoning, technological. Why not, then, make *technology* the unit and have done with it? The answer is not a quibble over labels. The word one chooses here decides what kind of claim the rest of the thesis is taken to be making, and *technology* — in the sense the word has hardened into — would commit the argument to a position it does not hold and does not need.

### Why not *technology*

In its dominant modern sense, *technology* names a means to an end. The standard reference works state this plainly. The *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy* records, as the widespread view, that "technology is a neutral instrument that can be put to good or bad use by its users," and notes the further reduction by which "technology is 'merely' applied science" — the device, the instrument, the application of theoretical knowledge to practical purposes.[^t1] This is the instrumental-and-anthropological picture: technology is a human activity, and it is a means to an end.[^t2] Whatever its limitations as a complete account — and the same literature spends most of its effort contesting it — this is the gravitational centre of the word. Say "technology" without qualification and the hearer supplies a tool, a machine, a contrivance built to accomplish something.

[^t1]: [VERIFY: confirmed verbatim | SEP, "Philosophy of Technology" (plato.stanford.edu/entries/technology/), §3.1 — "technology is a neutral instrument that can be put to good or bad use by its users"; §2.2 — the view that "technology is 'merely' applied science." Fetched 2026-06-02. Cite as Franssen, Lokhorst & van de Poel (SEP) if a formal citation is wanted, or paraphrase to "the standard reference works."]

[^t2]: [VERIFY: Heidegger's "instrumental and anthropological definition of technology" — *The Question Concerning Technology* (Heidegger 1977, pp.4–5): technology as "a means to an end" and "a human activity." Resolve exact page against print/Zotero before final; reconcile to ONE locator (the §IIA-era drafts elsewhere cite "Heidegger 1977 p.13").]

That gravitational pull is exactly the problem. If *technology* is the analytical unit, then everything the thesis wants to gather under one head — not only tools and platforms but institutions, laws, language, money, *and* science, ethics, and religion — must be called a technology. And that move imports the means-to-end presumption wholesale. To call religion a technology is to presume that religion is a human-made instrument for achieving some end; but the revelatory traditions present themselves precisely as *revealers* or *paths of truth*, not as devices contrived by their adherents, and the claim "religion is technology" reads to them — correctly — as a hostile reduction of revelation to engineering.[^t3] To call science a technology inverts the commonsensical ordering, on which science is the truth-seeking enterprise and technology its applied downstream; the scientific realist hears "science is technology" as a collapse of truth-claims into function-claims. Neither move is unthinkable — there is a respectable lineage, from Foucault's technologies of the self to Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics, on which such inversions are principled.[^t4] But each is *contestable*, each is agreeable only to some, and each would oblige the thesis to fight, on the side, a philosophical battle over the created-versus-revealed status of cultural forms that is no part of its argument. The philosophical baggage of that battle is heavy, and the thesis gains nothing by carrying it.

The decisive point is that even the tradition most committed to a broad, non-instrumental reading of *technology* concedes that the narrow instrumental sense is the word's centre of gravity. It shows that a broader reading is available; but it also shows that one must *fight the word* to reach it, because the word's default is the narrow instrumental one. A term that has to be wrested from its own dominant meaning every time it is used is a poor candidate for the load-bearing unit of an argument whose central worry is precisely that the metaverse will be misread as "just more advanced technology." ### Why *artificiality*

The umbrella the thesis adopts instead is *artificiality*: the whole class of humanly-produced organizing principles, material and nonmaterial alike — tools, built environments, institutions, laws, language, money, art, and, with no change of kind, science, ethics, and religion. The crucial difference from *technology* is what *artificiality* does *not* say. It names a thing's *origin and mode of being* — that it is humanly made, humanly instituted, humanly maintained — and it says nothing whatever about whether that thing is an instrument for some further end. To call religion an artificiality is to say only that religion is humanly instituted and transmitted, not that it is a mere device; to call science an artificiality is to say only that the institutions, theories, notations, and norms of science are humanly produced, not that science is reducible to applied function. *Artificiality* picks out the made, where *technology* picks out the *useful-made-for-an-end*. The means-to-end presumption that made "religion is technology" and "science is technology" intolerable simply never arises, because it was never part of the term. Science, ethics, and religion are not *technologies*; they are *kinds*, or *realms*, *within* artificiality — the same humanly-instituted ordering at work in law, in money, in language.[^o2]

That this broad unit is not an idiosyncratic coinage is warranted by a convergence of two traditions arriving at it from opposite directions. Stiegler's recovered *technē* is the broad one: technics "designates… first and foremost all the domains of skill," so that politeness, cooking, rhetoric, poetry, medicine, the architecture of the *polis*, and "something of poetry and rhetoric in all language" all fall within it — and "all human action has something to do with tekhne, is after a fashion tekhne."[^a1] From an entirely different starting point, Obara Hideo's 「モノ」 — the katakana form deliberately distinguished from the hiragana 「もの」 of concrete things — names everything humans have made, abstract language included, with religion, ideology, and music explicitly inside the category.[^a2] A late-modern French philosophy of technics and a contemporary Japanese ethological anthropology reach the same unit from opposite ends; that independent convergence is what licenses treating artificiality as a found category rather than a coined one — and what the present argument does is take that found category under a name, *artificiality*, that carries the breadth without dragging in the instrumental baggage that "technology," even in Stiegler's hands, must constantly be defended against.

### A third tradition, and the cross-linguistic name for the made

A third tradition, older than either, supplies the name in almost its exact classical form. In the *Xunzi* (荀子), the term 偽 picks out precisely the humanly-made: not nature's spontaneous endowment but what is deliberately learned, worked at, and accomplished by human beings. "What is good in people," Xunzi writes, "is their 偽" — "人之性惡，其善者偽也" — and he fixes the term by its mode of acquisition: "what can be learned and accomplished by effort, and lies in the person — this is called 偽" (可學而能、可事而成之在人者謂之偽).[^x1] The Tang commentator Yang Liang (楊倞) glosses the character by decomposing it: 偽 is a compound graph, 人 ("human") set beside 為 ("doing/making") — 「『偽』字『人』傍『為』，亦會意字也」 — so that 偽 just *is* 人為, the human-done.[^x2] This is about the most exact classical counterpart to *artificiality* the record offers, and it is in one respect more serviceable than the Western word: where English *artificial* descends from Latin *ars* ("skill, art") + *facere* ("to make") and has since drifted into the pejorative "fictitious… not genuine" and "insincere,"[^x3] Xunzi's technical 偽 names the made without that taint of the false. Two cautions govern the borrowing. The name is taken; the doctrine is not. Xunzi sets 偽 against 性 (inborn nature) as one term of a dualism — "the nature is the original raw material; 偽 is its refinement and adornment" (性者，本始材朴也；偽者，文理隆盛也)[^x4] — and that nature/artifice split is exactly the kind of divide Chapter I has already dismantled. The thesis adopts the neutral *name* 偽=人為 for the humanly-made; it does **not** re-import the 性/偽 opposition, and the reader should not read it back in.[^x5]

### One civilization's two categories of the artificial

The cross-cultural angle deserves to be developed, not just noted, because it is what keeps the umbrella from inheriting any one tradition's partition as if it were the only one. There is an old vocabulary that sharpens what artificiality is, and the chapter can enter through it — provided one discipline is observed. The ancient Greeks divided what there is, at one fundamental seam, between the grown and the made. On the grown side stood *phusis* — from the root \*bʰeh₂u-, "to grow, arise, be," the same root that gives φύω, "to grow, bring forth, be born"; *phusis* is the self-arising, that whose principle of motion lies within itself.[^g1] On the made side stood two terms, not one. The first was *technē* — from the root \*tek-, "to build," the very root of τέκτων, "the carpenter"; *technē* is craftsmanship, the systematic method of making, whose source of motion lies not in the thing made but in the maker.[^g2] The second was *nomos* — built on νέμω, "to distribute, to allot" — that which is in habitual practice, custom and usage, hardening into statute and ordinance made by authority.[^g3] Custom-becoming-law and craft-becoming-artefact: two categories, both on the made side of the seam.

[^g2]: [VERIFY: confirmed verbatim | Beekes 2010 s.v. τέχνη — "<IE *tek- 'produce', *te-tk- 'build, timber'>"; root of τέκτων; LSJ τέχνη sense III "a set of rules, system, method of making"; Cassin 2014 "ART" *teks- "construct"; CONSOLIDATED §A.3.]

Here is the move, and here is the discipline. The reframe the chapter makes is that *nomos* and *technē* are the two major categories the ancient Greeks treated as **artificial** — as the made, the humanly-instituted — set together against the grown. This is one civilization's metaphysics of the artificial, and the point of saying "one civilization's" is that other civilizations carved the made differently. We have already met one such alternative in 偽=人為, the *Xunzi*'s general name for the humanly-done; and ancient China articulated the made *as instrument* through a second, distinct term — 器 (*qi*), the vessel-or-tool, within a cosmotechnical order in which the made is subordinate to and resonant with the cosmic-moral way (*dao*) rather than standing over against a separate "nature." Yuk Hui states the asymmetry flatly: the Chinese have no equivalents of the categories the Greeks called *technē* and *physis*.[^g4] The grouping is therefore not a universal grammar of "the artificial" but a description of how *the Greeks* drew the line — which is precisely why the thesis can treat artificiality as its own analytical unit without inheriting the Greek partition as if it were the only one. (偽=人為 and 器 are two distinct Chinese angles on the made — the general field of the humanly-done, and the made-as-instrument — complementary, not equivalent; the thesis keeps both, and conflates neither.)[^g5]

### The residual objection, and why it dissolves

One objection presses exactly here, at the moment "science is artificiality" is asserted, and it must be met where it arises rather than deferred. The objection is that science, ethics, and religion are not human artefacts at all but *domains of truth that represent or track a reality independent of us* — a scientific theory answers to nature, a moral law to the good, a revelation to the divine; to file them under "the humanly-made" looks like a category mistake that confuses the *made* with the *false*, the *constructed* with the *merely conventional*. If artificiality means "set over against nature," then calling a true theory an artificiality seems to deny its truth.

But this objection presupposes precisely the nature/culture divide that Chapter I has already dismantled, and once that divide goes, the objection goes with it.[^o1] The thesis does not hold that artificiality stands *against* nature; that opposition is the very dualism Chapter I rejected, on ecological grounds and through Obara's duality of human existence, where there is no pre-cultural nature for the human to be measured against or to return to. Being *made*, on this account, does not make a thing unnatural, and it certainly does not make it false. A scientific theory can be at once an artificiality — humanly produced, instituted, revisable — *and* world-tracking, answerable to the universe it models; that a theory is built by human hands and minds is no more an argument against its tracking the universe than that an eye is built by evolution is an argument against its seeing.[^o2]

## A reserved provocation — *technē* becoming *phusis*

There is a companion provocation that uses the Greek vocabulary just introduced, and it must be located carefully so that it is not mistaken for an argument made here. The later chapters will say that the metaverse is, in a sense, *technē becoming phusis* — the made coming to comport itself as the given. That phrase is **not** a literal claim that the made acquires an internal principle of motion of the kind Aristotle reserved for natural things. It is a deliberate provocation in the tradition's own vocabulary, aimed at exposing that the inherited seam between the grown and the made — the very seam between *phusis* and *technē*/*nomos*, which maps onto the nature/culture/technology divide the thesis already rejected in Chapter I — has stopped doing reliable work. The provocation belongs to Chapters V and VI, where the metaverse-stage argument is made; it is mentioned here only to mark the trajectory this chapter opens. No source — Aristotle, Heidegger, Brague, Hui — asserts it, and it is not argued in Chapter II. What the chapter establishes here is only the umbrella and its registers; the migration between them is later business.

## §IIA — Exosomatization

The umbrella set and named, the chapter turns to what artificiality *does*. The first aspect of its role is **exosomatization** — the impetus to develop artificiality as extensions or enhancements of our functions, compensating for biological weakness. The biological grounding of this impetus was laid in §1: the human is the secondarily altricial animal that must be completed from outside itself; Lotka's *exosomatic evolution* and Stiegler's *de-fault of origin* were the two names, a biophysicist's and a philosopher's, for that one condition. This section does not re-narrate that grounding; it develops what the impetus *does* once it is under way, and draws from it the anti-instrumentalist payoff the umbrella frame needs.[^a3]

[^a3]: [VERIFY: cross-reference §1 ("The radically immature animal") for the biological grounding — secondary altriciality (Portmann), exosomatic evolution (Lotka GRKYNJKL p.188), de-fault-of-origin (Stiegler **API752Q9** p.189). De-duplication: §1 carries the biology + Lotka + the default-of-origin + the Epimetheus narration; §IIA must NOT re-cite Lotka or re-narrate the myth. It keeps only the impetus/loop, the anti-instrumentalist payoff, and tertiary retention.]

Since the dawn of history, tools and the processes of writing have extended memory; weapons have extended a blunt body and weak biological force. And the aids act back on us. Mastering and living with artificial tools becomes itself a new adaptational pressure, oriented toward the ever-emerging artificial environment — the self-domestication §1 identified as the recursive loop of human existence. The affordances of these aids — their range of possible usages and ways of being interacted with — direct emotion, thought, behaviour, and interaction; and with new thoughts and behaviours come new needs, which call for new aids. The loop closes on itself: artificiality extends what we can do and reshapes who we become in doing it.

Stiegler reads this ever-exteriorization of organs and memories as the very character of artificiality — or, on the other side of the same coin, artificiality as our indispensable organ.[^a4] Exosomatization cannot alter the laws of the universe, but it *localizes* them: it "locally trans-forms the cosmic order in unpredictable ways."[^a5] This tends to form *techno-geographical milieus*, kin to Obara's artificial ecosystems — environments dependent on, but not reducible to, the universe. That last phrase is the hinge to §IIC, and the chapter holds it for now.

### The anti-instrumentalist payoff

The §1 account of the de-fault of origin earns its keep here, in a single load-bearing claim. Because the human is born unfinished — naked, unshod, unarmed, "without qualities" until it produces them — artificiality is **not an instrument added to an already-complete creature; it is the constitution of this body *qua* human.** There is no pre-technical human standing behind the tools, waiting to be supplemented; the human and its artificiality come into being together. The mole *has* a world the way its body gives it one; the human, lacking a given niche, must *make* one — not as a convenience but because it has no other way to be at all. This is the strongest form of the §IIA claim, and the point the umbrella frame most needs: artificiality is constitutive, not supplementary. (The originary scene from which this is derived — Plato's Epimetheus, and Stiegler's reading of the human as "the forgotten one" whose origin is a default — was narrated in §1 and is not repeated here.)

### Tertiary retention

What is exteriorized is not only labour but *memory*. Beyond the primary retention that is the lingering "comet's tail" of the living present — the just-heard still sounding within what is now heard — and the secondary memory of individual recollection, Stiegler names a third: tertiary memory, the technically externalized record. Writing, documents, recordings, the inherited archive — the supports of the already-there, through which what has passed remains available to the living.[^a6] Tertiary memory is the *hypomnemata*, the written memory-aids, that precede any individual and into which each of us is born; it is the material condition of a world's historical depth, the reason world-making is cumulative and trans-generational rather than Sisyphean. (This is the same exteriorizing loop §1 named *epiphylogenesis* in its recursivity-criterion role; here it carries the memory-exteriorization load — both from *Technics and Time 1*.)[^a7]

## §IIB — Virtuality

Material artificiality is the easy half to picture — from stone tools to AI-powered "smart cities." But artificiality is not always manifested materially, and its nonmaterial forms are not a lesser appendix to the material ones; they are indispensable to them, and dialectically bound up with them. As Harari reminds us, artificially created nonmaterial things — gods, money, laws — have the power to direct our thoughts and behaviours and even to elicit emotions or biological reflexes.[^b1] Without such nonmaterial coordinating principles a world is impossible: material things remain mere aggregates, never integrated into functioning systems.

[^b1]: [VERIFY: Harari 2015; cf. DOCX [P0117]. Resolve citekey.]

I call this causally efficacious nonmaterial aspect of artificiality *virtuality*.[^b2] The phrasing is deliberate: virtuality is an *aspect* of artificial things — their nonmaterial-and-causally-efficacious side — not a separate ontological category set alongside artificiality. A coin has a material aspect (the metal) and a nonmaterial aspect (its being-money); the virtuality is the latter. Virtuality is thus the §IIB-specific lens on the nonmaterial subset of the umbrella, not a parallel to it. Every virtuality is an artificiality; not every artificiality is paradigmatically virtual — a wall compels by mass, not by meaning.

[^b2]: [VERIFY: locked definition, DOCX [P0118]: "I call this causally efficacious nonmaterial aspect of artificiality virtuality." KEEP VERBATIM.]

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

Three popular conceptions of "virtual" are declined, each for a precise reason, and each declining keeps something. The first is the simulationist view, which sees the virtual as a not-quite-real replica — a view traceable to Peirce's definition of a virtual *x* as "something, not an *x*, which has the efficiency (*virtus*) of an *x*."[^b3] The thesis keeps the *virtus* — the power, the efficacy — and rejects the "not an *x*," the "as if," because many virtual things have no original to imitate. Baudrillard supplies exactly that ground: many artificial things have no original or real counterpart to represent at all, and a virtual *x* may be original in its own right.[^b4] The second is Chalmers's reassertion of the colloquial "virtual = computer-based," advanced to defend the realness of digital objects against the as-if view.[^b5] The thesis adopts Chalmers's substrate-neutrality and his simulation-realism without modification, but parts from him on the analytical unit: where Chalmers asks whether the *x* inside a virtual environment is real, the thesis's unit is the *trans-historical layer of human-constructed nonmaterial structure* — stories, gods, ceremonies, laws, scientific theories, mathematical models, algorithms — that has always partly constituted human worlds. What Chalmers calls computer-based is, on this reading, no more than a temporal medium of a long historical continuum.

[^b4]: [VERIFY: confirmed | Baudrillard 1994 (Zotero 5495YCYV) | per DOCX [P0120]. The historical continuum is the third thing declined, and it is the positive claim the first two clear room for. As Durante observes, humans have always dealt with the virtual, ever since they lived by the stories they collectively invented.[^b6] At first virtuality appeared as gods and ceremonies — the nonmaterial yet causally efficacious basis on which worlds could form; over time, in many places, gods were displaced by sciences and ceremonies by laws, each transition a *change of substrate* — oral to written to printed to computational — operating on the *same underlying virtuality*. Money is the cleanest case: causally efficacious, intersubjectively binding, indifferent to its substrate, and older than the computer by millennia. As Brey notes, while "rocks and trees" or "screwdrivers and chairs" can only be *simulated*, "money can literally exist" in a digital environment.[^b7] Money is still money; money has always been virtual; shells, grain, salt, metal, paper, or digits are merely the temporal mediums through which money's virtuality is operationalized.

The claim that nonmaterial artificiality is genuinely causal finds independent support in Searle's social ontology, and the support belongs here at roughly footnote weight — a corroborating interlocutor, not load-bearing apparatus. Searle distinguishes *brute facts*, which obtain independently of human opinion (a mountain's height, light's wavelength — in this thesis's vocabulary, the universe), from *institutional facts*, which obtain only through collectively accepted *status functions* of the form "*X counts as Y in context C*," and which carry *deontic powers* — obligations and entitlements with real normative force.[^b8] This furnishes the social-ontological mechanism by which money's virtuality binds: money is not a simulation of some prior "real" money but is constituted by, and exhausted in, collective acceptance — which is exactly the as-if view's denial that §IIB needs. But Searle is, for the thesis's purposes, too anthropocentric: by gating deontic powers behind human language, he cannot recognize the proto-virtual phenomena that lie below the human line.[^b9] The thesis draws the line elsewhere — at the presence of a causally-efficacious nonmaterial layer, language-gated or not. Every Searlean institutional fact is therefore a virtuality (a paradigm virtuality, in the human case), but not every virtuality is a Searlean institutional fact.

The discipline carried from §1 is that these are *proto*-virtual, not fully virtual: the same nonmaterial-causal structure at incomparably lower complexity, lacking the linguistic-conventional and deontic articulation of the human case — which is exactly why Searle excludes them. They are the evolutionary precursors of virtuality, not virtualities *tout court*. The payoff is decisive against any suggestion that the virtual is recent or merely technological: long before Chalmers's computer-based virtual, and even before Durante's storytelling humans, the nonmaterial-and-causal had already organized animal life. Virtuality is not what computation added to the world; it is what the world has run on since long before there were humans to call it virtual.

## §IIC — The irreducibility of artificial-environment laws

The use-lessness of material objects in digital environments exemplifies a key feature of artificial environments: they are dependent on and constrained by the materiality and laws of the universe, yet *not reducible* to them. They have laws of their own. A screwdriver simulated in a digital environment is use-*less* — not from any functional limit of the environment, but because the conditions for its functioning are absent: there are no physical fasteners, no gravity, no soreness of legs desiring a chair to rest on. "Tightening up" there requires an algorithm, not a screwing object.[^c1] So too the "physically fittest" person: best survival odds in the wilderness, which is a purely biological environment, but in a *world* — an artificiality field — possibly a *higher* mortality rate, assigned to hunting, war, or heavy labour, while a physically unfit person uses charisma or wit to concentrate power and resources to her own advantage.[^c2] The fitness function differs because the laws differ.

[^c1]: [VERIFY: DOCX [P0125].]

[^c2]: [VERIFY: DOCX [P0126].]

The committee's challenge was that this irreducibility is asserted rather than argued — that the main text shows it by example without saying *why* the laws are not reducible. The argument runs first through Polanyi, then through three formal moves.

### Polanyi: dual control and boundary conditions

The author's own framework already states the load-bearing point, in his own voice: an artificial environment is a special kind of *boundary condition*. Following Polanyi, a set of conditions and principles at a higher ontological level restricts the behaviour of lower-level things, so that the latter, which on their own have higher indeterminacy, can be harnessed into specific functions; and while higher-level things depend on lower-level ones for their existence, they are neither determined nor explained by them — they are irreducible to them.[^c3] The physical-chemical features of raw materials neither determine nor suffice to explain their uses; it is only through integration into a system — metabolism in an organism, assembly into a machine — that their features are harnessed. An artificial environment introduces a *new level* of this kind: its "laws" are boundary conditions imposed on activity within it, harnessing what the universe makes available without being dictated by it.

[^c3]: [VERIFY: use the DOCX's OWN Polanyi formulation at [P0086] — boundary conditions / irreducibility, already in the author's voice. Do NOT introduce a second paraphrase. Polanyi 1958/1968 NOT in Zotero per Ch-2-4-7 — resolve citekey.]

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

### Three arguments for the irreducibility

Three formal moves make explicit what the main text argues only by example; they can be offered as an optional strengthening, likely a footnote or short addition at [P0126]. *First, multiple realizability.* The same artificial-environment law — "a posted message cannot exceed 280 characters" — can be implemented in indefinitely many physical ways, across languages, hardware, and data centres. A multiply-realizable property is not identical to any one physical configuration, and so is not reducible to a physical description. *Second, the semantic-normative gap.* Artificial-environment laws are normative: they distinguish correct from incorrect, permitted from prohibited. An electron cannot "violate" a physical law; a user can violate terms of service. The permitted/prohibited distinction is not derivable from any physical description — it requires a level at which meanings and social significance operate (which is, incidentally, an argument for *virtuality's* irreducibility too, so it strengthens §IIB). *Third, downward causation.* If these laws were wholly fixed by their substrate they could exert no independent causal influence — yet a corporation's legal structure demonstrably causes physical outcomes, factories built and forests cleared, by organizing the behaviour of agents who exist at both levels at once. Downward causation is incompatible with full upward reduction.

The irreducibility, then, is structural rather than merely epistemological: universe-laws constrain artificial environments only at the substrate-implementation boundary — servers need power, bodies need food, institutions need humans — and not at the level of semantic operation, where algorithms decide, institutions confer status, and narratives organize meaning.

### The two registers of artificiality

Here the Greek framing of §2 earns its keep, in what is the chapter's most consequential synthesis. The laws governing artificial environments fall into two registers.

The first is the **conventional register**, which I gloss as *nomos*-like. Here the artificiality compels because, and to the extent that, humans continue to enact it. A statute is enforced because police, courts, and prisons act; a norm holds because the community sanctions those who break it; a religious commandment compels because the faithful believe and enforce it. When the practice ceases, the artificiality ceases to operate. This is the register of *nomos* in the strict sense the lexicon gives it — that which is in habitual practice, custom, instituted by authority — the made-and-maintained.

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

The second is the **structural register**, which I gloss not as bare *phusis* but as *phusis-in-operation* — built, but operating as though given. Here the artificiality compels by the structure of the situation itself — its architecture, its affordances, its operational dynamics — rather than by social agreement. A locked door stays locked with no enforcer watching; an algorithm executes regardless of belief; a smart contract performs its conditional regardless of anyone's regret. The violation is not merely deterred by the threat of sanction; it is *foreclosed as an action-possibility*. The crucial qualification is the hyphenated one: this register is not *phusis*, because nothing here is self-arising — every structural law was engineered, instituted, built. It is *phusis-in-operation*: humanly made in its origin, yet given-like in the manner of its operating, confronting its subjects with the indifference of a substrate even though a designer stands behind it. That is why the reserved Chapters-V-and-VI provocation — "*technē* becoming *phusis*" — names, when it comes, the metaverse-stage *migration* of artificiality from the conventional register toward the structural one: not a literal acquisition of nature's internal principle, but the made coming to operate with the given's authority.

The registers name a *dominant* tendency, not an absolute partition. Conventional artificiality has structural margins (legal coercion ultimately rests on the state's monopoly on force), and structural artificiality has conventional margins (a platform's policies are, in principle, revisable). What the later squeeze argument identifies is the *progressive shrinking* of the conventional margin — the way initial decisions become ever harder to revise as a system reaches operational maturity. This register-distinction is also the cleanest answer to the committee's "rule versus law" question. Reserve *law* for structural artificialities whose violation is structurally prevented — the locked door, code-as-law; reserve *rule* or *norm* for conventional artificialities whose violation is externally punished but not structurally prevented — etiquette, customary law. Under this convention the metaverse genuinely has *laws of its own*: a structural homology with the universe's laws, differing only in that the universe's laws are naturally given while the metaverse's are humanly engineered. Juridical statutes sit awkwardly between the two — which is a real fact about them, since conventional law has always *aspired* to structural-compelling power and never fully achieved it. The metaverse's structural register is, in this light, the successful version of an ancient aspiration.[^c4]

## Close — the double-edgedness of artificiality

Whatever register it occupies, artificiality is double-edged, and this is no modern discovery. The structural claim, compressed: *artificialities at sufficient scale or depth tend to generate the very conditions of the harm they were instituted to relieve, absent active counter-work.*

Illich gives the historical shape of this. Every industrial institution crosses two watersheds. At the first it achieves genuine effectiveness — the year 1913, he notes, marks a watershed in modern medicine, the point at which a patient began to have more than a fifty-fifty chance that a trained physician would help rather than harm.[^d1] At the second watershed the effectiveness inverts: the progress demonstrated in a prior achievement becomes the rationale for exploiting society as a whole in the service of a value defined and constantly revised by self-certifying professional élites.[^d2] Past that point the institution enacts what Illich calls a *radical monopoly* — not market dominance but the elimination of the conditions under which alternatives could exist. Cars shape a city into their own image, ruling out locomotion on foot or by bicycle; that motor traffic curtails the very right to walk *is* the radical monopoly.[^d3]

[^d1]: [VERIFY: Illich 1973, *Tools for Conviviality*, p.8. NOT in Zotero; suggested citekey illichToolsConviviality1973; cross-check the canonical quote set against Ch-2-D / Ch-6-8.]

[^d2]: [VERIFY: Illich 1973, pp.15–16.]

[^d3]: [VERIFY: Illich 1973, p.65 — radical monopoly.]

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

Stiegler names the same ambivalence the *pharmacological* condition of all artificiality. The Greek *pharmakon* is at once remedy and poison; and, after Plato's *Phaedrus* — where Theuth offers writing as a remedy for memory that Thamus warns is also its poison — every exteriorized artificiality, once it has become independent of its user, can return as toxic.[^d4] Writing supplements memory and atrophies it; the platforms that extend sociality also industrialize it. The pharmakon is *undetermined* until a regime of *care* settles its disposition — Stiegler's *otium* against an exploitative *negotium* — and the metaverse-stage danger is that the pharmakon now mutates at platform speed while care still operates at biographical and intergenerational speed, so that the mismatch becomes structural rather than contingent.

This double-edgedness is the chapter's bridge forward. The thesis's later argument — the dedicated convergence of pharmakon, Illich, Frankfurt's *Dialectic of Enlightenment*, Obara's self-petification, and 君子不器, developed in `Ch-2-D` — treats the metaverse-stage as the *limit case* of a tendency-toward-inversion that pre-metaverse artificiality already exhibited, precisely because the structural register forecloses the very counter-work each of those traditions named. Where that five-tradition convergence sits is an open question of placement: it may close §II, or migrate toward Chapter IV, where its payoff lands most directly. The chapter need not resolve this. It needs only to establish that artificiality, in every register, cuts both ways — that the remedy and the poison are inseparable, and that which of them dominates is settled by counter-work, not by the artificiality itself.

### Pure-footnote alternative for the "not against nature" point (HYBRID — author chooses)
The body (§2, "The residual objection") uses the **hybrid** form per the brief: a brief main-text resolution + cross-reference to Ch1, with [^o2] noting the option to relegate the elaboration to a footnote. The **pure-footnote alternative** keeps only a single clause in the body and moves the whole resolution into a note. Drop-in version:

> **Body (pure-footnote variant):** "…Science, ethics, and religion are not *technologies*; they are kinds within artificiality — the same humanly-instituted ordering at work in law, money, and language.[^FN]"
>
> [^FN]: It may be objected that science, ethics, and religion are domains of *truth* that represent a mind-independent reality, and so cannot be human artefacts. The objection presupposes the nature/culture divide rejected in Chapter I [DOCX P0109; Haila 2000; Jones 2009; Obara]. On the account given there, being *made* entails neither falsity nor opposition to nature: there is no pre-cultural nature against which the made could be set, and a scientific theory may be at once an artificiality — humanly produced and revisable — and world-tracking, answerable to the universe it models. "Science is an artificiality" therefore says only "science is humanly instituted," which is true and innocuous; it does not impugn science's truth. Once the divide is gone, the objection has no purchase.

Rationale for preferring the hybrid (kept): the objection *arises exactly where* "science is artificiality" is asserted, so a reader needs to see it answered in the body, not hunt for a footnote; but Ch1 did the heavy lifting, so the body stays brief and the elaboration sits in the note.
