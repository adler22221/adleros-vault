## Opener — Where the chapter sits

Chapter III argued that one mode of artificiality among the many actual worlds — the mode whose lineage runs from the ancient Mediterranean through early-modern Europe to the present planetary order — has acquired a disposition to universalize itself: to overwrite the organizing principles of every other world it meets, and to present its own made world as the only world there is. That argument was about the mode's outward relation, its comportment toward the *other* worlds it has displaced. This chapter turns inward. It asks where the mode is heading next, not at the level of its relations with rival worlds, but at the level of the human beings who inhabit it.[^op1]

[^op1]: [VERIFY: DOCX [P0049] gives the author's own one-paragraph framing of this chapter — Uegaki's as-willedness, the axiological goodness of life-as-willed, the self-finishing tendency as its consequence; this opener tracks that framing. [P0050] foreshadows the §V self-completing tendency.]

The question is the author's own, and the main text already poses it in the Introduction: *where is the Western Modern mode of artificiality heading after achieving global dominance?* The present chapter answers at the level of the inhabitant; the next chapter answers at the level of the apparatus the inhabitants have built; and the chapter after that brings the two answers together into the structural condition the thesis calls the squeeze. I want to be careful about the order of these answers, because the temptation is to reach for the squeeze too early — to preface this chapter with the claim that its mechanism is already operative, already exerting pressure before its conditions are complete. That claim belongs to the apparatus-side argument of Chapter V, where the apparatus's trajectory is what is at issue. Here the squeeze is not yet introduced as a mechanism at all. What this chapter does is more modest and, I think, prior: it shows the *human* tendency whose maturation the squeeze will later presuppose.

That tendency has a name in the work of the Japanese philosopher Takahide Uegaki (上柿崇英), whose analysis of postwar Japanese society supplies this chapter its spine.[^op2] Uegaki's diagnosis is that the mode of artificiality dominant since the Enlightenment rests on a single axiological commitment — that life ought to be *life-as-willed* (意のままになる生), conforming to the will of the one who lives it — and that the pursuit of this commitment, carried across generations within an increasingly capable apparatus, produces what he calls the *self-finishing* (自己完結化) of human life: the progressive transfer of the burdens of living — survival, relation, succession, self-realization — from the lived person onto the apparatus that supports them. The chapter develops this in six movements. §IVA recovers the commitment to life-as-willed and the place of artificiality within it. §IVB tracks the commitment historically, through Uegaki's five periods of postwar Japanese development. §IVC names the self-finishing paradox and the relational pathology it produces — the first *shape* the squeeze takes, named here only lightly. §IVD follows the same logic into the body, where it appears as desomatization. §IVE differentiates the transhumanist cases that propose to overcome the body altogether. §IVF then hands the argument to Chapter V, where the human tendency this chapter has tracked meets its apparatus-side counterpart.

[^op2]: [VERIFY: name reading CONFIRMED Takahide (上柿 崇英), born 1980, environmental philosophy / 総合人間学; NOT "Katsumi 上岡克己" (a prior error). Confirmed against researchmap / J-GLOBAL / Osaka Metropolitan University DB — ch3-6-verbatim §Target 3. Propagate the corrected reading throughout.]

One vocabulary note must be stated once and held, because it bears on how the apparatus is described throughout the chapter. As in Chapters I and II, the thesis does not use "technology" as a major concept. Where the inherited philosophy-of-technology vocabulary writes "technology," I read **artificiality** in the broad sense — the umbrella of all humanly-produced organizing principles, material and nonmaterial alike. So when this chapter says that the mode mobilizes its apparatus to render life as-willed, I mean **artificiality in both its aspects, material and virtual**: not machines on one side and meanings on the other, but the single artificial environment by which a world is constituted, working through interfaces that are at once material and nonmaterial. The reader should hear "artificiality (material and virtual)" wherever the older idiom would have said "technology and virtuality."

## §IVA — Ontological freedom, life-as-willed, and artificiality as interface

Uegaki's point of departure is a worldview he reconstructs rather than endorses — a "worldview-and-anthropology" (世界観＝人間観) that he finds at the root of Western modern philosophy and, through it, at the root of the contemporary human sciences. He states it twice, in nearly the same words, in the standalone essay and in the second volume of the monograph: the ideal of an **〈無限の生〉** (the "infinite life"), which is to say *life-as-willed* — 「意のままになる生」 — is taken to be what a human being *ought* to be; and the human vocation is correspondingly to reform and overcome the reality of *life-not-as-willed*, 「意のままにならない生」, wherever it stands in the way.[^a1] The structure is worth pausing on, because everything in the chapter follows from it. It is not merely that life-as-willed is *pleasant* or *preferable*. It is that life-not-as-willed is figured as a *wrong* — a condition to be corrected, an oppression to be lifted. Uegaki gives this its proper name: an **存在論的抑圧** (ontological oppression), the standing condition of a being whose reality does not yet conform to its will and who is therefore, by the lights of this worldview, not yet free.[^a2]

[VERIFY: re-extract char-perfect from P:\books PDF before final insertion — OCR artefacts present.]]

[^a2]: [VERIFY: 存在論的抑圧 / 存在論的自由 — Uegaki's terms; the "anthropology of freedom" (自由の人間学) lineage is laid out at 〈自己完結社会〉の成立（下）p.111, where Uegaki names Locke, Rousseau, and Kant as its founders. ch3-6-verbatim §3a. [VERIFY: re-extract char-perfect from local PDF.]]

Where does the worldview come from? Uegaki is explicit, and his genealogy matters for the thesis because it is *not* a genealogy through the philosophy of technology. He traces the ideal to what he calls the "anthropology of freedom" (自由の人間学): a lineage he founds on Locke and Rousseau and sees brought to "one completion" in Kant.[^a3] The hinge figure is Rousseau, and the hinge claim is what Uegaki names "promised authenticity" (約束された本来性): the supposition that there exists a "true human being," not yet actual but promised for the future, whose realization is the point of the whole project. Uegaki finds this supposition "splendidly embodied" in a single sentence of Rousseau's — the sentence that opens the first book of the *Social Contract*.[^a4]

[^a4]: [VERIFY: confirmed | Uegaki 〈自己完結社会〉の成立（下）p.112 | ch3-6-verbatim §3a — 「約束された本来性」, with the Rousseau "born free… in chains" sentence given as its emblem. [VERIFY: re-extract char-perfect.]]

It is worth quoting Rousseau directly here, rather than through Uegaki's paraphrase, because the corroboration is part of the argument: the thesis is not relying on a secondary report of what Rousseau "more or less" said but on the primary text in a standard edition.

> Man is born free; and everywhere he is in chains. One thinks himself the master of others, and still remains a greater slave than they.[^a5]

[^a5]: [VERIFY: confirmed verbatim | Rousseau, *The Social Contract*, Book I, Ch. 1 ("Subject of the First Book") | G. D. H. Cole translation (Everyman / J. M. [AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

The decisive move, for Uegaki and for the thesis, is what happens to this sentence *after* Rousseau. In Rousseau the chains are political; the freedom that is born and then bound is a freedom *within the order of the city*. Uegaki's claim is that the determination of the human as free gradually slips its political context and is generalized into a statement about human existence as such — an **存在論的自由** (ontological freedom) that pertains not to one's standing in a polity but to one's standing in being. "And it is precisely there," he writes, "that we opened the door that leads to 〈無限の生〉."[^a6] Once freedom is ontologized, every not-as-willed feature of existence — not just unjust laws but other people, one's own body, the cosmos itself — becomes a chain in principle, an oppression to be overcome rather than a condition to be lived. This is the generalization that turns a doctrine of political liberty into a program for the technical reconstruction of reality.

[^a6]: [VERIFY: confirmed | Uegaki 〈自己完結社会〉の成立（下）p.113 | ch3-6-verbatim §3a — the extension from 「政治的自由」 to 「存在論的自由」 ("人間存在そのものへと一般化された『存在論的自由』"). [VERIFY: re-extract char-perfect.] AUTHOR-SYNTHESIS NOTE: reading Uegaki's ontologization as the warrant for a *technical* reconstruction of reality is the thesis's extension of Uegaki, kept consistent with his 存在論的抑圧 framing.]

Here is where artificiality enters, and where the thesis takes up Uegaki for its own purpose. If life-not-as-willed is an oppression, and if the not-as-willed comes in three principal forms — the not-as-willed *other* (意のままにならない他者), the not-as-willed *body* (意のままにならない身体), and the not-as-willed *cosmos* — then the obvious instrument for lifting the oppression is the very thing this thesis has been describing all along: the artificial environment, in both its material and its virtual aspects, functioning as an *interface*. Artificiality is developed, on this reading, to stand between the willing self and each form of the not-as-willed. It interposes interfaces between people, so that the friction of the not-as-willed other can be reduced or routed around. It interposes interfaces between the self and its own body, extending the body's capacities, muting its discomforts, deferring its decay. And it interposes interfaces between the self and the cosmos — weather prediction, agricultural standardization, disease management, the whole infrastructural buffering of environmental volatility — so that the not-as-willed cosmos is rendered, at least at the surface, technically tractable. The realization of survival, of one's existence-as-a-member-of-a-world, and of succession to those who come after is, by these interfaces, increasingly *entrusted* to artificiality.[^a7]

[^a7]: [VERIFY: the three not-as-willed forms (他者 / 身体 / cosmos) and the three "realizations" — 〈生存〉の実現 (survival/livelihood), 〈現実存在〉の実現 (existence-in-reality), 〈継承〉の実現 (succession) — are Uegaki's articulation as carried in the current DOCX Ch IV draft + Ch-4-2_v2 §2.1; confirm wording against Uegaki 2022a/b PDFs. The "artificiality as interface" framing is the thesis's reading of Uegaki, consistent with the Ch I/II apparatus.]

This entrustment is the seed of everything that follows, and it is important to register at the outset that it is not, in the first instance, a loss. The freedom it delivers is real; the equality of access it delivers is real; the apparatus that delivers them is real. People become, genuinely, freer and more equal *as users* of the apparatus — and they choose it, for reasons that from the inside are intelligible and often good. What the rest of the chapter develops is not a denunciation of that choice but a description of its structural cost: that the same arrangement which delivers life-as-willed, sustained across generations, hollows out the conditions under which a willed life has content. That cost is the self-finishing tendency, and it is best seen first in its historical unfolding.

## §IVB — Uegaki's five periods: Japan as the legible case

Uegaki anchors his account of self-finishing in a historical reading of postwar Japanese society, and the thesis takes that reading as its empirical spine — but the framing must be set carefully, because the periodization is easy to misuse in two opposite ways. The first misuse treats Japan as the *cause* of the self-finishing condition, as if some peculiarity of postwar Japan generated a pathology now exported to the rest of the world. The second treats it as a merely *parochial* case, an ethnographic curiosity with nothing to say beyond its own islands. Both collapse the distinction the thesis needs to hold open: between the underlying tendency and its particular surfacings. The position I take is that **Japan offers an unusually legible case of the self-finishing tendency, not because Japan caused it, but because the Modern mode of artificiality reorganized Japanese existence in compressed historical time** — Meiji to the present in roughly four generations — and a process that elsewhere unfolded slowly enough to be mistaken for the natural course of things here unfolded fast enough, and was documented closely enough, to be seen.[^b1]

[^b1]: [VERIFY: Japan-as-legible-case-not-cause framing per Ch-4-4_v2 §"Reframed Introduction"; the compression argument is the thesis's, consistent with Uegaki's own treatment of postwar Japan as a 構造転換 (structural transformation) of the lifeworld.]

Uegaki sets out the periodization as a table — his "table 6" — and gives each period both a date-range and a figure. The five periods run as follows.[^b2] The **first** (1868–1945), from the founding of the modern Japanese nation-state to defeat, is the period in which a "weighty" lifeworld and the first buds of the *social apparatus* (社会的装置) coexist. The **second** (1945–1970), from postwar reconstruction through the high-growth era, is a transitional period whose representative figure Uegaki calls the **〈旅人〉** — the *traveller* or wayfarer, the person who has left the "home port" of an inherited world and set out toward a great "destination."[^b3] The **third** (1970–1995), from the flourishing of high consumer society to the bursting of the bubble, is the period in which the structural transformation of the lifeworld advances most sharply, symbolized by the rapid expansion of the **〈郊外〉** (the suburb) and the "settling-down" of the traveller. The **fourth** (1995–2010), the period of informatization and globalization, is the one in which "information technology" takes hold and the lifeworld is convulsed — and it is here that the traveller gives way to a new figure, the **〈漂流人〉** (the *drifter*). The **fifth** (2010–present) is the period "where we now stand": the emergence of the **〈自己完結社会〉**, the self-contained society.

Period titles are Uegaki's own. ⚠ This table-6 framing (旅人 in Period 2, 漂流人 emerging in Period 4) supersedes where it conflicts the earlier T0-derived list in Ch-4-4_v2, which compressed the 旅人/漂流人 distinction. [VERIFY: re-extract char-perfect from local PDF.]]

[^b3]: [VERIFY: confirmed | Uegaki Vol.2 下 — 「われわれはこの過渡期に生きた人々のことを、象徴的に〈旅人〉と呼ぶことにしよう」 | ch3-6-verbatim §3b. The 旅人 (Period 2) → 漂流人 (Period 4) sequence is Uegaki's; the v1 scaffold ran the figures together — keep them distinct.]

The two figures at the heart of the sequence repay attention, because they carry the argument. The **drifter** (漂流人) is defined by contrast with the traveller. If the traveller is a ship that has left the "mother port" called *home* (故郷) and is rowing out toward a great destination, the drifter is a drifting vessel that has, from the very start, lost its mother port, lost its destination, and lost even the "compass" by which it might fix its own position.[^b4] The drifter "does not know roles, and cannot build trust" — which is to say that the drifter has grown up without inheriting either the *meanings* or the *skills* that communal life requires, because the lifeworld that once transmitted them has thinned to the point where it transmits neither. And the condition that produces the drifter is what Uegaki calls **承認不安** (recognition-anxiety): in the latter half of the fourth period, "not a few people came to suffer over their own 'place to be,' facing a situation that might be called recognition-anxiety" — the anxiety, as a footnote glosses it, of those who had naïvely believed "the full unfolding of free individuality" to be life's ideal, and who break against the gap between that ideal and the reality.[^b5] The drifter is not, in his own experience, a failure or a deviant. The drifter is simply what it feels like to inhabit a self-finishing world at this stage of its trajectory.

The fifth period names the terminus toward which the sequence tends. The **self-contained society** (自己完結社会) is, in Uegaki's own definition, "a society in which, through deep dependence on the *social apparatus* (社会的装置) that swells together with information technology, robotics / artificial-intelligence technology, and life-manipulation technology, the necessity of relating to flesh-and-blood others, and the necessity of living together with a flesh-and-blood body, are lost."[^b6] Read through this thesis's vocabulary, the social apparatus Uegaki names — the market economy, the bureaucratic institutions, the infosphere at its core — *is* artificiality in its highly developed virtual aspect; and the self-contained society is what a world looks like when the entrustment described in §IVA has run far enough that the inhabitant no longer *needs* the not-as-willed other, or the not-as-willed body, in order to live. Its characteristic ethical posture follows directly: an **ethics of non-intervention** (不介入の倫理), under which the highest courtesy one can extend to another is to refrain from intruding on their self-curated world — and to bear, in exchange, sole responsibility for one's own.[^b7]

Two disciplines travel with the periodization, and both are scope-protective. The first is that the five periods are *not* a calendar against which one could check off any given society. They are an ideal-typical sequence — confrontation, reconstruction, hollowing, recognition-anxiety, self-containment — that makes the variations across particular societies legible *as* variations of one trajectory rather than as unrelated national stories. Whether a given society has reached the fifth period or remains in the fourth or the third is an empirical question for that society; that the trajectory exists, and that the inhabitants of the Modern mode are somewhere along it, is the claim. The second discipline is the attribution guard already noted: Uegaki names the antecedents he actually invokes — Locke, Rousseau, Kant, Hegel — and the thesis must not lend him interlocutors he does not name. Where this chapter reaches for the broader European theory of mass society or the critique of instrumental reason, it does so on its own authority, not Uegaki's. With those two disciplines in place, the periodization can do its work: it gives the abstract claim of §IVA — that life-as-willed produces self-finishing — a documented historical shape, and it gives §IVC its central concept, which I turn to now.

## §IVC — The self-finishing paradox and the relational pathology

The mechanism Uegaki names **自己完結化** — the self-finishing of human life — can now be stated plainly. By drawing on an increasingly capable apparatus, the inhabitant of the Modern world is able to lift, one by one, the burdens that the not-as-willed other imposes. The need to deal with others can be routed through interfaces, without the unmediated friction of direct encounter and without the laborious upkeep of difficult relationships. The apparatus can even be enlisted to *affirm* one's ontological freedom — to confirm the self exactly as it already is, the "true self" (ありのままの私), by guaranteeing non-intervention from others. The result is that the cherished ideal of the **independent individual** (自立した個人) is more and more fully realized — but at a cost Uegaki states precisely: a **dilution of relationality** (関係性の希薄化).[^c1]

[^c1]: [VERIFY: 自己完結化 / ありのままの私 / 自立した個人 / 関係性の希薄化 — Uegaki's terms as carried in the current DOCX Ch IV draft + Ch-4-4_v2; confirm page-anchored wording against Uegaki 2022a/b PDFs. [VERIFY: re-extract char-perfect.]]

The paradox the dilution produces is the conceptual centre of the chapter, and it has a precise shape. Without sufficient relational experience and the skills that only such experience can build, people become — paradoxically — *more* sensitive to the not-as-willedness of others, more easily threatened or disturbed by it. The thinner the lifeworld of shared relational practice, the more painful any not-as-willed encounter is; and the more painful the encounter, the stronger the incentive to route the next one through the apparatus, which thins the lifeworld further still. This is a self-reinforcing spiral with no internal stopping point: a thinner relational fabric makes the not-as-willed other harder to bear, which drives further recourse to apparatus-mediation, which thins the fabric again. Uegaki names the outcome **関係性の病理** (relational pathology) — the social anxiety, the difficulty sustaining intimate relation, the steadily rising preference for mediated over unmediated contact — and the thesis reads the spiral as the engine that carries a society from the fourth period into the fifth. The drifter of §IVB is the figure this spiral produces; the self-contained society is the social form in which the spiral has run to something near its limit.[^c2]

[^c2]: [VERIFY: 関係性の病理 — Uegaki's term; the self-reinforcing-spiral formulation is preserved from Ch-4-2_v2 §1 (flagged there as elegant in the original annotation) and the current DOCX draft. The "engine carrying a society from Period 4 to Period 5" reading is the thesis's synthesis.]

## §IVD — Desomatization: the body as the universe's first claim

The not-as-willed comes in three forms, and §IVC followed only one of them — the not-as-willed *other*. The second form is closer in, and harder to route around: the not-as-willed *body* (意のままにならない身体). The body imposes a biological hindrance on the realization of ontological freedom, and the self-finishing logic, holding consistently to its premise, draws the consequence: the less one's life depends on the body, the freer one is. Uegaki names the trajectory this opens a **desomatization of life** (生の脱身体化) — the progressive detachment of what it means to live from the biological body — and pairs it with a **confusion of life** (生の混乱): the paradoxical condition in which, the less one is biologically *oneself*, the more ontologically realized one may appear to be.[^d1]

[^d1]: [VERIFY: 生の脱身体化 / 生の混乱 — Uegaki's terms as carried in the current DOCX Ch IV draft; confirm against Uegaki 2022a/b PDFs. [VERIFY: re-extract char-perfect.]]

The argument is not a lament for the body's diminished place in modern life; it is a structural observation, and the structure is best stated through the thesis's own triad. Among all the ways the self touches the universe, the body holds a unique position: it is the *only* stratum at which the universe and the self are not separable. Every other access the self has to the universe is mediated — by perception, by tool, by language, by interface. Only at the body is the substrate itself the self. The body is, in this exact sense, the **universe's first claim on the self** — not first in time, but first in the order of necessity: to be a self at all is to be a body the universe has produced and continues to produce.[^d2] This is why the self-finishing tendency is *structurally bound* to register the body as an obstacle. Self-finishing is the project of arranging a life every element of which passes through the filter of apparent choice; and the body is the one element the project cannot incorporate, because the body is precisely what the self did not choose and could not have chosen, being the condition of choosing. Against a world in which everything else has been curated, the body's unchosenness becomes conspicuous — and what in another configuration was simply *the body*, the lived ground of a life, comes to be experienced as the residual recalcitrance the curated layer cannot absorb.

[^d2]: [VERIFY: "body as the universe's first claim / the only stratum at which substrate and self are not separable" — Ch-4-5_v2 §1, de-jargoned here from the original CR phrasing (no "dr-level"; use Ch1's universe/world triad per [P0106]).]

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

The point sharpens once one sees that the body the contemporary self inherits as obstacle is *already* a worked-over body — which is why the obstacle is felt at several depths at once. The thesis has, in earlier chapters, set the human body within a long history of self-modification: the self-domestication by which our lineage selected against reactive aggression across evolutionary time, and the self-petrification by which routinized practice and built environment progressively patterned the body and its dispositions into a fixity fitted to a curated milieu rather than an unchosen cosmos.[^d3] These do not succeed one another; they *layer*. The contemporary inhabitant carries a self-domesticated body, a self-petrified set of dispositions, and an increasingly self-finished relational structure simultaneously — and the body bears the load of all three at once. So when the body asserts its own agenda against the will's projects — when it demands sleep the will would override, imposes hunger the will would ignore, breaks down where the will would perform — that assertion is registered not as a *given* to be lived with but as a *malfunction* to be corrected. The phenomenology is diagnostic: where the body is felt as a bug rather than a baseline, the self-finishing horizon has already supplied the standard against which "baseline" is being measured.

The contemporary repertoire of responses runs across three registers, and naming them keeps the argument concrete. There is a **labour register**, in which the apparatus absorbs the forms of work that required embodied presence — agricultural, craft, care-giving — and substitutes thinly-embodied or disembodied alternatives: the office work of the late nineteenth century was already a partial desomatization relative to the field and the workshop; the screen-mediated work of the late twentieth century went further; the remote-work configuration normalized in the early 2020s went further still. There is a **somatic register**, in which the not-as-willed body is addressed directly as material to be managed — stimulants against fatigue, appetite-suppressants and metabolic optimization against hunger, sleep-tracking and chronopharmacology against the body's own clock, the cosmetic and longevity and supplement industries against aging. And there is a **phenomenological register**, in which, as the body's role in labour shrinks and its recalcitrance is increasingly technically managed, the body comes to be something one *has* — an instrument, an object of management — rather than something one *is*.[^d4]

[^d4]: [VERIFY: the three registers (labour / somatic / phenomenological) per Ch-4-5_v2 §2 and v1 §IV.4. One example earns its place because it measures the dynamic in its purest form. Clinical dermatologists reported, during the pandemic years, a marked rise in patients seeking procedures specifically to correct what they had observed not in the mirror but in their own video-call self-image — the laterally-inverted, ring-lit, webcam-foreshortened face they had stared at for hours daily. The request is precisely *not* a request to match the mirror; it is a request to bring the material face into agreement with the mediated image that overwhelming exposure has made the *primary* self-reference. The discipline the thesis insists on here is that the patient is not failing, not deceived, not a victim of false consciousness. The patient is responding *rationally* to a real change in the relative authority of mirror and medium, with the means available within the domain the medium has made amenable — the body's appearance. The critique is directed at the conditions of the choice, never at the chooser: to pathologize the patient would be both unjust and analytically misdirected, while to name the condition — that under saturated image-mediation the medium-self acquires primary authority and the body becomes legible as the obstacle to alignment with it — is exactly the structural diagnosis the thesis is in a position to make.[^d5]

The deeper point is what desomatization does *not* resolve. The metaverse — anticipated here, argued for later — offers the will an environment in which the avatar need not age, need not tire, need not conform to the body's material limits. But this does not resolve the body-as-obstacle; it *relocates* the body from the foreground of attention to an unattended background, where the organism continues to require the universe's metabolic sustenance while the self's competence to navigate that requirement atrophies. The body's claims on the self do not diminish; the self's capacity to answer them does. The body, as the universe's first claim, calls the self back to a stratum no apparatus can incorporate — which is the somatic form of the same first shape §IVC reached at the relational level, and which is why the apparatus, however far it extends, cannot be the resolution of what it produces.

## §IVE — Transhumanism: the cases differentiated

The self-finishing tendency reaches its most ambitious contemporary expression in the transhumanist program: the cluster of research, technology, and conviction oriented toward overcoming the body's not-as-willed character by technical means. It is tempting to treat transhumanism as a single thing and to pronounce a single verdict on it — and the temptation should be resisted, because "transhumanism" names a loose family of programs whose relations to the argument of this chapter are heterogeneous and, in places, opposed. To say "transhumanism deepens the self-finishing tendency" without qualification is a category error: it predicates of the whole family what is true at most of certain branches under certain configurations. The thesis does better, and is more defensible, by reasoning case by case — and the variable that orders the cases is precisely *how much of the universe-calibrated organism each program preserves*.[^e1]

[^e1]: [VERIFY: the case-by-case differentiation and the ordering variable ("how much of the universe-calibrated organism is preserved") per Ch-4-6_v2 §1–§2. **Longevity research**, in its mainstream biomedical form, is the case that most cleanly *resists* the self-finishing reading. Its aim is more time in a recognizably continuous body — more decades of circadian sleep, seasonal physiology, sensorimotor encounter with terrain and weather and other bodies. To the extent that the human's calibration to the universe just *is* these biological rhythms, extending the lifespan of an unmodified body extends, rather than attenuates, the organism's engagement with the universe-substrate. It can become an ally of self-finishing when the added decades are filled with curated immersion, and a counter-tendency when they are spent in embodied practice; the thesis cannot read it as deepening anything without specifying those further conditions.[^e2]

[^e2]: [VERIFY: longevity-research case per Ch-4-6_v2 §2.1; distributive objection via Al-Rodhan `N5QQHPIA` (confirmed Zotero).]

**Restorative prosthesis and brain–computer interface** — the cochlear implant, the retinal prosthesis, the motor interface for spinal-cord injury, the deep-brain stimulator for a movement disorder — are paradigmatically technologies that *return* an organism to a universe-engagement that pathology had foreclosed. A deafened person whose implant restores conversational hearing has been returned to the acoustic universe, not removed from it. The self-finishing-relevant question for restoration is never simply whether the body is modified but *what relational structure the modification restores or extends the body into*: the same cochlear implant thickens engagement when its owner rejoins ordinary speech communities and thins it when it streams curated content directly to the auditory cortex. The technology is not the tendency; the configuration of use is.[^e3]

[^e3]: [VERIFY: restorative-prosthesis case per Ch-4-6_v2 §2.2.]

**Cognitive and affective enhancement** — nootropic pharmacology, neurostimulation, eventually direct augmentation — is the most ambiguous case, and its valence turns on what it is *for*. An attentional regime that sustains engagement with a long musical phrase, a contemplative practice, or a difficult text is engagement-thickening; the same molecule deployed to sustain twelve-hour sessions of platform-mediated work shifts the calibration sharply toward the apparatus. What can be said from the structure alone, independent of any individual case, is that the mere *availability* of fine-grained cognitive modulation lowers the cost of apparatus-adaptation and so weakens one of the natural limits to it — the unmodified organism's biological resistance to sustained apparatus-demand. That is a structural claim about the equilibrium, not a verdict on any user.[^e4]

## §IVF — From the self-finishing of life to the self-completion of the apparatus

The chapter has tracked one half of a coupled dynamic. Beginning from the Modern mode's axiological commitment to life-as-willed (§IVA), and following it through the historical trajectory Uegaki documents (§IVB), into the relational pathology it produces (§IVC), the desomatization it imposes (§IVD), and the transhumanist program that crystallizes its logic (§IVE), the argument has described the **self-finishing of human life** — the steady transfer of the burdens of living, the not-as-willed others and the not-as-willed body, from the lived person onto the apparatus that stands in for them. At every step the same thing happens: the apparatus absorbs a function the person once carried, and the human portion of functionality in the world correspondingly shrinks.

But a function absorbed by the apparatus is a function the apparatus now performs — and there the other half of the dynamic comes into view. As the human portion shrinks, the apparatus's portion of functionality in the world grows; and the growth is not merely additive. Each function the apparatus takes over makes further self-finishing possible on the human side, which entrusts a further function to the apparatus, which enlarges the apparatus's portion again. The two movements are coupled: the human side's self-finishing enables, and is enabled by, a corresponding tendency on the side of the apparatus. The main text states the coupling directly — that the self-finishing tendency of human life "is followed by a self-completing tendency of artificiality, as it is only through the mediation of artificiality that humans are able to maximize their life-as-willedness, which in turn increases artificiality's portion of functionality in the world, including its self-maintenance and renewal."[^f1]

[^f1]: [VERIFY: confirmed | DOCX [P0050] — the author's own statement of the human-self-finishing → apparatus-self-completion coupling, and the "minimally actualizable with a cybernetic system that seamlessly coordinates artificial intelligence, big data, the Internet of Things, and code is law" specification, which belongs to Ch V.]

That apparatus-side tendency — the apparatus achieving operational independence from the very humans whose tasks it has absorbed, and acquiring its own logic of self-maintenance and renewal — is the subject of Chapter V, and it has a name in this thesis that I deliberately withhold here, because to name it now would be to claim before its argument what that chapter must earn. Chapter V will ask what becomes structurally possible when the apparatus crosses the threshold from tool to background environment: when the minimally sufficient artificialities the author identifies — artificial intelligence, big data, the Internet of Things, and code-as-law — are integrated into a system that can persist and operate without continuous human participation, and so begins to function as a *second* environment the inhabitants must adapt to rather than merely a set of instruments they wield. It is at that point, and not before, that the two tendencies this thesis distinguishes — the self-finishing of life and the self-completion of the apparatus — close into the coupling whose consequence Chapter VI will name the squeeze.

One observation belongs at the close, because it governs how the whole chapter should be read. The self-finishing tendency is not a chosen pathology and not an imposed alienation. It is the *expected* trajectory of a mode of artificiality whose deepest commitment is the pursuit of life-as-willed. Throughout the trajectory the inhabitants of the Modern world have been doing exactly what their world's organizing principles call for — pursuing freedom, lifting burdens, extending capacities, mediating relations, deferring the body's limits — and the self-finishing tendency is simply what that pursuit looks like when its conditions are sustained across generations within a totalizing apparatus. The chapter has not been a critique of anyone's choices. It has been a description of the structural dynamic those choices, in aggregate and over time, produce — and of the first shape, at the scale of a single life, of the condition the rest of the thesis sets out to specify.
