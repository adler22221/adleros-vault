## Opener — One mode among many actual worlds

Chapter II asked what a world is *made of*, and answered with a single category: artificiality, the umbrella of all humanly-produced organizing principles, material and nonmaterial alike, by which a world is constituted. That chapter abstracted away from any particular case to describe the structure common to every world — exosomatization, virtuality, the irreducibility of an artificial environment's laws to those of the universe. This chapter does the reverse. It moves from the type to its instances, and asks why one instance among many — the mode whose lineage runs from the ancient Mediterranean through early-modern Europe to the present planetary order — has acquired a disposition that the others, on the whole, did not: a disposition to overwrite the organizing principles of every other world it meets, and to present its own made world as the only world there is. I call this disposition **universalization**, and the chapter's task is to show that it belongs to *one* mode rather than to artificiality as such. This matters because the disposition is the precondition the squeeze hypothesis later needs. The metaverse-stage squeeze of Chapter VI becomes intelligible only because *this* mode first made its artificiality planetary.[^op1]

[^op1]: [VERIFY: squeeze-ontology.md §III↔§VI boundary — §III establishes "this mode is one of many" and "modes can fail under pressure"; §VI specifies the metaverse-stage configuration.]

One vocabulary note must be stated once and held. As in Chapter II, the thesis does not use "technology" as a major concept. Where the philosophy-of-technology tradition writes "technology," I read artificiality in the broad sense. The disposition examined here is therefore not a disposition built into *technology* — the capacity for artificiality is a human universal — but a disposition built into one *mode* of artificiality.

A second note, and this one runs deeper because it bears on the thesis's own central pair of terms. The **universe**, in Chapter I, is the realist substrate — "the source of laws, energies, or matters that are indifferent and unaffectable by anything,"[^op2] a thing that neither a god nor a man made. A **world** is the idealist meaning-space, a human-made artefact nested within it. *Universalization*, despite the echo, is **not** a property of the universe. It is a property of a *world*: a process, not a thing, by which one made world comports itself as if it *were* the given universe — unbounded, self-evident, the standard against which every other world counts only as an approximation or a failure. That a made world should carry itself as a given substrate is the very trajectory the thesis will later name "tekhne becoming phusis." But that phrase is a non-literal provocation, and it belongs to the metaverse-stage argument of Chapters V and VI; here I only foreshadow it. Universalization is the disposition; its terminal form, where the made hardens into a substrate no one chose, is argued later, not now.

[^op2]: [VERIFY: DOCX [P0105] — universe as realist substrate; [P0109] nature/culture rejection underwrites the universe/world pairing.]

## §IIIA — The plurality of actual worlds

The first move relativizes the mode under examination by establishing that the worlds it would universalize over are *really* plural — not as a space of modal alternatives to a single real world, but as a multiplicity of *actual* worlds, each a different realization of the adaptational operating system Chapter I specified. This is where the current main text already opens: by putting worlds into perspective, we find the world we live in to be relative rather than universal and absolute, which "opens the possibility of a new, concrete sense of other possible worlds (in contrast to the abstract Leibnizian one)."[^a1]

[^a1]: [VERIFY: DOCX [P0132].]

The philosophical warrant is Nelson Goodman's *Ways of Worldmaking*, and I take it for one specific job. Not "worlds are made, not found" — Chapter I already secures that, through niche construction, Obara's dual ecosystems, and Brague's *kosmos*. The job is the plurality of *actual* worlds against the Leibnizian abstraction. Goodman describes the movement "from unique truth and a world fixed and found to a diversity of right and even conflicting versions or worlds in the making."[^a2] And — this is the disambiguation the main text flags — these are not Leibnizian *possible* worlds: we are speaking "not in terms of multiple possible alternatives to a single actual world but of multiple actual worlds."[^a3] The plurality at issue is a plurality of *actual* worlds, each a different mode of artificiality realized under different conditions, none of them the world "fixed and found." In this light the `[TERMINOLOGY-PENDING]` mode is one such actual world — a remaking of worlds already on hand — not the universal terminus its self-understanding takes it to be.

[^a2]: [VERIFY: confirmed | goodmanWaysWorldmaking1978 (Zotero WZEEWC4D) | Foreword, p. ix.]

[^a3]: [VERIFY: confirmed | goodmanWaysWorldmaking1978 | Ch. I §2, pp. 2–3. The "remaking… already on hand" phrase is Ch. I §4 p. 7 — cross-reference rather than re-quote if Ch-1-11 §B already carries it.]

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

One borrowing-discipline must stay explicit, because a reader on philosophical precision will check it. The thesis takes Goodman's plurality of *actual* worlds but not his full irrealism. Goodman denies any single world independent of symbol systems; this thesis stays realist about the universe — the substrate that neither a god nor a man made — and constructivist only about the worlds nested within it. The borrowing is therefore selective: Goodman's plurality, not his anti-realism about the given.[^a4]

[^a4]: [VERIFY: goodmanWaysWorldmaking1978, Ch. The selective borrowing is the thesis's positioning of Goodman, not Goodman's own qualification.]

From the recognition of plurality follows a constraint the chapter must honour. Any account of one world's mode of artificiality — including the mode whose disposition this chapter examines — is necessarily one account from one position among others. The thesis claims no view from nowhere. What it claims is that Chapter I's apparatus — the adaptational operating system, the irreducibility of each world's organizing principles, incommensurability without incompatibility — permits a comparative description that does not smuggle in any one world's principles as the universal standard. The account that follows is offered in that spirit: a structural description of a specific configuration, not the denunciation of one culture by another. This is also the first half of an answer to a worry §IIIE will complete — the worry that the chapter has cherry-picked. The claim is not "this mode dominates and the others do not." It is that this mode has a *structurally distinctive disposition* whose character requires explanation.

## §IIIB — The material basis of this mode's universalization

That this mode universalized is not a brute fact about cultural superiority; it has a traceable material basis. The current main text already places the relevant claim under the heading of mechanistic intervention's self-reinforcement, and already grants that this mode "was an exception rather than the commonplace."[^b1] What this section adds is the grounding of that claim — replacing a paraphrase that read as name-dropping with the source's own voice.

[^b1]: [VERIFY: DOCX [P0134].]

Joseph Needham — biochemist, Sinologist, lead author of *Science and Civilisation in China* — spent half a century on a double question, and his answer roots divergent worldviews in divergent material conditions, running geography to mode of production to worldview. The most citable statement of the triangle is the *Grand Titration* "four factors" passage:

> "So we come to the fundamental question, why did modern science not arise in China? **The key probably lies in the four factors: geographical, hydrological, social and economic.** … On the geographical side, China is a continental country, bound to agriculture, not an archipelago teeming with maritime mercantile city-states, as Europe always was… **Correspondingly the imperial bureaucracy gained in importance, and while on the one hand it prevented any recrudescence of feudalism, it also absolutely prevented the rise of the merchants and the coming into being of capitalism.**"[^b2]

[^b2]: [VERIFY: confirmed verbatim | Needham, *The Grand Titration*, "On Science and Social Change" | NLM "Joseph Needham" notebook, source 7c55bfb3 | ch3-6-verbatim §5a — page TBD, locate in print.]

The geography-to-production layer feeds a worldview layer, and here Needham makes the co-emergence explicit, in the organicism-versus-atomism passage from Volume 2:

> "…can we consider it a mere coincidence that the former [Taoist organicism] arose in a highly organised society where conservancy-dictated bureaucratism was dominant while the latter [Democritean-Epicurean atomism] arose in a world of City-states and individual merchant-adventurers? I believe that we cannot… **Chinese bureaucratism was fundamentally agrarian, and based upon agricultural production in a context of irrigation and water-control; as opposed to the maritime emphasis of the European city-states.**"[^b3]

[^b3]: [VERIFY: confirmed verbatim | needhamScienceCivilisationChina (SCC Vol. 2) | pp. 337–338 (model-reported — verify against print) | NLM 8d2da12e | ch3-6-verbatim §5b.]

Needham even names the reflexive relation the thesis needs — worldview as the "mirror image" of the production-grounded social order: the entire system of correlative organismic thinking was, in one sense, "the mirror image of Chinese bureaucratic society."[^b4] And the materialist underwriting, which keeps this from collapsing into idealist intellectual history, is stated in the Volume 7 conclusions — the volume actually held in the library: however great the importance of ideology, it "can never obscure the basic fact that underneath lie the **material forces of climate, geography and social integration**."[^b5]

Two disciplines must travel with the Needham material. First, his own nuance must be preserved: he does not present this as East-good and West-bad. The non-interventionist conception was, in his words, "propitious for the development of the natural sciences" even where it had limits, set against the "occidental 'interventionism', so natural to a people of shepherds and sea-farers."[^b6] Keeping this guards the chapter against the essentialism the thesis elsewhere disavows. Second, the bridge from Needham's descriptive "interventionism" to the thesis's "universalization" is mine, not his — though Needham himself names a mechanistic imperative under which "the more complete the domination of Nature, the more fully does it become possible for ruling elites to increase their control of individual human behaviour."[^b7] Needham supplies the material-civilizational mechanism; the ontological diagnosis is the thesis's.

Needham's binary — Sinosphere against Mediterranean — is only one contrast within a far wider field of non-Western, non-Sinosphere alternatives, and the committee asked for exactly this breadth. Recalling them is a structural point, not a romantic one. Each is a *real* mode of artificiality whose organizing principles are not derivable from universe-level laws, and whose configuration did not generate the universalizing disposition. Islamic civilisational science developed mathematics of the highest order — the Maragha school's Tusi-couple, Ibn al-Shatir's lunar models, transmitted into Renaissance astronomy — within a contemplative cosmology rather than an interventionist one.[^b8] Pacific wayfinding cultivated an embodied attunement to ecological pattern — swell refraction, star paths, the behaviour of cloud and bird — navigating open ocean *through* the environment rather than by instrumental mastery *over* it.[^b9] Indigenous North American land management — controlled burning, the Three-Sisters polyculture, prairie management — optimised for ecological relationship rather than extractive yield.[^b10] The umbrella authority is James Poskett's *Horizons*, which makes the structural point that modern science "has always depended upon bringing together people and ideas from different cultures around the world," and that the narrative of European scientific uniqueness "served as a convenient fiction" of empire and of the Cold War.[^b11] The lesson is that the universalizing disposition is not a consequence of scientific rationality as such — Islamic, Pacific, and Indigenous traditions were all scientifically sophisticated — but a specific configuration that *one* mode gave to its artificiality.

There is also a "before" that universalization displaced, and recalling it makes its character legible — another point the committee asked to see drawn out. Pre-universalization livelihood was characterised by *plural pathways*: a household sustained itself through a portfolio — cultivation, craft, communal labour exchange, local market participation, embodied savoir-faire — no single one essential, the whole resilient precisely *because* it was plural.[^b12] This is not an idealised golden age but a structural contrast. It is *plurality itself* — the condition in which several non-universalized pathways could combine — that universalization, and later the metaverse's radical monopoly in Chapter VI, displaces. The thread runs forward; §IIID and Chapter VI pick it up.

Domination is widespread: Aztec sacrificial cosmology, Mongol conquest, Ottoman and Mesopotamian state-building all show as much.[^b13] The defensible claim is the combinatorial one developed in §IIIE — not "non-Western means non-dominating," but that this mode integrated a specific compound of components that no other tradition assembled together. ## §IIIC — The ancient-Greek epistemic fault line

The material basis explains *that* the mode could reach planetary scale once some sufficient configuration came online; it does not yet explain the *character* of what was unleashed — the totalizing, all-or-nothing form of its universalization. That character has an epistemic condition, and it is genealogically older than the modern period: a fault line in the Greek philosophical inheritance.

"Fault line" does not name a biographical fact about Plato and Aristotle. It names a structural tension constitutive of Western philosophy's development — the unresolved oscillation between two poles that Roy Bhaskar traces across the whole span of Western thought, where philosophy "was thus stuck — on what I am going to characterize as the Platonic/Aristotelian fault-line."[^c1] The Platonic pole treats the universal, the formal, the mathematically necessary as what is most real; the Aristotelian pole insists on immanence but accepts formal cause as the organizing principle. Neither resolves the gap between universal and particular, and the oscillation repeats — from the medieval debate over universals, through rationalism against empiricism, into the present. The thesis's claim is the careful one: that the Platonic pole became structurally dominant in shaping the institutions of modernity, not that all Western thought is Platonic.[^c2]

[^c1]: [VERIFY: confirmed | bhaskarPlatoEtc (88YLH48Y, nlm:synced) | p.4 | NLM 5f7e7ef5 | ch3-6_v2.]

[^c2]: [VERIFY: ch3-6_v2 §1 — do NOT overclaim.]

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

The mechanism by which the Platonic pole produces universalization is what Bhaskar calls the **epistemic fallacy**: "the analysis or definition of statements about being in terms of statements about our knowledge (of being)… the ontification of knowledge… characteristically spawning what, following Adorno, can be called 'identity-thinking.'"[^c3] When knowledge is taken as the measure of reality, whatever cannot be known in the privileged mode — the formal, the universal, the calculable — is denied full reality. **Totalization** is this fallacy raised to the level of institutions: the drive to render all of nature and society available to technical-scientific reason, so that what resists — the contextual, the contingent, the irreducibly particular — is treated as a residue to be eliminated rather than a feature of reality to be respected. **Exclusion** is its complement: the undatafiable is not merely left unexplained but actively marginalized, denied ontological standing, dismissed as pre-scientific or backward. The chapter can keep this triad — fault line, totalization, exclusion — compact, and let the reader feel its force without the full Bhaskar apparatus.[^c4]

[^c3]: [VERIFY: confirmed | bhaskarPlatoEtc | p.201 (Glossary) + pp.103–104 | NLM 5f7e7ef5 | ch3-6_v2.]

[^c4]: [VERIFY: ch3-6_v2 §2–§3. The false-binary objection (Heraclitean/Stoic/Epicurean counter-traditions) is answered by the weaker claim: the Platonic pole became the organizing logic of *the institutions of modernity*, not that counter-traditions don't exist.]

A crucial discipline follows. The genealogical claim is held at *strand* level, not tradition level. What Adorno and Horkheimer, Heidegger, and Derrida diagnose is not the whole classical inheritance but a productionist-substantialist strand within it.[^c5] The disposition is the historically actualized outcome of *one strand* — detached from its corrective practices and scaled in modernity — not the necessary outcome of "the Greeks." The tradition is internally plural and contains genuine anti-domination resources; conceding this substantively is what lets the chapter survive the objection that it has indicted all of philosophy at once.

[^c5]: [VERIFY: Ch-3-4_v2 §A5 strand-genealogical position; defenders Hadot/MacIntyre/Gadamer/Strauss. The strand/tradition distinction is the thesis's load-bearing move here.]

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

The fault line sharpens when set against modes that did *not* run on it — which is also where §IIIA's plurality returns in a stronger, lived register. Yuk Hui's **cosmotechnics** supplies the framing: "technics is not anthropologically universal; technologies in different cultures are affected by the cosmological understandings of these cultures… technics is always cosmotechnics."[^c6] There has never been one single technics, only different cosmotechnics — which denies the universality of the `[TERMINOLOGY-PENDING]` mode at the level of its self-understanding, and treats non-Western artificial traditions as cosmologically different activities rather than lagged versions of one trajectory. Read through the thesis's vocabulary, Hui's "technics" is artificiality, and his point is that each civilization has its own categories of the made: where the Greeks had *technē* and *nomos* as their two great categories of the artificial, ancient China articulated the artificial differently, through 器 (*qi*) within a cosmotechnical order. The phenomenological complement is Watsuji Tetsurō's 風土 (*fūdo*) — climate-and-habitat not as bare geography but as "the agent by which human life is objectivised," the field within which a world becomes irreducibly particular and locally situated.[^c7] Hui gives the philosophical plurality of modes; Watsuji gives the *lived* plurality, each world emerging from and sustained by a different *fūdo*. The mode whose fault line totalizes is the one mode that mistakes its own cosmotechnic for the cosmos as such.

## §IIID — Universalization in operation

The material basis (§IIIB) and the epistemic fault line (§IIIC) are *dispositions* — generative tendencies. This section asks what universalization actually *does* when those dispositions operate at planetary scale. The answer reframes a familiar moral category as an ontological mechanism — and it is the mechanism Chapter VI will meet again in the metaverse.

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

Begin from something Chapter I makes available: components transfer across worlds at different costs, depending on the depth at which they operate. Using Chapter I's three layers of description — the enduring conditions or principles that organize things into structures or dynamics, which are then manifested in the context-specific phenomena experienceable by a subject[^d1] — the cost of cross-world transfer scales with the layer it touches. At the level of context-specific phenomena, a recipe, a tool, a textual genre transfers cheaply. At the level of structures and dynamics, an institution or practice — a parliamentary form, an examination system, a market mechanism — requires functional re-coding to operate within the recipient world. But at the level of enduring conditions or principles — the recipient world's foundational mode of artificiality — re-embedding is no longer adjustment but *replacement*: the recipient world's organizing principles are overwritten by the imposing world's. This is the structure of colonization.[^d2]

[^d1]: [VERIFY: DOCX [P0106] — use THIS de-jargoned three-layer language; do NOT use "real/actual/empirical."]

[^d2]: [VERIFY: Ch-3-4_v2 §A3 + the §III.4 v1 colonization-as-imposition material — relabelled to [P0106]'s three layers.]

Colonization, on this reading, is not best understood as a contingent moral injury layered onto an otherwise neutral cultural exchange. ## §IIIE — Counterarguments

Three counter-positions deserve explicit engagement, and the committee asked precisely for this — for the chapter to take up viewpoints it does not hold and say why it favours its own. The response to each preserves what is correct in the objection while showing why it does not undermine the chapter's claim.

The first objection holds that a disposition to dominate is not specifically this mode's at all, but a universal feature of complex artificiality: every world tool-makes, institution-builds, and intervenes in its environment — the Sinosphere's own 大禹治水 hydraulic engineering and its imperial deforestation among the examples. What is correct here is decisive and must be granted: the *capacity* for artificiality is universal, and every world is an operating system with organizing principles that direct its inhabitants. What the objection misses is the distinction between a *mode* of artificiality and its *disposition relative to other modes*. Most modes have organized their relations with other modes through exchange, conflict, alliance, syncretism, and coexistence that do not require displacing the other mode's organizing principles. This mode's disposition is structurally distinct: it treats its own principles as generic and other modes' as local approximations or failures — exactly what §IIIC's fault line names and §IIID's colonization formalizes. The capacity for artificiality is universal; *this disposition* is one mode's.

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

The second objection holds that the chapter's account is already available within the mode's own self-critical traditions — the Frankfurt School, post-structuralism, decolonial theory, science studies — so the thesis is redundant. What is correct: significant resources for the critique do exist within the mode's reflexive traditions, and the chapter draws on them, from Adorno and Horkheimer in §IIIC to Said and Sen in §IIID. What the objection misses is twofold. First, the existence of an internal critique *confirms* rather than refutes the structure it criticizes — that Romanticism arose as a reaction to instrumentalism shows the disposition was powerful enough to generate a reactive formation against itself. Second, the internal critiques typically operate at the level of specific institutional failures — the structures-and-dynamics and phenomena layers — without articulating the *enduring-principle* layer at which the disposition operates and which connects it to the squeeze. The internal traditions are sources, not redundancies. The thesis triangulates: internal critique and external comparison reach the same structural feature from different angles.

The third objection is the sharpest. It holds that to call "the `[TERMINOLOGY-PENDING]` mode" a real entity with a real disposition is to reproduce the very totalizing operation the chapter criticizes — and that the source-selection has been one-sided, picking critics of Enlightenment rationality over its defenders. What is correct: the thesis must avoid hypostatizing a configuration that is internally diverse, multiply contested, and historically variable, and a fully rigorous treatment would acknowledge that its chosen sources share a critical orientation. What the objection misses is the distinction between a *configuration* and a *culture*. The thesis tracks a configuration — a specific arrangement of material, epistemic, and institutional principles whose dispositional features can be characterized without flattening the diversity of its instances — not a homogeneous bloc. Hui's point recurs: the configuration's components circulate well beyond the geographical West — Shenzhen, Bangalore, São Paulo — and the disposition operates wherever they are adopted at the level of enduring principles. And the thesis's framework is structural-environmental, not moral-evaluative: it does not argue that Enlightenment rationality is worthless, only that this configuration tends toward totalization — a structural claim that does not require settling the normative debate.[^e1]

[^e1]: [VERIFY: Ch-3-1 Positions A/B/C; the combinatorial-structure synthesis in Ch-3-4_v2 §A3.]

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

This is where the cherry-picking worry of §IIIA finally becomes answerable, through what I will call the **combinatorial-structure** formulation. Domination is widespread; what is distinctive about *this* mode is not that it dominates but that it *integrated* a specific compound of components into a self-reinforcing loop that produced a planet-scale technospheric outcome — a Christian universalism, a substance-metaphysics, a colonial extractive base, a Newtonian mechanism, and a logic of capitalist accumulation, no one of them unique, but no other tradition having assembled all of them together.[^e2] This concedes that domination exists widely; preserves the genealogical specificity the squeeze hypothesis needs; makes the argument falsifiable in principle — find a tradition outside this lineage that assembled the same compound and produced a comparable technosphere; and avoids essentialism, since the compound is contingent and historical, not a civilizational essence. The disposition is what the coal, the Republic of Letters, and the silver circuits *all* actualize — a real generative tendency underdetermined by any single trigger, which is exactly why the thesis need not adjudicate the Great Divergence debate to make its claim. The disposition operates *underneath* the historiographical argument over triggers and timings: Pomeranz's coal does not refute it, and Mokyr's Republic of Letters does not confirm it.[^e3]

## §IIIF — The historical plurality of modes, and their collapses, as evidence for plurality

The chapter closes by returning to §IIIA's plurality and strengthening it with the historical record — but the framing here matters, because it is easy to get wrong. The record of *many* modes of artificiality across history, and of their documented collapses and even extinctions, is **not** a parade of failed modes that ranks configurations by survival. It is evidence that a plurality of *actual* worlds existed *prior to* universalization — that the world was many worlds before one mode set out to make itself the only one. Read this way, §IIIF reinforces §IIIA rather than opening a separate argument about collapse.

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

The structural premise, recalled from §IIIA and Chapter I: modes of artificiality are *adaptational operating systems*, and any adaptational strategy is subject to the constraint that under sufficient environmental pressure it can fail to maintain itself. The historical and paleontological record documents this across the whole spectrum of operating systems.[^f1] At the worlds-end, civilizational collapse is documented by a convergent literature — Tainter's decreasing marginal return on complexity, where "after a certain point, increased investments in complexity fail to yield proportionately increasing returns";[^f2] Diamond's five-factor framework, with a society's response-capacity decisive;[^f3] Brook on the Ming-Qing climate-economic disjunction;[^f4] Schwartz and Nichols on the regeneration-base that lets post-collapse populations reorganize.[^f5] At the species-end, the natural-extinction record — the great mass extinctions, and the steady background extinction beneath them — documents the same structural form at the scale of life itself.[^f6]

## Close — one mode among many, and the precondition it sets

The chapter has argued that the `[TERMINOLOGY-PENDING]` mode is one mode of artificiality among many actual worlds (§IIIA, §IIIF), and that its present planetary dominance rests on a specific compound: a material basis in the geography-to-production-to-worldview triangle (§IIIB); an epistemic fault line that gives its universalization a totalizing form (§IIIC); and a mechanism — imposition at the level of enduring principles — by which it has overwritten other worlds (§IIID). These work together. The material basis enables the global reach; the fault line organizes how that reach is intellectually justified; the imposition is what the disposition *does* when it meets another mode. The counterarguments (§IIIE) sharpen the claim rather than defeating it, because the claim is about a *configuration* with a *combinatorial* structure, held at *strand* level, and framed structurally rather than morally.

What the chapter sets up is the precondition the squeeze needs. Universalization is the disposition by which a *made* world comports itself as the *given* universe — and its terminal form, where the made hardens into a background substrate that confronts humans the way nature does, is the metaverse-stage claim reserved for Chapters V and VI. Chapter IV turns inward, to what universalization does to the *inhabitants* of the mode whose disposition has succeeded at planetary scale — Uegaki's pursuit of 〈意のままになる生〉, life-as-willed, and the self-finishing tendency it produces. Chapter V examines the parallel tendency at the level of artificiality itself; Chapter VI shows how these, together with the universalization mechanism of the present chapter, produce the structural condition the thesis calls the squeeze.[^philosophy-role]

It claims that the disposition this chapter names is a real generative mechanism whose *articulation* requires a philosophical register — because the convergent findings of sociology (empirical patterns), history (a contingent trajectory), and science-and-technology studies (technical-material networks) cannot be brought together *as evidence for a single underlying mechanism* in any one discipline's native vocabulary alone.
