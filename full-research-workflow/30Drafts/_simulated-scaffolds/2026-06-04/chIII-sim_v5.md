## Opener — One mode among the many actual worlds

Chapter I established that a world is a human-made habitat — an adaptational operating system (AOS) nested within, and irreducible to, the universe that precedes and outlasts it; Chapter II asked what such a world is *made of*, and answered with a single umbrella category, artificiality, the whole range of humanly-produced organizing conditions, material and nonmaterial alike, by which a world is constituted. Both chapters held to the type. This chapter turns to an instance. I argue that one mode of artificiality among the many — the mode whose lineage runs from the ancient Mediterranean through early-modern Europe to the present planetary order, and which I follow the thesis in calling **the Enlightenment mode of artificiality** — has acquired a disposition that most modes did not: a disposition to overwrite the organizing conditions of every other world it meets, and to carry its own made world as though that world were the given universe. I call this disposition **universalization**. The chapter's task is to show that it belongs to *one* mode and not to artificiality as such — for that disposition is the precondition the squeeze hypothesis will later need. The metaverse-stage squeeze of the closing chapters becomes intelligible only because *this* mode first made its artificiality planetary.

One pair of terms must be disambiguated at the outset, because the thesis's own vocabulary invites the confusion. The **universe**, in Chapter I, is the given substrate — the encompassing background "that is the source of laws, energies, or matters that are indifferent and unaffectable by anything" ([P0119]), the totality Uexküll would call the *Umgebung*, "neither a god nor a man" having made it. A **world** is a made meaning-space, an *Umwelt*, nested within that substrate. *Universalization*, for all the echo, is therefore **not** a property of the universe. It is a property of a *world*: the process by which one made world comes to comport itself as if it *were* the given universe — unbounded, self-evident, the measure against which every other world figures only as an approximation or a failure. In the thesis's own gloss, drawn from Uexküll, universalization "can be understood as an act of treating what is of a specific *Umwelt* as what is of the *Umgebung* of all" ([P0111]). That a made world should harden, in the limit, into a substrate no one chose is the trajectory the thesis will later name with the non-literal provocation "*tekhnē* becoming *phusis*"; but that phrase belongs to the metaverse-stage argument of the final chapters, and here it is only foreshadowed, never argued.

A note on vocabulary, held throughout. As in Chapter II, the thesis does not use "technology" as a major concept; where the philosophy-of-technology literature writes "technology," I read artificiality in the broad sense, so that the disposition examined here is built not into *technology* — the capacity for artificiality is a human universal — but into one *mode* of artificiality. And I retire the prefix "neo-": where the later argument needs a noun for the self-completing artificial background, it says "metaverse-stage artificiality," because what is at issue is a change of standing, not merely of date.

The chapter proceeds in seven movements. **A** recovers the plurality of worlds and shows that a world *can* be squeezed against its universe — a figure already met, in two thinner forms, in the preceding chapters. **B** reads collapse as the extreme case of that squeeze, and isolates the regeneration base that has, until now, always survived it. **C** defends the name "the Enlightenment mode" against "Western" and "modern." **D** and **E** give, respectively, the material and the nonmaterial conditions of the mode's rise and its universalization. **F** rebuts the reading that universalization is evidence of fitness. **G** turns toward Chapter IV.

## A. The plurality of worlds and the universe–world squeeze

It is best to begin not with a theory of worlds but with a difficulty about them, one Chapter I has already pressed: that what a world takes a *person* to be is not the same from world to world, and the differences do not reduce to a single underlying fact about persons. In the late-Republican and Imperial Roman world a person was first a position within a legal household, so that an adult son — "even a married senator commanding armies — owned no property in his own name" while his father lived ([P0103], after Saller, 1994). In the Classic Maya world a person was "not a single bounded individual but a divisible, permeable constellation composed of multiple substances," a "mosaic corporeality" distributed across detachable parts ([P0103], after Houston & Stuart, 1998). In the Han-Chinese world a person was at once a node in the Five Relationships (五倫), "as relational persons … rather than as individual selves," and a registered unit of the state, the household-registration apparatus (編戶齊民) not counting pre-existing individuals so much as constituting them as taxable, conscriptable subjects ([P0103], after Ames & Rosemont, 2011; Tu, 1990). The point is not that these worlds *disagreed* about a common object, "the person," but that the object itself was differently composed in each. As Chapter I put it, a person teleported between them, even with full fluency in the destination's language, "would not be able to function seamlessly"; the traveller is transferable only as a component to be recalibrated against the destination's organizing conditions. The incommensurability is the evidence; the plurality of worlds is the inference.

The plurality at issue must be stated with care, because it is easily mistaken for a weaker and more familiar claim. The thesis does not say merely that worlds are *made* rather than *found* — Chapter I secured that through niche construction and Obara's dual ecosystems — nor that there are many *possible* worlds arrayed, as unrealised alternatives, around a single real one. It says that there are many *actual* worlds: each a realised adaptational operating system, a different mode of artificiality brought into being under different conditions, none of them the world fixed and found. The personhood cases just rehearsed are not competing descriptions of one underlying person; they are different persons, actually composed and actually lived in different worlds. This is plurality at the level of what *is*, not of what might have been — and it is the level the squeeze hypothesis requires, since nothing can be squeezed between a real world and a merely possible one.[^a-goodman]

Anthropology supplies a second register of the same plurality, provided it is not mistaken for the whole inventory. Descola's comparative ontology sorts the ways human collectives compose the boundary between themselves and what surrounds them into four schemas, according to whether a being's *interiority* (its inwardness, soul, point of view) and its *physicality* (its material form) are judged similar to or different from a human's. In **animism**, interiorities are continuous across humans, animals, and plants while physicalities differ — the animal is a person in a different body. In **totemism**, both interiority and physicality are shared within a totemic class that cuts across the human/non-human line. In **analogism**, both differ, and the world is re-knitted by a dense web of correspondences among its singular fragments. In **naturalism** — the schema of the modern West — physicalities are continuous (one nature, described by one science) while interiorities are held to be uniquely human, yielding "a single unifying nature and a multiplicity of cultures" (Descola, 2013: 201 [VERIFY: p.]). Descola's quartet is one illustration of the plurality, not its measure: I use it to make vivid that the modern settlement is *one* way of composing a world among others — and, looking ahead, that the Enlightenment mode's "nature" is the naturalist schema universalized — while declining to treat the four as an exhaustive grid, since to fix the plurality into a single master typology would be to perform the very totalization this chapter is against. Each of these is, in the thesis's terms, an enduring organizing principle on which a mode of artificiality, and so a world as an AOS, is built.

If worlds are made against a given, then what is made can fall into *strain* with that given — and here the thesis's central figure, the squeeze, must be stated with more care than a slogan allows, because it arrives in stronger and weaker forms. The preceding chapters have already shown two weaker forms. The Great Oxidation Event (Chapter I) was one: the proto-cyanobacteria so altered the atmosphere they lived in that the traits adapted to the older air became maladaptive, and most life was extinguished or driven to refugia. The hominin predicament (Chapter II) was another: an under-specialised body, its inherited equipment lagging the habitat it now had to live in, was driven into artificiality as its compensating environment. Both, in the thesis's own terms, are *dyadic and diachronic*: a single inhabitant's inherited structures and dynamics lag behind a changed environment's structures and dynamics, across time ([P0073]). The universe–world squeeze this chapter needs is stronger. It is *triadic and synchronic*: an inhabitant stands at once within two environments whose demands need not agree — and the disagreement can reach below the level of structures and dynamics to the level of *enduring organizing principles* themselves ([P0073]). A world emerges as a collective answer to the structures and dynamics of its local universe; but as that world elaborates its own principles and the structures they organise, the conditions it now requires can come to exceed what its background universe will supply. The founding fit becomes a misfit. This is the universe–world squeeze.

Two thinkers give this its content, and their words are load-bearing enough to be set out rather than gestured at. Obara, tracing the human "artificial ecosystem" (人工生態系) back to the domestication that detached the human food-chain from the wild one, names the widening gap between what the made order presupposes and what the body and its niche can still deliver a *ズレ* — a slippage, a lag, a being-out-of-register. The point is not metaphorical: domesticated existence runs increasingly on an internal logic of its own, "selected for function rather than ecologically given" ([P0087]), so that the registers of the artificial and the natural slide apart. Uegaki, building directly on Obara and carried forward into the chapters on as-willedness, hardens the *ズレ* into a *亀裂* — a fracture, a severance — between the artificial and the natural orders: a "切断," a cutting-apart of what was once continuous (Uegaki, 2022a: 110 [VERIFY: p.]). The escalation from Obara's slippage to Uegaki's fracture is exactly the escalation the squeeze needs: a slippage is a misfit one might still close; a fracture names a misfit that has reached the organizing principles, where the two orders no longer share the terms in which a fit could be measured. In comparative history the same dynamic is visible in a flatter register, and I borrow it à la carte: Morris's observation that "as social development rises, the resources it demands change," and that societies "push through" the resulting bottlenecks by adopting whatever innovations are at hand (Morris, 2010: [VERIFY: p.]) — taken as a description of the demand-outrunning-supply dynamic, and pointedly *not* as his picture of history as a race between homogeneous regional teams, a picture §C will have to set aside.

Here, finally, is the qualification that governs the whole chapter: a world *can* be squeezed; it is not *fated* to be. The squeeze is a risk a world runs, not a law it obeys — and it is precisely because the risk is real that so many traditions have made its *avoidance* their highest wisdom rather than a footnote to it. The Confucian aspiration to a "unity of Heaven and humanity" (天人合一), the Daoist counsel of *wu wei* — "refraining from activity contrary to Nature," a cultivation that produces "without possessing, acts without self-assertion, develops without dominating" (Needham, 1956: [VERIFY: p.]) — the reciprocity protocols of many indigenous orders: these are not decorative pieties but *squeeze-prevention*, the deliberate keeping of a world's structures, dynamics, and governing principles within what its background universe can sustain. A world that cultivates such restraint is one that has organised itself *not* to outrun its given. It follows — and this is the hinge into the rest of the chapter — that a mode which abandons restraint for intervention, taking its relation to the universe to be one of mastery rather than fit, is a mode that does not merely *risk* the squeeze but *courts* it. That mode is the subject of everything that follows.

## B. Collapse and the regeneration base

Collapse is the universe–world squeeze in its extreme case: the point at which a world can no longer satisfy the conditions it has come to require, and the order it sustained gives way. I want to read it carefully, because the reading is what §F will need: collapse is **mismatch, not unfitness** — a morphing universe meeting an over-reaching world — and not a verdict that the fallen world deserved to fall.

The fall of Ming China is the cleanest case, and Timothy Brook has given it twice. In *The Price of Collapse* he makes the squeeze quantitative, taking the price of grain as the index of a world failing against its universe. As the Little Ice Age deepened — plunging, late in the 1630s, toward the coldest phase on record — harvests failed, and famine grain prices, which Brook calls "the longest series of prices in China before the eighteenth century" (Brook, 2023: 106), climbed past what people could pay; contemporaries reached for "the price of rice to quantify the disaster and track its course" (Brook, 2023: 5). His causal claim is pointed: "global climate, not global trade, drove Ming grain prices to crippling levels" (Brook, 2023: 105). Read in the thesis's terms, this is the squeeze with its layers exposed: a shift in the universe's *structures and dynamics* (the cold, the failed harvest) met a world whose own structures and dynamics — a silver-monetised fiscal-military order calibrated to a warmer mean — could no longer be satisfied, until, as Brook puts it, the witness to "the collapse of the Ming price regime" was watching "the collapse of the Ming political regime"; Beijing fell to rebels in April 1644 (Brook, 2023: 131). Two details matter for the argument. First, the Ming was not a world without squeeze-prevention: it "maintained a system of grain storage explicitly to adjust the price of grain in times of dearth" (Brook, 2023: 111) — an ever-normal-granary apparatus built, exactly in the spirit of §A, to keep the world within what its universe could give — and what failed was that a downturn of sufficient depth overwhelmed the apparatus, not that no apparatus existed. Second, Brook is scrupulous in the way §F will require: the dynasty "was not singlehandedly destroyed by climate, but its collapse cannot be explained in the absence of climate and human responses to climate" (Brook, 2023: x). In *The Troubled Empire* he supplies the longer rhythm — the "sloughs," nine extended disasters across the Yuan and Ming, the Chongzhen slough at the close the worst on record — and the same multi-causal verdict: "fiscal insolvency? rebellions? Manchu military might? the weather? … their combination was what brought down the house of the Ming" (Brook, 2010: 255). That multi-causality *is* the squeeze: not one cause indicting the world, but a convergence in which the universe's pressure and the world's over-extension meet at the level of structures, dynamics, and the principles that organise them.

Two consequences follow, and both bear on the chapter's larger claim. The first is that what came after the Ming was *a different world*, not the absence of one. The population "came out the other side of the Chongzhen slough to find themselves the subjects of a new dynasty" (Brook, 2010: 255), and the Qing was not the Ming restored but a substantially different adaptational operating system. It integrated the steppe and the sown under a single sovereign — Mongol (蒙古), Tibetan (吐蕃), and Inner-Asian/Turkestani (新疆) domains held with the Han core (華夏) in a multi-constituency imperial logic no prior dynasty had run: what Crossley calls the Qing's "simultaneous" (*hebi* / *kamcime*) emperorship, its edicts issued at once in Manchu, Chinese, Mongolian, Tibetan, and the Arabic script of Central-Asian Muslims, addressing each constituency in its own idiom rather than a single Confucian mould (Crossley, 1999: 10–11; the anti-sinicization thesis cf. Rawski, 1998: [VERIFY: p.]). It re-installed identity at the level of the body — what Elliott names the Qing's "ethnic sovereignty," a "*pax Manjurica*, not *pax Sinica*," with the imposed queue and Manchu dress as its visible token and the Eight Banners as the frame of a distinctively Manchu rule (Elliott, 2001: 2–5). And it policed the integrity of its organising principles directly: the literary inquisitions (文字獄) of the Qianlong reign — at their height during the *Siku quanshu* compilation — functioning as control protocols for the new order's ideological integrity (Guy, 1987: [VERIFY: p.]). That 1644 "was only the end of the Ming, not the end of the imperial system" (Brook, 2010: 23) is precisely the point: a world ended and another succeeded it. Collapse, read this way, is itself *evidence of the plurality of worlds* — worlds succeed worlds, each a distinct AOS, and the record of succession is the historical face of §A's plurality of actual worlds.

The second consequence is the one the whole chapter turns on. Collapse, historically, has left a *regeneration base*. Tainter's analysis keeps the phenomenon from being read moralistically: collapse is what happens when "after a certain point, increased investments in complexity fail to yield proportionately increasing returns," so that a society economises by shedding complexity — a "return to lower complexity," not a catastrophe deserved (Tainter, 1988: [VERIFY: p.]). And Schwartz and Nichols give the recovery its mechanism: "it is likely that a crucial factor in regeneration is the survival of institutions or ideas from the era before collapse, supplying a base for the eventual re-creation of complex societies" (Schwartz & Nichols, 2006: [VERIFY: p.]); collapse "is almost never total," and the rural, vernacular substrate that survives is what new worlds are grown back from. This is the hinge into the second half of the chapter. Every collapse considered so far has been the collapse of *one world among many*, each leaving a floor from which other worlds could regrow. The danger the Enlightenment mode poses — argued across §C–§F and consummated only in the closing chapters — is that, by universalizing, it threatens to break this pattern: a single planetary monoculture-of-mode would make the squeeze planetary and consume the regeneration base itself, leaving no vernacular floor outside it from which to begin again. Plurality, on this reading, is not a luxury of the species; it is the species' regeneration base.

## C. Why "the Enlightenment mode"

If something is universalizing, the thesis must say *what*; and "the Enlightenment mode" is a name that has to earn its keep. The responsible way to fix it is not to assert a portrait of the mode on the thesis's own authority and call it history, but to begin from the most authoritative accounts of what "the Enlightenment" was, and then ask which feature of it the thesis means to name. Those accounts converge. The encyclopedic characterisation traces the Enlightenment to "its primary origin in the scientific revolution of the 16th and 17th centuries" and centres it on a confidence in the unaided intellect, a "suspicion or hostility toward other forms … of authority (such as tradition, superstition, prejudice)," and above all the Newtonian conception of "nature as an orderly domain governed by strict mathematical-dynamical laws" — a nature reconceived as "a very complicated machine," disenchanted and fully law-governed (Bristow, 2017: SEP, "Enlightenment", §1.4). Kant gives the motto: enlightenment is "man's emergence from his self-imposed immaturity," that immaturity being the "inability to use one's understanding without guidance from another," its watchword *sapere aude* — "Have courage to use your own understanding!" (Kant, 1784 / Humphrey trans.: Ak. 8:35). And Horkheimer and Adorno give the critical counter-reading the thesis most needs: the programme of enlightenment "was the disenchantment of the world" (Horkheimer & Adorno, 2002: 1), yet the same reason that "aimed at liberating human beings from fear and installing them as masters" turns total — "enlightenment is totalitarian" (2002: 4) — so that what human beings "seek to learn from nature is how to use it to dominate wholly both it and human beings" (2002: 2), the mastery of outer nature recoiling at last into mastery *over* human beings themselves.

From these accounts the thesis takes one thread and leaves the rest. "The Enlightenment mode" does not name the whole eighteenth-century settlement — not its emancipatory politics, its tolerance, or its critical public sphere; it marks the single feature on which the canonical definitions agree and which the chapter builds upon: the installation of one nature-mastering, tradition-displacing, universal reason as the measure of all worlds. Mapped onto the chapter's own witnesses, the convergence is exact. The disenchanted "nature as a machine under one set of laws" of the encyclopedias is Descola's *naturalism* (§A) — one nature, many cultures. Kant's autonomous, universal reason is the very faculty Bhaskar will show "totalizes itself and excludes other aspects of reality and ways of knowing" (§E; [P0060]). The mechanistic mastery-stance toward nature is what Needham, comparatively, calls "interventionism" (§D). And Horkheimer and Adorno's dialectic of enlightenment is the period-internal name for what the thesis calls universalization, with Hui's *monotechnology* its technical terminus. "The Enlightenment mode of artificiality," then, is not a coinage imposed on the period from outside but the period's own dominant strand, lifted from intellectual movement to *mode of artificiality* and followed to its planetary disposition — which is why the name marks the juncture at which that strand fused and went global, without claiming any one of its elements began there.

Why not, then, simply call it "Western"? Two reasons, the second decisive. First, "Western" essentialises a region and a people, smuggling back in the homogeneity the thesis denies — as if the geography carried the disposition in its soil. Second, and more importantly, *the mode outlives Western regional dominance*. Suppose the coming century is led from the East rather than the West; this would not touch the thesis at all, provided the leading order still runs the interventionist, totalizing, mastery-seeking mode. A geographically Eastern future that operates the Enlightenment mode is that mode *universalized*, not refuted. This is exactly where the thesis parts from a historian like Morris: Morris asks which *region* will lead and narrates a contest between regional teams; the thesis is indifferent to the locale and asks which *mode of artificiality* rules — and the two questions are orthogonal, so that an Eastern-led future is entirely compatible with the principles grounding that lead being an extension of the Enlightenment mode. Hui makes the corresponding point from the side of artificiality: the danger is *monotechnology* and the homogenisation of technical worlds — that "the slogan of pluralism … is accompanied by the disappearance of … cosmologies" — and his counter-concept, cosmotechnics or technodiversity, is a name for the very plurality the universalized mode erases, on whichever continent it is run (Hui, 2016: [VERIFY: p.19]). "Western" points at a map; "the Enlightenment mode" points at the thing that travels off the map.

Why not "modern"? Because "modern" is an empty temporal index — the ever-receding now — that names *when* and not *what*, and so leaves the regress of "post-", "post-post-", "neo-", "neo-neo-" unsolved: every successor is dated rather than characterised, and the substance keeps slipping out from under the period-word. "The Enlightenment mode" names the *what* — a lineage and a set of triangulated features — and so can survive the calendar. (The thesis lets "universalization" carry the trans-historical work, and reserves the period-word for the modern culmination, where the features fused; the placeholder of earlier drafts is, as of the current Chapter I, resolved in favour of this name.)

## D. The material conditions of the mode's rise

The mode rose; the question is why, and the answer is not "because it was superior." It is that a specific and partly fortuitous set of material conditions let an interventionist world break a constraint that had bounded every world before it. Three bodies of work anchor the account — Needham on the comparison that makes the mode visible as one mode, Pomeranz on the contingency of its escape, and Miura on the feedback by which the escape entrenched itself — and because the chapter has been faulted for name-dropping where it should argue, each is given room to speak.

Joseph Needham spent half a century on a double question — why modern science arose in Europe and not in China, given China's long technological lead — and his mature answer, set out in the *Conclusions* to the final volume of *Science and Civilisation in China* (Needham et al., 2004), refuses both racial essentialism and idealist intellectual history. It roots the divergence in material conditions, insisting that however great the importance of a world-view, it "can never obscure the basic fact that underneath lie the material forces of climate, geography and social integration" (Needham et al., 2004 [SCC Vol. 7, Pt. 2]: [VERIFY: p.]). The chain Needham draws runs from a geo-ecological base, through a dominant mode of production and its institutions, to a metaphysics and a stance toward nature, and so to a characteristic technoscience — and it runs differently in the two cases. Set side by side, the two configurations make the thesis's point legible at a glance: it is the *ecological* difference that favours a mechanistic over an organicist metaphysics, and an interventionist over a non-interventionist technoscience.

**Table III.1 — the two modes after Needham (2004): the chain from geo-ecology to technoscience**

| | **China — the organicist mode** | **Europe / Mediterranean — the mechanist mode** |
|---|---|---|
| **Geo-ecological base** | continental land-mass; agrarian; dependent on large-scale irrigation and water-control | archipelagic and maritime; trade-borne; a pastoral-and-seafaring inheritance |
| **Dominant mode of production** | hydraulic-agrarian, centrally managed; merchants kept subordinate | mercantile and merchant-adventurer, tending to capitalist accumulation |
| **Characteristic institutions** | a centralised imperial bureaucracy that "absolutely prevented the rise of the merchants and the coming into being of capitalism" | autonomous city-states and an independent merchant class |
| **Metaphysics** | organicism / correlative thinking — the cosmos an organism of immanent pattern (理), "the mirror image of Chinese bureaucratic society" | atomism passing into mechanism — the cosmos a machine of separable parts under externally legislated law |
| **Axiology / stance to nature** | non-interventionist: *wu wei*, accord, harmony with the given | interventionist: mastery and domination, "so natural to a people of shepherds and sea-farers" |
| **Characteristic technoscience** | observational and correlative — "propitious for the development of the natural sciences" yet non-experimental | experimental-interventionist — "the more complete the domination of Nature, the more fully does it become possible … to increase … control of individual human behaviour" |

The metaphysics row is the one Needham documents most carefully, and it deserves his own voice. It is no "mere coincidence that the former [Taoist organicism] arose in a highly organised … bureaucratic … society while the latter [Democritean atomism] arose in a world of city-states and individual merchant-adventurers," for "Chinese bureaucratism was fundamentally agrarian … as opposed to the maritime emphasis of the European city-states" (Needham, 1956 [SCC Vol. 2]: 337–338 [VERIFY:]); the whole correlative cosmology was, "in one sense, the mirror image of Chinese bureaucratic society" (Needham, 1956: 288 [VERIFY:]). The earlier *Grand Titration* compresses the same chain into its "four factors" — "geographical, hydrological, social and economic" — and may be cited in support, but the load-bearing statement is the later *Conclusions*, where Needham settles the materialist underwriting (Needham, 1969: [VERIFY: p.]).

Two disciplines must travel with this. The first is Needham's own refusal to read the contrast as East-good, West-bad: the left-hand column is a *different configuration*, not a verdict of worth, and he held the non-interventionist conception "propitious for the development of the natural sciences" even where it had limits. The second is that the thesis takes from Needham the *contrast* and pointedly *not* his teleology: Needham imagined the "ethnic cultural rivers" all flowing at last into the single "ocean of modern science," and that image of one science as the destination of all is precisely the universalist self-understanding this chapter is trying to relativise. The interventionist mode is one mode that universalized; it is not the sea every river was always seeking.

Kenneth Pomeranz supplies the contingency that Needham's longue durée leaves open: granting that the interventionist mode *could* reach planetary scale once some configuration came online, why did it, and when? Pomeranz's answer dismantles the story of an inner European virtue. Europe was, on his account, "a none-too-unusual economy," and the Great Divergence was "late and contingent … not wholly produced within Europe" (Pomeranz, 2021: x). It was not superior capital or institutions that decided it, for the Lower Yangzi delta was a core every bit as developed — it "may have produced as much cloth per capita in 1750 as Britons did in 1800," so that the honest question reverses into "why didn't England become the Yangzi Delta?" (Pomeranz, 2021: 23, 27). The binding constraint everywhere was land and energy, and Europe — Britain first — escaped it through "two crucial discontinuities, one created by coal and one by colonies" (Pomeranz, 2021: 7).

Each discontinuity, in Pomeranz's telling, was a windfall and not an achievement, and this is what §F will need. English coal lay "in particularly fortunate places," near water transport in a society already starved of fuel, so that the breakthrough rested "as much on geographic accident" as on ingenuity — "fortuitous, and specifically English, conditions" (Pomeranz, 2021: 36, 66, 72). And the New World supplied what Pomeranz calls "ghost acreage": a land-equivalent, in sugar, cotton, timber, and silver, that Europe's own soil could never have yielded — on his reckoning some 25–30 million "ghost acres," a contribution "exceeding even" that of coal, each acre of Caribbean sugar sparing many acres of European wheat-equivalent (Pomeranz, 2021: 274–276). The rise of the mode was thus a fortunate freak compounded by a vast externalisation, its costs displaced onto colonised land and labour and onto the deep carbon past — a point I will be careful, in §F, to state as a fact about *fragility-accruing drawdown* and not as a moral complaint.

三浦永光 (Miura) completes the picture by closing the loop. On Miura's account the mechanistic view of nature — nature as "pure matter wholly devoid of anything mental" — was not a neutral discovery but a *convenient* one: it freed the human being "from the old religious view of nature, from taboos, reverence, and ethical restraint," and so "answered precisely to the demand of capitalism and the modern state for domination over nature" (Miura, 2006: 162). That mastery was paid for elsewhere: the colonies became "resource-supply zones," the "periphery" of a "core"–"periphery" "modern world-system" whose unequal division of labour "still basically persists" between advanced and developing nations after formal empire ended (Miura, 2006: 233–234). And this is not mere extraction but the manufacture of *dependence*: the colony is "pinned" (押しとどめられた) in the position of resource-supplier and dumping-market, so that — its own self-sufficient base dismantled — the developing country "must export resources in great quantity to earn foreign currency," a forced export that accelerates its own ecological ruin even as it feeds the industrial nations' wealth, and that "still basically persists" long after formal empire ended (三浦/Miura, 2004: 15–16). And the windfall does not settle the mode; it ratchets it — each technical fix, drawing further on the buffer, "falls into a vicious cycle (悪循環) in which, at the very moment it achieves its specific purpose … it simultaneously produces new evils and intractable problems" (Miura, 2006: 156). Intervention yields surplus, surplus underwrites further intervention, and the mode's self-image as the universal and necessary form of human world-making hardens with each turn. The upshot, which §F will require, is plain: the mode rose by accident and by drawing down a buffer, not by an inner virtue — and contingency at the origin is the first thing the fitness reading cannot accommodate.

## E. The nonmaterial conditions of the mode's universalization

The windfalls of §D explain how the Enlightenment mode *rose*; they do not explain how it came to be *universalized* — projected as the universe, installed as the measure to which every other world must answer. Rise is material; universalization is, at root, a matter of how the mode *knows*. The nonmaterial driver is a particular structure of knowing, and I locate it — as the spine of this section, not as one option among equals — in Roy Bhaskar's diagnosis of what he calls the primal squeeze. The neighbouring critiques of Enlightenment reason each light up a real facet; Bhaskar is the spine because the thesis needs the one thing only he supplies: a realist, ontological, nature-relating account of what the mode does to *being*.

Bhaskar's diagnosis is of a fault in the Western theory of knowledge — and it is essential to the thesis's use of him that the diagnosis is, by his own framing, an account of *Western* philosophy's development and not a claim about human thought as such. He finds Western thought "stuck … on what I am going to characterise as the Platonic/Aristotelian fault-line" (Bhaskar, 2009: 4): an oscillation between a pole that takes the universal and formal as most real and a pole that takes immanent particulars as most real, neither able to secure the structured, mind-independent depth that the working scientist's practice in fact presupposes. The mechanism by which this fault generates a totalizing knowledge is what he names the *epistemic fallacy*: "the analysis or definition of statements about being in terms of statements about our knowledge of being," the "ontification of knowledge … characteristically spawning what, following Adorno, can be called 'identity-thinking'" (Bhaskar, 2009: 201 [VERIFY:]). When knowledge is made the measure of reality, whatever cannot be known in the privileged mode — the formal, the calculable, the universal — is denied full reality; and the indubitable-foundation that the fallacy seeks "inevitably splits reality into two: that which conforms to its criterion and that which does not" (Bhaskar, 2008: [VERIFY:]). That split is the engine of the mode's drive to *totalize* — to render all of being available to one form of reason — and to *exclude* — to consign the unconformable to the status of the backward and the not-yet-known. It is, in just the thesis's words for it, a knowledge that "totalizes itself and excludes other aspects of reality and ways of knowing" ([P0060]).

I want to extend Bhaskar one step, and to be exact about where the extension lies. Bhaskar gives this totalizing knowledge a deeper name, the *anthropic fallacy* — "the analysis of being in terms of human being," with its two faces, an *anthropomorphism* that reads the cosmos in the image of mind and an *anthropocentrism* that makes the human the measure of the real. My extension is to identify the Enlightenment mode as the historical form in which this fallacy goes planetary — what I call **enlightenment-morphism / enlightenment-centrism**: the projection of being only in the terms of the Enlightenment mode, which is, in the thesis's vocabulary, universalization itself — the carrying of a made *world* as the given *universe*. The warrant is in Bhaskar, who treats "any regional geo-historical variant of anthropocentrism (e.g. Eurocentrism)" as an ethnocentrism that assumes "an extreme form … in post/modernity." But the deeper warrant is one I should make explicit against my own earlier over-statement: because Bhaskar's diagnosis is *of the Western philosophical inheritance specifically* — the Platonic/Aristotelian fault-line is not a universal feature of human reason, and it would be absurd to find it constitutive of Māori or Amazonian thought — the "anthropic" in this particular diagnosis is *already*, in substance, the Enlightenment "man." The fallacy Bhaskar anatomises is not a sin every culture commits in equal measure (though any might); it is the specific intellectual signature of one lineage. So the extension is less a stretch of Bhaskar than a making-explicit of the locus his own diagnosis already occupies — with the contribution mine: the historicising of the fallacy into one *mode* and its planetary universalization, the coining of the mode-specific name, the recasting of the error in the world/universe vocabulary, and the yoking of this nonmaterial driver to the material drivers of §D. Two guardrails remain non-negotiable. The error named is a *projection*: the mode mistakes its made world for the given universe; it is not that there is no given universe — the thesis stays realist about the substrate. And "other ways of knowing" must mean other fallible ways of knowing the *same* universe, plural routes to one mind-independent ground, not a flat relativism that would dissolve the thesis's own realism along with the mode's pretension.

That the diagnosis is not Bhaskar's idiosyncrasy is shown by three neighbours who reach the same totalizing knowledge from other directions — and each deserves to be argued, not named. Adorno and Horkheimer give the *pathos* and the self-undoing: in *Dialectic of Enlightenment* the program of disenchantment, having dissolved myth into knowledge, turns on its own concepts until "enlightenment is totalitarian," and reason, having made itself the measure of all things, can give no account of the ends it serves; their "identity-thinking" — the subsumption of the non-identical particular under the concept — is the same liquidation of what resists the criterion that Bhaskar describes, and they alone keep steadily in view that this knowledge is bound up with the *domination of nature* (Horkheimer & Adorno, 2002: [VERIFY: p.]). Their limit, for the thesis, is that *Dialectic of Enlightenment* is ontology-shy: it diagnoses the drive with unmatched force but, committed to a negative dialectics, declines to ground what is flattened in a stratified account of being — which is the office Bhaskar fills. Derrida gives the *form* of the split: logocentrism, the metaphysics of presence, the privileging of one term of every pair (speech over writing, presence over absence) as the self-evident origin — a precise anatomy of how a knowledge installs one pole as the measure and reads the other as derivative (Derrida, 1998: [VERIFY: p.]). His limit is the mirror of his strength: in dissolving the stability of the referent, he supplies the *shape* of the totalizing operation but not the positively real thing it excludes, and the thesis, being realist about the universe, needs the latter. Habermas gives the *social* form: the "colonization of the lifeworld," the encroachment of the system-imperatives of money and administrative power upon the communicatively structured lifeworld, the displacement of understanding by steering-media (Habermas, 1985: [VERIFY: p.]). His limit is twofold — the diagnosis is social rather than nature-relating or ontological, and it frames the encroachment as in principle *resistible* by a renewed communicative reason, a hope too local for a squeeze the thesis takes to be planetary and to reach the level of being. Bhaskar alone names the positively real excluded middle, supplies a genealogy and a mechanism rather than a pathos or a periodisation, explains the totalizing drive *structurally* as built into a certainty-seeking knowledge, and integrates the epistemic, the ontological, and the nature-relation in a single figure — which is why he is the spine and the others the corroborating facets.

Three further witnesses, reached independently, confirm the projection without my prompting. Husserl, decades before Bhaskar, described the *mathematisation of nature*: Galileo, he writes, "substitutes the mathematically substructed world of idealities for the only real world, the world actually given … our everyday life-world," so that a "garb of ideas" (*Ideenkleid*) comes to be taken *for* true being (Husserl, 1970: 48–51 [VERIFY:]) — which is exactly the projection of a made world as the given universe, named in 1936. Heidegger's "mathematical projection of nature" and his "age of the world picture" describe modern science projecting nature in advance as a calculable nexus of forces, and the world becoming a *picture* set before a representing subject — the same move in another idiom (Heidegger, 1977: [VERIFY: p.]). And Dewey traced the whole apparatus to a motive, the "quest for certainty," the flight from a precarious world to the fixed and the immutable, and the spectator theory of knowledge it underwrites (Dewey, 1929: [VERIFY: p.]) — corroborating from the pragmatist side the very craving Bhaskar puts at the root of the squeeze. Four thinkers, four vocabularies, one structure. And because the projection does not merely *err* but *excludes*, the chapter ends this movement where the political and the epistemic meet: Mignolo's "colonial matrix of power," in which the "rhetoric of modernity" is inseparable from "the logic of coloniality," so that the universal is built by disqualifying other knowledges (Mignolo, 2011: [VERIFY: p.]); de Sousa Santos's "epistemicide," governed by an "abyssal line" that renders the knowledges on its far side not false but *non-existent*, outside the sayable (Santos, 2007: [VERIFY: p.]); and Hui's monotechnology, the technical terminus at which "the disappearance of … cosmologies" follows the universalisation of one. That Mignolo, Santos, and Hui each reach "Eurocentrism" without passing through Bhaskar is itself the strongest corroboration of the extension this section proposes.

The figure resolves into the thesis's own. Bhaskar's primal squeeze is *epistemic*: the structured, knowable middle is squeezed out of *knowledge*, between a reason that needs no world and an experience that yields no necessity. The thesis's squeeze is *ontological*: a made world is squeezed out of *being*, between the universe and the metaverse. It is the same figure — the elimination of the middle by a knowledge that admits only what conforms to its criterion — and Bhaskar's primal squeeze is the nonmaterial ancestor of the ontological one: the founding intellectual move which, once materially realised by the interventionism and externalisation of §D, becomes the planetary squeeze the rest of the thesis traces.

## F. Universalization is not fitness

A natural objection now stands in the way. If the Enlightenment mode has universalized — outcompeting, absorbing, or displacing every rival world — is that not just what it *looks like* for a mode to be the *fitter*: selected, superior, the winner? The objection must be met on its own ground, evolutionary rather than moral, and meeting it requires a distinction the earlier drafts blurred: between fitness *to a world* and fitness *to the universe*.

That the Enlightenment mode is the fitter *within the terms of its own world* is not in dispute; it is close to a tautology. A mode that prevails is, by that fact, the one that has set the standard of prevailing — gross product, energy throughput, technical power, rate of growth — so that "the Enlightenment mode is the fittest, by the Enlightenment mode's own metrics" is a circle and not a finding. Crosby states the ecological form of the point: "superiority" is meaningless except in relation to a particular environment and a particular fit (Crosby, 1986: [VERIFY: p.]); fitness is always fitness-*to*-a-context, never in the abstract. This is the incommensurability the thesis marks as F2, and it disposes of the *circular* version of the objection. But it does not yet answer the harder version, which grants the incommensurability of world-internal metrics and asks whether the mode is not, simply, fitter to the *universe* — better at persisting against the one substrate every world must answer to. That question cannot be dissolved by noting who keeps the scoreboard; it must be answered in the universe's own coin, which is endurance under real constraint.

Answered there, the verdict reverses, and this is the section's central argument: a universalized mode is a *monoculture of mode*, and a monoculture is, by the best understanding of complex systems, fragile rather than fit. The ecological record of resilience is unambiguous that diversity is what lets a system absorb shock and reorganise after it; Holling's foundational distinction separates an engineer's *stability*, the swift return to a single equilibrium, from *resilience*, the capacity to persist and reorganise through disturbance — and his adaptive-cycle analysis shows that a system locked too long in its accumulating, rigidifying "conservation" phase becomes maximally vulnerable, so that "the longer a system is locked in to its growth phase, the greater its vulnerability and the bigger and more dramatic its collapse will be" (Holling, in Gunderson & Holling, 2002: [VERIFY: p.]). Walker and Salt make the trade-off exact: a system optimised for *persistence* — for holding its present configuration, maximising one set of returns — purchases that persistence at the cost of *adaptability* and *transformability*, the very capacities resilience consists in (Walker & Salt, 2006: [VERIFY: p.]). Taleb names the same pathology from the other side: suppressing the small volatilities that keep a system supple does not abolish fragility but stores it, transferring it forward into a single, larger, hidden collapse — the fragility of a system driven to "zero optionality" (Taleb, 2012: [VERIFY: p.]). Tainter supplies the long-run cost-curve, the diminishing and then negative marginal returns on the complexity a maximising mode keeps adding (Tainter, 1988: [VERIFY: p.]). Put together, these say something precise about universalization: in eliminating the plurality of worlds — the diversity that is the substrate's stock of alternative adaptive strategies — the mode is not maximising fitness to the universe but dismantling it, trading a present dominance for the loss of the system's capacity to absorb what the universe will eventually send. The survivor is fitter to the world it has made; it is, in the same motion, growing less fit to the universe that made the world possible.

This is also where the externalisation of §D belongs, correctly described. That the mode's rise was paid for by drawing down a buffer — the New World's ghost acreage, the carbon of the deep past — is *not*, as an earlier draft implied, an indictment for unfairness; evolutionary advantage is frequently built on what is, morally, cruelty, and the universe keeps no ledger of fairness. The relevant fact is structural, and it is Ponting's: human history can be read as "a succession of ever more complex and environmentally damaging ways of meeting the same basic human needs" (Ponting, 1991: [VERIFY: p.]), each world drawing more from the substrate to do what the last did with less. A mode that meets its needs by drawing steadily on a non-renewing buffer is not thereby *wicked*; it is thereby *fragile*, its persistence mortgaged to a stock that depletes. And the buffer is not only physical: as Miura shows, the mode also draws on a *manufactured dependence* — pinning other worlds in the position of resource-suppliers and dismantling their self-sufficiency so that they must keep feeding it (三浦/Miura, 2004: 15–16). A dominance that must continually *produce* the dependence it lives on is relational and contingent, not the intrinsic superiority the fitness-reading claims; and the dependence it consumes — other worlds' self-sufficient bases — is itself a depleting stock, so the same fragility verdict follows. Externalisation is fitness-to-the-world bought with rising fragility-to-the-universe — the same trade, named again.

The deepest form of the point returns to the layered ontology of §A, and to a mechanism §B and the colonial literature of §E have already supplied: *colonization*. Colonization, on the thesis's reading, is not best understood as a contingent moral injury added to an otherwise neutral exchange of goods; it is the name for what happens when one world's mode of artificiality overwrites another's at its *enduring organizing principles* and not merely at its surface. Components transfer across worlds at a cost that scales with the layer they touch: a recipe or a tool passes at the level of context-specific phenomena almost for free; an institution must be functionally re-coded at the level of structures and dynamics; but at the level of enduring principles, transfer is no longer adjustment but *replacement* — the recipient world's organising principles are overwritten by the imposing world's. This replacement-at-the-principle-layer is exactly the elimination of the diversity that resilience requires, carried out at the depth where it does the most damage: not the loss of a phenomenon or a practice, which a world can absorb, but the loss of an *alternative way of organising a world at all*. Universalization, then, is not the proof of fitness it takes itself for; it is, in the universe's own coin, its opposite — a planetary monoculture purchasing present dominance with the systematic destruction of the conditions of renewal. And this coheres exactly with §B: collapse is mismatch, not unfitness, so neither is dominance fitness. The Ming's fall did not prove the Ming unfit, and the Enlightenment mode's present reach does not prove it fit; dominance and collapse are alike *squeeze-phenomena*, not verdicts in a contest that was never neutrally scored.

## G. Bridge to Chapter IV: the turn to as-willedness

The chapter has argued that the Enlightenment mode of artificiality is one mode among the plural actual worlds (§A, §B), that its planetary reach rests on a contingent material basis (§D) and a totalizing structure of knowing (§E), and that this reach is not the fitness it takes itself for but a fragility it is busy accruing (§F). What remains is to ask after the *content* this mode universalizes — and that is the threshold of Chapter IV. For the disposition examined here has an inward correlate: the mode does not only project its made world outward as the universe; it also turns its mastery inward, onto the life of its inhabitants, as the pursuit of a *life-as-willed* — Uegaki's 意のままになる生 — in which whatever is *not* as one wills it, beginning with the resistant body and the claims of others, is recast as a hindrance to be overcome (Uegaki, 2022b: [VERIFY: p.]). Chapter IV takes up that pursuit and the self-finishing tendency it sets in motion; and it is there that this chapter's squeeze, between a world and its universe, begins its passage toward the terminal squeeze the thesis is named for — between the universe and the self-completing, metaverse-stage artificiality that as-willedness, fully actualized, calls into being.
