## Opener — Where the chapter sits

Chapter III argued that one mode of artificiality among the many actual worlds — the Enlightenment mode — has *universalised*: it carries its own made world as though that world were the given universe, and overwrites the organising principles of the other worlds it meets. That argument concerned the mode's *outward* relation, its comportment toward the worlds it has displaced. This chapter turns inward. It asks where the mode is heading next — not at the level of its relations with rival worlds, but at the level of the human beings who inhabit it.

The question is the thesis's own, posed already in the Introduction: where is the Enlightenment mode of artificiality heading after achieving global dominance? The present chapter answers at the level of the inhabitant; the next answers at the level of the apparatus the inhabitants have built; and the chapter after that brings the two answers together into the structural condition the thesis calls the squeeze. The order matters, and I want to keep it, because the temptation is to reach for the squeeze too early — to preface this chapter with the claim that its mechanism is already operative, already exerting pressure before its conditions are complete. That claim belongs to the apparatus-side argument of Chapter V. Here the squeeze is not yet introduced as a mechanism at all; what this chapter does is more modest and, I think, prior — it shows the *human* tendency whose maturation the squeeze will later presuppose.

That tendency has a name in the work of the Japanese philosopher Takahide Uegaki (上柿崇英), whose analysis of postwar Japanese society supplies the chapter its spine. Uegaki's diagnosis is that the mode of artificiality dominant since the Enlightenment rests on a single axiological commitment — that life ought to be *life-as-willed* (意のままになる生), conforming to the will of the one who lives it — and that the pursuit of this commitment, carried across generations within an increasingly capable apparatus, produces what he calls the *self-finishing* (自己完結化) of human life.

One conviction frames the whole chapter, and everything depends on not mistaking it: this is not a verdict on the pursuit of life-as-willed but a description of it. The freedom the pursuit delivers is real; the equality of access is real; people choose it for reasons that, from the inside, are intelligible and often good. The chapter passes no normative judgement on that choice. It traces the *structure* of the pursuit, because that structure is what the next chapter needs — the human-side process whose artificial counterpart, the self-completion of the apparatus, Chapter V will take up.

A vocabulary note, held throughout. As in Chapters I–III, the thesis does not use "technology" as a major concept; where the inherited idiom would write "technology," I read **artificiality** in the broad sense — material and virtual together, the single artificial environment by which a world is constituted. So where this chapter says the mode mobilises its apparatus to render life as-willed, I mean artificiality in both its aspects, working through interfaces that are at once material and nonmaterial.

The chapter develops in seven movements. **§A** recovers the commitment to life-as-willed and the anthropology of freedom it rests on. **§B** names the self-finishing tendency it produces. **§C** reads Uegaki's five periods, through the typology of Chapter II, as the genetic narrative of a single migration. **§D** maps the pursuit across contemporary life. **§E** shows that the diagnosis is not Uegaki's alone. **§F** takes up the sharpest twist — that the emancipatory tradition itself fuels the pursuit. **§G** hands the argument to Chapter V.

## A. Life-as-willed and the anthropology of freedom

Begin with what is taken for granted. Behind the most cherished commitments of the world the Enlightenment mode has made — the pursuit of freedom, the pursuit of equality, the call to liberation — Uegaki discerns a single, rarely-examined supposition: that a human being *ought* to live a life that answers to its own will, a "life-as-willed" (意のままになる生). He gives the supposition its sharpest name, the *worldview-and-view-of-the-human* (世界観＝人間観) of a "limitless life" (〈無限の生〉): the conviction that "'life-as-one-wills-it' is the human being's proper form, and that the human being's mission is to overcome the 'life-not-as-one-wills-it' that obstructs it" (Uegaki, 2022b: 6). It is not that a willed life is merely pleasant or preferable; it is that the not-as-willed is figured as a *wrong* — a condition to be corrected.

Where does the supposition come from? Uegaki traces it to what he calls the **anthropology of freedom** (自由の人間学): "the understanding of the human being founded by J. Locke and J.-J. Rousseau and brought to *one completion* in I. Kant," the matrix of the thought of the autonomous individual, around which "the history of Western philosophy over these past 250-odd years could even be called the story of inheritance-and-critique" (Uegaki, 2022b: 110). The lineage is worth following in its own primary voices, because the thesis is not relying on a secondary report of what these thinkers "more or less" held. Locke posits freedom and equality as the *given* natural condition: to understand the human we must consider "what state all men are naturally in, and that is, a state of perfect freedom … a state also of equality" (Locke, 1689: §4). Rousseau supplies the emblem Uegaki returns to — "Man is born free; and everywhere he is in chains" (Rousseau, 1762: I.1) — together with the open-ended capacity to remake the self he calls *perfectibility*, "this distinctive and almost unlimited faculty [which] is the source of all man's misfortunes" (Rousseau, 1755), and the suspicion of the given as that which human artifice corrupts: "God makes all things good; man meddles with them and they become evil … he will have nothing as nature made it, not even man himself" (Rousseau, 1762b: Bk I). Kant brings the lineage to its philosophical apex by reconceiving freedom as *autonomy*: "What else then can freedom of the will be but autonomy, that is, the property of the will to be a law to itself? … a free will and a will subject to moral laws are one and the same" (Kant, 1785: Ak. 4:447) — freedom as a causality "independent of foreign causes determining it" (Ak. 4:446). And Hegel states life-as-willed as a metaphysical telos, the sharpest foil the thesis will meet: "freedom constitutes the substance and essential character of the will, and the system of right is the kingdom of actualised freedom" (Hegel, 1821: §4), the free will being the will whose object is itself, so that "only in this freedom is the will wholly by itself, because it refers to nothing but itself, and all dependence upon any other thing falls away" (Hegel, 1821: §23).

Before extracting what the thesis needs, a discipline must be stated, the same one Chapter III imposed on its handling of "the West." The thesis does not claim that Locke, Rousseau, Kant, and Hegel are uniform champions of life-as-willed, or that "the West" is a single tendency culminating in the limitless life. It tracks *one extracted strand* — which is exactly why the mode is named the *Enlightenment* mode rather than "the Western mode" or "the modern mind": the name is a deliberate mitigation against essentialising, marking a determinate strand of a determinate period, not the totality of a civilisation. But the discipline cuts in an unexpected direction. It is tempting to soften the strand by observing that each of these thinkers, read whole, *resists* the extension the thesis traces — and for three of the four that is true and important. It is not true of the first. Locke is not a hedge against ontological self-ownership; he is its source. The very workmanship that seems to bind the self to its Maker — men "the workmanship of one omnipotent, and infinitely wise maker … his property, whose workmanship they are" (Locke, 1689: §6) — issues, through the labour that mixes the self into the world, in the tradition's most uncompromising statement of self-ownership: "every man has a property in his own person … the labour of his body and the work of his hands, we may say, are properly his" (Locke, 1689: §27); and by that labour the earth is subdued into property, until "in the beginning all the world was America" (Locke, 1689: §49). This is the founding charter at once of ontological self-ownership, of the objectification of nature as ownable matter, and — narrowed to those judged "industrious and rational" — of the dispossession of those who do not so improve the earth.[^locke] Locke does not resist the extension; he opens it.

[^locke]: On the communitarian reading (Shapiro; Tully, *A Discourse on Property*) the workmanship model *bounds* self-ownership — the Maker's prior claim and the duty to "preserve the rest of mankind" (§6) hedge the self on every side. But read with the proviso's "enough, and as good" clause and its "industrious and rational" qualification, the same apparatus *licenses* appropriation: unimproved land counts as waste, the proviso is satisfied, and consent may be waived — Locke "fulfilling the role of a theorist justifying the colonisation of America," the ownership of land bound up with "domination over others — the lower classes and alien peoples" (三浦永光 / Miura, 2006: 95–99; cf. Tully's later *Locke in Contexts*; Arneil, *John Locke and America*). The thesis follows this second reading. The §6 / §27 / §49 verbatim are clean (OA); the Shapiro / Tully / Arneil attributions are `[VERIFY]` against Zotero before final.

The other three *do* resist — but their resistance is the very thing the chapter must grasp, because it is not a brake on life-as-willed so much as an attempt to *complete* it. Rousseau subordinates the individual will to the *general will*, so that one may be "forced to be free" (Rousseau, 1762: I.7); Kant binds the autonomous will to a universal law under which humanity is treated "in every case as an end withal, never as means only" (Kant, 1785: Ak. 4:429); Hegel makes the will that wants "all dependence upon any other thing" to fall away merely the abstract moment, the "freedom of the void" whose actualisation is "the fury of desolation," to be overcome in the concrete ethical life of *Sittlichkeit* (Hegel, 1821: §5). Read together these are not three hedges but one programme — the demand that each individual's freedom be realised *through*, not against, the freedom of all. Hold that programme: it is the hinge on which the chapter turns.

One twentieth-century figure states the underlying anthropology so plainly that he becomes the thesis's clearest foil. Max Scheler, extending Kant into philosophical anthropology, defines the human as the being that is "world-open" (*weltoffen*) without limit: where every other organism lives sunk in its environing world, the human alone possesses *Geist* — the power to inhibit impulse and to objectify, analyse, and judge — and is thereby "released from the fetters of the Umwelt," a free being (Uegaki, 2022a: 90 [VERIFY-PAGE; Scheler's own *weltoffen* formulation at 2022a: 91 n. 19]). Scheler is the foil because his claim is the precise negation of the thesis's Chapter-I scaffolding: where Uexküll's *Umwelt* is the very form of any creature's world, Scheler makes the human the creature that *transcends* its Umwelt — the self that stands prior to, and free of, every environing world, raised into an explicit thesis in philosophical anthropology.

It is here that Uegaki's deepest point about the mode emerges, and it inverts the usual complaint. The Enlightenment mode is *not* naïve about the friction between the free individual and the others among whom it must live; its highest expression *demands* that the friction be resolved. The programme just named — Rousseau through Kant — is what Uegaki calls the **idyllic-dialectical theory of the commons** (牧歌主義的-弁証法的共同論): the conviction that once "true individuality and true autonomy" are achieved, the common good is sublated into a spontaneous extension of one's own will, so that the other's joy is felt as one's own (Uegaki, 2022a: 262–264 [VERIFY-PAGE]). This is *positive liberty* — freedom *to* — and Uegaki's verdict is unsparing: it is a **"magical rhetoric"** (魔術的なレトリック) that never explains *why* mutually contradictory demands should be synthesisable (2022a [VERIFY-PAGE]). Two thought-experiments expose the void. A "hundred-person village" run purely on voluntariness gives no one any ground to bear an unwanted burden, and the commons collapses; install a "cleaning rota" to distribute the burden and compulsion, sanction, and imperfect consensus return at once — positive liberty re-producing the coercion it set out to abolish. Universalised, the higher will would require *universal benevolence* (博愛主義) of everyone — "manifestly contrary to human reality." The conclusion is flat: **a commons without burden is a fiction** (Uegaki, 2022a: 257–263 [VERIFY-PAGE]).

And here the paradox closes into the chapter's central claim. If a commons without burden is a fiction, there is exactly one way to obtain it: let something other than human beings carry the burden. The three things a working commons actually needs — **roles** (役割), **trust** (信頼), and **forgiveness** (許し), what Uegaki calls "the manners and wisdom for the commons" (〈共同〉のための作法や知恵) — are progressively *entrusted to the social apparatus* (社会的装置), which absorbs the toll of living-together while continuing to deliver each individual's ontological freedom. The apparatus bears the commons so that the individual need not. This is why the **self-finishing of life** (自己完結化) is not an aberration of the Enlightenment mode but its **immanent completion** — the sole form in which the mode's own demand, the reconciliation of total individual freedom with a working common life, can in fact be met. Uegaki puts it at full strength: we have not failed to become independent individuals but *succeeded*, and the apparatus delivers the promise rather than betraying it, so that "the most free human being is at the same time the most fragile" (Uegaki, 2022a: ch. 8 [VERIFY-PAGE]; 2022b: 54). The mode is not betrayed by self-finishing; it is *fulfilled* by it. To see how that fulfilment became available at all, we must look beneath the social paradox to the presuppositions about the self on which it rests.

Uegaki finds in this inheritance two presuppositions that, "from its very beginning," lead toward the limitless life. The first he calls **spatio-temporal self-subsistence** (時空間的自立性): "the supposition that the human being posited there can exist as a single individual substance, a subject, *prior to* the others and the systems of meaning that surround it" — in its ultimate form a "thought-entity" (思念体), a self that has shed even the body, "independent of every concatenation of existence" (Uegaki, 2022b: 110–112). That thought-entity is, structurally, Descartes' *res cogitans* — the thinking thing whose existence is independent of the body — a resonance the thesis presses, though Uegaki himself names not Descartes but Sandel's "unencumbered self." The second he calls the **human's true endowed nature** (約束された本来性): "the supposition that there exists a human as it ought to be (本来の人間 / あるべき人間) which, though not yet actually appearing in this world, is *promised* to be realised in the future" — splendidly embodied, he notes, in Rousseau's "born free … in chains." This is the Enlightenment's *vocation of the human* — Kant's *Bestimmung des Menschen* — rooted in the *imago Dei* and carried in the natural-rights idiom of a nature "endowed by their Creator": *endowed*, and yet still to be realised. Its strangeness is that "if the true human is realised only in the future, no one can ever have witnessed it, so there is no ground at all for calling it the true human, yet people are convinced of it" — a conviction whose pedigree Uegaki traces to Renaissance Christianity, the *imago Dei* creature that "cannot but have a special plan and purpose given it by God" (Uegaki, 2022b: 112, 117).

The decisive move comes when these suppositions slip their original context. The determination of the human as free, Uegaki argues, "was conceived only for the sake of 'political freedom' — to criticise despotism and argue for a different polity," but it "departed from the context of political freedom and was gradually extended into one that discusses an 'ontological freedom' (存在論的自由) generalised to human existence as such. And it was probably precisely then that we came to open the door leading to the limitless life" (Uegaki, 2022b: 112–113). Once freedom is *ontologised*, every not-as-willed feature of existence — not just unjust laws but other people, one's own body, the cosmos itself — becomes a chain in principle, an oppression to be overcome rather than a condition to be lived. Kant's autonomy is the high-water mark of the move: leaving "entirely unasked whether a will untouched by any external influence is truly possible," it comes to "extol nothing but liberation from the chains and the realisation of the true human being" (Uegaki, 2022b: 113). (This is the same Kantian autonomy whose self-binding the synthesis-programme above recovered; the thesis follows Uegaki in reading the *extension* of autonomy-into-ontology as the fateful step, while granting that Kant himself bound the autonomous will to a universal law.)

One objection must be met before the chapter proceeds, because Uegaki raises it himself: if human beings have always struggled to bend the not-as-willed to their will, is the limitless life not simply *human nature*, rather than the signature of one historical mode? His answer is decisive, and it is what licenses the chapter's whole vocabulary of a *mode*. Across almost every worldview handed down from antiquity, he argues, the baseline orientation was the opposite one — the **finite life** (有限の生), which takes the inescapable not-as-willed (the body, the other, mortality, the uncertain future) not as a wrong to be abolished but as the condition to be lived. Buddhism, reading the world as impermanence (無常), is rooted in the finite life; so, *originally*, was Christianity, whose "love your enemy" was spoken into the reality of conflict by people who knew themselves finite. The limitless life is therefore not the human condition but a *deviation* from the cross-cultural baseline — and the real question is why this one tradition produced it. Uegaki's genealogy is specific: a Greek inheritance that already denigrated mere survival in favour of "life as spirit" severed from the body, joined to a Renaissance-Christian conviction that the human, as *imago Dei*, bears reason as a divine capacity to grasp the "perfect human" and a mission to realise it on earth — so that the anthropology of freedom could "discover," in *freedom*, the secret of that perfection (Uegaki, 2022b: ch. 10 §§2–3 [VERIFY-PAGE]). It is because the mode has *this* determinate pedigree — Greek-and-Christian, not human-universal — that the thesis names it the **Enlightenment mode**, and that Chapter III's plurality of worlds stands: most worlds were, and some still are, built on the finite life.

But no life is lived entirely as willed (意のままにならない生). The not-as-willed comes in forms one cannot escape — most centrally the not-as-willed **other** (意のままにならない他者) and the not-as-willed **body** (意のままにならない身体) — and under the limitless-life worldview this is not a fact to be lived with but a wrong to be corrected: an **ontological oppression** (存在論的抑圧). The oppression at issue, Uegaki is careful to specify, is "not political thought-control or the suppression of speech, but the very chains that bind the individual as an individual, the oppression that others and 'the world' (世間) impose in seeking to define it" (Uegaki, 2022b: 114) — the concept used, as his own glossary puts it, "as illuminated from the ideal of ontological freedom, to grasp critically, as an illegitimate external force, whatever determines human existence" (Uegaki, *keyword* 16-09).

Here is where artificiality enters, and where the thesis takes Uegaki up for its own purpose. If the not-as-willed is an oppression to be lifted, the instrument for lifting it is the artificial environment — material and virtual together — functioning as an *interface* between the willing self and each form of the not-as-willed. It interposes interfaces between people, so that the friction of the not-as-willed other can be reduced or routed around; between the self and its body, extending its capacities and deferring its decay; between the self and the cosmos, buffering the volatility of weather, harvest, and disease. Uegaki names the mechanism with precision: "to be liberated from mutual aid with our local neighbours, we *needed* the market economy and the bureaucratic apparatus … much of the freedom actually obtained was gained in exchange for *dependence on the social apparatus*" (Uegaki, 2022b: 190–191). By these interfaces the three realisations of a life — *survival* (生存), *existence-in-society* (現実存在), and *succession* (継承) — come to be "experienced in shrunken form" as economic activity, self-realisation, and schooling, each "entrusted" to the apparatus (Uegaki, 2022a: 138). And the apparatus that receives the entrustment swells into a single fused thing, the **social apparatus** (社会的装置): an accumulation of made things "fused in complex ways with the market economy, the bureaucratic apparatus, and net-space — the infosphere," permeating "every corner of our living-space, seamlessly, like an amoeba," on which "we depend so deeply that we can no longer live without it" (Uegaki, 2022a). The entrustment is, in the first instance, no loss — the freedom and the equality it delivers are real, and people become genuinely freer and more equal *as users* of the apparatus. What the next section develops is the structural cost of that gain.

## B. The self-finishing tendency

Start from the success, because the diagnosis is unintelligible without it. The pursuit of life-as-willed has, by any honest measure, *worked*. The freedoms the anthropology of freedom demanded were not merely demanded; they were, across the societies the Enlightenment mode reorganised, substantially won — freedom from arbitrary power, freedom of conscience and movement and association, and an equality of access to these that earlier orders never approached. Uegaki does not dispute this and neither does the thesis: the achievement is real, and to read what follows as declension or nostalgia would be to miss its point entirely. Uegaki's distinctive contribution lies one step further on — not in questioning *whether* these values were realised but in identifying the *form* of their realisation. We came to enjoy freedom and equality, he argues, not predominantly through the organic relations of human beings to one another — the mutual aid of neighbours, the thick reciprocity of a shared lifeworld — but through an *artificialised social apparatus* that delivers them on our behalf. "To be liberated from mutual aid with our local neighbours," as the passage already quoted has it, "we *needed* the market economy and the bureaucratic apparatus … much of the freedom actually obtained was gained in exchange for dependence on the social apparatus" (Uegaki, 2022b: 190–191). The freedom is genuine; what changed is its delivery mechanism. And a value delivered by an apparatus is a value one holds *as a user of that apparatus* — not as a participant in a web of organic relation that confers it, but as the individual terminal to which the apparatus pipes it. The author's figure for this condition is exact and I will keep it as the chapter's: each of us hangs *like a cocoon under the apparatus* — sustained by the threads that run up into it, and by exactly those threads severed from the cocoons alongside. We are kept, and we are kept apart, by the same structure. This is the sense in which the freedom is real and the cost is real at once: the cost is not that we are less free but that we are free *as users*, each suspended separately from the machine that frees us. Hold the word *user*; it is doing more work than it looks. It is the threshold concept of the entire next chapter, where the user of an apparatus becomes the *inhabitant* of one — a being whose very environment is the apparatus it depends on. The cocoon is hung here in §B so that §G can cut it down into Chapter V.

The cost has a name: the **self-finishing of human life** (自己完結化). Drawing on the increasingly capable apparatus, the inhabitant can lift, one by one, the burdens the not-as-willed other imposes — routing the need to deal with others through interfaces, without the unmediated friction of direct encounter and without the laborious upkeep of difficult relationships. The apparatus can even be enlisted to *affirm* one's ontological freedom — to confirm the self exactly as it already is, "the me just as I am" (ありのままの私), by guaranteeing **non-intervention** (不介入) from others. So the cherished ideal of the **independent individual** (自立した個人) — "an anthropology-and-ideology that locates the essence of the human in the individual" and holds that "each individual, bound by nothing, achieves autonomy of will through full self-judgement and self-determination" (Uegaki, 2022a: 67) — is realised more and more fully, at a cost Uegaki states precisely: a **thinning of relationality** (関係性の希薄化).

The paradox this thinning produces is the conceptual centre of the chapter. Without sufficient relational experience, and the skills only such experience builds, people become — paradoxically — *more* sensitive to the not-as-willedness of others, more easily threatened or disturbed by it. The thinner the lifeworld of shared relational practice, the more painful any not-as-willed encounter; and the more painful the encounter, the stronger the incentive to route the next one through the apparatus, which thins the fabric further still. This is a self-reinforcing spiral with no internal stopping point, and Uegaki names its outcome a **relational pathology** (関係性の病理).

The trap is sharper than a mere thinning, and Uegaki turns the knife twice. First, "the me just as I am" is a fantasy — not the self that arises within the "site of relationality," but "an unchanging me cut off from other-existence … a fictional this-me bloated by self-consciousness" (Uegaki, 2022a: 227) — and it is *asymmetric*: one wishes "to have all of one's own just-as-I-am accepted" while having "not the slightest intention of accepting anyone else's," since accepting a disagreeable other's just-as-it-is "would distort the me just as I am" (Uegaki, 2022a: 226–227). Second, and decisively, the oppression the ideal would abolish is *constitutive* of the very thing it wants: "it is impossible to erase ontological oppression from relationality," so that "if we want a relationality without burden, we have no option but either to replace the not-as-willed other with an as-willed other, or to strive never to build relationality at all" (Uegaki, 2022b: 133). The independent individual, pursued to the end, can keep relationality only by emptying it of the other.

The same logic, turned from the other to the body, yields the second symptom. Since the not-as-willed body imposes a biological hindrance on the realisation of ontological freedom, the self-finishing logic, holding to its premise, draws the consequence: the less one's life depends on the body, the freer one is. Uegaki names the trajectory this opens the **disembodiment of life** (生の脱身体化) — the progressive detachment of what it means to live from the biological body, as "even the finite body comes to be placed under the control of prediction and control" for the sake of self-realisation (Uegaki, 2022a). Read against Chapter II, this is *de-somatisation* in the precise sense: the terminus of the exo-/endo-somatic lineage (Lotka's exosomatic organs, Stiegler's exteriorisation) — once the body's functions have been progressively externalised into the apparatus, the body itself becomes dispensable, and disembodiment is exteriorisation completed. And he pairs it with a **confusion of life** (生の混乱): the condition of those who, "half-believing that life ought by nature to be entirely self-determined," face the reality of life-not-as-one-wills-it and so are "torn between ideal and reality — irritated, troubled, wounded," unable "to affirm life-sized human existence because they agonise over the meaning and felt-reality of living and wish everything to be realisable" (Uegaki, 2022a: ⅴ–ⅵ, 8).

Two disciplines hold this section in place. The first is the diagnostic reversal that separates Uegaki's claim from the familiar lament that modern life *frustrates* the individual. His claim is the opposite. The contemporary person "who accepts self-responsibility, desperately minds others so as to cause no trouble, carries a world all their own, and gamely goes on battling solitude" is "the autonomous individual itself"; "we already live quite sufficiently as autonomous individuals, and it is precisely *because* we are self-standing as users that we are tormented by an unprecedented oppression" (Uegaki, 2022b: 54). The pathology is not the ideal's frustration but its *success* — and note that Uegaki's own word here is again *user*: it is *as users* that we are self-standing, and as users that we suffer. The second discipline is the non-normative one of the Opener: the relational pathology and the disembodiment are not anyone's moral failing and not an alien imposition, but the predictable internal consequence of a world organised around life-as-willed. The drifter of the next section is squeezed — but by the success of his own world's deepest commitment, not by an enemy.

## C. Japan as a case: the five stages as a migration from convention to structure

Uegaki anchors the self-finishing tendency in a reading of postwar Japanese society, and I take that reading as the chapter's empirical case — but the framing must be held exactly, because it is easy to misuse. Japan is not the *cause* of the self-finishing condition, as though some peculiarity of these islands had generated a pathology now exported to the world; nor is it a merely *parochial* instance with nothing to say beyond itself. Japan is the unusually *legible* case. The Enlightenment mode reorganised Japanese existence in compressed time — Meiji to the present in roughly four generations — so that a process which elsewhere unfolded slowly enough to pass for the natural course of things here unfolded fast enough, and was documented closely enough, to be seen. The case illustrates a trajectory; it does not own it, and the plurality of worlds argued in Chapter III is untouched: other societies sit at other points along the same path. Because the reader may not carry the social history that makes the trajectory vivid, I anchor each period in concrete material — leading, wherever he supplies it, with Uegaki's own illustration, and supplementing with well-documented Japanese social history, every statistic sourced.

Read through the typology of Chapter II, the five periods are more than a chronology. They are the genetic narrative of a *migration* — the slow movement of the social apparatus (社会的装置) from one cell of artificiality to another. The apparatus is nonmaterial artificiality: its compelling power is given by its meaning, not by any material instantiation. What the periods track is its *register* shifting — from the **conventional** (Cell 3, compelling only so far as people keep enacting it: the *nomos*-register) toward the **structural** (Cell 4, compelling by its own architecture whether or not anyone enacts it: the *phusis*-like register). And this is precisely the shift the typology assigned to *this* chapter to explain: why the mode "shifted from heavy cell-3 reliance toward cell-4 dominance" is, in §IIC.8's words, the genetic claim about life-as-willed and self-finishing. The five periods are that claim told as a history.

In the **first period** (1868–1945), from the founding of the modern nation-state to defeat, the lifeworld is still "weighty" and the apparatus is only in bud. The world runs, at the level of its enduring organising principles, on Cell-3 conventional order — community, custom, mutual aid — compelling through ongoing human enactment. Concretely, this is the world of the village community (ムラ) and the stem household (家, *ie*) as the load-bearing units of life, of mutual-aid associations (結, *yui* — reciprocal labour-exchange for rice-planting and roof-thatching) and pooled-credit and worship associations (講, *kō*), where survival, social existence, and succession were carried by face-to-face obligation rather than by any system standing apart from the people enacting it. Even at war's end the population was still predominantly rural and agricultural — roughly half of the workforce was in agriculture in 1950 `[VERIFY: source — Japan Statistical Yearbook / Labour Force Survey, agricultural share of employment c.1950 ≈ 48%]` — so the home-port of which Uegaki's Traveller will soon take leave was, in this period, still materially intact.

The **second period** (1945–1970) is the age of the **Traveller** (旅人) — those who, "with the home port at their back, continue their voyage toward a great destination" (Uegaki, 2022b: 4). Here the apparatus expands, and by the mechanism §A named: to be "liberated from mutual aid with our local neighbours," Uegaki writes, "we *needed* the market economy and the bureaucratic apparatus" (Uegaki, 2022b: 190). The concrete face of this leave-taking is the postwar migration of the young from countryside to industrial city — the *collective employment* (集団就職) trains that carried rural middle-school graduates en masse to metropolitan employers from the mid-1950s, the graduates themselves celebrated in the period's idiom as "golden eggs" (金の卵), scarce and prized labour for the high-growth economy. What received them at the destination was an apparatus that took over the integrating functions the village had performed: the lifetime-employment firm as a substitute community (会社, the company-as-community, with its seniority wages, company housing, and enterprise unions), and the public housing estate (団地) as the built form of the new nuclear-family life, the *danchi* with its standardised units and its dining-kitchen the aspirational emblem of the era. The apparatus is still largely Cell-3 — institutions one enacts, the firm and the estate still requiring the member's daily participation — but the entrustment has begun; the realisation of survival starts to pass from the *ie* and the *mura* to the company and the state.

The **third period** (1970–1995), the era of the suburb (郊外) and high consumption, is where the structural transformation of the lifeworld advances and "what could be called local community substantively lost its meaning" — the thinning of relationality sets in (Uegaki, 2022b). Concretely, this is the build-out of the commuter suburb and the planned *new town* (ニュータウン — Senri and Tama the canonical cases, vast dormitory developments dating from the 1960s–70s), the consolidation of the *nuclear family* (核家族) as the normal household form, and the arrival of mass consumption: the household stocked with the "three sacred treasures" of the appliance age and then their colour-television-car-cooler successors, the convenience store and the family restaurant and the suburban roadside as the new texture of daily life. Relationship to neighbours is now mediated through consumption and the commute rather than through shared productive labour; the apparatus thickens and mediates ever more, and while the conventional register still dominates, its structural margin is widening — the suburb is a place one consumes more than a community one enacts.

The **fourth period** (1995–2010), the period of informatisation and globalisation, is the typological hinge. Net-space, the infosphere (情報世界), takes hold — Cell 4, nonmaterial-*structural* artificiality, at scale. As the apparatus moves toward Cell 4, the Cell-3 lifeworld that once transmitted both the *meanings* and the *skills* of communal life thins to the point of transmitting neither — and its representative figure changes. The Traveller gives way to the **Drifter** (漂流人): a generation "like a drifting vessel that has lost the home port to return to, the destination to head for, and even the compass by which to know its own position" (Uegaki, 2022b: 5). The concrete markers are sharp and well-documented. The collapse of the bubble economy opened the *employment ice age* (就職氷河期) for those graduating roughly 1993–2005, who found the lifetime-employment escalator withdrawn just as they reached it; out of that breach came the new social types the period named — the *freeter* (フリーター, the serially casually-employed young adult) and the *NEET* (ニート, not in employment, education, or training), and, at the extreme of withdrawal, the *hikikomori* (引きこもり), the acute social recluse who does not leave the room. Across the same years the mobile phone (携帯) and then the consumer internet rewired how relation itself was conducted, and the period's keynote affect surfaces precisely here — *recognition-anxiety* (承認不安), the suffering of those who "naively believed in the full development of free individuality as the ideal of life" and broke against the gap between that ideal and the reality (Uegaki, 2022b: n.144). The Drifter "does not know roles, and cannot build trust" — not from any failing of his own, but because the order that transmitted role and trust has gone structural and silent.

The **fifth period** (2010–), "the point where we now stand," is the **self-contained society** (自己完結社会): a society in which, "through deep dependence on the social apparatus that swells together with information technology, robotics / AI technology, and life-manipulation technology, the necessity of relating to flesh-and-blood others, and of living together with a flesh-and-blood body, are lost" (Uegaki, 2022a). The concrete face of this period is a life that can be conducted, end to end, through interfaces: the single-person household (単身世帯) now the most common household type in Japan `[VERIFY: source — National Census 2020 / IPSS household projections: single-person households the plurality/largest category, ~38%]`, sustained by delivery, subscription, and matching-app provisioning that route food, goods, work, and companionship through the apparatus rather than through standing relation; and at the end of such a life, increasingly, the *solitary death* (孤独死) that goes unremarked until the apparatus, not a neighbour, registers the absence. Its signature ethics follows directly — the **ethics of non-intervention** (不介入の倫理): "since I trouble no one, you too should cause me no trouble whatsoever," and, inverted, "since I am troubled by no one, I likewise cannot trouble you" (Uegaki, 2022a) — the etiquette codified in the everyday Japanese imperative *not to cause others trouble* (迷惑をかけない), the felt-norm of a world that has handed relation itself over to the architecture. Typologically, this is Cell-4 *dominance*: the apparatus now compels structurally, by its own architecture, at the level of the world's enduring organising principles — the inhabitant "entrusts his very living, his very life, wholly to a high-level social system," which is what Uegaki names the self-finishing of life, 自己完結化 (Uegaki, 2022a).

Two things should be read off this migration. First, it is not only a move *across* the cells (conventional → structural) but a deepening *down* the strata: in the early periods the apparatus operates at the surface — the level of context-specific phenomena one knowingly uses — while by the fifth it operates at the level of the world's enduring organising principles, so that survival, relation, and succession are no longer things the inhabitant does *through* the apparatus but things the apparatus *is*. Second — and this is the seam to the next chapter — the apparatus's acquisition of Cell-4 structural compulsion, its coming to operate "by itself" without the inhabitant's continuing enactment, is the very disposition Chapter V will take up on the side of artificiality. Here it appears only as the destination of a *human* trajectory; there it is argued in its own right. What this chapter carries forward is that the self-finishing of life (§B) and this Cell-3 → Cell-4 migration of the apparatus are one process seen from two sides: as the inhabitant entrusts more, the apparatus structures more — until the organising principles of his world have migrated into a background that confronts him the way a given does.

## D. Life-as-willed today: the pursuit across the cells

The trajectory §C told as a history is also a description of the present, and it can be read off contemporary life directly once the not-as-willed forms of §A are mapped across the cells of artificiality. The pattern at the scale of a single life is the pattern at the scale of a society: the management of the not-as-willed migrates from the *conventional* register, where it depends on someone's ongoing enactment, toward the *structural*, where the apparatus's own architecture does the work — and the deeper it migrates, the nearer the inhabitant comes to the self-contained condition.

Take first the not-as-willed **other** (意のままにならない他者). Its oldest management is Cell-3 and conventional: the etiquette of non-intervention (不介入), a norm that holds only so far as people keep honouring it. Its contemporary management is increasingly Cell-4 and structural: the recommendation feed that need never surface a discordant voice; the block and the mute that foreclose an encounter rather than weathering it; the matching and filtering that pre-select whom one meets at all. This is an apparatus that does not *ask* the inhabitant to avoid the not-as-willed other but forecloses the encounter as an action-possibility within its field — the friction of the other neither endured nor even refused, but engineered out in advance, "without our being aware of it" (cf. Uegaki, 2022a). The same office, in a single generation, has migrated from a Cell-3 norm one enacts to a Cell-4 architecture that enacts it on one's behalf.

Take next the not-as-willed **body** (意のままにならない身体), where the cells divide by substrate. The material registers — Cell 1 and Cell 2 — carry the somatic interventions: the pharmacology against fatigue and appetite, the wearable, the cosmetic and longevity industries, the whole apparatus that meets the body as material to be managed. The nonmaterial-structural register — Cell 4 — carries the quantified self: the body rendered as a stream of data placed, in Uegaki's words, "under the control of prediction and control" for the sake of personal self-realisation (Uegaki, 2022a), the algorithmic optimisation that makes even sleep and heartbeat into outputs to be tuned. This is the substrate of what §B named the disembodiment of life: the body re-described as something one *has* and administers rather than something one *is*.

The transhumanist program is best read just here — the field in which the life-as-willed pursuit of the not-as-willed body becomes explicit and reaches its limit, and the cells order its cases. It states the premise of §A without disguise: human nature, Max More writes, is "just one point along an evolutionary pathway," with "no claim on our allegiance," which "we can learn to reshape … in ways we deem desirable" (More, 2013: §I), so that the body's mortality and limits are recast as remediable defects of a "half-baked beginning" (Bostrom, 2005: §1). And the cases sort by *how much of the universe-calibrated organism each preserves*. At one pole stand the interventions that *preserve* the body while extending it — mainstream longevity research, "more time in a recognisably continuous body," which in Cell-1/Cell-2 terms extends rather than abolishes the body's engagement with the given. In the middle stand enhancement's ambiguous cases, whose valence turns on what they are *for*. At the far pole stands mind-uploading, the proposal to migrate personhood to a non-biological substrate: Moravec's promise that the pattern *is* the person, the biological substrate "mere jelly" (Moravec, 1988: 117), Kurzweil's that we shall "transcend … our biological bodies and brains" and convert "our frail version 1.0 human bodies" into "durable … version 2.0" (Kurzweil, 2005). In the typology this is the limit case: the self proposed *wholly* into Cell 4 — nonmaterial-structural artificiality — with the universe-pole (the body, its rhythms, its metabolic claim) abolished by design. As the thesis has put the diagnosis, and it bears keeping: such a program "promises to resolve the difficulty by making the organism better adapted to the apparatus, but it achieves this by destroying the organism's capacity to *experience* the difficulty," leaving "an organism that no longer knows it is being squeezed." Mind-uploading does not so much *deepen* the self-finishing tendency as *complete* it: it states as an explicit goal what the tendency reaches toward as its unstated terminus.

The same migration governs the three realisations of §A — survival (生存), existence-in-society (現実存在), and succession (継承) — each now "experienced in shrunken form" as economic activity, self-realisation, and schooling, and each entrusted to the apparatus (Uegaki, 2022a: 138): livelihood to the platform that allocates the day's gig-work; social existence to the infrastructure that curates an online self; succession to the technologies of reproduction and of digital inheritance. Across all of them the cross-cutting fact is the one §C established at the scale of a society: today's pursuits of life-as-willed run predominantly through Cell-4 artificiality, or are migrating toward it, and operate ever nearer to the level of the world's enduring organising principles. The deeper and the more structural the pursuit, the closer the inhabitant stands to the self-contained society — and to the self-completion of the apparatus that the next chapter takes up.

## E. Convergent — and divergent — diagnoses

That the self-finishing tendency is not an artefact of one Japanese philosopher's vocabulary is shown by a convergence of independent diagnoses, reached from other disciplines and other languages. But convergence is not coincidence of doctrine, and to present these thinkers as a chorus would flatten exactly what makes their agreement evidential. Each reaches Uegaki's diagnostic core — the drive to render life as-willed, and its cost — by a different route, and each *diverges* from him at a different point: on the remedy, on the engine, on the valence, on the genealogy's lesson, on the programme. The convergences corroborate; the divergences locate Uegaki's own claim by contrast. I take the five in turn, marking both.

The nearest is **Hartmut Rosa**, whose *Unverfügbarkeit* (uncontrollability) names, in a sociology of resonance, almost exactly the axis the thesis marks as as-willed / not-as-willed. "The driving cultural force of that form of life we call 'modern,'" Rosa writes, "is the idea, the hope and desire, that we can make the world *controllable*" — render it "known, mastered, conquered, made useful," until "the world has simply become a point of aggression" (Rosa, 2020: x, 6). And the cost he names is precisely the thesis's: the controlling attitude "is incompatible with an orientation toward resonance … it destroys any experience of resonance," because resonance cannot be fabricated — "there is no way to 'fabricate' resonance, to instrumentally bring it about" (Rosa, 2020: 48, viii). That Rosa reaches this from Frankfurt critical theory, not from Uegaki, makes the convergence the chapter's strongest external corroboration: the not-as-willed is not an accident of the world but the very condition of being touched by it — "only in encountering the uncontrollable," he writes, "do we feel touched, moved, alive" (Rosa, 2020: x). *But Rosa diverges on the remedy, and with it on the ontology.* For Rosa the answer to the controlling attitude is *resonance* — a responsive, non-instrumental relation to a world that remains, in his phrase, *halbverfügbar*, "semi-available" or semi-controllable: the resonant world "cannot be fully controlled, yet it is not simply uncontrollable either." His diagnosis is therefore *hopeful and recuperative*: the resonant relation is recoverable within modern life, a corrective available to the late-modern subject who learns to let the world reach it. Uegaki's structure is darker and more closed: the self-finishing spiral has *no internal stopping point*, and what Rosa hopes to recover is, on Uegaki's account, exactly what the apparatus has rendered structurally unavailable. They share the diagnosis of the controlling attitude; they part on whether its cost is, in the present configuration, reversible. `[VERIFY-PAGE: halbverfügbar / "semi-controllable" — confirm the exact English wording and folio against the Polity 2020 text; the "fabricate resonance" and "point of aggression" quotes above are page-pinned in the corroboration dossier.]`

**Byung-Chul Han** supplies the psychic correlate. His "achievement-subject" is the inhabitant of life-as-willed read as self-imposed performance: "free from any external instance of domination," "lord and master of itself," and for that very reason an *animal laborans* "that exploits itself — and it does so voluntarily, without external constraints," under "the free constraint of maximizing achievement" (Han, 2015: 11, 10). The disappearance of external domination, Han insists, "does not entail freedom" but makes "freedom and constraint coincide" — auto-exploitation being "more efficient than allo-exploitation, for the feeling of freedom attends it" (Han, 2015: 11). This is the self-finishing inhabitant seen from inside the exhaustion it produces. *But Han diverges on the engine.* For Han the mechanism is *psychic and internal*: the achievement-subject is driven by an excess of positivity it has internalised, exploiting itself in the privacy of its own will; the "site" of the pathology is the over-affirming psyche. Uegaki's mechanism is *infrastructural and external*: the self-finishing turns not on an internalised compulsion to achieve but on the *entrustment* of life's burdens to a social apparatus that then delivers them back as user-services. Han's burnout is what auto-exploitation feels like; Uegaki's self-finishing is what apparatus-entrustment builds. The two are complementary precisely because they name different causes of an overlapping symptom — which is why the thesis can hold Uegaki's apparatus-side account without reducing it to Han's psychology.

**Arnold Gehlen**, from philosophical anthropology, supplies the conceptual complement — and here the divergence is the sharpest of all, because it is an *inversion of valence*. Gehlen's thesis is that the human is a "deficient being" (*Mängelwesen*), instinctually under-determined, and that institutions function as *Entlastung*, "relief" or "unburdening": they stabilise conduct so the individual need not re-decide everything from the subjective ground up — institutions, on this account, "have the formal and informal function to unburden and give coherence and continuity, to compensate for the human's lack of instinctual determination" (Aydin, 2022: 314). The *concept* is shared with Uegaki almost exactly — the offloading of the burden of self-determination onto a standing structure. *But Gehlen valorises what Uegaki diagnoses.* For Gehlen, *Entlastung* is a good and a necessity: the danger he fears is its loss — *de-institutionalisation*, the erosion of the relieving structures, which throws the individual back onto an overburdened, unstable subjectivism. For Uegaki, the very entrustment Gehlen prizes is the engine of the pathology; what Gehlen would call healthy institutional relief, Uegaki reads as the self-finishing handover that hollows relation out. Same concept, opposite sign — and it is worth foregrounding the inversion rather than smoothing it, because it sets Uegaki's claim in relief: the *Entlastung* whose absence Gehlen mourns is, in the self-contained society, present in a degree and a form (structural, apparatus-borne, total) that Gehlen never envisaged, and it is *that* over-supply of relief — not its withdrawal — that produces the contemporary distress. The point of contact corroborates the mechanism; the opposed valence shows that the mechanism's danger is not given with the concept but turns on how far, and through what, the unburdening is carried. `[Gehlen's own Entlastung wording is on a SECONDARY (Aydin) source here; the inversion-of-valence claim does not depend on a primary quote — it is a reading of the concept's direction — but pin the primary (Man in the Age of Technology) before final.]`

**Charles Taylor** traces the genealogy of one of the thesis's load-bearing terms. The ideal of authenticity — "a certain way of being human that is *my* way," a "contact with myself, with my own inner nature" one is "called upon" to live (Taylor, 1991: 29) — is the moral source of what Uegaki calls "the me just as I am," and Taylor's genealogy is the same one Uegaki invokes, independently traced: the ideal descends from Rousseau, who "presents the issue of morality as that of our following a voice of nature within us," and from Herder's idea that "each of us has an original way of being human," each with "his or her own *measure*" (Taylor, 1991: 27, 28–29). *But Taylor diverges by defending what Uegaki dismantles.* Where Uegaki reads the ideal's structure as an engine of self-enclosure, Taylor's whole project is a *recovery* of authenticity from its degraded forms: "what we need," he writes, "is neither root-and-branch condemnation nor uncritical praise … what we need is a work of retrieval, through which this ideal can help us restore our practice" (Taylor, 1991: 23). And the retrieval turns on two claims Uegaki's "me just as I am" is built to refuse. First, the self is "fundamentally dialogical": "we become full human agents, capable of understanding ourselves, and hence of defining an identity, through our acquisition of rich human languages of expression" — identity is achieved *with* and *against* significant others, not prior to them (Taylor, 1991: 33). Second, authenticity is intelligible only against *horizons of significance* it does not itself create: "authenticity can't be defended in ways that collapse horizons of significance," for even the sense that the significance of a life comes from its being chosen "depends on the understanding that independent of my will there is something noble, courageous, and hence significant" (Taylor, 1991: 39). Taylor's authentic self is thus *bounded by a given it must answer to* — a dialogical formation answerable to horizons it did not choose — which is precisely the bounding the self-finishing ideal sheds. The thesis takes Taylor's genealogy of the term and lets his defence stand as the strongest internal correction the tradition offers itself; that the correction has to be *argued against* the ideal's drift is itself evidence of the drift Uegaki names.

**Ivan Illich**, finally, names from the 1970s the mechanism of entrustment and the dilution of skill. His "radical monopoly" is what arises when "one industrial production process exercises an exclusive control over the satisfaction of a pressing need" and so "rules out *natural competence* … imposes compulsory consumption and thereby restricts personal autonomy" (Illich, 1975: 65–66). And his account of the disabling professions describes exactly the entrustment Uegaki names: professions "could not have become dominant and disabling unless people were already experiencing as a lack that which the expert imputes to them as a need," until the layman's "world turns into an echo-chamber of needs" (Illich, 1977: 29). The apparatus that receives the entrustment, on Illich's reading, does not merely serve a competence; it *disables* it — the vernacular skill of living-together that the self-contained society can no longer transmit. *But Illich diverges by prescribing what Uegaki only diagnoses.* Illich's analysis is frankly *programmatic*: it issues in *conviviality* and *limits* — the call for "convivial tools" that "guarantee [a person's] right to work with independent efficiency," and for politically-chosen ceilings on industrial growth ("people need not only to obtain things, they need above all the freedom to make things … and to use these in caring for and about others"). Where Uegaki is, on principle, *non-prescriptive* — diagnosing the structure of the limitless life without legislating an exit — Illich names the exit: the deliberate, collective imposition of limits on the very tools whose unchecked growth produces the radical monopoly. The convergence is on the mechanism (entrustment disables); the divergence is that Illich believes the mechanism can and must be reversed by a politics of limits, while Uegaki holds the diagnostic register and leaves the question of the limit open. `[VERIFY-PAGE: the "convivial tools / freedom to make things" wording — confirm the exact phrasing and folio in Tools for Conviviality against the held NLM/Zotero copy; the radical-monopoly and disabling-professions quotes above are page-pinned in the corroboration dossier.]`

The shape of the section, then, is convergence *plus* divergence: five thinkers, reached independently, confirm that a controlling/achieving/entrusting drive carries a structural cost — and five thinkers locate, by where they part from Uegaki, what is distinctive in his account: that the cost is apparatus-borne (vs Han's psyche), structurally closed rather than recuperable (vs Rosa's resonance), produced by an *over*-supply of unburdening rather than its lack (vs Gehlen's valence), unanswerable to a given the authentic self must honour (vs Taylor's horizons), and held, on principle, short of a programme (vs Illich's limits). The agreement makes the diagnosis credible; the disagreements make it precise.

## F. The emancipatory paradox — a critique from within

This is the chapter's most delicate move, and it must be stated so as not to be mistaken for what it is not. It is not an attack on the emancipatory tradition from outside; it is a question raised *within* the emancipatory project, about a premise that project has tended to leave unexamined. So let me begin where Uegaki himself begins — by affirming the project and its achievements. The struggles against despotism, against arbitrary power, against the subordination of persons by sex or race or colony, are struggles the thesis honours and shares the goal of; Uegaki is explicit that he writes "while affirming the historical role" these traditions of critique have played (Uegaki, 2022b: 80, n.90), and so do I. The political emancipation these movements won is precisely the achievement §B insisted was real. The argument that follows takes nothing from that. It identifies, rather, a *tacit premise* — something the most powerful emancipatory approaches "unconsciously take for granted," in the sense that it operates in them without being thematised: the premise that the destination of liberation is the *removal of all hindrance*, a self finally answerable to nothing it did not choose. The contribution of this section is to surface that premise and ask whether it is, on reflection, the destination the project meant to reach.

The premise has a structure, and Uegaki gives it. The ideal of ontological freedom, he writes, "demands of us liberation from ontological oppression. But each time we remove one of the chains that binds us, we notice yet a further chain … and there is no end to this," because the ideal "can never accept the human reality that no human being exists who is bound by no chain whatsoever." "Freedom, equality, autonomy, coexistence — so long as they are spoken of as 'ideals that negate reality,' the same contradiction inheres in them": remove one unfreedom and one "discovers" a new one, in an endless negation Uegaki calls the "endless hell" (無間地獄) the limitless life brings about (Uegaki, 2022b: 118). Note what is and is not being claimed. The endlessness is not a flaw in the *fight against oppression*; it is a property of an ideal that has set its terminus at the removal of *all* hindrance, the given included. An emancipatory project aimed at a determinate injustice — this domination, that exclusion — has a destination it can reach. A project that has, perhaps without noticing, reset its destination to *a self answerable to nothing given* has set a destination that recedes as it is approached.

This is where Uegaki turns to the critical theories that prosecute the emancipatory drive, and where the immanent character of the critique must be held hardest. Of the discourses he names — "power-theory" (権力論, i.e. Foucault), "gender-theory" (ジェンダー論), "postcolonial theory" (ポストコロニアル論) — he writes that, while affirming the historical role they have played, he takes "a critical view of the very framework they *tacitly presuppose* — that of *limitless* liberation from power and oppression," because "that framework is precisely what … embodies the worldview-and-view-of-the-human of the limitless life" (Uegaki, 2022b: 80, n.90). The word that carries the argument is *tacitly*: the claim is not that these approaches are wrong to expose domination, but that, in pressing exposure toward *limitlessness*, they have inherited — unthematised — the very life-as-willed premise they would not, on reflection, endorse as their goal. "Power-theory," Uegaki argues, "as a consequence of exposing every form of oppression as ideology, ends up desiring a limitless liberation from power, and thereby a limitless ontological freedom," and so "necessarily reaches a dead end"; and "postmodern theory — which was supposed to set out from a critique of the autonomous individual — because it craves ontological freedom, conversely undergoes an 'atavistic reversion' back toward the autonomous individual" (Uegaki, 2022b: 207–208). The irony is internal to the project: the critique of the sovereign subject, carried by the demand for limitless freedom, circles back to the sovereign subject. To surface this is not to oppose the critique of power; it is to ask the critique of power to examine the destination its own momentum has selected.

The precise hedge, then — and everything turns on stating it exactly — is this. The thesis does *not* question political-freedom emancipation: not the fight against despotism, not the dismantling of unjust hierarchy, not the demand that persons not be subordinated by what they did not choose. On that, it stands with the tradition, as Uegaki "wholly supports the institutional establishment of political freedom" (Uegaki, 2022b: 190, 193). What it questions is the *extension* of that emancipation into an *ontological* register — the often-unspoken slide from "no person should be subordinated by unjust power" to "the self should be answerable to nothing given at all," from a determinate liberation to the removal of the given as such. The first is the project's achievement; the second is the premise this section asks the project to examine. The critique is immanent because it shares the project's commitment to freedom and presses only on the question of freedom's terminus.

This must be set carefully beside Chapter III, where Adorno and Horkheimer's critique of instrumental reason served as a witness *against* the Enlightenment mode. There is no contradiction, but there is a distinction the thesis must hold. What the Frankfurt critique indicts is the instrumental reason that masters nature; what Uegaki indicts is the unexamined assumption that the destination of emancipation is a self liberated from every constraint of the given. These are not the same target, and a thinker may attack the first while tacitly running the second. The deeper point — and it is the chapter's, not Uegaki's alone — is that the will to liberate from *all* hindrance is *itself* the life-as-willed engine, whatever it takes as its object; so a critique can be impeccable against domination and still, in its demand for a self answerable to nothing it did not choose, be carrying the mode's own premise unawares. The thesis does not, on this account, stand outside the emancipatory tradition in order to reject it. It stands within the predicament that tradition shares with what it criticises, and asks after the given that both have learned to read as a chain — a question Chapter VI will return to as the difference between resisting the squeeze and refusing the world that produces it.

## G. From the self-finishing of life to the self-completion of the apparatus — and from user to inhabitant

The chapter has tracked one half of a coupled dynamic. From the Enlightenment mode's commitment to life-as-willed (§A), through the self-finishing it produces (§B), the migration it drives (§C), the forms it takes today (§D), the convergent — and divergent — witnesses to it (§E), and the emancipatory paradox that deepens it (§F), the argument has described the *self-finishing of human life* — the steady transfer of the burdens of living, the not-as-willed others and the not-as-willed body, from the lived person onto the apparatus that stands in for them.

But a burden absorbed by the apparatus is a function the apparatus now performs, and there the other half of the dynamic comes into view. As the human portion of functionality in the world shrinks, the apparatus's portion grows — and not merely additively: each function the apparatus takes over makes further self-finishing possible, which entrusts a further function to it, which enlarges its portion again. The main text states the coupling directly — the self-finishing of human life "is followed by a self-completing tendency of artificiality, as it is only through the mediation of artificiality that humans are able to maximize their life-as-willedness, which in turn increases artificiality's portion of functionality in the world, including its self-maintenance and renewal" ([P0050]).

Here the figure planted in §B is ready to be harvested, because it is the exact hinge into Chapter V. Recall the cocoon: through this coupled dynamic we have come to hold our freedom and equality not organically but *as users* of the apparatus, each suspended beneath it like a cocoon — sustained by it, and by the same threads severed from one another. The word *user* was load-bearing there, and now it bears the whole weight of the transition. For consider what a user *is*: a being that lives by an apparatus it depends on but does not inhabit — the apparatus remains, for the user, an instrument *within* a world. The trajectory of this chapter has been the steady deepening of that dependence, the migration of more and more of life *into* the apparatus, until — as §C's fifth period showed — survival, relation, and succession are no longer things one does *through* the apparatus but things the apparatus *is*. And a being for whom the apparatus has become not an instrument within its world but the very *environment* it lives in is no longer merely a *user* of the apparatus. It is an *inhabitant* of it. That is the single step the next chapter takes: **from user to inhabitant** — from a cocoon hung *beneath* the apparatus to a being whose *environment itself is the apparatus*, which is what the thesis means by the metaverse. The cocoon does not fall; the threads do not loosen. What changes is that the apparatus the cocoon hangs from ceases to be a thing in the world and becomes the world the cocoon hangs in.

That apparatus-side tendency — the apparatus achieving operational independence from the very humans whose tasks it has absorbed, the disposition §C watched the social apparatus acquire as it migrated toward structural compulsion — is the subject of Chapter V, and it has a name in this thesis that I withhold here, because to name it now would be to claim before its argument what that chapter must earn. What this chapter carries forward is only this: that the self-finishing of life and the self-completion of the apparatus are the coupled halves of one process; that the user is the inhabitant in embryo; and that the chapter has passed no verdict on the pursuit that drives them. It has traced the pursuit, in the non-normative spirit of the Opener, precisely so that its artificial counterpart can be seen for what it is. Chapter V turns to that counterpart — to the apparatus that has become an environment, and to the inhabitant the user has become; Chapter VI shows how the two, together, produce the structural condition the thesis calls the squeeze.
