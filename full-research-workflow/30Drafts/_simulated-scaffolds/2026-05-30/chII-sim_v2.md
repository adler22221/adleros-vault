## Opener — Artificiality as the umbrella concept

The previous chapter argued that a world is an adaptational operating system: a bounded, humanly-made environment nested within the universe, through which a population organizes its collective survival. This chapter asks what such a world is *made of*, and answers with a single category: **artificiality**. Artificiality is the umbrella concept of the thesis — the whole class of humanly-produced organizing principles, material and nonmaterial alike, by which a world is constituted, transmitted, and adapted. It includes not only tools and built environments but institutions, laws, symbols, language, customs, religion, ideology, and music.

That this broad category is the right unit of analysis is warranted by the convergence of two independent traditions. Bernard Stiegler's broad reading of *technē* holds that technics "designates… first and foremost all the domains of skill" — politeness, rhetoric, poetry, medicine, the polis, written law — such that "all human action has something to do with *technē*" [VERIFY: Stiegler 1998, *Technics and Time 1*; cf. Heidegger 1977 p.13, "*technē* belongs to bringing-forth… it is something poietic," nomos-phusis-tekhne dossier §1c]. From an entirely different direction, Obara Hideo's 「モノ」 (the katakana form, distinct from the hiragana 「もの」 of concrete artefacts) names "everything humans have created, including abstract language," with religion, ideology, and music explicitly included [VERIFY: Obara 1995, *ペット化する現代人*; Obara NOT in Zotero per Ch-2-9 — acquire/resolve citekey]. Late-modern French philosophy of technics and contemporary Japanese ethological anthropology arrive at the same category from opposite ends; that convergence is what licenses treating artificiality as the umbrella rather than as a coined term.

A deliberate vocabulary discipline follows from this. The thesis does **not** deploy "technology" as a major thesis concept. Where the philosophy-of-technology tradition (Stiegler, Heidegger, Hui, Foucault, Feenberg, Ellul) writes "technology," this thesis relabels their concept as *artificiality* in the broad sense. Stiegler himself diagnoses the narrow modern reading of technics as "an inadequate understanding, one that is already restrictive and derived" [VERIFY: Stiegler 1998]. The discipline is not pedantry: it forecloses the thesis-defeating misreading "the metaverse is just more advanced *technology*." Working at the artificiality level keeps that misreading shut. (The current DOCX section heading "Technology as exosomatization" at [P0112] is itself a candidate relabel under this frame — but that is a DOCX edit, not this scaffold's call.)

### The author's nomos-as-tekhne synthesis (NOT Brague's claim)

There is a Greek vocabulary that sharpens what artificiality is, and the chapter can enter through it — provided one attribution discipline is observed scrupulously. The Greeks distinguished *phusis* (the given, the self-arising — "the arising of something from out of itself," in Heidegger's gloss, whose principle of motion lies *within itself*, per Aristotle) from what is made by human hand and human institution [VERIFY: Aristotle *Physics* II.1, 192b8–193a, Hardie–Gaye trans., dossier §2; Heidegger 1977 pp.10–11, dossier §1b]. On the made side stood two terms: *technē*, the artificial, "the human activity of fabrication," whose source lies *in another* (the maker, not the thing); and *nomos*, the custom-becoming-law, "that which is in habitual practice, use or possession… usage, custom," hardening into "statute, ordinance made by authority… law" [VERIFY: LSJ s.v. νόμος, Perseus, dossier §3a — verbatim; SEP "The Sophists" §2: norms as "mere products of human customs, conventions or beliefs," dossier §4a; IEP §3.a: *nomos* "extending from customs and conventions to positive law," dossier §4b].

Here the attribution must be exact. **Rémi Brague groups *tekhnē* and *nomos* together as the human-made class** — and *only* that. In his words, the idea of "nature" (*phusis*) is "already the result of a reflection, more precisely of a separation between that which has its principle in the human activity of fabrication or estimation, i.e., the artificial (*tekhnē*) and the conventional (*nomos*), and that which grows by itself, spontaneously, the natural" [VERIFY: confirmed verbatim — Brague 2004, *The Wisdom of the World*, p.14, citekey `bragueWisdomWorldHuman2004`, dossier §5a]. Brague *historiographically pairs* nomos with technē against phusis; he does **not** assert that nomos *is* a species of technē.

The identity-claim — **"nomos has always been tekhne"** — is the **author's own synthesis**, *evidenced-by* not *stated-by* the sources [AUTHOR SYNTHESIS — no source asserts this; see dossier §6]. The building blocks license the grouping of nomos under "the made"; the reading that *convention simply is a kind of making* — that a community's law, even when presented as eternal and divinely sanctioned, is one of the bounded artefacts by which a people sustains itself — is the thesis's own contribution. This is exactly why artificiality is the umbrella: the world's nonmaterial ordering (the nomos) is not a separate ontological kind from its tools (the technē); both are artificiality.

## §IIA — Exosomatization under artificiality

The first aspect is the *impetus* to develop artificiality as extensions or enhancements of our functions, compensating for biological weakness. Since the dawn of history, tools and writing have extended memory; weapons have extended a blunt body and weak biological force. The biophysicist Alfred Lotka named this adaptation through artificial aids *exosomatic evolution* — adaptation *outside* the body, in tools, techniques, records, and institutions — and held that it distinguishes *Homo sapiens* from animals that evolve *endosomatically*, by genetic mutation [VERIFY: Lotka 1945; cf. DOCX [P0113]]. The distinction is one of kind, not degree: exosomatic adaptation accumulates through non-genetic inheritance and at a rate biological evolution alone cannot deliver.

The aids act back on us. Mastering and living with artificial tools becomes itself an adaptational pressure toward the ever-emerging artificial environment — the **self-domestication** Obara identifies as the watershed marker of human existence [VERIFY: Obara 2000, *現代ホモ・サピエンスの変貌*; cf. DOCX [P0114]]. The affordances of these aids direct emotion, thought, behavior, and interaction; new needs arise and call for new aids. The exosomatic loop closes on itself: artificiality extends what we can do and shapes who we become in doing it.

Stiegler reads this ever-exteriorization of organs and memories as the nature of artificiality itself — or, on the other side of the same coin, artificiality as our indispensable organ [VERIFY: Stiegler 1998; note relabel of DOCX's "technology" → artificiality per the opener's discipline]. Exosomatization, while unable to change the laws of the universe, *localizes* them: it "locally trans-forms the cosmic order in unpredictable ways" [VERIFY: confirmed | Stiegler 2018: 42 | per DOCX [P0115]]. This tends to form *techno-geographical milieus* — kin to Obara's artificial ecosystems: environments dependent on, but not reducible to, the universe. (That last clause is the hinge to §IIC; hold it.)

### The fault of Epimetheus — compact (per the decision memo)

The exosomatic compensation is **constitutive, not merely supplementary**, and the oldest stratum of Greek myth already encodes this. In Plato's *Protagoras*, Epimetheus distributes natural powers to every creature — speed, claws, wings, shells — but *forgets* the human, leaving it "naked, unshod, unbedded, and unarmed," so that Prometheus must steal fire and *technē* to compensate [VERIFY: Stiegler 1998, pp.188–189; page-confirmed in Ch-1-8 lookup, re-confirm against print]. Stiegler reads this as structure, not narrative accident: "humans are the forgotten ones; they only occur through their being forgotten" — there will have been "nothing at the origin but the fault… the *de-fault of origin*" [VERIFY: Stiegler 1998, p.188]. The myth figures what paleoanthropology states as **secondary altriciality**: *Homo sapiens* is born biologically unfinished, without an instinct-complete niche, and must be finished from outside itself [VERIFY: Portmann's "extra-uterine year" / neoteny — standard developmental anthropology; uncited here, add a source if a citation is wanted]. Artificiality is therefore not an instrument added to an already-complete creature; it is "the constitution of this body *qua* 'human'" [VERIFY: Stiegler 1998, pp.152–153]. The human does not *have* a world as a mole has its burrow; it must *make* one — because, lacking a given niche, it has no other way to be at all.

This is the strongest form of the §IIA claim, and the anti-instrumentalist point the umbrella frame needs: there is no pre-technical human to be supplemented. It also discharges the author's own premature-birth/neoteny intuition. Keep it compact — roughly this length — per the decision memo; the seven-tradition comparative-cosmogony survey is dropped, and the single myth earns its place by being the originary scene from which Stiegler derives the very *défaut d'origine* the exosomatization claim already invokes.

### Tertiary retention — memory itself exosomatized

What is exteriorized is not only labor but *memory*. Stiegler's analysis sharpens this. Beyond **primary retention** (the "comet's tail" of the living present — the just-heard lingering within what is now heard) and **secondary memory** (individual recollection, re-presentation), he adds **tertiary memory**: the technically externalized record — writing, documents, recordings, the inherited archive — the "supports of the already-there" through which what has passed remains accessible to the living [VERIFY: Stiegler 1998, pp.247, 251; pages confirmed against extracted text, attachment QS6TCGH4]. Tertiary memory is the *hypomnemata* (Plato's term for written memory-aids) that precede any individual and into which each is born; it is the material condition of the world's historical depth, the reason world-making is cumulative and trans-generational rather than Sisyphean.

Keep the forward-looking payoff brief here and let Chapter V (grammatization) carry its weight: the metaverse will figure as the *industrialization of tertiary retention* — when the social archive and the historical deposit are administered by algorithmically managed platforms that select what is amplified, buried, and at what speed history scrolls, imposing a single commercially optimized temporality. That is Stiegler's "proletarianization of memory" [VERIFY: Stiegler 1998/2018 — coordinate one Stiegler edition across Ch II per Ch-2-5 note]. In §IIA it is enough to register that *memory itself is among the things exosomatized*, and that this is what gives the world its inheritable depth. The toxicity is a §V/§VI matter (and a §IID matter — see the close).

## §IIB — Virtuality as the nonmaterial aspect of artificiality

Material artificiality is the easy half to picture — from stone tools to "smart cities." But artificiality is not always material. The nonmaterial forms are indispensable, and dialectically bound up with the material ones. As Harari reminds us, artificially created nonmaterial things — gods, money, laws — "have the power to direct our thoughts and behaviors or even elicit emotions or biological reflexes" [VERIFY: Harari 2015; cf. DOCX [P0117]]. Without such nonmaterial coordinating principles, a world is impossible: material things remain mere aggregates, never integrated into functioning systems.

**I call this causally efficacious nonmaterial aspect of artificiality *virtuality*** [VERIFY: locked definition, DOCX [P0118], line 197 — KEEP VERBATIM]. The phrasing matters: virtuality is an *aspect* of artificial things — their nonmaterial-causally-efficacious side — not a separate ontological category. A coin has a material aspect (metal) and a nonmaterial aspect (its being-money); the virtuality is the latter. Virtuality is thus the §IIB-specific **lens** on the nonmaterial subset of the umbrella concept, artificiality — not a parallel category. Every virtuality is an artificiality; not every artificiality is paradigmatically virtual (a wall compels by mass, not by meaning).

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

### Three views declined

Three popular conceptions of "virtual" are declined, each for a precise reason.

**Against Peirce's "as if."** The simulationist view sees the virtual as a not-quite-real replica — traceable to Peirce's definition, "a virtual *x*… is something, *not an x*, which has the efficiency (*virtus*) of an *x*" [VERIFY: Peirce 1902 — NOT in Zotero per Ch-2-6 v4; LOOKUP or paraphrase-with-secondary-attribution before final]. The thesis keeps the *virtus* (power/efficacy) and rejects the "as if": many virtual things have no original to imitate.

**Against Baudrillard's no-original (Phase-1 ally).** Baudrillard supplies exactly that ground — many artificial things "have no original or real counterparts to represent whatsoever"; a virtual *x* may be original in its own right [VERIFY: confirmed | Baudrillard 1994 | Zotero key `5495YCYV`]. (The thesis takes Baudrillard's no-original point and his dissimulation/simulation precision, but **declines his terminal-stage "Integral Reality" framing** — virtuality here is a *trans-historical genus* present from oral ritual on, not the digital phase's terminal negation of the real. That distinction is fuller in Ch-2-6 v4 ¶3b; keep it light in the chapter.)

**Against Chalmers's computer-based.** Chalmers reasserts the colloquial "virtual = computer-based" to defend its realness against the as-if view [VERIFY: confirmed | Chalmers 2022 | NLM A-Sources UUID `d672826d`]. The thesis adopts Chalmers's substrate-neutrality and simulation-realism *without modification*, but differs at the **analytical unit**: Chalmers asks "is the *x* inside a virtual environment real?"; the thesis's unit is the *trans-historical layer of human-constructed nonmaterial structure* — stories, gods, ceremonies, laws, scientific theories, mathematical models, algorithms — that has always partly constituted human worlds. What Chalmers calls computer-based is "no more than a temporal medium of a long historical continuum" [VERIFY: per DOCX [P0122]]. (Note: the DOCX still carries the "biologically programmed and powered" sentences at [P0122] that Ch-2-6 v4 ¶5c marks for replacement — the highest-value remaining surgical edit, since those sentences are most exposed to a Chalmers-style objection. Flag for author.)

### The historical continuum, and the money paradigm

As Durante observes, humans have always dealt with the virtual, "ever since humans lived by the stories they collectively invented" [VERIFY: Durante 2022 | Zotero key `SBXRDXBB`]. Initially virtuality manifested as gods and ceremonies — the nonmaterial yet causally efficacious basis on which worlds could form; over time gods were displaced by sciences and ceremonies by laws, each transition a *change of substrate* (oral → written → printed → computational) operating on the *same underlying virtuality*. **Money** is the cleanest case: causally efficacious, intersubjectively binding, substrate-promiscuous, predating the computer by millennia. As Brey notes, while "rocks and trees" or "screwdrivers and chairs" can only be *simulated*, "money can literally exist" in a digital environment [VERIFY: Brey 2003 | Zotero key `J4QDKEKB` — DUPLICATE at `DE59HP7U`, dedup before final]. Money is still money, and money has always been virtual; shells, grain, salt, metal, paper, or digits are merely temporal mediums to operationalize money's virtuality [VERIFY: DOCX [P0123]].

### Searle: institutional facts corroborate the realism (footnote-weight)

The claim that nonmaterial artificiality is genuinely causal finds independent support in Searle's social ontology. Searle distinguishes **brute facts** (which obtain independently of human opinion — a mountain's height, light's wavelength; in the thesis's vocabulary, the **universe**) from **institutional facts**, which obtain only through collectively accepted **status functions** — the form "*X counts as Y in context C*" — and carry **deontic powers**: obligations and entitlements with real normative force [VERIFY: Searle, *The Construction of Social Reality* 1995 / *Making the Social World* 2010 — NOT in Zotero per Ch-2-8; the money formulation "such and such counts as a $20 bill in our society" is confirmed via NLM Searle, `searlePhilosophyNewCentury2008` p.33, and the electronic-money "no physical reality… other than the representation," `searleLanguageSocialOntology2008` p.457]. Searle furnishes the social-ontological mechanism — status function plus deontic power — by which money's virtuality binds, and his realism corroborates §IIB's rejection of the as-if: money is not a simulation of prior "real" money; it is constituted by, and exhausted in, collective acceptance.

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

But Searle is **too anthropocentric** for the thesis's purposes — by gating deontic powers behind human *language*, he cannot recognize proto-virtual phenomena [VERIFY: author's DOCX note, "Searle's dichotomy still too anthropocentric — wouldn't say that rituals in animals have deontic powers"]. The thesis draws the line elsewhere: at the presence of a causally-efficacious nonmaterial layer, language-gated or not. Every Searlean institutional fact is therefore a virtuality (a *paradigm* virtuality in the human case); but not every virtuality is a Searlean institutional fact. This belongs at **footnote weight** on §IIB (per Liu's scope discipline) — corroborating interlocutor, not load-bearing apparatus.

### Obara proto-virtuality: virtuality predates the human lineage

The de-anthropocentrizing move has a concrete payoff: virtuality has an **evolutionary floor below the human line**. Just as exosomatization reaches below *Homo sapiens*, so does its nonmaterial dimension. The gorilla's **chest-beating display** does real causal work — it produces deference, social ordering, the avoidance of costly combat — through a nonmaterial principle whose compelling power is given by what the display *means* within the group, not by the physical force of the beat; the sound and posture are merely its temporal medium [VERIFY: Obara's framing as proto-virtual — confirm examples are Obara's own; cf. de Waal on primate displays]. The crane's **courtship dance** follows the same logic in the reproductive register; the coordinated **management of collective excitement** (synchronized alarm, sentinel behaviors, ritualized dominance/submission) completes the picture [VERIFY: Obara's specific examples — which species?].

The discipline here, carried from Ch-2-9: these are *proto*-virtual, not fully virtual. The claim is **continuity-with-difference** — the same nonmaterial-causal structure at incomparably lower complexity, lacking the linguistic-conventional and deontic articulation of the human case (which is exactly why Searle excludes them). Do not overclaim: animal displays are the evolutionary *precursors* of virtuality, not virtualities *tout court*. The payoff is decisive against any suggestion that the virtual is recent or merely technological: "long before Chalmers's computer-based virtual, and even before Durante's storytelling humans, the nonmaterial-and-causal had already organized animal life" [VERIFY: framing per Ch-2-9]. Virtuality is not what computation added to the world; it is what the world has run on since long before there were humans to call it virtual.

## §IIC — The irreducibility of artificial-environment laws

The use-lessness of material objects in digital environments exemplifies a key feature of artificial environments: they are **dependent on and constrained by** the materiality and laws of the universe, yet **not reducible** to them. They have laws of their own. A screwdriver simulated in a digital environment is use-*less* — not from any functional limit of the environment but because the *conditions* for its functioning are absent: no physical fasteners, no gravity, no soreness of legs desiring a chair to rest on; "tightening up" there requires an algorithm, not a screwing object [VERIFY: DOCX [P0125]]. So too the "physically fittest" person: best survival odds in the wilderness (a purely biological environment), but in a *world* — an artificiality field — possibly a *higher* mortality rate (assigned to hunting, war, heavy labor) while a physically unfit person uses charisma or wit to concentrate power and resources [VERIFY: DOCX [P0126]]. The fitness function differs because the *laws* differ.

Mi's committee challenge was that this irreducibility is *asserted, not argued* ("laws為何reducible?"). The argument runs through Polanyi, and then through three formal moves.

### Polanyi: dual control and boundary conditions

Michael Polanyi argues that hierarchically organized systems exhibit **dual control**: at every level, the higher-level organizing principle operates *through* the lower without being *determined by* it. A machine's parts obey physics — friction, thermodynamics, electromagnetism — but the machine's *function* (what counts as it working) is not given by the physics of its parts; the design specifies a **boundary condition** that harnesses the parts' physics without being dictated by it, and is irreducible to it [VERIFY: Polanyi, *Personal Knowledge* 1958 / *The Tacit Dimension* 1966 — NOT in Zotero per Ch-2-4-7; **use the DOCX's own Polanyi formulation at [P0086]**, which already states the boundary-conditions / irreducibility point in the author's voice — do NOT introduce a second paraphrase]. The hierarchy runs upward — physics → chemistry → biology → psychology → sociology → culture — each level harnessing the one below without reduction. Artificial environments introduce a *new level*: their "laws" are boundary conditions imposed on activity within them, just as a blueprint constrains its parts.

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

### Three arguments for irreducibility (the argued response to Mi)

These three are the explicit moves the DOCX argues only *by example*; offer them as an optional strengthening (likely a footnote or short addition to [P0126]).

1. **Multiple realizability.** The same artificial-environment law — e.g., "a posted message cannot exceed 280 characters" — can be implemented in indefinitely many physical ways (languages, hardware, data centers). A multiply-realizable property is not identical to any one physical configuration; hence not reducible to a physical description. (Putnam's argument against the identity theory, applied to institutional/technical structures.)
2. **The semantic/normative gap.** Artificial-environment laws are *normative*: they distinguish correct from incorrect, permitted from prohibited. An electron cannot "violate" a physical law; a user can violate terms of service. The is/ought (permitted/prohibited) distinction is not derivable from any physical description — it needs a level at which meanings and social significance operate. (This is really an argument about *virtuality's* irreducibility — so it strengthens §IIB as well.)
3. **Downward causation.** If these laws were fully fixed by their substrate, they could exert no independent causal influence — yet a corporation's legal structure demonstrably causes physical outcomes (factories built, forests cleared) by organizing the behavior of agents who exist at both levels at once. Downward causation is incompatible with full upward reduction.

The irreducibility is **structural**, not merely epistemological: universe-laws constrain artificial environments only at the *substrate-implementation* boundary (servers need power; bodies need food; institutions need humans), not at the *semantic-operation* level (algorithms decide; institutions confer status; narratives organize meaning).

### The nomos / phusis / tekhne synthesis — the registers of artificiality

Here the opener's Greek framing earns its keep, as the chapter's most crucial synthesis. The laws governing artificial environments fall into two **registers**:

- **Conventional ≈ nomos.** The artificiality compels because and to the extent that humans continue to enact it. A statute is enforced because police, courts, and prisons act; a norm holds because the community sanctions violators; a religious commandment compels because the faithful believe and enforce. **When practice ceases, the artificiality ceases to operate.** This is the *nomos* register — "that which is in habitual practice, use… custom," instituted by authority [VERIFY: LSJ νόμος, dossier §3a; IEP §3.a, dossier §4b].

- **Structural ≈ phusis** `[TERMINOLOGY-PENDING]`. The artificiality compels by the **structure of the situation itself** — the architecture, affordances, operational dynamics — rather than by social agreement. A locked door stays locked with no enforcer watching; an algorithm executes regardless of belief; a smart contract performs its conditional regardless of regret. **The violation is foreclosed as an action-possibility**, not merely deterred by sanction. This register has the *phusis*-like character of a given substrate whose principle operates of itself — which is precisely why the author's reserved Ch V/VI claim ("tekhne becoming phusis") names the metaverse-stage migration of artificiality from the conventional into the structural register.

> (1) "Structural" is `[TERMINOLOGY-PENDING]` (leading alternative: "structurally compelling"); cross-ref `20Wiki/squeeze-ontology.md` §1.3 Aspect A. It is **not** Humean constant-conjunction, and **not** the generic critical-realist claim that all real structures have powers — it names the specific case where an artificiality's architecture/affordance-field forecloses action-possibilities (Gibson affordances / Lessig architecture-as-regulation / Foucault *dispositif*). (2) The mapping **conventional ≈ nomos, structural ≈ phusis** is the **author's synthesis**, evidenced-by not stated-by the sources; do not attribute it to Brague or to any Greek source. (3) Greek-philology glosses for φύσις/τέχνη in the dossier are PARAPHRASE (§3b/§3c) — `[VERIFY: LSJ φύσις/τέχνη — upgrade to verbatim before submission]`.

The register is a *dominant* claim, not absolute: conventional artificiality has *structural margins* (legal coercion via the state's monopoly on violence) and structural artificiality has *conventional margins* (platform policies are revisable). What the squeeze ontology names is the *progressive shrinking* of the conventional margin — initial decisions becoming harder to revise as systems reach operational maturity.

This is also the cleanest answer to the committee's "rule vs. law" question (Mi/Ch-2-2): reserve **"law"** for *structural* artificialities whose violation is structurally prevented (the locked door; code-as-law); reserve **"rule"** or **"norm"** for *conventional* artificialities whose violation is externally punished but not structurally prevented (etiquette; customary law). Under this convention the metaverse genuinely has *laws of its own* — a structural homology with the universe's laws, differing only in that universe-laws are *naturally given* (phusis) and metaverse-laws are *humanly engineered* (technē acquiring phusis-character). Juridical statutes sit awkwardly between the two — a real fact about how conventional law has always *aspired* to structural-compelling power and never fully achieved it; the metaverse's structural register is the successful version of that long aspiration. ## Close — the double-edgedness of artificiality (pharmakon)

Whatever register it occupies, artificiality is **double-edged**, and this is no modern discovery. The structural claim, compressed: *artificialities at sufficient scale or depth tend to generate the conditions for the harm they were instituted to relieve, absent active counter-work.*

**Illich's two watersheds.** Every industrial institution crosses two thresholds. At the **first watershed** it achieves genuine effectiveness ("the year 1913 marks a watershed in the history of modern medicine… a patient began to have more than a fifty-fifty chance" of effective treatment) [VERIFY: Illich 1973, *Tools for Conviviality*, p.8 — NOT in Zotero; suggested citekey `illichToolsConviviality1973`; cross-check the canonical quote set against Ch-2-D / Ch-6-8]. At the **second watershed** its effectiveness inverts — "the progress demonstrated in a previous achievement is used as a rationale for the exploitation of society as a whole in the service of a value… determined and constantly revised by… self-certifying professional élites" [VERIFY: Illich 1973, pp.15–16]. Past it, the institution enacts a **radical monopoly** — not market dominance but the elimination of the conditions under which alternatives could exist: "cars… shape a city into their image — practically ruling out locomotion on foot or by bicycle in Los Angeles… That motor traffic curtails the right to walk… constitutes radical monopoly" [VERIFY: Illich 1973, p.65].

**Stiegler's pharmakon.** Stiegler names the same ambivalence the *pharmacological* condition of all artificiality: the Greek *pharmakon* is both remedy and poison, and (after Plato's *Phaedrus* — Theuth's writing as a remedy for memory that Thamus warns is also its poison) every exteriorized artificiality, once independent of its user, can return as toxic [VERIFY: Stiegler — pharmacological works NOT in Zotero per Ch-2-1 (only *T&T1* YPJ94B8W is held); acquire e.g. *What Makes Life Worth Living*; Ch-2-D carries NLM-verified pharmakon passages that supersede the knowledge-based account here]. Writing supplements memory but atrophies it; social media extends sociality but industrializes it. The pharmakon is *undetermined* until a regime of **care** (Stiegler's *otium* against exploitative *negotium*) settles its disposition — and the metaverse-stage danger is that the pharmakon now mutates at platform speed while care operates at biographical and intergenerational speed, so the mismatch becomes *structural* rather than contingent.

This double-edgedness is the chapter's bridge forward. The thesis's later argument (the dedicated §IID convergence — pharmakon, Illich, Frankfurt's *Dialectic of Enlightenment*, Obara's self-petification, and 君子不器 — developed in `Ch-2-D`) treats the metaverse-stage as the *limit case* of a tendency-toward-inversion that pre-metaverse artificiality already exhibited, because the structural register forecloses the very counter-work each tradition named [VERIFY: per Ch-2-D §IID]. **Placement is an open author decision** (Liu's scope question): the five-tradition convergence may sit best as a §II closer, or migrate toward Chapter IV (Uegaki's 自己完結化 / desomatization), where its payoff lands. The chapter need not resolve this; it need only establish that artificiality, in every register, cuts both ways — the remedy and the poison are inseparable, and which dominates is settled by counter-work, not by the artificiality itself.
