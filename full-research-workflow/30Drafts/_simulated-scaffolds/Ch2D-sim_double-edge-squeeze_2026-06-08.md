# Chapter II — §IID. The double edge of the made — and the squeeze it prepares

The previous section fixed *what kinds of made there are*: the grid of register and aspect, with one cell — the structural-virtual — newly populated at scale. This section asks what follows from that fact, and the answer is not a catalogue of risks. It is a single claim, and I want to state it before defending it, because everything in the chapters ahead leans on it.

I argue that artificiality carries its harm not as an accident that befalls it from outside but as a tendency internal to its own working: artificialities at sufficient scale, or sufficient depth of social embedding, tend to generate the very conditions of the harm they were instituted to relieve, *unless* active counter-work holds the tendency off. This is stronger than the familiar observation that tools have unintended consequences. Unintended consequences are external — a misuse, a side-effect, a failure of foresight that better design might have caught. What I am claiming is that the inversion is *built in*: it follows from the autonomy established in the last section, the made running ahead of its maker, and it would arrive even if every maker were prudent and every user well-intentioned. The double edge is not a flaw in the artificiality; it is the shape of the artificiality.

This is not my discovery, and the strength of the claim rests on its not being mine alone. Five traditions, developed in settings that had never seen a metaverse, each arrived independently at the same structural fact, each naming a different mechanism by which the inversion proceeds, a different counter-work that holds it off, and a different failure-state when the counter-work is absent. I set them side by side not to borrow their authority but because their convergence is itself the evidence: a structural property of artificiality-at-scale, diagnosed five times over from five vantages that did not consult one another.

**Table 2-3 — Five diagnoses of the made's tendency-toward-inversion.**

| Tradition | Mechanism of inversion | Counter-work that holds it off | Failure-state when counter-work is absent |
|---|---|---|---|
| The *pharmakon* (Stiegler, reading Derrida reading Plato) | Every exteriorised making is at once remedy and poison; which it becomes is undetermined by the artifice itself | *Care* — the cultivated, time-taking relation that keeps the remedy a remedy | Drift to the poison-edge under time-economies that foreclose care |
| The two watersheds (Illich) | Past a second threshold a tool inverts its function and works a *radical monopoly* — it eliminates the conditions under which alternatives could exist | Threshold-vigilance; conviviality; deliberate scale-limitation | Radical monopoly: the structural elimination of the unmediated path |
| Self-petification (Obara; 自己ペット化, *jiko petto-ka*) | Having sealed itself inside its own artificial ecosystem, the human is bred to the capsule; biological capacities atrophy because they go unexercised | Preserving the ecological exposure that keeps biological capacity in use | Capsulised life in which somatic competence rots unfelt |
| "The noble person is not a vessel" (君子不器, *jūnzǐ bù qì*; *Analects* 2.12) | Institutional artificiality tends to reduce the human to a vessel (器, *qì*), legible only as its function | Self-cultivation (修身, *xiūshēn*) of the capacities no function exhausts | Generalised instrument-ification (器化, *qìhuà*): human-being-ness collapses into what the apparatus can read |
| The dialectic of Enlightenment (Adorno & Horkheimer) | The reason that emancipates becomes the instrumental reason that administers | *(developed in Chapter IV.A)* | *(developed in Chapter IV.A)* |

Read down the third and fourth columns and the convergence is plain: the counter-works differ, but each is a labour that runs *against* the artificiality's own momentum, and each fails the same way — not through the tool's breakdown but through its success. I take the four traditions whose home is here in turn, briefly, and reserve the fifth almost entirely for the chapter where it belongs.

The *pharmakon* is the oldest name for the double edge. In the *Phaedrus* Plato has Socrates report the Egyptian myth of Theuth, who offers writing to King Thamus as a remedy (*pharmakon*) for memory, and Thamus answers that the same gift is a poison: it will breed forgetfulness, an outer mark standing in for inner recollection. Bernard Stiegler, by way of Derrida, generalises the lesson to every made thing: once a making is exteriorised from the body it gains an independence from its maker, and in that independence it can return as either cure or contagion. "Any tertiary retention is a *pharmakon*," he writes — any of the material supports into which we offload memory and mind (Stiegler, 2016: 32). Writing extends memory and atrophies it; the question of which is never settled by the artifice but by what Stiegler calls *care* — the cultivated, time-taking relation that keeps the remedy a remedy. The pharmacological disposition is decided by the regime, not by the artifact. Here is the first diagnosis of a pre-metaverse squeeze: the made already pulled two ways, and only a labour of care holding it to the curative edge.

Illich gives the inversion its historical mechanism. In *Tools for Conviviality* he argues that every industrial institution crosses two watersheds: at the first it achieves genuine effectiveness, delivering on its promise; at the second its effectiveness turns against its purpose, and it begins to produce the opposite of what it was built for. Past that second threshold a tool exercises not a commercial but a *radical monopoly* — not market dominance over a need, but the elimination of the conditions under which the need could be met by any other means. His clearest case is the automobile: "That motor traffic curtails the right to walk, not that more people drive Chevies than Fords, constitutes radical monopoly" (Illich, 1973: 65). The car achieves its monopoly not by outselling its rivals but by remaking the city so that walking ceases to be a way of getting anywhere. This is the second diagnosis of a pre-metaverse squeeze, and the one whose full development this thesis reserves for Chapter VI, where the car-restructured city is worked through as the precursor case: an artificial ecosystem grown so encompassing that the unmediated path out of it has been engineered away.

Obara reaches the same structure through biology rather than philosophy or sociology. The human, on his account, is the animal that secreted its own ecosystem and then settled inside it — and, settling, came under selection pressures of its own making, a process he names auto-artificial selection (自己人為淘汰, *jiko jin'i tōta*). The benign form is self-domestication; the severe form, which he sees gathering in modern sealed life, is self-petification (自己ペット化, *jiko petto-ka*): a total capsulisation in which the biological capacities of the organism atrophy because the capsule no longer calls on them. The whole made world around us, he writes, is "the *tools* and things (物, *mono*) that self-domesticate the human" — televisions, toys, the relations they engender, the pets, the furnished room.[^obara] The diagnosis is exact and unsettling because the failure-state is not felt as failure: the lapdog does not know it has lost the yard — not, at least, until its own biology recoils, in the diseases of civilisation that register the loss the mind does not. This is the third diagnosis of a pre-metaverse squeeze — the squeeze registered in the body, as competence quietly going out of use.

The fourth diagnosis is older than all of these and comes from a tradition that built its counter-work into an ethics. "The noble person is not a vessel" (君子不器, *jūnzǐ bù qì*), in the *Analects* (2.12), is a negation that states a positive: the cultivated person is precisely what cannot be reduced to a single function, to a vessel (器, *qì*) good for one use and legible only as that use. The Confucian tradition understood, that is, that institutional artificiality at scale tends toward instrument-ification (器化, *qìhuà*), the reduction of the human to the role the institution can read; and it answered with self-cultivation (修身, *xiūshēn*), the cultivation of capacities no office exhausts — a counter-work whose seeds I take up again in the Conclusion. This is the fourth diagnosis of a pre-metaverse squeeze, and it has no other home in the thesis but this one. I note, tentatively and for the author's correction, that the late-imperial examination system in its eight-legged essay (八股, *bāgǔ*) form may furnish a worked historical case of the diagnosis turned real. The eight-legged essay was the rigidly formatted prose form made mandatory in the late-imperial civil examinations, prescribing a fixed eight-part structure into which every candidate's argument had to be poured; and it is dehumanising in precisely this thesis's terms, for it subordinated the mind to a template — a training of the person into a vessel, an instrument-ification (器化) of exactly the kind the ideal of the noble person who is not a vessel was meant to forbid. A bureaucratic artificiality, that is, whose selection criteria narrowed, over centuries, the very kind of person who could pass through it.

The fifth tradition I name and then set aside. Adorno and Horkheimer's dialectic of Enlightenment gives the inversion its most uncompromising form — "Myth is already enlightenment, and enlightenment reverts to mythology" (Horkheimer & Adorno, 2002: xv) — but the labour of unfolding that claim, and of showing how the reason that emancipates becomes the reason that administers, belongs to Chapter IV.A, where it does its proper work; to attempt it here would be to import the engine of a later chapter into the closing of this one.

What unites the four, and the fifth, is not a shared dislike of artificiality. It is a shared discovery about its structure, and the discovery is what licenses the move the rest of the thesis makes. For if the inversion is internal — if the made tends to turn against its purpose by its own logic rather than by misuse — then the metaverse-stage condition is not an avoidable corruption of the structural-virtual artificialities mapped in the last section. It is what those artificialities *become* when the tendency every tradition named is carried to its limit. The metaverse is not a misuse of cell-4 making; it is cell-4 making come into its own.

And so each diagnosis points past itself. None of the five describes a metaverse — they could not — but each describes a tendency that the metaverse-stage does not abolish but *radicalises*, and the radicalisation takes a distinct form in each case, which I can state here and elaborate in Chapter VI. The first is **rate-compression**. The *pharmakon* always required care to hold it to its remedy-edge, and care is slow — it moves at the pace of a life, of a generation, of a culture learning to use what it has made. The structural-virtual artificiality mutates at the pace of its own optimisation, in months; care cannot be paced to meet it; and so the mismatch that was contingent in the case of writing becomes structural, the inversion outrunning the counter-work as a matter of design rather than of neglect.

The second is **typological migration with monopoly-extension**. Illich's second watershed was crossed, in his cases, by artificialities of brick and asphalt and institution — the car, the school, the clinic — and it took them a century or more to cross it. The same dynamic, migrating into the structural-virtual cell, accelerates by an order of magnitude and widens its reach: where the pre-metaverse radical monopoly captured a specific function — mobility, schooling, healing — the platform monopoly captures the conditions of *any* function, mediating participation, recognition, and livelihood as such. The path out is not narrowed; it is removed.

The third is **scope totalisation** — the mode named by the Frankfurt tradition, and, like that tradition, developed elsewhere (Chapter IV). The administered domain that instrumental reason carved out of social life ceases to be a domain and becomes the encompassing environment: the outside contracts toward zero. I state the mode and pass on.

The fourth is **structural enforcement**, or instrument-ification (器化) generalised. What was, for the Confucian diagnosis, a *tendency* under institutional conditions — a pressure the cultivated person could resist, in a culture that prized the resistance — becomes the operative logic of a field in which what cannot be measured, optimised, or rendered legible is, by that fact, unsupported. The not-a-vessel ideal (不器) loses its institutional ground; the vessel is no longer one form a life may decline to take but the only form the apparatus can register.

These four are not four phenomena. They are four readings of one transition, and the transition is the subject of the rest of this thesis: the artificial side of the universe–world–metaverse triad acquiring, at the structural-virtual cell, its own stratum of enduring laws — a made order operating with the authority of a given one, as the previous section's grid already foresaw. Read as the *pharmakon*'s, the transition is the inversion-cycle outrunning the care-cycle; read as Illich's, the second watershed migrating and totalising; read as Obara's, the capsule grown seamless; read as the *Analects*', the vessel made compulsory. Each is the same event seen from one tradition's vantage. And that event — the made acquiring its own laws-stratum, and so standing as a second given against the first — *is* the squeeze, the structural condition this thesis is written to name. The double edge of artificiality, which pre-metaverse making already bore, is what the metaverse-stage sharpens to a point; the convergence of these five diagnoses is the warrant for treating that stage not as one more instance of artificiality-at-scale but as its limit case. Why it is the limit case, and not merely the latest case, is the question Chapters IV through VI take up.
