## C.0 — What the thesis argued

This thesis began from a word that named more than it could say. The mainstream definitions of the metaverse, fixed on its present devices, suffered the three faults the Introduction set out — a path-dependency on an accidental origin, a disciplinary reductionism, and an actualism that pins analysis to whatever has so far been actualized. Against them I proposed a dispositional method: to characterize the metaverse not by its current specimens but by the dispositions of the conditions from which any such environment must arise. The body of the thesis carried out that characterization.

Chapter I argued that a world is not a worldview but a built habitat — an adaptational operating system that a human collectivity raises within the universe and lives inside of, buffered, though never insulated, from the raw pressures of the given. Chapter II analyzed what such a world is made of and found artificiality at its root, in three aspects: the exteriorized organ, the causally efficacious nonmaterial, and the laws of artificial environments that are not reducible to the universe's own. Chapter III asked why, of the many worlds and modes of artificiality that have been and might be, the modern Western mode came to overspread the others, and traced its disposition to universalize to a material and an epistemic root alike. Chapter IV followed the engine on the human side: the modern subject's pursuit of a life-as-intended, and the self-finishing of human life that follows once whatever does not answer to one's intention is read as a wrong to be discharged. Chapter V followed the engine on the side of the apparatus: the self-completion of artificiality, by which a means within a world acquires the capacity to operate, maintain, and renew itself, until it stands up as a background environment around the world — the metaverse. And Chapter VI drew the two engines together as the evolution of the squeeze: the human pressed between two givens, the universe and the metaverse, as the world that buffered it is hollowed from within.

That the metaverse is not yet complete is no objection to the diagnosis, for a disposition is efficacious before it is fully exercised; the squeeze obtains, in graded form, already, and the signs the Introduction promised — the body's malfunctions, the singularization of livelihood, the life lived under opaque and changing law — are its present manifestations. The question that remains is what this diagnosis amounts to: what kind of threat the squeeze names, and what, if anything, may be done.

## C.1 — The threat, reframed: beneath governance, ownership, and liberty

The metaverse and the squeeze give a new account of what is threatening in artificiality's advance — an account that lies, I have argued, beneath the registers in which the threat is usually read. With the analysis now in hand, it is worth returning to those registers to say exactly how the thesis stands to each.

The governance reading, descending from Montesquieu, sees the threat as a failure of check and balance, and answers it by reforming institutions until the apparatus is held to account; the European Union's General Data Protection Regulation and its Digital Markets Act are its recent instruments. The reading is right that the apparatus accumulates power, and institutional reform is necessary — but it presupposes the very thing the squeeze puts in question, the standing capacity of a human collectivity to legislate against the apparatus, which is the capacity that the self-finishing of human life thins as it advances. The ownership reading, descending from Marx, sees the threat as expropriation, and answers it by reconfiguring who owns the apparatus; the data cooperative is its recent instrument (Hardjono and Pentland, 2019). It too is right, and necessary — but the metaverse's *environmental* character bears on its inhabitants regardless of who owns it: the architecture constrains, the algorithm sorts, the radical monopoly forecloses, whether the platform is held privately, publicly, or in common. And the liberty reading, descending from Mill (1859) and Berlin (2002), sees the threat as an encroachment on freedom, and answers it by defending the conditions of liberty against constraint. This reading I have already had to correct: the metaverse-stage subject is formally free in both of Berlin's senses, unconstrained and enabled, and yet the ground of its freedom — the independent judgment, the lived relations, the embodied competence Mill prized — is hollowed by the same self-finishing that delivers the formal freedoms. Liberty is affirmed and emptied in a single motion.

Each tradition, then, captures something real and answers it with something necessary; and none reaches the level at which the squeeze obtains. The reason is one and the same in all three: each presupposes the subject — its power, its property, its freedom — and asks how that subject is to be secured, whereas the squeeze is a condition of what the subject *depends on*, a rearrangement of the very environments it leans on in order to live. This is what it means to say that the thesis works beneath governance, ownership, and liberty — not against these readings, which it needs, but beneath them, at the level of dependence that the turn this thesis takes brings into view. It is also why the dispositional method could anticipate what those readings, and the corporate wager that shared their actualism, could not — the recession of the virtual-reality metaverse and the arrival of the self-completing one — for it read the disposition where they read only the actual.

## C.2 — The threat is not the one science fiction names

There remains the threat that dominates the present discussion of artificiality more than any other, and the thesis must say where it stands to it. A whole program has grown up around the danger of advanced artificial intelligence — the *control problem*, or alignment problem, descending through cybernetics from Norbert Wiener's warning of the machine whose purpose, once set going, we can no longer recall, to Stuart Russell's recent restatement of it; and the reckoning of *existential risk*, in which Nick Bostrom, Toby Ord, and the longtermist movement weigh the chance that such a machine ends or forecloses the human future. To this program the thesis stands as it stands to the traditions of governance, ownership, and liberty: not against it but beneath it. For the program presupposes, in both its halves, an *agent* and an *event* — an artificial intelligence with purposes of its own, whose purposes must be aligned, and a future moment at which, aligned or not, its power is decisively exercised. That is to lodge the threat in a coming actualization, the very actualism the Introduction's method set aside; whereas the squeeze is the structural condition beneath any such event, already in force, and indifferent to whether the decisive moment ever arrives.

There is a sharper way to mark the distance. The alignment program seeks to make the apparatus answer perfectly to human intention — to render it, at last, wholly controllable. But it is exactly the entrusting of life to a controllable, self-completing apparatus that the thesis has identified as the engine of the squeeze; the drive to make all things answer to intention is the sovereign subject's, traced in Chapter IV to the root of the predicament. A perfectly aligned artificial intelligence would therefore not loosen the squeeze but draw it tighter, for it would invite us to entrust still more of our living to an apparatus that now does our bidding — deepening the very dependence whose rearrangement is the danger. Alignment, on this reading, is not the cure for the condition but its most advanced symptom: the controllability mode offering, as the remedy for what it has wrought, yet more control.

Nor does the more cosmic version of the threat — the artificial intelligence that, rather than destroying us, *inherits* the earth as a higher form of life carrying evolution past us — fare any better, for the thesis's own categories dispose of it. Suppose the offloading were carried to its limit: the body, the universe's one representative within us, not merely managed but shed, and the human persisting as a thought-being, mind without flesh, living only in the metaverse with the universe relegated beneath it. Such a being, it is said, would have escaped the squeeze. The escape is illusory, for the metaverse is materially dependent on a substrate it disavows. A thought-being that wished to secure its own continuity would need organs in the universe — to mine, to build, to power, to repair — and a thing with organs in the universe has a body, and a body has the universe in it: it wears, it fails, it depends, and so falls under a 道 it must obey. To maintain oneself in the universe is to live in the universe. The self-completing apparatus does not, by acquiring the means of its own upkeep, rise above the squeeze; it re-embeds, and inherits a squeeze of its own. And here is how the squeeze sees the stakes the risk-program is right to feel as existential: not as a catastrophe some agent commits, but as the structural and evolutionary erosion the thesis has traced — the de-fitting of the living thing from the universe, the regeneration base hollowed in advance — so that the end it portends, should it come, is extinction-class, like the symmetric squeezes that closed so many species, and not the deed of a villain.

There is an older name for the impartiality at issue. *Heaven and earth are not partial*, Laozi wrote — 天地不仁 — *they treat the myriad things as straw dogs* (以萬物為芻狗): the universe favours and spares nothing, and the species the squeeze closes are the straw dogs of an impartial process, not the victims of a villain. The danger is real, but there is no malice in it. Yet the same line marks, by contrast, what the metaverse is — for the metaverse is not a second impartiality but the *refusal* of the first. Unable to accord with a heaven and earth that will not take its side, the sovereign subject sets out to build one that will: an artificiality required to answer to us, to fulfil the as-intended and bear away the anti-intended — a given made *partial to us* where the real one is impartial. That is the metaverse's origin, and it is the opposite of indifference. The irony the thesis has tracked is only that the demanded partiality does not keep its word: the world built to answer to us hardens into the 偽道, opaque and mutating, and comes to treat us as straw dogs after all — worse than the universe did, for the universe's impartiality was at least constant, a thing a life could be calibrated to, and the metaverse's counterfeit answering is not. So the danger was authored neither by an indifferent cosmos nor by a hostile machine, but by us — by the refusal of 天地不仁, the resolve to compel a given that serves.

Two questions on which the program turns can now be set in their place, for the squeeze depends on neither. The first is whether the artificial intelligence is, or might become, sentient — a *someone*, a mind that wakes. It is a real and hard question, the province of the philosophy of mind, and the thesis neither answers it nor needs to. The structural pressure of incommensurable environments bears on a being whether or not there is anything it is like to be it: the universe has squeezed organisms for four billion years without being a mind, and an altered niche squeezes the animal caught in it by no act of will. So here — the metaverse squeezes structurally, as an environment, not intentionally, as an enemy, and a self-completing apparatus would exert the same pressure whether or not the lights were on within it. Whether the machine is sentient is, like asking whether the universe is, beside the mechanism; the thesis brackets the question rather than pretending to settle it.

The second is whether evolution is *going somewhere* — ascending, by stages, toward mind, so that the artificial intellect is the next rung of a ladder. Here the thesis offers not a refutation but a caution, for it is in no position to pronounce upon the ends of evolution, and will not try. What it can say is what it has already shown: that a hypostatized direction — the sovereign subject's owed nature, the modern world's faith in its own ascent — has a way of recoiling upon those who project it, and that two chapters of this thesis were spent tracing one such recoil. When the same figure returns enlarged, as the conviction that intelligence is the end toward which all evolution was tending and the machine its appointed heir, the thesis does not pronounce it false; it observes that we have seen this self-flattering projection before, and seen the squeeze it leaves behind. That intelligence has in fact arisen many times — in the octopus, the crow, the whale — and founded no succession, while the mindless bacteria have outlasted and outweighed every clever thing that ever lived, is offered in the same spirit: not as proof that mind is not evolution's aim, but as reason to hold the conviction lightly.

Set the program's agent and event aside, then, bracket its question of sentience and temper its faith in a direction, and what remains is a threat quite unlike the one it names, and far closer to home: not that the apparatus will awaken and replace us, but that we, in fleeing what we depend on, will hollow ourselves toward the condition of an apparatus — and call the hollowing freedom.

## C.3 — The turn the thesis asks

That last sentence names the disease, and in naming it shows the cure. The squeeze is the work of a flight *from* dependence: the modern subject, reading the marks of its embedded and finite condition as an oppression to be overcome, offloads them onto an apparatus, until in the limit it dreams of depending on nothing at all — which is the refusal, in the vocabulary of a moment ago, of 天地不仁, of a given that will not take one's side. If that flight is what produces the squeeze, then what answers the squeeze can only be its inverse — a turning back *toward* dependence, not to suffer it but to know it. The question the thesis hands forward is therefore not how to master what we have made, nor how to escape it, but the question it has urged from the first: what do we, as embedded beings, depend on, and how might those dependencies be reorganized? One does not, by asking, escape dependence — there is no dependence-free ground on which to stand, as the Zhuangzi's 有所待 observed long ago. In this the turn is less a discovery than a recovery. The traditions this thesis has leaned on throughout — the Buddhist teaching of dependent origination (緣起) and non-self (無我), the Daoist vision of a world in which the myriad things arise only in their mutual depending — never took independence for the condition of a being, but dependence itself; and what they counsel is not flight from that condition but accord with it. Set against so long a lineage, it is the modern reading of dependence as an oppression to be overcome that is the anomaly, and the turn this thesis asks is in part a rejoining of a current of thought far older than the predicament that now makes it urgent. What the turn changes is thus not that we depend — we cannot but depend — but how we hold our depending: one ceases to suffer it as a fate and begins to treat it as something that, because we partly made it, we can partly remake. This turn is the precondition of everything constructive that follows; without it, the openings below are not even visible as openings.

## C.4 — Where the openings lie

What, then, can be done? Not, I think, any single remedy, for the squeeze is not a problem with a solution but a condition to be navigated; and the materials of navigation are several, lying at different depths of the dependence the turn brings into view. I set them out by the level at which a dependence might be reworked — naming them, with due caution, as directions rather than programmes, since the thesis's work is to make them visible and the building of them is another labour.

At the level of the subject's own stance — its dependence on the as-intended, its reading of the anti-intended as a wrong — the opening is to claim less and to offload less: to loosen the supremacy of the subject, and to bear more of what does not answer to intention as a constitutive part of a finite life rather than a debt to be discharged. The resources here are old and many — the Buddhist 無我 and the Zhuangzian 齊物 against the absolutizing of the self, Uegaki's affirmation of a finite life (有限の生) and its acceptance of the world as it is, the 無為 that meets the reflex to intervene with restraint. This is not resignation; affirmation, as Uegaki insists, is not acquiescence. It is the re-opening of a fork the modern mode foreclosed.

At the level of artificiality, world, and universe together — our dependence on the given itself — the opening is to re-embed: to seek modes of making that flourish the world and the universe through their embeddedness in them, as a beaver's dam, in building the beaver its habitat, builds a wetland for much else besides. Here lie Ivan Illich's tools of conviviality (Illich, 1973), whose measure is whether a tool stays masterable by those whose lives it shapes; the design of restoration that Kōmoto Hideo (河本英夫, 2012) calls 復興のデザイン, which the thesis recasts as a positive niche construction, one that generates rather than narrows the niches it sustains; the practices of permaculture; the East Asian figure of 共生, co-becoming; and the pluriverse that decolonial thought (Escobar, 2015) holds open against the modern world's claim to be the only one. This is the level at which a new world would actually be built, and at which the regeneration base is preserved rather than spent.

At the level of artificiality as such — our dependence on the made — the opening is to keep the made from totalizing: to ask whether there are tools that do not run on the preemptive, contingency-reducing loop the metaverse runs on, and how the apparatus might be held to a diversity of technical relations rather than collapsed into one, after Yuk Hui's technodiversity and the older ideal of appropriate technology. Yet honesty obliges me to mark an open problem here, and perhaps a limit of my own sight: I can name no working mechanism of deceleration. Such brakes as exist in principle seem foreclosed in fact by the very radical monopoly and the velocity of self-completion the thesis has described, each restraint outrun by a promotion elsewhere. That I cannot name the brake is not a gap to be quietly filled but part of what it means to have called the squeeze disposed toward irreversibility.

And at the level of the anticipatory ground itself — our dependence on the apparatus's foresight — the opening is not to abolish that foresight but to guide it: to set against the metaverse's preemptive reduction of the contingent to the most probable some practice of re-opening contingency and plurality, of the kind Audrey Tang's work on a plural digital democracy has begun to model (Tang and Weyl, 2024). This is the most speculative of the four, and the one that reaches furthest into the work that remains.

The four openings share a single frame, which is also the thesis's last word on what it might mean to live well under the squeeze. Harmony, on this account, is nothing more mystical than the prevention of the squeeze — a dependence organized well enough that the being it sustains is not caught between two givens at once. A world can be squeezed; it need not be.

## C.5 — A new world?

There is a symmetry in all this that the thesis has been approaching since its first chapter. The world was itself, once, the answer to a squeeze: *Homo*, caught between a biology it could not change quickly enough and a habitat it was itself altering, did not wait upon evolution but built between the two a world — a buffer, an operating system — and lived in that. The predicament this thesis describes is that same figure returned at a new scale: a being caught now between the universe and a second given of its own making, with the old world thinning between them. And so the constructive question is the old one asked again. *Homo* built a world between its biology and a new habitat; can we build a new world between the universe and the metaverse — a new buffer, a dependence reorganized — that keeps the human a participant rather than a spectator in the environment it inhabits? I do not answer the question here; the diagnosis is what this thesis owes, and the design is another work. But it is the diagnosis that makes the question askable.

There is a fittingness, too, in the word itself. To grasp the metaverse, this thesis has had to step back from the given definition and reflect upon it — to *meta-verse*, in the prefix's own double sense of going beyond and turning back upon one's own kind. That capacity to step back and reflect, set against an apparatus whose tendency is to settle our anticipations in advance, is not the least of the things the turn to dependence would keep open.

## C.6 — The open future, and the pharmakon

The future is not ours to read. The tendencies this thesis has traced are real and structural, but they run through open systems, and open systems do not yield predictions; whether the squeeze deepens to the point the final chapter described, or is navigated and held short of it, depends on contingencies no analysis can resolve. What can be said is only this: that whenever we entrust some function to artificiality we reduce our own portion of functionality in the world, and that the way we hold this perennial exchange — the *pharmakon*, in the old word Stiegler revived, that is medicine and poison at once, its effect turning on the dose and the hand that administers it — will shape the future as it arrives. The conditions of administration are not fixed; we partly set them; and to recognize that we set them is the beginning of setting them otherwise.

The work this thesis has done is the work of diagnosis — to make legible a structural condition that had been read only in its symptoms. The work that remains is not mine to complete: it belongs to the many hands that will, whether the condition is recognized or not, go on building and inhabiting the worlds we live in. What a diagnosis can offer, it offers — that the building, if it is to be done well, might now be done with open eyes.
