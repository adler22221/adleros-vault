## I.0 — The metaverse, and why it is not a closed case

By the time these words are read, the word that names this thesis's subject may sound like a closed case. In October 2021 the company then called Facebook renamed itself Meta and staked its future on the metaverse; for a season the term was everywhere; and then, in the popular reckoning, it was gone — written off as a costly corporate misadventure, its flagship virtual worlds thinly populated and its losses mounting into the tens of billions. A reader might fairly ask why a thesis should be given to a fashion that has so visibly passed. The answer I will defend is that the fashion that passed was never the metaverse. What failed was one *definition* of it — the definition that equated the metaverse with a particular kind of technology — and the failure of that definition is, as I will show, the first confirmation of the account this thesis offers in its place.

This thesis aims to conceptualize the emerging concept of the metaverse in a manner that sheds light on the techno-social predicaments of our time and our future, and in particular on the perennial human anxiety about artificiality's threat to humanity. Two considerations led me to take the metaverse as the subject of inquiry (C.-K. Mi, personal communication, January 7, 2022). The first is a mistranslation. In the Chinese-speaking world the term has been rendered, following the People's Republic of China's usage, as 元宇宙 (yuanyuzhou); but 元 means origin, source, or what comes before, so that 元宇宙 says, absurdly, "the original universe" — building the new term out of the very word, 宇宙 (universe), from which this thesis will be at pains to distinguish it. The second is a capture. When Facebook renamed itself Meta and appropriated the metaverse to its own vision, it threatened to bind our sense of the possible to one envisioned by a single corporation; and since our definitions shape our thinking, our thinking our imagination, and our imagination the boundary of what we take to be possible, to leave the term in those hands is to narrow the future in advance. A more adequate understanding is needed — both to repair the translation and to reclaim the futures the capture would foreclose.

It matters, for what follows, *when* this inquiry began. It began at the height of the metaverse's moment — the question was first posed in early 2022, weeks after the rename, when the term's prestige was at its peak — and the argument it produced was set down in a research proposal in 2024 and examined and passed at the doctoral defense in January 2025. I stress these dates because the account this thesis gives was, from the first, a claim about where the metaverse was *heading* rather than a description of what it then *was*; and the years since have been, in the relevant respects, a test of that claim.

## I.1 — Why the prevailing definitions fail

The metaverse entered the language as fiction. The term was coined in Neal Stephenson's novel *Snow Crash* (1992) for a world "where virtual and reality interact and create value through various social activities" (Park and Kim, 2022), and for three decades it lay mostly dormant — less common than its neighbours "virtual worlds" and "cyberspace" — until Meta's announcement of late 2021 brought it suddenly into vogue. The definition that came with that vogue is captured in the Mark Zuckerberg-endorsed account of the entrepreneur Matthew Ball, for whom the metaverse is "a massively scaled and interoperable network of real-time rendered 3D virtual worlds that can be experienced synchronously and persistently … with continuity of data, such as identity, history, entitlements, objects, communications, and payments" (Ball, 2022). The scholarly literature has largely followed: a systematic review of some two hundred and sixty papers defines the metaverse as "a compound word of transcendence *meta* and *universe* … a three-dimensional virtual world where avatars engage in political, economic, social, and cultural activities" (Park and Kim, 2022); and even Luciano Floridi, who observes that in practice the word has become a "catch-all" for Web 3.0, blockchain, cryptocurrencies and the rest, settles in the end on the narrower equation of the metaverse with extended reality, or XR (Floridi, 2022). Across the popular and the academic registers alike, the metaverse is taken to be a kind of technology — at bottom, immersive three-dimensional interfaces and the devices that deliver them.

Three problems make this prevailing approach inadequate to the philosophical work the metaverse demands.

The first is a **path-dependency on the term's arbitrary origin**. The equation of the metaverse with XR is an inheritance from Stephenson's accidental coinage of a realm "beyond" the universe (Mystakidis, 2022; Smart, 2022) — the same arbitrariness that produced the Chinese mistranslation — and it carries two costs. It risks rendering "metaverse" an empty word, replaceable by "XR" with no loss of meaning; and, more seriously, it overlooks the metaverse's *disposition*, shared with many modern technologies, to become all-encompassing, blurring the boundaries not only among technologies but between the artificial and the natural. Here the years since 2022 are instructive, for they have run the experiment. Meta committed itself, and tens of billions of dollars, to precisely the XR definition — to headsets and rendered virtual worlds — and that wager has substantially failed. Meanwhile the development that has in fact reorganized economic and social life is not virtual reality at all but the self-completing artificiality this thesis will describe: generative artificial intelligence, autonomous software agents, the algorithmic mediation of ever more of ordinary life. The thing that everyone called the metaverse has receded; the thing this thesis calls the metaverse has arrived, and arrived under other names. That this could be said *in advance* — the obsolescence of the XR definition was argued in the 2024 proposal of this work and examined at its defense in January 2025, while the virtual-reality vision still commanded the field — I offer not as a vindication of the author but as the first evidence for the method to which I will turn: an account that reads dispositions rather than actualities can anticipate a direction that an account fixed on present specifics cannot.

The second problem is **disciplinary reductionism** (Montuori, 2013). Once the metaverse is reduced to a specimen of XR — set beside other technological specimens such as artificial intelligence — and inquiry is confined within that boundary, then however many intersections one explores (with ontology, ethics, governance, well-being), one drifts along the inertia of specialization, which builds the closed systems that "frame" knowledge and "can prevent some thoughts from being thought, and some possibilities entertained" (Montuori, 2013). A specializing approach serves questions whose boundaries are settled; it ill serves a phenomenon still emerging, still being constructed, and so still volatile, uncertain, and ambiguous in its very nature.

The third problem I will call **actualism**. To settle the question of artificiality's threat by reference to what has so far been *actualized* — what can presently be observed, or plausibly extrapolated from present devices — is to commit what critical realism, after Roy Bhaskar (1975; 2008), names the conflation of the *real* (the level of generative structures and dispositions) with the *actual* (the events those structures happen to produce) and the *empirical* (those events as observed). Given how early we stand in the metaverse's emergence, any conclusion resting on its current actualizations can be voided the moment those actualizations change. The worries raised about XR — "cybersickness," "fuzzy and incongruent identities" (Meinlschmidt et al., 2023) — are already being overtaken by developments like decentralized identifiers (Reed et al., 2020) and use-case-agnostic design (Parisi et al., 2023), on which those worries were contingent. An inquiry pinned to the actual is forever a step behind the thing it studies. (I prefer "actualism" to the more usual "positivism" here, because the fault is precisely the collapse of the real into the actual, which the critical-realist vocabulary names exactly.)

## I.2 — The stance of the inquiry: what an embedded being depends on

To escape these three problems, this thesis declines to characterize the metaverse by its currently actualized specimens, and seeks instead the *dispositions*, or causal powers, of the underlying anthropological, social, and cultural conditions from which any metaverse-stage environment must emerge. Grasping these dispositions lets us discern the direction of the metaverse's development without overcommitting to its particular and revisable specifics — which is, as the previous section argued, the only footing on which a claim about so unfinished a thing can hope to stand.

But the choice of a dispositional method is bound up with a choice about *which question to ask*, and it is worth making that choice explicit, for it sets this thesis apart from the most familiar ways of worrying about the artificial. The dominant framings ask how a powerful apparatus is to be checked, who ought to own it, or how our freedom is to be preserved against it. Each takes for granted a human subject already standing over against the apparatus, and asks how to secure that subject's position. This thesis asks the prior question. Before we ask how the subject's power, property, or liberty is to be protected, we may ask what the subject *depends on* in order to be a subject at all — what we, as beings embedded in environments we did not author, rely upon to live, and how those reliances are arranged. The metaverse matters here not first as a technology to be governed but as a transformation in the structure of our dependence: a new environment that rearranges what we lean on to survive. To investigate the metaverse, on this thesis's understanding, is to investigate what we depend on, and what is becoming of it.

This attention to dependence is no invention of mine; it is one of the oldest threads of East Asian thought. The Buddhist teaching of dependent origination (緣起) holds that nothing possesses a self-standing nature of its own — to be is to arise in dependence on conditions — and the doctrine of non-self (無我) follows from it. The Daoist 有所待 observes the same from another side: even the bird borne on the wind has something it depends on, so that dependence is not the defect of this or that being but the condition of being as such. What is distinctively modern, against this long background, is not the fact of dependence but the resolve to deny it — to take independence from all one did not choose as the very mark of a free and finished self. This thesis's turn to dependence is, in part, a recovery of the older recognition against that modern denial; and the chapters that follow will show the denial, and its cost, at work.

Throughout, I read each environment — the universe, the world, and the metaverse — through three strata: the enduring conditions and principles that organize it; the structures and dynamics those principles generate; and the phenomena in which they become observable to a situated subject. This stratification, which the body chapters render in a classical Chinese vocabulary — 道, 理, 相 — and which corresponds to the critical-realist distinction of the real, the actual, and the empirical, is what allows the thesis to keep its primary claims at the level of dispositions while connecting them, at the level of the observable, to the evidence of the present.

## I.3 — Three terms: the universe, the world, and the metaverse

Three working definitions must be set in place; the body chapters develop them at full depth, but the argument already requires them in outline.

The **universe** is the background environment that precedes us — the realist substrate within which we are situated and which is not subject to our intention. Its laws — gravitational, thermodynamic, biological, ecological, chemical — hold whether or not we know them; we neither make nor remake them; we adapt to them, or we fail to.

The **world** is the artificial environment that a human collectivity builds, defends, and sustains, nested within the universe, as a habitat it can actually live in. Following the ecologist Hideo Obara (小原秀雄) and the historian Yuval Noah Harari (2015), the thesis treats the world not as a worldview but as a built sub-environment — an artificial food chain, and the techniques that maintain it — which a population inserts between itself and the raw pressures of the universe. In the analytic vocabulary of Chapter I, a world is an *adaptational operating system*: a collective strategy of survival. There have been many such worlds, many modes of artificiality, and Chapter III examines how one of them came to predominate.

A word on the term **human**. The thesis does not treat the human as a biological given that subsequently adopts an artificial environment; the human is constituted in the coupling of a biological substrate with the artificial worlds it builds. Where the biological organism alone is meant, I follow Obara's vocabulary and write *Homo sapiens*, or ヒト; "human" names the coupled, world-having being. The distinction will matter, because the developments this thesis traces reorganize the coupling.

And a word on the **metaverse**, whose definition the thesis deliberately withholds until it can be earned. To fix the metaverse in advance would be either to take it for one of its present specimens — the actualism already refused — or to stipulate by fiat what ought to be argued. The thesis therefore builds toward its definition: the metaverse is the second background environment that artificiality *becomes* when its self-completion is sufficiently advanced — an environment, as I will put it, materially dependent on yet irreducible to the universe, running not by the universe's laws but by laws of its own. The metaverse is not a class of devices; it is a structural condition, and to name that condition is the work of Chapters V and VI.

## I.4 — The squeeze, and the level beneath governance, ownership, and liberty

The central claim can now be stated. As artificiality self-completes into a second background environment, the human comes to be situated *between* two environments rather than *within* one — between the universe, the given it has always inhabited, and the metaverse, the second given its own making has produced — each running by organizing principles of its own, and each exacting a cost from those who fail to meet it. This is the **squeeze**, and the hypothesis of this thesis is that it is the structural condition of human existence at the metaverse stage. It is produced by two coupled tendencies that Chapters IV and V develop: a *self-finishing tendency of human life*, by which the modern subject entrusts ever more of living to artificiality in pursuit of a life that answers to its intention; and a *self-completing tendency of artificiality*, by which the apparatus so entrusted comes to operate, maintain, and renew itself without us. The two are the faces of one coin — the more thoroughly we delegate, the more the apparatus completes itself; the more it completes itself, the smaller our own part in the world becomes.

Put this way, the thesis locates artificiality's threat at a level *beneath* the registers in which it is usually diagnosed. The predicament is read, by one influential tradition descending from Montesquieu, as a problem of **governance** — of an apparatus whose power outruns the institutions meant to check it; by a second, descending from Marx, as a problem of **ownership** — of an apparatus concentrated in private hands that extract from the rest; by a third, descending from Mill (1859) and Berlin (2002), as a problem of **liberty** — of a freedom to be defended against encroachment; and, most prominently in our own moment, by the program of AI safety and existential risk — the alignment problem that runs from Norbert Wiener to Stuart Russell, and the reckoning of catastrophic risk in Nick Bostrom and Toby Ord — as a problem of **control**, of an artificial agent whose purposes must be brought into line with ours before its powers grow decisive. Each names something real, and each is necessary; none, I will argue, reaches the level at which the squeeze obtains. The first three presuppose the subject whose governance, property, or freedom is in question, and ask how to secure it; the fourth presupposes an artificial agent whose decisive act lies in the future, and asks how to control it — lodging the threat thereby in a coming event rather than a present condition. But the squeeze is a condition of what the subject *depends on* — a rearrangement, beneath governance and ownership and liberty and control alike, of the very environments a human being leans on in order to live. This is the level the turn to dependence opens, and the level at which this thesis works.

The case of liberty is the most telling, because it shows why the threat is so easily mislaid. One expects a threat to freedom to take the form of constraint — an authority forbidding what the subject would otherwise do. But the metaverse-stage subject is, in the formal sense, freer than ever: unconstrained in its choices, and equipped by the apparatus to realize them. What it loses is not freedom but the *ground* of freedom — the embodied competence, the lived relations, the capacity to meet what does not answer to its intention — and it loses these not against its will but in the very pursuit of a life that answers to its intention. The offloading by which it draws nearer to such a life is experienced, genuinely and not falsely, as liberation; the diminishment is the same act seen from its other side. Freedom, then, is not the axis on which this threat is measured. And this is also why the threat requires none of the apparatus of science fiction — no artificial intelligence awakening to outwit or destroy us. That the apparatus is becoming a second environment we must adapt to, and that we are losing the means to adapt to the first, is threat enough, and is under way already; the more dramatic scenario, and why even its strongest form does not hold, I reserve for the Conclusion.

## I.5 — The course of the argument

The argument proceeds in six chapters. **Chapter I** develops the conception of a world as an adaptational operating system nested in the universe, grounding it in the niche-construction record across the tree of life and in the emergence of *Homo* — the animal that, meeting a mismatch between its biology and its habitat, built a world between the two and lived in that. That a world is, at root, something built to keep its makers from being squeezed will return at the very end as a question: whether a new world might be built now. **Chapter II** analyzes what worlds are made of — artificiality in its three aspects: technology as the exteriorization, or exosomatization, of human organs and memory (Lotka, 1945; Stiegler, 1998); virtuality as causally efficacious nonmaterial making; and the irreducibility of the laws of artificial environments to those of the universe. **Chapter III** asks why, of the many modes of artificiality, the modern Western mode came to predominate, tracing its disposition to universalize to both a material basis (Needham et al., 2004; Pomeranz, 2021) and an epistemic one (Bhaskar, 2008). **Chapter IV** turns to the human-side engine: through the work of the philosopher Takahide Uegaki (上柿崇英, 2022a; 2022b), it examines the modern pursuit of a *life-as-intended* and the self-finishing of human life that follows from reading whatever does not answer to one's intention as a wrong to be overcome. **Chapter V** turns to the apparatus-side engine: the self-completion of artificiality, minimally realized in the cybernetic coupling of artificial intelligence, big data, the Internet of Things, and code-as-law (Lessig, 2006), and the emergence from it of the metaverse as a second background environment. **Chapter VI** draws the two together as the *evolution of the squeeze*, distinguishing the metaverse-stage squeeze from its precursors and reading its present signs. The **Conclusion** returns to the threat, sets the thesis's account apart from the governance, ownership, and liberty traditions and from the science-fiction scenario, and asks where, given the diagnosis, the openings lie — whether, in the end, a new world can be built between the universe and the metaverse.

The contribution I claim is this: that beneath the familiar readings of our technological predicament there lies a structural condition they do not name — the squeeze — and that to see it requires a turn from asking how to master what we have made to asking what we have come to depend on. The chapters that follow make that turn.
