## §E (recalibrated)

**¶ (opening caution — retained):** The movement still opens with the caution that giving a mode its conditions of emergence is *not* to call it superior (current l.297): "What the material conditions explain is not a victory but a possibility — how a set of fortuitous circumstances allowed one interventionist world to break a constraint that had held its rivals in check."

**¶ (Needham's question + the multi-causal chain — replaces l.299–304, integrating #E1/#E2):**
Joseph Needham spent a scholarly lifetime on the question these conditions answer: why the systematic, controlling science that remade the world arose in early-modern Europe and not in a China that had long been its technical equal. His answer was never that Europeans were cleverer; it was that a web of material conditions ran differently in the two civilizations and produced, at its far end, two different relationships between knowledge and nature. I take that web from him — but as a *web*, not a chain. The relations are reciprocal, not a single line of dominoes: geo-ecology shapes a mode of production, which shapes institutions and a stance toward nature, which shapes a characteristic science — but the science funds the military competition that rewards it, the institutions entrench the mode that produced them, and the metaphysics licenses the very interventions that confirm it. With that caution, the strands can be set out.

The first strand is the mode of production and the stance toward nature it breeds. Where a civilization lives primarily by tending plants, Needham observed, "the less he interferes with the growth of his crops the better… they follow their Tao" — and he found the disposition crystallized in a parable: "Let us not be like the man of Sung. There was once a man of Sung, who was grieved that his growing corn was not longer, so he pulled it up" — and killed it (Needham, 1956). The would-be improver who yanks the seedling to hasten it is the very figure of interventionism rebuked; the agrarian world's wisdom is *wu wei*, "no action contrary to Nature." Where a civilization lives instead by herding and seafaring, the psychology inverts. Following André Haudricourt, Needham traces the contrast to the work itself: the command attitude is bred by the labour of herding and seafaring — "the cowboy or shepherd drives his animals about," and "the sea-captain gives orders to his crew" — so that "pastoral dominance over animals consorts well with legislation over things as well as men" (Needham, 1956). The cosmos such a people pictured was a machine of separable parts under externally legislated law, where the agrarian cosmos was an organism to be accorded with.

One feature of this command-psychology must be drawn out, because the disposition does not confine itself to the universe. The command Haudricourt describes does not stop at animals: "pastoral dominance over animals," in Needham's words, "consorts well with legislation over things *as well as men*," so that "law in Nature would have been derived from the masteries of shepherds and sea-captains as well as kings" (Needham, 1956). The same command-and-control comportment is exerted upon whatever it takes as its object. The West exerted it, historically, upon bonded human beings — mass chattel-slavery, "such as one found in the Mediterranean civilizations," and largely absent from a China "modelled… not [on] slavery, but [on] free farmers" (Needham, 1969b, *Within the Four Seas*): the command turned upon men. And it exerted the same comportment, in the interventionist science of the modern age, upon nature: the command turned upon things. Chattel-slavery and interventionist technoscience are not cause and effect; they are two *objects* of one disposition — command over men and command over things — both downstream of the masteries of shepherds and sea-captains. (This is to be kept distinct from Needham's separate, economic observation that mass slavery also *retarded* labour-saving invention; that concerns why China gained an early technical lead, not the command-disposition as such.)

The second strand is institutional, and it is the heart of Needham's "why not China." The European side ran through "the aristocratic-military feudalism of Europe, out of the womb of which mercantile and then industrial capitalism… could be born" — a hereditary landed aristocracy, on terrain fragmented by sea and forest into many jostling small lordships, out of whose decay an autonomous merchant class and the city-states arose. The Chinese side ran through "an extremely elaborate civil service… the 'mandarinate', enjoying no hereditary principle… but recruited afresh in every generation," atop a social order whose "classical enumeration of the four ranks" placed the merchants dead last (scholars, farmers, artisans, merchants). That mandarinate "nipped capitalist accumulation in the bud" and "creamed off… the best brains… into its own service," so that science kept a "relatively 'official' character" — the astronomer "a civil servant lodged… in part of the imperial palace" rather than an independent inquirer (Needham, 2004). Needham's own hinge is exact: "whoever would explain the failure of Chinese society to develop modern science had better begin by explaining the failure of Chinese society to develop mercantile and then industrial capitalism" (Needham, 1969a, *The Grand Titration*, ch. 1, "The Role of the Merchants" [VERIFY page against P:\books — print page not embedded in the PDF; passage confirmed in ch. 1, "The Role of the Merchants"]). The same hinge is developed in *Within the Four Seas* (Needham, 1969b), to which the citation can be re-anchored if *The Grand Titration* is not added to Zotero.

The third strand is military, and it loops back into the second. Fragmented Europe was a field of "numerous warring nations… jostling military aristocrats," and "in the West war was mainly a conflict between nations," a perennial competition that "gave… stimuli… to science and technology." Unified China, "a coherent agrarian land-mass… since the third century B.C.," had civil wars but, in Needham's striking phrase, "internal national conflicts never," and "correspondingly there was to some extent an absence of the stimuli that war gave to science and technology in Europe" — so much so that gunpowder, lacking knightly cavalry or feudal castles to overturn, "produced no perceptible effect upon the age-old civil and military bureaucratic apparatus" (Needham, 2004). The intergenerational private accumulation that hereditary aristocracy and then mercantilism permitted both funded and was spurred by that competition; in China the non-hereditary mandarinate and the suppression of commerce starved the same loop. *(This is the "multi-causal interrelating" reading you asked for: the strands condition one another; I keep your arrows as the prose's organizing heuristic but resist the single-line chain.)*

**¶ (Table 3-1 — rebuilt, English, elaborated; replaces the 6-row table l.322–329):**

Table 3-1. Two modes of world-making and their material conditioning (after Needham).

| | Sinographic sphere (East Asia) | Mediterranean littoral (proto-Europe) |
|---|---|---|
| **Mode of production** | Self-sufficient settled agriculture; its hydraulic basis (water-works, flood control) *demanded* large-scale coordination, yet the imperial bureaucracy's reach into everyday peasant production stayed limited — "heaven is high, the emperor far away" (cf. the "Asiatic mode of production"): central coordination for taxation, conscription, and waterworks, not for production itself. | Animal husbandry (Needham's term) together with oared/manned seafaring and trade; terrain fragmented by archipelago, peninsula, and forest, scattering land among small, independent feudal lords — a hereditary landed aristocracy. |
| **Ontology** | Organicism: the cosmos as a living, self-regulating whole, known by correlative resonance (yin–yang, the five phases) — "a mirror image," in Needham's phrase, of an integrated bureaucratic society. | Mechanism: the cosmos as separable parts moved by externally legislated law — a machine to be taken apart and reassembled. |
| **Praxis / stance toward nature** | Non-intervention: *wu wei*, accord, attunement, harmony; an observational and correlative science, "propitious for the natural sciences, yet non-experimental." | Intervention: command, mastery, the subordination and obedience of the object; an experimental science pursuing "the more complete… domination of nature." |

**¶ (the two disciplines in reading the table — retained):** Needham's own (the left column is "a different configuration, not a moral victory") and the thesis's departure from his teleology ("there is no ocean toward which the rivers were flowing; there is one mode of artificiality that universalized, and presented its universalization as the ocean") are retained unchanged (l.331). One guardrail must be stated before the contrast can do any work. The opposition is *ideal-typical*, not a description of two uniform blocs: each civilization is internally diverse and shot through with exceptions and counter-currents, and the table makes no claim that every Chinese thinker was an organicist or every European a mechanist. What the contrast explains is a *dispositional tendency* — the enduring organizing principles that gave each mode of world-making its characteristic slope — not the local particulars of any one place, period, or thinker. It is a claim about the underlying disposition of each mode, not a census of its instances.

**¶ (Pomeranz — retained, correctly attributed):** Pomeranz's contingency thesis is retained (l.332): Western Europe c. 1750 was "a none-too-unusual economy"; what broke England out were "two crucial discontinuities, one created by coal and one by colonies" — coal lying "fortuitous, and specifically English," and the New World's "ghost acreage" exceeding even coal (Pomeranz, 2021). *This* is where the colonies-and-minerals → externalized-growth-burden → release-from-the-agrarian-ceiling argument lives — it is Pomeranz's, not Miura's (see Scaffold-notes).

**¶ (Miura — retained, attributed to what he actually argues):** Miura (三浦, 2006) is retained for the entrenchment argument that is genuinely his (l.333): the mechanistic conception "freed the human… from the old religious view of nature, from taboos, reverence, and ethical restraint, and so answered… the demand of capitalism and the modern state for domination over nature" (Miura, 2006: 162); the colonies fixed as a "core–periphery structure that… still basically persists" (Miura, 2006: 233–234); the built-in "vicious cycle" (*akujunkan*, 悪循環) by which the mode "at the very moment it achieves its specific purpose produces new evils requiring further intervention" (Miura, 2006: 156); and the dependence engine of the later essay (Miura, 2004: 15–16). The material conditions "did not merely permit [the mode's] rise; once in place, they wound it tighter, converting a contingent advantage into a self-perpetuating disposition to intervene" (retained l.333).
