# II §C. Why the made is irreducible to the material: virtuality

While it might be easy to picture how tangible material technologies have extensively reshaped our worlds — from cave- and stone-based tribes to AI-powered "smart cities" — artificiality is not always manifested in material forms. The nonmaterial forms of artificiality are indispensable to the development of their material counterparts, and the two lead dialectically to one another. Artificially created nonmaterial things — gods, money, laws — have the power to direct our thoughts and behaviours, and even to elicit emotions or biological reflexes such as the fight-or-flight response. Without these nonmaterial things, or nonmaterial aspects, that facilitate the interactions and dynamics among a system's components, a world — "an artificial environment nested in the universe for human survival" — would not be possible at all, for its material things would remain mere aggregates rather than being integrated into functioning systems.

With this in view the central claim of the section can be stated without hedging. The world the human builds is irreducible to the material that underlies it. I do not mean that the world floats free of matter — every artefact is some arrangement of matter and obeys matter's laws. I mean that what an artefact *is*, and what it can *do*, cannot be read off from the matter that composes it: a made thing has properties and exercises powers that its material substrate neither possesses nor explains, and a world built of such things is therefore a stratum of reality with laws of its own, not derivable from the physics beneath it. The argument turns on identifying the precise feature of the made that its material cannot supply.

That feature is what I will call virtuality. The made works, very often, through something that is causally efficacious yet nowhere material — a status, a rule, a meaning, a relation that does real work in the world without being any of the matter through which it works. **I call this causally efficacious nonmaterial aspect of artificiality virtuality.** Its medium is the sign: virtuality is the *semiotic* dimension of the made — the work done by what a thing *means* rather than by the matter it is made of. Two qualifications fix the term against predictable misreadings. First, virtuality is an *aspect* of artificiality, not a separate category of thing: every virtuality is an artificiality, but not every artificiality is virtual. A wall compels chiefly by its sheer mass rather than by what it means, and is thus artificial without being, in the relevant sense, virtual — though even a wall is rarely mute, since its mass and its meaning (a boundary, a prohibition, a frontier) typically work together. Second, and against the word's everyday connotations, virtuality is not unreality. It is the opposite: the dimension in which the made does its most consequential work. The authority in an office, the obligation in a promise, the value in money, the boundary in a border — each is fully effective and nowhere locatable in the matter that bears it, present in that matter only in the way a melody is "in" the air that carries it, which is to say not reducible to that air at all.

## The simulationist view, and why I depart from it

Because "virtual" is a worked-over word, I must mark my sense off from the conceptions already in circulation, keeping from each what is sound and declining what would mislead.

The first and most entrenched is what I will call the *simulationist* view — the view that a virtual *x* is a mere "as-if" copy of a real *x*, possessing certain characteristics or features of the simulated real thing yet not being completely real, in the sense Baudrillard gives the term. This view can be traced back to C. S. Peirce's definition (1902): "a virtual *x* (where *x* is a common noun) is something, not an *x*, which has the efficiency (*virtus*) of an *x*" (emphasis added). My usage carries forward the "efficiency (*virtus*)" aspect of Peirce's definition — the sense descending from the Latin etymon *virtus* (virtue) and *virtualis* (power or potency) — but I find the "as if" aspect of his definition unhelpful. As Jean Baudrillard shows (1994), many artificial things have no original or real counterpart to represent whatsoever. Seeing a virtual *x* as a simulation of a real *x* — as a copy whose reality-status is parasitic on some original it stands in for — overlooks the fact that many virtual things are novel: a virtual *x* may be original in its own right. The money in the banknote is not a stand-in for some realer money elsewhere — it *is* money, fully and without remainder. Without a radical departure from the simulationist view, we cannot make sense of an emerging future in which we are increasingly incapable of finding the real that is supposedly represented or simulated by the virtual.

The simulationism just described is, however, only one of three positions on the reality of the virtual that are easily run together, and it will help to be exact about which I am rejecting by setting all three side by side. The first is *fictionalism*: the view that virtual objects are mere fictions, no more real than the characters in a novel — efficacious only as we pretend, with no causal standing of their own. The second is the *as-if simulationism* I have just traced to Peirce's "as if" and Baudrillard's copy-without-an-original: the virtual is a stand-in for the real without being it, possessing the features of the real thing yet falling short of its reality, and so not really efficacious in its own right. The third is *virtual realism* (or *simulation realism*): the view that virtual objects are *real* — that they exist, exercise causal powers, and are no illusion. The thesis rejects fictionalism outright; its quarrel over *reality-status* — virtuality is really efficacious, not a derived stand-in — is a quarrel with the *Baudrillardian as-if*, not with realism about the virtual. On that decisive question the thesis stands with David Chalmers, whose view I take up at length below. Chalmers is a *simulationist*, but of the realist, anti-fictionalist kind: he holds that the virtual is computer-based *and* fully real, the opposite of a fiction. He is not, in any usable sense, an "anti-simulationist"; what he is against is *fictionalism*, and so am I. My departure from Baudrillard is therefore a departure in Chalmers's direction on the reality of the virtual. From Baudrillard I keep only the precise distinction between *dissimulating* — masking a reality that is there — and *simulating* — generating a "reality" with no original behind it; what I decline is his terminal, epochal framing, in which the virtual names a late historical condition, an "integral reality" that has finally displaced the real (Baudrillard, [VERIFY citekey] — *The Intelligence of Evil; or, The Lucidity Pact*, on *la réalité intégrale*). Virtuality, in my account, is not a stage that modernity reaches; it is a *trans-historical genus*, as old as money, law, and rite, of which the digital is only the latest and most concentrated species. (My one quarrel with Chalmers, set out below, is not over the reality of the virtual at all but over what *makes* a thing virtual — the computational-versus-semiotic axis.)

That this is no eccentric usage is best seen by recovering two thinkers in whose work the very same conception is already at home — and who supply the *reasons* for it that a bare etymology cannot. The sociologist Anthony Giddens holds that social structures exist *virtually*: on his analysis, structures are rules and resources that have no continuous material existence of their own but exist only as instantiated in social practice and as memory traces in the minds of agents (Giddens, 1984). Here "virtual" plainly does not mean an "unreal or not-as-real representation or simulation" of something else that is truly real; it marks, rather, that social structures of the kind Giddens analyses are, as he analyses them, human-dependent and do not exist materially *per se* — which is exactly the sense I require. I take this as a feature of social structures as Giddens describes them in the present human dispensation, not as a permanent law of all virtuality: the later chapters will argue that under a maturing metaverse the nonmaterial apparatus may begin to detach from the humans who instantiate it, so that the *human*-dependence Giddens observes is precisely one of the things at stake, not a fixed point. What I borrow from him is the narrower and more durable claim — that such structures are real and causally efficacious without existing materially. The philosopher Roy Bhaskar reaches the same place from the side of ontology: he treats the virtual not as something contrasting with reality but as constituting "a *sui generis* real emergent stratum of reality with its own irreducible causal powers" (Engholm, 2015). The virtual, on Bhaskar's account, is at once *real* and *causally efficacious* and *not material* — a higher stratum dependent on the lower, yet with powers of its own that the lower does not possess. Giddens gives the non-material instantiation of structure; Bhaskar gives the emergent-stratum realism; between them they say precisely what virtuality is, and why calling it "virtual" need carry no whiff of the unreal.

## Chalmers's computational view, and the axis at which I differ

The most demanding of the rival conceptions is the most recent — and the closest to a genuine rival, because it too insists on the *reality* of the virtual. This is the now-colloquial view, championed by David Chalmers (2022), which takes the virtual to be *computer-based*. Chalmers states it plainly: "a 'virtual reality' is a computer-based reality" (Chalmers, 2022: 192–93), and "at least to a first approximation, a virtual object is a structure of bits inside a computer" (Chalmers, 2022: 198). His motive is anti-*fictionalist*, and admirable in it: precisely to avoid the illusory or fictitious implications of the older "as if" view, he reasserts the computer-based reading in order to underwrite the realness of virtual things — a realness he grounds in the existence, causal efficacy, mind-independence, non-illusoriness, and genuineness of digital objects within computer systems. This is the burden of his best-known provocations. A virtual cat, he insists, is a real cat: "The cats around you are real cats — it's just that cats in your reality are made of bits" (Chalmers, 2022 [VERIFY page]); and from this it follows that even if we *are* in a simulation — even, that is, if we ourselves are virtual humans — we are not thereby cheated of reality, for "you can lead a fully meaningful life in a virtual world," and "the world we're living in could be a virtual world" without being on that account any less real (Chalmers, 2022 [VERIFY page]). And his account is, importantly, *substrate-independent*: "the same sequence of bits," he notes, "can be encoded in all sorts of substrates — punched cards, mechanical switches, transistors, or patterns of beer cans" (Chalmers, 2022: 165). Chalmers is therefore no naïve materialist about the virtual; he is a realist about it, as I am.

From Chalmers I adopt two things: that the virtual can be fully real, and that what matters is the structure rather than the particular substrate that carries it. Where I depart from him is *not* on substrate-independence — on that we are agreed, since I too hold that virtuality is tied to no particular matter — but on what the structure in question *is*. For Chalmers the relevant structure is *computational*: a virtual object is "a structure of bits inside a computer" (Chalmers, 2022: 198), so that, whatever substrate carries those bits, it is *computation* that makes a thing virtual. On my account the relevant structure is *semiotic*: a thing is virtual in so far as it does its causal work by what it *means* — by standing within a fabric of signs, statuses, and relations — of which the computational is only one species. The axis between us, then, is not substrate-independent versus substrate-bound; both of us are substrate-independent. The axis is *computational versus semiotic*. Chalmers, I will argue, mistakes a *species* — computational virtuality — for the *genus* — semiotic virtuality; the bit-structure inside a computer is genuinely virtual, but it is virtual because it is meaningful, not the other way around, and there are virtualities aplenty that are meaningful without being computational at all.

The cleanest proof that the computational cannot be the genus is a case that does not presuppose my own meaning-definition in the first place: money. Money is the strongest single challenge to the computer-based view, and it is decisive precisely because it can be pressed without begging the question against Chalmers. The money in a contemporary bank account just *is* a structure of bits inside computers — it runs on exactly the computational substrate Chalmers points to — and yet no one calls the balance in their account "virtual money." We do not say it, and we are right not to, because money is still money: its identity has been invariant across cowrie shells, grain, salt, minted metal, paper, and now digital digits, none of which is what the money *is*. As Brey notices (2003), where "rocks and trees" or "screwdrivers and chairs" can only be *simulated* in a computer, "money can literally exist" there. If being a structure of bits inside a computer were sufficient for being virtual, then digital money would be virtual money — but it is not, on anyone's ordinary usage; it is simply money, in its latest medium. The identity of money is therefore institutional and semiotic, not computational: what makes it money is a status borne by a sign, and that status is the same whether the sign is shell, coin, paper, or bit. The case establishes, on grounds independent of my definition, that *computational is not coextensive with virtual* — that the bit-substrate is neither necessary (shells were money) nor sufficient (digital money is not called virtual) for virtuality. Computation is one shell that the semiotic can occupy, not the thing itself.

Having shown by the money case that the computational is only a species, I can now extend the point downward, below the human line — to a domain where leading would have begged the question, since it is a domain that fits my meaning-definition but not Chalmers's computational one. Long before any computer, and indeed before language, animals already trade in something that does causal work by what it *means* rather than by what it physically delivers. Obara describes the gorilla's chest-drumming as a ritual the gorilla performs before entering into true combat, one that serves the function of *avoiding* a fight (Obara & Hani, 1995: 17–19). The drumming does real causal work — it establishes deference, orders the troop, forestalls violence — and it does so by what it signifies, not by any blow struck; the outcome is settled though no contact is made. The same holds for the courtship displays Obara catalogues among cranes and ibex, where a patterned performance accomplishes its intended end in the reproductive register (Obara & Hani, 1995: 22–26). These proto-virtual displays are compelled by *shared meaning*, not by bits — and, as a growing body of animal-culture studies shows ([LOOKUP cite — Whiten et al. on animal culture; de Waal; Hobaiter & Byrne on great-ape gestural meaning; add to Zotero]), such meaning is itself shared, transmitted, and answered to within the group, not the private epiphenomenon the older picture took it for. The "meaning" at issue here is to be understood *biosemiotically*, in Jakob von Uexküll's sense (Uexküll, [VERIFY citekey]): each animal inhabits an *environment-world* (*Umwelt*) populated by *meaning-carriers* (*Bedeutung*) — significances to which it is attuned and which orient its conduct — so that to speak of meaning below the human line is not to project a human category downward but to recognise the meaningful *Umwelten* in which animals already live. Biosemiotics is, in this respect, Peirce's general theory of the sign — invoked above for the *virtus* of the virtual — carried below the human line (the line Uexküll's *Umwelt* was read back into by Sebeok): this is the same Peircean point made one register lower, since the drumming and the display are *signs* before they are anything human, and they do their work as signs. The Enlightenment, mechanistic habit of mind — Bacon's and Descartes's — would dismiss them as biologically determined behaviours "without meaning," mere mechanism; but that dismissal is anthropocentric, and animal-cognition research no longer licenses it. Once we grant that meaning can be causally efficacious below the human line, a view that ties the virtual to the *computer* cannot be the *definition* of virtuality; it can only be the description of its latest medium.

A disclaimer is owed here on the word "proto-virtual," lest it claim either too much or too little. In calling these animal displays *proto-virtual* I am deliberately *bracketing* — not settling — the long debate over whether animals possess reason, concepts, or anything we would dignify as full intentionality; "proto" marks that I decline to enter that quarrel, not that I have adjudicated it. What the term is meant to register, positively, is that virtuality is not a human novelty: it is a continuum that extends from animal origins into human worlds, and the human's traffic in signs is an inheritance and elaboration of something already running below the human line, not an unprecedented invention. Obara makes precisely this kind of move for ritual: his *That's Why We Cannot Get Rid of Rituals* (だから儀式はなくならない, *Dakara Gishiki wa Nakunaranai*) (Obara, [VERIFY citekey]) traces human ritual back to its animal origins, and the gesture of this thesis is the same — virtuality already has its roots in the animal. The "proto" thus does double service: it brackets the reason-debate, and it names the continuity, the rootedness of the human-virtual in something older than the human.

One caution must be entered here, lest the word "pre-computational" claim more than I intend. In calling these displays pre-computational I am making a point about *manifest human meaning-efficacy* and about the *digital-computer substrate* — not denying that the universe might itself be, at bottom, a computation or a simulation. Chalmers is right that this is an open possibility, and nothing in my argument turns on closing it. My claim is independent of the simulation question: virtuality operates *by meaning*, trans-historically, whatever the universe's ultimate substrate turns out to be. Even if the universe were a simulation all the way down, the gorilla's drumming and the value of a coin would still do their work by what they mean — and that is the only sense of "non-computational" the argument needs.

That virtuality is trans-historical rather than a digital novelty has, moreover, been argued independently. Massimo Durante observes that the human "has always dealt with the virtual," and that "the virtual existed long before and regardless of any technologically digitized virtual reality" (Durante, 2022: 318–19). I cite Durante not as a rebuttal of Chalmers — he does not name Chalmers, and engages a different set of interlocutors — but as independent support, arrived at by another route, for the trans-historical conception this thesis defends.

The continuum is easy to trace once it is seen for what it is. Initially, humans told stories of gods and practised ceremonies in the hope of bettering their lives in a vast, unknown, and unpredictable universe; these orally communicated gods and embodied ceremonies formed the nonmaterial yet causally efficacious basis of our early worlds. Over time, in many places, gods were displaced by science(s) and ceremonies by laws — documented and conveyed in writing. Nowadays we increasingly receive guidance from AI and are regulated by algorithms through computer-based interfaces and objects. The medium changes — oral, written, printed, computational — while the virtuality persists. And the trajectory does not stop at the computer: some commentators imagine that the virtual will one day move beyond computational substrates altogether, to interfaces that are biologically embedded rather than screen-based. But this rests on a confusion of substrate with form. A biologically embedded or directly neural interface is a *different substrate*; it is not therefore non-computational, since computation is, as Chalmers rightly says, substrate-neutral — biological, neuromorphic, and not-yet-imagined substrates all qualify provided they implement the requisite structure. Virtuality is tied to *no* substrate whatever; that is exactly why it can survive every substrate-shift, and why no medium — beer cans, transistors, or living tissue — can be its definition.

## Money, use-lessness, and laws of their own

The money case put to work above against the computational reading carries, beyond that negative lesson, a positive one about *function* — and it is the functional point that I want to draw out here. Searle states the constitutive structure definitively. "It is only given the institution of money," he writes, "that I now have a five dollar bill in my hand. Take away the institution and all I have is a piece of paper with various gray and green markings" (Searle, 1969: 51). The form of the fact is "such and such counts as a $20 bill in our society" (Searle, 2008a: 33); and where money is electronic the point grows starker still, for "there need be no physical reality to the $1,000, other than the representation" (Searle, 2008b: 457). The monetary fact is not in the paper, and where there is no paper there is no less money.

Brey, however, does not notice that the reason technological objects such as screwdrivers and chairs can only be simulated, and not *reproduced*, in a digital environment may not lie — as he supposes — in the functional limitations of that environment. Given that artificiality is the exosomization of our organs and memories to perform certain functions in fulfilment of our needs, those objects are not reproducible because they are *redundant* — simply *use-less*, the conditions for their specific modes of functioning being absent in such systems. A screwdriver is use-less because "tightening up something" in a digital environment calls for an algorithm rather than a physical object that screws; a chair is use-less because there is neither gravity nor the soreness of our legs that craves a physical object to rest upon. Simulating a screwdriver or a chair may then be only a matter of attending to perceptual inertia, or one day to nostalgia — much as people once used scissors and glue to cut and paste two-dimensional objects from one place to another, where now we simply cut and paste in our devices, with no need to simulate scissors and glue at all; later generations may not even know what scissors and glue were, once every act of cutting and pasting, art-making included, has been digitised.

The use-lessness of so many material objects in digital environments exemplifies another key feature of artificial environments. While artificial environments are dependent on, and constrained by, the materiality and laws of the universe, they are not reducible to them: in Michael Polanyi's terms, they impose *boundary conditions* that the lower-level laws of physics leave open but do not determine, and so — being irreducible to those laws — they have *laws of their own* (Polanyi, 1968; 2009). In purely biological terms the physically fittest person has the best odds of surviving in the wilderness; yet in a world — an artificial environment — this may no longer hold. The fittest may face a higher mortality rate, being assigned to hunting, war, or heavy labour, while a physically unfit person turns charisma or wit into concentrated power and resources, and so into survival. Despite every human being subject to the materiality and laws of the universe, the local materiality and laws of artificial environments — the mode of artificiality, or world — that direct their members' adaptation, or domesticate them, are not reducible to the former.

## Extending Searle: brute facts, institutional facts, and the social

Searle's framework lets me say what *kind* of fact a monetary fact is — and also where this thesis must go further than he does. He distinguishes *brute* facts, which obtain independently of human institutions (the mass of a stone), from *institutional* facts, which obtain only within them (the value of a coin); institutional facts are erected by status functions of the form "*X counts as Y in context C*," and they carry deontic powers — rights, duties, entitlements, authorisations — that move people as surely as any physical force (Searle, 1995). I should be careful here about what I am doing with Searle. Searle himself never uses the word "virtuality"; what I undertake is not a quarrel with him but an *extension* of him. I adopt his realism about the observer-relative — that collectively recognised, causally efficacious nonmaterial structures are real, not "as if" — and I adopt his constitutive-rule analytic, "*X counts as Y in context C*." I then draw the line *wider* than he does. Searle gates the rich apparatus of status and deontic power behind language and collective acceptance, which makes institutional reality constitutively human; I draw it at the causally efficacious nonmaterial *as such*, whether or not it is language-gated. On my account every Searlean institutional fact is a virtuality, but not every virtuality need be a Searlean institutional fact.

The warrant for drawing the line more widely is, importantly, already present in Searle's own scheme. He does not place the gorilla's display — discussed above among the proto-virtual cases — outside the realm of collective intentionality; he places it outside the *institutional*, which is a narrower thing. Social facts, in Searle's own terms, require "no cultural apparatus, cultural conventions, or language": his own instance is a pack of hyenas hunting a lion, which "and Congress passing legislation are both cases of social facts," the institutional being merely "a special subclass" of the social (Searle, 1995: 37–38). The gorilla's drumming, then, is a Searlean *social* fact — causally efficacious by meaning rather than by mass, social in Searle's full sense, but not yet woven into a fabric of stated rules — which is exactly why it counts, in the proto-virtual sense set out above, as virtuality below the institutional threshold. The extension falls at the social/institutional boundary, not at the human/animal one.

That the line between the merely social and the properly institutional should fall *exactly* at language — and so exactly at the human — is itself a substantive commitment rather than a neutral observation, and one that animal-cognition research and posthumanist thought increasingly press. Eduardo Viveiros de Castro's Amerindian perspectivism furnishes the sharpest counter-case, and pointedly against Searle by name: in the perspectival ontology, "what we see as 'nature' is seen by other species as 'culture,' i.e., as institutional facts — what we see as blood, a natural substance, is seen by jaguars as manioc beer, an artefact," so that "these institutional facts are here universal, something that is quite foreign to Searle's alternatives" (Viveiros de Castro, 2015: 255). One need not adopt perspectivism wholesale to take its methodological point: the confinement of the institutional, and of deontic standing, to the linguistic human is a contestable boundary — and the proto-virtual, in the bracketing sense set out above, marks the place where the question is forced rather than where it is settled. Virtuality, in short, is not what computation added to the world; it is something the world has run on since before there were humans to formalise it — which is only what one should expect once the tool is granted to be "the core of humanization" (Obara & Hani, 1995: 29), the made and the human having grown up together.

How this notion of virtuality is grounded ontologically — at the *real* stratum of the stratified picture of the real, the actual, and the empirical introduced in Chapter I §E — is the work of the typology that follows, where the irreducibility just established is shown to be not uniform but graded.
