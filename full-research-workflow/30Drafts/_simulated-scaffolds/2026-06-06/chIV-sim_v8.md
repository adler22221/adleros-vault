# Chapter IV — The Inward Drive of the Enlightenment Mode

> **Scaffold note (read first).** This is an AI-simulated, voice-matched read-through — a clean version of the argument, **not** thesis prose and not to be copy-pasted. Working apparatus is at the foot; each major section closes with a **▣ Sources engaged** block for audit. CJK terms are glossed at first use. `[VERIFY]` marks a claim to confirm; `[TRANSLATION-PENDING]` a rendering not yet author-locked (see [[ch4-translation-decisions-2026-06-06]]).
>
> **The mode, named.** The mode whose inward drive this chapter follows is the **Enlightenment mode of artificiality**, defined in Chapter III. "Enlightenment" is used there, and here, in Adorno and Horkheimer's "widest sense as the advance of thought" — the *metaphysical revolution* that aims at "liberating human beings from fear and installing them as masters" of a disenchanted world (Horkheimer and Adorno, Jephcott trans. [1947] 2002: 1) — **not** the eighteenth-century period. The mode is *characterized by its universalizing of controllability*: "controllability" in Hartmut Rosa's precise sense (the drive to render the world *verfügbar*). In running text I call it **the Enlightenment mode**, or simply **the mode**.
>
> **Where the chapter sits.** Chapter III examined how the mode *expands* — how, as one actual world among many, it overwrites the organizing conditions of the worlds it meets and carries its own made world as though it were the given universe. This chapter turns *inward*: how the same mode drives the internal dynamics of the world it has made — what it does to its members, their others, and their bodies. The argument proceeds dialectically, through two diagnoses of the *same* mode in opposite valences: Adorno and Horkheimer, who see only its darkness, and Takahide Uegaki, who lets us see its brightness without illusion. Habermas is the hinge. The chapter's claim is that neither valence is the truth of the mode; both are *actual-empirical manifestations* of one *real* mechanism — universalizing controllability — whose final form is the metaverse, and whose terminus is the squeeze.

## §IV.1 — A view from its darkness: Adorno and Horkheimer

The most unsparing account of what the mode does to its own world is the oldest of the three this chapter uses — and it is no accident that it comes from the thinkers who first defined "enlightenment" as a metaphysical revolution rather than a period. In *Dialectic of Enlightenment*, Horkheimer and Adorno give the inward drive a single name: **self-preservation** (*Selbsterhaltung*). For them this is not one motive among others but the constitutive principle of reason as such. "Self-preservation is the constitutive principle of science, the soul of the table of categories" (DoE, p.68); reason is "the organ of calculation, of planning" whose "principles are those of self-preservation" (p.65). They locate the maxim at the head of the whole tradition, in Spinoza's *conatus*: the "endeavour of preserving oneself is the first and only basis of virtue," which they call "the true maxim of all Western civilization" (p.22).

From this principle the dark account unfolds in three movements.

First, **the man/nature divide makes reason instrumental.** What human beings "seek to learn from nature is how to use it to dominate wholly both it and human beings. Nothing else counts" (p.2). Once nature is disenchanted into a field of mere objects — "industrialism makes souls into things" (p.21) — reason is reduced to the calculation of means for mastering it. The price of dominating outer nature is the domination of inner nature: civilization becomes "the history of the introversion of sacrifice — in other words, the history of renunciation" (p.43), the bourgeois self mutilating its own impulses to survive (the bound Odysseus).

Second, **instrumental reason totalizes and congeals into a second nature.** The drive to render everything calculable admits no remainder — "enlightenment is totalitarian as only a system can be" (p.4) — because "nothing is allowed to remain outside, since the mere idea of the 'outside' is the real source of fear" (p.11). What the made order produces is a *zweite Natur* (second nature): the historically-produced confronts its makers as a fate-like, natural-seeming totality. Adorno's *Naturgeschichte* (natural-history) names exactly this — the moment at which "nothing [is left] outside any more, nothing unaffected by mediation, which is total," and "nature turns into an irresistible parable of imprisonment" (*Negative Dialectics*, pp.354–361). The made becomes the given; enlightenment "reverts to mythology" (DoE, p.xv). `[Scaffold: the dark-valence statement of the thesis's own tekhnē→phusis / second-nature claim; cross-ref §IV.4 and Ch V's 自足完成化.]`

Third, **the second nature escapes its makers and turns against the self it was to preserve.** Self-preservation, absolutized, "proved to be a destructive natural force no longer distinguishable from self-destruction. The two principles combined in a murky fusion" (p.71). The reified totality, having "made the control of internal and external nature the absolute purpose of life" (p.24), destroys the very subject in whose service it was undertaken.

### The genuine limitation of the dark view

The power of this account is also its defect. To make self-preservation the *whole* of enlightenment reason, A&H must read the entire canon — not only the empiricists and political realists (Hobbes's terror of violent death; Locke's preservation-of-property), not only the scientific program (Bacon's "knowledge is power"), but the rationalists and even the idealist zenith — as variations on one engine. Spinoza's *conatus*, Descartes' reduction of nature to *res extensa* to be calculated and mastered, and even Kant's categorical imperative (the autonomous subject ruthlessly suppressing its inner nature) are, for them, the same self-preservation in different dress. `[VERIFY: the Kant reading — DoE Excursus II "Juliette" + Adorno, Problems of Moral Philosophy — and a Descartes res cogitans/res extensa locus; T0 pending. The whole-canon scope is the author's NLM-confirmed point; Habermas names it from outside, §IV.2.]`

This is a *monism*, and its cost is precisely what the thesis cannot give up. By essentializing enlightenment and reason to their dark, **instrumental** side, A&H cannot account for the flourishing the enlightenment has *genuinely* pursued and, in part, *genuinely* brought. If reason is nothing but domination, then the freedoms, equalities, and forms of autonomy and conviviality that modern people actually enjoy must be illusion — and so must the critique that says so. The dark view sees the second nature truly; it cannot see the brightness that the second nature also, and in the same motion, delivers.

## §IV.2 — The hinge: Habermas

Habermas gives the limitation of §IV.1 its name, and supplies the thread that runs from A&H's darkness into Uegaki's brightness. A&H, he argues, "see themselves forced … to expand instrumental reason into a category of the world-historical process of civilization as a whole, that is, to project the process of reification back behind the capitalist beginnings of the modern age into the very beginnings of hominization" — and "with this … the contours of the concept of reason are in danger of becoming blurred" (*TCA* 1:366). The whole of history is thereby reduced to "a reason that is instrumentalized for the purpose of self-preservation, which is posed as an absolute end" (*TCA* 1:380). This is the monism named from outside: Habermas confirms that A&H's self-preservation runs through the *entire* canon — exactly the whole-canon reading §IV.1 attributed to them. And the totalization is self-undermining: A&H, "no longer wanting to overcome theoretically the performative contradiction inherent in an ideology critique that outstrips itself" (*PDM* 127), denounce "the Enlightenment's becoming totalitarian with its own tools," and remain — as Adorno "was quite aware" — inside "this performative contradiction inherent in totalized critique" (*PDM* 119).

Habermas's rescue is the hinge into the brightness. The trouble with *Dialectic of Enlightenment*, he holds, is that it "does not direct our thought to the path that is nearest at hand, a path which leads through the inner logics of the different complexes of rationality" (*TCA* 1:382) — and so misses the dimension of reason that is not domination at all: **communicative rationality**, oriented to mutual understanding rather than to mastery. The genuine value the dark view cannot see is real, and it has its own logic.

But Habermas also supplies the dark thread's continuation into the present, and this is what makes him the bridge to Uegaki specifically. His diagnosis of modernity is the **colonization of the lifeworld** (*Kolonialisierung der Lebenswelt*): "the rationalization of the lifeworld makes possible a heightening of systemic complexity, which becomes so hypertrophied that it unleashes system imperatives that burst the capacity of the lifeworld they instrumentalize" — "the irresistible irony of the world-historical process of enlightenment" (*TCA* 2:155). The system — money and power — uncouples from, then invades, the communicatively-structured lifeworld, until "in place of 'false consciousness' we today have a 'fragmented consciousness' that blocks enlightenment by the mechanism of reification. It is only with this that the conditions for a colonization of the lifeworld are met" (*TCA* 2:355). Habermas thus holds both halves at once: the genuine value (communicative reason) *and* its strangulation by the system.

This is not a bridge the thesis imposes. Uegaki builds his own central term on Habermas's: he derives the **autonomization** (自立化) of the social apparatus *from* the lifeworld directly from Habermas's colonization-of-the-lifeworld, naming the *Theory of Communicative Action* and the "Kolonialisierung der Lebenswelt" (Uegaki, 2022a, ch.6, n.⑼). So the hinge runs in his text, not only in ours: Habermas **rescues the genuine value from A&H's reduction**, and Uegaki **takes the colonization to its terminus** — the self-finishing society — but re-reads that very uncoupling as the user's *liberation*, not only as pathology.

## §IV.3 — A view from its brightness: Uegaki

### §IV.3.0 — Orientation: who Uegaki is, and his claim in one breath

Before the argument grows complicated, let me put it simply, because the rest of this section is dense and a reader meeting Uegaki for the first time deserves the plain version first.

Takahide Uegaki (上柿崇英) is a Japanese philosopher whose two-volume study of postwar Japanese society, 〈自己完結社会〉の成立 (*The Formation of the Self-Finishing Society*, 2022), supplies this section its spine. His diagnosis can be stated in a single sentence and then unfolded: **the mode of artificiality dominant since the Enlightenment rests on one conviction — that life ought to be as one *intends* it — and the pursuit of that conviction, carried across generations within an ever-more-capable social apparatus, issues in what he calls the *self-finishing* of human life.** Three terms in that sentence carry the weight; once they are glossed the whole argument is in view.

The first is **the as-intended life** (意のままになる生) — and here a word of caution, because the translation does real work. "Intended" must be heard in the full reach of the Sinic 意: the mind-heart inclusive of cognition, affect, and conation at once — will, wish, reason, emotion, motive, attention, intention, meaning — what Kant gathered under *Gemüt* and what Brentano named, as the mark of the mental, *intentionality*: the directedness common to every mental act. It is **not** the narrower English "will." (Brentano, *Psychology from an Empirical Standpoint*, p.88: every mental phenomenon is characterised by "the intentional … inexistence of an object," a "reference to a content, direction toward an object"; his three classes — presentation, judgement, and the love–hate phenomena of feeling and will — *all* share that directedness, which is why intentionality, not "will," anchors the full 意. Kant's *Gemüt* and its three faculties — cognition, the feeling of pleasure and displeasure, desire — supply the inclusive "mind-heart": *Critique of the Power of Judgment*, Ak. 5:198.) Uegaki's claim is that the world the Enlightenment mode has made treats *the as-intended life* as the human's proper form, and figures its opposite — **the anti-intended** (意のままにならない): the body that ails, the other who refuses, the world that will not conform — as a *wrong* to be overcome. He gives this conviction its sharpest name, the worldview-and-view-of-the-human (世界観＝人間観) of **the limitless life** (〈無限の生〉). "Limitless" is precise and easily mistaken: not eternal life or immortality, but "the limitless *actualisation* of an imagined ideal upon reality" (glossary 16-02) — the conviction that reality can be remade without end until it answers to the intending self. (Its opposite, **the finite life** (有限の生), is the affirmation of the constitutive limits: the body, the other, mortality.) `[Renderings 意のままになる=as-intended / 無限の生=limitless life: see [[ch4-translation-decisions-2026-06-06]] — "as-intended/anti-intended" author-locked, Brentano/Kant warrant now confirmed; "limitless life" dossier-grounded on Condorcet's *indefinite*.]`

The second is **the social apparatus** (社会的装置), the term most easily misread. The apparatus is what one *entrusts* (委ねる) one's living to, and what it does is *alleviate* the burdens that relationship and common life used to lay on persons. Its work is felt as **relief**, as liberation — the *opposite* of domination. Whatever the apparatus turns out to cost, the experience it delivers is freedom, and the thesis will not let A&H's vocabulary of "domination" be imported onto it. `[Anchor: [[thesis_uegaki_apparatus_entrustment_not_domination]].]`

The third is **self-finishing** (自己完結化) — a life closed into itself, complete-in-itself, needing no other; the loop of living closed without other people. (Gloss once and hold the slight irony: "finishing" carries both *completion* and, faintly, *finishing-off* — and Uegaki's argument is that this completion is, in the end, self-undoing.)

Put the three together and the brightness is plain: through an apparatus that absorbs the anti-intended on our behalf, people come to hold the great modern values — freedom, equality, autonomy, conviviality — **as users** (〈ユーザー〉) of that apparatus. And these, against A&H, are *not* illusions. Set beside the lives of people a century ago, the freedoms of the user are real. The chapter's task is to show how this genuine brightness and A&H's true darkness are two readings of one mechanism. The rest of §IV.3 reconstructs Uegaki's account — non-linear in his own text — in the order the argument needs.

### §IV.3.1 — The political problematic, ontologically grounded

Uegaki lets us see what A&H cannot: that the mode's values are *genuinely pursued and genuinely realised*. To see how, we must reconstruct the lineage he addresses — a specific, structurally **soteriological** thread (in Bhaskar's terms, the *Platonic* pole — on which more below), whose genealogy he traces from the ancient Greek elevation of spirit over earthly life through Renaissance Christianity's belief in a perfect human created by God. What unites this thread is not a shared doctrine but a shared *form*: the great modern value-ideals — 自由 (freedom), 平等 (equality), 自律 (autonomy), 共生 (conviviality), all matters of **value rationality** rather than instrumental rationality — are grounded **ontologically**, despite the thinkers' fierce disagreements about their content.

The structure is, term for term, a secularised salvation-history (see [[uegaki-soteriological-reading-2026-06-05]]):

- **存在論的自由 (ontological freedom) ≈ the *imago Dei* and the covenant.** The Enlightenment pursuits are held to be inherent in the human as an *endowed nature* — the human as it ought to be (本来の人間) with its true endowed nature (約束された本来性), the teleological あるべき人間 — whether the source is named God, Reason, or Nature. This is the structure of what the German Enlightenment called the *Bestimmung des Menschen*, the vocation or destiny of the human: a nature implanted at the beginning yet realised only in the future through freedom. Uegaki grounds it in his own words: the conviction that a 本来の人間 exists "probably derives from Renaissance Christianity," from "the belief that a special creature made in God's image cannot but have a special plan and purpose given to it by God" (glossary 16-05/16-07). `[本来の人間=the human as it ought to be / 約束された本来性=the human's true endowed nature: dossier-grounded on Bestimmung/imago Dei.]`
- **存在論的抑圧 (ontological oppression) ≈ the Fall.** The present is read as alienation or corruption of the human, or as the brutality and chaos of nature — attributed differently by each thinker (society's chains in Rousseau, estrangement in Marx, self-incurred immaturity in Kant).
- **解放 (emancipation) ≈ redemption.** Remove the oppression, and the human as it ought to be, with its true endowed nature, can actualise.

This narrative does not end with secularisation; it continues to the present — and, crucially, **A&H themselves belong to this lineage**, which is why their critique reads as enlightenment turning on itself.

### §IV.3.2 — The slippage from a political into an ontological programme

The problems these thinkers set out to solve were largely *political* — the critique of despotic power, the claim to rights against unjust subjection. The decisive move is that, by grounding the political programme **onto-teleologically**, they converted it into something else. Uegaki's term for the result is 「現実を否定する理想」, the **reality-negating ideal**: an ideal that "begins from an imagined ideal and seeks to overcome the very reality that does not fit it" (glossary 16-11), as against the 「現実に寄り添う理想」 (the reality-accompanying ideal).

It is worth pausing on **human rights**, because the slippage is most visible in them, and because they show where the political programme and its ontological extension *overlap* and *diverge*. Human rights and 存在論的自由 (ontological freedom) overlap at the political stage: the claim that no person should be subordinated by arbitrary power, that persons are owed a standing they cannot be stripped of — this is the legitimate, bounded, *political* vehicle, which the thesis affirms without reserve. They diverge at the point of grounding. Human rights as a *political-institutional* settlement can rest on agreement, on the hard-won refusal of cruelty, on a determinate list of protections against determinate wrongs. But once the *same* claim is read **onto-teleologically** — once the rights one holds are taken to flow from a true endowed nature, an inviolable essence answerable to nothing it did not choose — the bounded political claim becomes an unbounded ontological one. The right *against unjust subjection* slides into a right *against the given as such*. That slide is the slippage, and the rest of this section follows where it leads.

Read through the thesis, the slippage is an instance of what Bhaskar calls the **ontic fallacy** — which the thesis aligns with the *Platonic / idealist* pole. (The Platonic/Aristotelian labels are *this thesis's* gloss on Bhaskar's pair, not Bhaskar's own wording — see the note below.) Bhaskar's ontic fallacy is "the ideology of the compulsive determination of knowledge by being — for instance, in the guise of *reified facts or hypostatized ideas*" (*Dialectic*, p.4). Here the hypostatised idea is *the anthropic ideal itself*: the projection of "what the human really is" (本来の人間) is taken *as* the real, and the non-ideal present — "the Fall" — is condemned in its light, with no consideration given to the emergence of that non-ideal across a stratified ontology. `[Scaffold: ontic fallacy = Platonic/idealist pole; epistemic fallacy = Aristotelian/empiricist (§IV.3.5). The mapping is the thesis's gloss; the legitimate Bhaskar hooks (hypostatized ideas; the dissolution of the world; the fallacies' complicity; the Parmenides/Plato lineage) are in [[chIV-bhaskar-ontic-epistemic-fallacy-T0-2026-06-06]]. Both fallacies foreclose the intransitive real → the squeeze, §IV.4.]`

It is this slippage that licenses Uegaki's central narrative: "the story of individuals being liberated from 'ontological oppression' and acquiring the 'ontological freedom' that ought to be theirs — that is, the very story of the limitless life (〈無限の生〉) = the **as-intended life** (意のままになる生)" (glossary 16-09).

### §IV.3.3 — The problem of others

The as-intended life founders on a permanent fact: others are, finally, **anti-intended** (意のままにならない) — never finally as one intends them. The soteriologists' solution is an axiological **universalisation**: the freedom, equality, autonomy, and conviviality of *one* are made to entail those of *all* — a solidary liberation from man-made oppressions so that the endowed good may revive in everyone. This is the generous and genuine heart of the rights tradition, and it is real.

But this *Aufhebung* is, by itself, naïve, because it leaves the anti-intended unaddressed. *Who* is to carry the anti-intended burdens? What if one person's as-intended life is interfered with by the anti-intended other? And here the human-rights slippage reaches its sharp form: what if the *right not to bear* the anti-intended burdens comes to be counted as an inalienable part of the irreplaceable self (かけがえのないこの私 / ありのままの私, the me just as I am) endowed with 存在論的自由? At that point a right that began as protection-against-subjection has become a right against the *other's very recalcitrance*. As the political programme turns onto-teleological, the anti-intended condition of others is read as 存在論的抑圧 to be lifted — Uegaki states it explicitly: "ultimately the very 〈concatenation of existence〉 becomes an evil oppression, and the 'anti-intended other' (意のままにならない他者) itself comes to be regarded as … a high-risk, high-burden being that oppresses the irreplaceable self's spontaneity and self-determination" (glossary 16-09).

### §IV.3.4 — The problem of the body

The same logic reaches the body. Since the ancient Greeks the soul, spirit, reason, or mind has been held superior, divine, essential; the body inferior, mortal, contingent — a piece of baggage. As the programme turns onto-teleological, the anti-intended condition of one's *own* body is likewise read as 存在論的抑圧, the **anti-intended body** (意のままにならない身体) interfering with the self-actualisation of the human as it ought to be. Its limit-form is the 思念体 — a self contracted to a bodiless thinking substance, structurally Descartes' *res cogitans*, "having lost even the body" (16-04/06): the secular-Gnostic terminus at which "salvation" means liberation *from* the body. `[TRANSLATION-PENDING: 思念体 rendered descriptively — author rejected "thought-entity"; the res cogitans identification is the thesis's. The body-chain is entailed by Uegaki's 脱身体化 (disembodiment) / 時空間的自立性 logic, not stated as a syllogism.]`

### §IV.3.5 — Confluence with instrumental reason

Here the two engines meet, and here is the chapter's spine. As Needham, Pomeranz, and Uegaki each show in their own register, the toll of the anti-intended has historically been **externalised** — offloaded onto those held *not* to possess the true endowed nature: animals, slaves, women, colonies. And 存在論的自由 has been, genuinely, the major pillar that has sought to *correct* these externalisations — the liberation of women, of the enslaved, of colonised peoples, and now, increasingly, of animals. This corrective work is, again, where human rights do their legitimate labour, at the political stage.

But — the decisive point — the universal emancipation the value-engine promises is *self-contradictory* so long as the anti-intended burden must be borne by *someone*. It is **only through the maturation of the apparatuses of instrumental reason** that the liberation of the previously-offloaded-to can, *in practice*, occur *simultaneously* with their own 存在論的自由 — because only then can the burden be offloaded onto **non-persons (artificiality)** rather than onto other persons. Without that maturity, 政治的自由 (political freedom) inevitably arrives bound to 存在論的不自由＝抑圧, since someone must still live as a 担い手としての〈生〉, a *bearer* of life who cannot offload his own anti-intended conditions onto other politically-free beings. By the same token, it is only through instrumental reason's maturity that the **as-intended body** (意のままになる身体) becomes increasingly possible at all; absent it, longevity and health have meant attuning oneself — one's very 意 — to harmonise with the universe, not the reverse.

So the soteriological value-engine and the empiricist/instrumental engine are not rivals. The thesis aligns the value-engine with Bhaskar's *Platonic* pole and the **ontic** fallacy (§IV.3.2), and the instrumental engine with the *Aristotelian* pole and the **epistemic** fallacy — Bhaskar's "view that statements about being can be reduced to or analysed in terms of statements about knowledge" (*A Realist Theory of Science*, p.36), which "results in the systematic dissolution of the idea of a world … independent of but investigated by science" (RTS p.26). The two engines are two genuine expressions of one root — the human posited as prior to and over the disenchanted given (時空間的自立性, spatio-temporal self-subsistence) — and the value-engine's promise *cannot be kept* without the instrumental engine's apparatus. This is *why* the two converge: not by accident of history, but by the internal logic of the promise. It is also here that the political vehicle (human rights) and the ontological extension (the as-intended life answerable to nothing given) become hard to keep apart in practice — for the apparatus that finally lets rights be *universal* is the same apparatus that lets the anti-intended be, for the first time, offloaded rather than borne.

> **Scaffold note on Bhaskar (the eponym discipline).** The **Platonic↔ontic / Aristotelian↔epistemic** mapping is *this thesis's* interpretive gloss, **not** Bhaskar's wording — Bhaskar nowhere labels the ontic fallacy "Platonic." Present it as *"in the terms of this thesis…"* The verbatim Bhaskar that legitimately anchors it: the epistemic fallacy "results in the systematic dissolution of the idea of a world" (RTS p.26 = the squeeze from the empiricist side); the ontic fallacy = "the ontologisation of knowledge" via "hypostatized ideas" (*Dialectic* p.4 — "hypostatized ideas" is Bhaskar's own, the closest he comes to the Platonic register); the two "characteristically presuppose each other" (*Scientific Realism*, p.16 — warrant for "two engines, one squeeze"); and Bhaskar routes both back through Parmenides/Plato. Both fallacies foreclose the intransitive real. ### §IV.3.6 — The offloading to artificiality, and the Enlightenment values achieved "as users"

What the mature apparatus offers is, precisely, **entrustment** — not domination. Uegaki's 〈社会的装置〉 is what one *entrusts* (委ねる) one's living to, and it *alleviates* the burdens of 関係性 (relationality) and 〈共同〉 (the common). The offloading runs in two directions:

- **Horizontally**, the apparatus takes over portions of *function* (役割), *trust* (信頼), and the loosening-and-forgiveness (許し) that relationship and common life used to demand of persons.
- **Vertically**, it takes over the "realisation of survival" (〈生存〉の実現, livelihood), the "realisation of existence-in-society" (〈現実存在〉の実現, one's standing as a member of a society), and the "realisation of succession" (〈継承〉の実現, the inheritance and transformation of things for the lives to come).

The underlying directionality, in the thesis's own typology, is a migration **from the conventional to the structural cells** of artificiality (Ch II): the burden moves from forms that compel by ongoing human enactment to forms that compel by the structure of the apparatus itself. `[Scaffold: tie to Ch-2-C typology; where Stiegler's exteriorisation/exosomatisation sits if invoked — and the hinge into Ch V's 自足完成化.]`

The result is the genuine, increasing — "universalising" — achievement of the Enlightenment values, but achieved by people *as 〈users〉 hanging from* (ぶら下がっている) the social apparatus, the ever-more-omnipotent artificiality system: 「〈ユーザー〉としての 自由・平等・自律・共生」 (glossary 16-10, 17-04). Uegaki's point, and ours against A&H, is that these are *not* illusion. Set beside the lives of people a hundred years ago, the freedom, equality, autonomy, and conviviality of the 〈user〉 are real — even if not the rose-coloured things the ideals imagined (16-10).

### §IV.3.7 — The completion of the Enlightenment ideal

This is why **self-finishing (自己完結化) is not an aberration of the mode but its immanent completion** — the sole form in which the mode's own demand, the reconciliation of total individual freedom with a working common life, can in fact be met. The values are realised; they are realised *as the apparatus delivers them*; and the apparatus can deliver them universally only by taking the anti-intended onto itself. In the thesis's reading, this is **instrumental rationality guided by value rationality** — the Aristotelian engine made to serve the Platonic end.

The emerging future this projects is the techno-utopian vision in which everyone is equal and free to do or become whatever they want (自己実現) with the aid of artificiality, while everything anti-intended is done *for* us by artificiality. Its entailed consequences are real but, strikingly, *none of them runs against the Enlightenment values*: the self-finishing of life (生の自己完結化) brings the thinning of relationality (関係性の希薄化) and the disembodiment of life (生の脱身体化).

> **Scaffold note on normativity.** Uegaki judges these to issue in 関係性の病理 (relational pathology) and 生の混乱 (the disarray of life). Present these as *Uegaki's normative reading*, contextualised as the actual of his moment — and do NOT adopt the moral verdict as the thesis's own. The thesis's verdict is the *ontological* squeeze (§IV.4), not the moral pathology. `[関係性の病理=relational pathology: author-leaning, not final.]`

> **Scaffold note on the Geist.** The technological *realisation* of the two engines' confluence — **AI as autonomised reason** (the idealist engine) and **big data as autonomised sense-data** (the empiricist engine) converging into a new "Hegelian Geist" (cf. 総合人間学 18) — belongs to **Chapter V** (the metaverse / apparatus realised, 自足完成化), not here. Ch IV establishes the two engines and their convergence *in principle*; their autonomised consummation is Ch V. (This is the IV→V seam: the two engines are the through-line.)

## §IV.4 — Real-world index, and A&H vs Uegaki as two sides of one coin

### §IV.4.1 — The present as index

Uegaki writes from late-modern Japan — and it matters in what sense. Japan is not, here, the story of the mode's *imposition* upon a once-separate world; that narrative belongs to Chapter III's *outward* argument. Here Japan is the **vantage**: the site at which the mature apparatus and its inward dynamics are most fully observable, because the as-intended life is being pursued across the cells of artificiality at once and can be read off the present. `[Scaffold: keep the "five-stage assimilation of Japan" in Ch III; per Han/Rosa, the soft/entrustment face is a matter of phase, not West-vs-Japan geography.]`

What can be read off that present is the steady migration of living *into* the apparatus. A life increasingly lived as a single household; meals, goods, and companionship summoned through delivery, subscription, and matching platforms; care, payment, scheduling, and memory entrusted to systems that ask nothing of a neighbour; the quiet ethic of 迷惑をかけない (not imposing on others) raised almost to a first principle; and, at the far edge, 孤独死 (dying alone and unnoticed) as the shadow the arrangement casts. None of this is decline in the eyes of those who live it — it is *relief*, the anti-intended other no longer required, the loop of living closed without imposition. That is precisely the point: the self-finishing of life shows up in the present not as deprivation but as a more frictionless freedom, which is why the bright reading is not naïve. `[VERIFY: any specific figure (single-person-household share; platform-life penetration) needs a cited source — Japan Census / IPSS — before prose; kept qualitative here.]`

### §IV.4.2 — Two sides of one coin

Adorno-and-Horkheimer and Uegaki are not two competing theories to be adjudicated. Their works are, in the main, *actual* — each a faithful rendering of the actual structures and dynamics of its own moment (the industrial, world-war, modernist moment; the digital, "globalised", postmodernist moment). The charge of essentialism each invites can be understood, in critical-realist terms, as a **conflation of the actual with the real** — an essentialising of the real *by* the actual: A&H take the actual face of domination for the whole, and a reader of Uegaki could take the actual face of liberation for the whole.

The **real** beneath both is one mechanism: **universalising controllability** — in Rosa's precise sense, the drive to render the world *verfügbar* (controllable) along its four dimensions, making it "visible" and so knowable, "reachable" and so accessible, "manageable", and "useful" (Rosa, 2020: 14–17) — legitimated by the supremacy of the subject and made possible by the subject/object — culture-man/nature — rupture. `[VERIFY: "supremacy of the subject" = the subject/object rupture + the self posited prior-to/over the disenchanted given (時空間的自立性), NOT the narrowly transcendental subject — so as to include the empiricists. A&H's "subject" here is the self-preserving instrumental ego over disenchanted object-nature, not Kant's transcendental subject.]` Domination and liberation are *not* properties of this real mechanism; they are its two actual-empirical manifestations — the same offloading-onto-an-autonomised-apparatus read once as a cage (A&H) and once as relief (Uegaki). The discipline of the given does not vanish in the bright reading; it is *outsourced* — borne now by the apparatus rather than by the self, which is exactly why the self experiences as freedom what the system enacts as control.

### §IV.4.3 — The emancipatory paradox: the critique that carries the mode's own premise

There is one more turn of the coin, the most delicate in the chapter, because it must be stated so as not to be mistaken for what it is not. If domination and liberation are two readings of one mechanism, then so, on reflection, is the *critique* that would side with liberation against domination — the emancipatory critical theories that prosecute the drive against oppression can themselves carry, unawares, the very premise that drives the mode. To show this is **not** to attack those theories from outside, and **not** to essentialise or condemn them. It is a question raised *within* the emancipatory project, about a premise it has tended to leave unexamined — and Uegaki, who raises it, is explicit that he does so "while affirming the historical role" these traditions have played (2022b:80, n.90). So do I. The struggles against despotism, against arbitrary power, against the subordination of persons by sex or race or colony, are struggles the thesis honours and shares the goal of; the political emancipation they won is precisely the achievement §IV.3 insisted was real.

The premise has a structure, and Uegaki gives it. The ideal of ontological freedom "demands of us liberation from ontological oppression. But each time we remove one of the chains that binds us, we notice yet a further chain … and there is no end to this," because the ideal "can never accept the human reality that no human being exists who is bound by no chain whatsoever" — an endless negation Uegaki calls the **無間地獄** (the no-respite hell) the limitless life brings about (2022b:118). Note exactly what is and is not claimed. The endlessness is not a flaw in the *fight against oppression*; it is a property of an ideal that has reset its terminus at the removal of *all* hindrance, the given included. An emancipatory project aimed at a determinate injustice — this domination, that exclusion — has a destination it can reach. A project that has, perhaps without noticing, made its destination *a self answerable to nothing given* has set a destination that recedes as it is approached.

Of the discourses Uegaki names — power-theory (権力論), gender-theory (ジェンダー論), postcolonial theory (ポストコロニアル論) — he writes that, while affirming their historical role, he takes a critical view of "the framework they *tacitly presuppose* — that of *limitless* liberation from power and oppression," because "that framework is precisely what embodies the worldview-and-view-of-the-human of the limitless life" (2022b:80, n.90). The word that carries the argument is *tacitly* (暗に): the claim is not that these approaches are wrong to expose domination — they are right, and their exposures are real — but that, in pressing exposure toward *limitlessness*, they have inherited, unthematised, the very as-intended-life premise they would not, on reflection, endorse as their goal. The irony is internal to the project: a critique of the sovereign subject, carried by a demand for limitless freedom, can circle back toward the sovereign subject.

The precise hedge — and everything turns on stating it exactly — is Uegaki's own political-versus-ontological distinction. The thesis does *not* question political-freedom emancipation: not the fight against despotism, not the dismantling of unjust hierarchy, not the demand that persons not be subordinated by what they did not choose. On that it stands with the tradition, as Uegaki "wholly supports the institutional establishment of political freedom" (2022b:190, 193). What it questions is the *extension* of that emancipation into an *ontological* register — the slide, already met in §IV.3.2, from "no person should be subordinated by unjust power" to "the self should be answerable to nothing given at all." The first is the project's achievement; the second is the premise this turn of the coin asks the project to examine. The critique is immanent because it shares the project's commitment to freedom and presses only on the question of freedom's terminus.

This is why the move belongs *here*, with the two sides. A&H's critique indicts the instrumental reason that masters nature; Uegaki's surfaces the unexamined assumption that the destination of emancipation is a self liberated from every constraint of the given. These are not the same target, and a thinker may attack the first while tacitly running the second. The deeper point — the chapter's, not Uegaki's alone — is that the will to liberate from *all* hindrance is *itself* the as-intended-life engine, whatever its object. So a critique can be impeccable against domination and still, in its demand for a self answerable to nothing it did not choose, carry the mode's own premise unawares. The coin has more than two faces painted on it; the hand that would flip it to "liberation" is moved by the same mechanism. `[Scaffold: restored from v5 §F, handled as IMMANENT critique — no theory essentialised or condemned; the hedge is political-vs-ontological, Uegaki's own.]`

### §IV.4.4 — The metaverse, where the two sides are revealed as one — and the squeeze

The metaverse is the point at which the coin can be seen whole. There, universalising controllability does not *necessarily* dominate or liberate — those are the actual-empirical readings — but it does something more fundamental: it forms a **real, new background environment**, a second nature (A&H) and an autonomised 社会的装置 (Uegaki) at once, parallel to the universe rather than nested within the world. (This is the threshold of Chapter V's 自足完成化 — the apparatus's self-completion — and the chapter hands off to it here.)

And here the dialectic recoils. The controllability that realises the values is the same second nature that hollows them (A&H's *Eclipse of Reason*: value-reason swallowed by instrumental reason); the entrustment that delivers freedom is the same uncoupling that pathologises relation (Habermas's colonisation; Uegaki's 無間地獄). But the thesis's verdict is neither A&H's domination nor Uegaki's pathology. It is **ontological**: as long as we are human, "there always remains an anti-intended" (意のままにならない) — Uegaki's own concession (2022b:120) — the intransitive, stratified **real** that no controllability can subsume. This is the figure Rosa names in his own authorized vocabulary: the controllability drive "produces, behind our backs, a world that in the end is utterly uncontrollable"; "modernity's program of making the world controllable threatens to produce a new, radical form of uncontrollability, one that is categorically different from and worse than the original" (Rosa, 2020: ~115, "The Monstrous Return of the Uncontrollable"). The anti-intended *is* Rosa's *Unverfügbarkeit* (uncontrollability); the squeeze is its monstrous return. And the return is not contingent but structural: a world made fully controllable would be, in Rosa's words, a "dead world" — total controllability is self-defeating, which is why a liveable relation to the given must remain only *semi-controllable* (Rosa, 2020: 44, Thesis 2). The Enlightenment mode, with its universalising controllability perfected in the metaverse, collides with the very given it declared independence from. That collision — the universe reasserting against the metaverse, between which the human is caught — is **the squeeze**, and it is where Chapter IV hands off to Chapters V and VI. `[Rosa anchor confirmed: [[ch4-rosa-verfugbarkeit-T0-2026-06-06]]; folio ~115 [VERIFY-PAGE] (OCR-garbled in the source). Ch VI develops the squeeze on the locked F1–F5.]`
