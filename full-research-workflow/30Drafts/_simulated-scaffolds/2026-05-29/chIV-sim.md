# Chapter IV — The Pursuit of Life-as-Willed and the Self-Finishing Tendency of Human Life

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD — friction-reducer for organizing existing scaffolds + corrections. Author rewrites in own voice before promotion.]

## §IV.0 — Where the Chapter Sits

Chapter III argued that the Modern mode of artificiality has, over the past five centuries, exhibited a structural disposition to universalize itself and to overwrite the organizing principles of other modes with which it has come into contact. The disposition rests on a material basis (the production-mode shifts Needham and Pomeranz have traced), an epistemic apparatus (the Platonic-Aristotelian fault line Bhaskar's analysis names), and a real-level imposition mechanism (the critical-realist account of colonization developed at §III.4). Through these mechanisms, the Modern mode has largely succeeded in establishing itself as the dominant mode of artificiality at planetary scale. Where is it heading next?

The present chapter answers that question at the level of the human inhabitant of the Modern mode. The next chapter answers it at the level of the artificial apparatus the inhabitants have built. Chapter VI brings the two answers together to specify the structural condition the thesis calls the squeeze.

The chapter's argument runs as follows. The Modern mode's underlying axiological commitment, identified by the Japanese philosopher Takahide Uegaki, is the pursuit of *life-as-willed* (意のままになる生): the normative ideal that life ought to conform to the will of the individual who lives it. This commitment is in tension with the fact that no life is, in fact, lived entirely as willed: other people are not as one wills them; one's own body is not as one wills it; the cosmos in which one lives is not subject to one's will. The Modern mode mobilizes its artificial apparatus to mitigate this tension — to mediate relations with others, to extend or substitute for the body, to render the cosmos technically tractable. As the apparatus succeeds, however, two transformations occur in parallel. The first, examined in this chapter, is the *self-finishing* tendency of human life: the progressive transfer of responsibility for survival, relationality, succession, and self-realization from the lived person to the apparatus that supports them. The second, examined in Chapter V, is the *self-completion* tendency of the apparatus: the progressive achievement, by the apparatus, of operational independence from the humans whose tasks it has absorbed. The two are dialectically coupled: self-finishing on the human side enables self-completion on the apparatus side; self-completion on the apparatus side enables further self-finishing on the human side. The squeeze hypothesis names what happens when this coupling reaches a tipping point that the inhabitants of the Modern world increasingly seem to be approaching.

A note on causal efficacy before the substantive sections begin. The squeeze is not a condition that obtains only when the apparatus has *fully* completed itself; it is a condition that obtains *now*, in a graded sense, while the apparatus's self-completion is still ongoing. The structural mechanisms the thesis identifies are *causally efficacious* in advance of their full empirical actualization — to use Bhaskar's vocabulary, a real-level mechanism can operate even when its actual-level effects are only partial. The viva committee asked, sensibly, whether the squeeze could be a meaningful concept before its supposed conditions had fully obtained. The answer is yes: the squeeze is the condition under which inhabitants of a world experience adaptational pressure from two incommensurable background environments — the universe and the increasingly self-completing apparatus — and that condition is observable now, in graded form, in the psychosomatic, livelihood, and governance-symptoms Chapter VI will examine. The thesis's account does not require that the apparatus be complete; it requires that the apparatus be on a trajectory whose causal efficacy is already operative. ## §IV.1 — Ontological Freedom and the Demand for Life-as-Willed

Japanese philosopher Takahide Uegaki (上柿崇英, 2022a; 2022b) identifies a common underlying drive of the Modern mode of artificiality that is often taken for granted today: be it the pursuit of freedom and equality or the strive for liberation, they all manifest a common view that we ought to live a *life-as-willed* (意のままになる生). According to Uegaki's analysis, the mainstream of Modern thought, especially since the Enlightenment, presumes an *ontological freedom* (存在論的自由) in human nature. Following this premise, it is always axiologically good — whether in deontological, utilitarian, or virtue-theoretical terms — to liberate humans from any external hindrances to realizing their true but yet-to-be-realized free nature. The stance is encapsulated in Rousseau's famous declaration, "Man is born free and everywhere he is in chains."

However, this is in immediate conflict with the reality that no life is lived entirely as willed (意のままにならない生). Not only do others not exist, become, live, or interact in the way we will (意のままにならない他者); neither do our bodies exist, function, grow, or become as we will (意のままにならない身体); and neither does the cosmos comply with our will. This conflict renders reality something inherently wrong and to be corrected — what Uegaki names an *ontological oppression* (存在論的抑圧) that must be overcome.

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

Reading Uegaki in the context of this thesis, here is where artificiality comes into play. Technology and virtuality of the Modern world are developed to serve as interfaces to reduce mutual interference among people, mitigating the hindrance one's realization of free nature would otherwise encounter in the existence of not-as-willed others. They are also developed to allow one's body to become increasingly as-willed — to extend its capacities, to mute its discomforts, to defer its decay. And they are developed to render the cosmos technically tractable: weather prediction, agricultural standardization, disease management, infrastructure-mediated buffering against environmental volatility. As a result, the *realization of survival* (〈生存〉の実現, loosely "livelihood"), the *realization of existence-in-reality* (〈現実存在〉の実現, one's existence as a member of a society or culture), and the *realization of succession* (〈継承〉の実現, the inheritance, reproduction, and transformation of things for the lives to come) become increasingly entrusted to technology and virtuality.

This entrustment, in Uegaki's analysis, eventually produces a totalization of the *social apparatus* (社会的装置), which consists of the market economy, bureaucratic institutions, and the infosphere at its core — all of which are highly developed forms of virtuality from the perspective of this thesis. Within the totalized apparatus, people increasingly achieve greater freedom and equality as *users* of the apparatus. The freedom is real; the equality is real; the apparatus is real. What is also real, and what the rest of the chapter develops, is the structural cost of the arrangement that delivers the freedom: the self-finishing tendency.

## §IV.2 — Uegaki's Five Periods: A Historical Trajectory

Uegaki's analysis of self-finishing is anchored in a historical reading of Japanese modernity that the thesis takes as a representative case for the trajectory of the Modern mode of artificiality at the level of inhabitant-experience. The five-period periodization names a sequence of stages through which the inhabitant of the Modern apparatus moves, and which together describe the path from the modern subject's emergence to what Uegaki calls the *self-contained society* (自己完結社会) and its predicted ethical horizon: the *Ethics of Non-Intervention* (不介入の倫理). [VERIFY: Uegaki 2022a, 2022b — locate the explicit five-period structure in the published volumes.]

*First period*: the confrontation with the West. The early modern Japanese encounter with Modern artificiality forces a reorientation of inherited modes — the import of mechanistic technoscience, the disruption of inherited livelihoods, the radical reconfiguration of legal, educational, and economic institutions. The period closes with a partial settlement: the inherited mode of artificiality has been partly displaced, partly reorganized; the new Modern apparatus is installed but its long-term inhabitant-effects are not yet visible.

*Second period*: reconstruction. The mid-twentieth-century reconstruction following the wars consolidates the Modern apparatus on a mass scale. The high-growth economy, the corporate-employment institution, the nuclear-family household, the credentialing-and-bureaucratic state — each of these settles into a configuration whose internal logic the third period will exhaust. Uegaki's analysis treats this period as the *concretion* of the Modern apparatus at scale, not its critical examination.

*Third period*: the consumerist nuclear family and the *hollowing-out of the lifeworld* (生活世界の空洞化). The apparatus that the second period consolidated begins to absorb domains of life that had previously been managed through inherited practices: childrearing, care for the elderly, sociality, meaning-making. The nuclear-family form, materially supported by the consumer economy and ideologically backed by the post-war achievement-narrative, succeeds at delivering material comfort while emptying the lifeworld of the substantive practices that had given inherited modes their texture. The hollowing-out is not experienced as such by its inhabitants; it is experienced as comfort, as opportunity, as the disappearance of certain kinds of burden. The structural correlate of the comfort, however, is the third period's signature: the lifeworld is now thin.

*Fourth period*: *recognition-anxiety* (承認不安) and the figure of the *Drifter* (漂流人). The thin lifeworld, lacking the texture that inherited modes had provided, produces a generalized recognition-anxiety: inhabitants need recognition from the apparatus (and from one another, mediated by the apparatus) to confirm their existence-in-reality. The figure of the Drifter names the contemporary subject who, lacking the substantive lifeworld of inherited modes, drifts through the apparatus-mediated alternatives — employment, consumption, identity-formation, relational arrangements — without stable anchorage. The Drifter is not pathological in the inhabitant's own experience; the Drifter is what it feels like to be the inhabitant of a self-finishing world at this stage of its trajectory.

*Fifth period*: the *self-contained society* (自己完結社会) and the *Ethics of Non-Intervention* (不介入の倫理). The apparatus, now substantially developed at every domain of life, makes possible an existence in which the inhabitant's primary relational mode is non-intervention: do not impose on others; do not be imposed on by others; let the apparatus mediate. The ethical horizon of the self-contained society is the ethics of non-interference between individuals whose relations with one another are now apparatus-mediated. Uegaki's analysis suggests, though does not insist, that the fifth period is the form the Modern mode's trajectory is approaching in its current decades. The thesis takes this approach as the empirical-historical anchor for the squeeze hypothesis: it is in the self-contained society that the apparatus's self-completion and the human side's self-finishing approach the tipping-point Chapter VI examines.

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

The five-period reading is, importantly, *not* a strictly chronological account that can be checked against calendars: different societies move through the stages at different rates, with different ordering, under different external pressures. What Uegaki's account provides is an *ideal-typical* trajectory in Max Weber's sense — a structural sequence that helps make legible the actual-level variations across particular societies. The Japanese case is the closest historical instantiation Uegaki examines in detail; the Anglo-American, Continental European, East Asian, and Latin American cases each instantiate the sequence with variations the thesis does not undertake to map exhaustively. What is load-bearing is the structural sequence: confrontation → reconstruction → hollowing → recognition-anxiety → self-containment. Whether a society has reached the fifth period or remains in the fourth or third is an empirical question for that society; that the trajectory exists, and that the Modern mode's inhabitants are somewhere along it, is the thesis's claim.

## §IV.3 — Self-Finishing: The Mechanism, the Paradox, the Pathology

Based on the analysis above, Uegaki outlines the *self-finishing* (自己完結化) tendency of human life in the emerging future: by utilizing the increasingly omnipotent social apparatus, one can alleviate the burdens of the not-as-willed others. The need to deal with others can be outsourced to artificiality without non-interfaced direct interaction and the painstaking maintenance of difficult relationships. This includes utilizing the apparatus to unconditionally affirm the ontological freedom as expressed in the way one is — the *true self* (ありのままの私) — by ensuring non-interference (不介入) from others. As a result, despite the ideal of *independent individuals* (自立した個人) becoming increasingly realized, this comes at the cost of a *dilution of relationality* (関係性の希薄化).

The paradox the dilution produces is precise. Without sufficient relational experience and skills, people become, paradoxically, *more* sensitive to the not-as-willedness of others' existence and to their threat or disturbance of one's true self. The thinner the lifeworld of relational practices, the more painful any not-as-willed encounter is. This sensitivity leads to *relational pathologies* (関係性の病理) — anxiety in social settings, difficulty with sustained intimate relations, increasing recourse to apparatus-mediated alternatives — and reinforces the dependence on the apparatus to arbitrate relational struggles and entanglements that, were the lifeworld thicker, the inhabitants could navigate themselves. The paradox is the self-reinforcing dynamic: thinner lifeworld produces more pain from not-as-willed encounters, which produces more dependence on the apparatus to avoid such encounters, which thins the lifeworld further.

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

It is here, in the structural dynamics of relational pathology, that the chapter's argument first takes on the shape of the squeeze hypothesis the thesis has been promising. The inhabitant of the self-finishing world is squeezed between two incommensurable demands. From the side of the universe — the inherited biological-anthropological substrate that the human body and its evolved sociality instantiate — comes the demand for direct relational engagement, for embodied co-presence, for the kind of relational competence that develops only through the friction of not-as-willed encounters. From the side of the apparatus — the Modern mode's totalized infrastructure — comes the demand to render relations as-willed through apparatus-mediation, to substitute the smooth interface for the textured encounter, to convert the relational into the transactional. Both demands are operative; neither can be fully satisfied without compromising the other. The squeeze is not yet at its maximum intensity in the self-finishing world; it intensifies further in the self-completing apparatus of Chapter V and reaches its full structural articulation in Chapter VI.

## §IV.4 — Desomatization: The Body as Not-as-Willed Obstacle

On the other hand, since the not-as-willed nature of the human body imposes a biological hindrance on the realization of the presupposed ontological freedom, the less one's survival is dependent on biology, the freer the person is. This leads to a *desomatization of life* (生の脱身体化), as what it means to live becomes increasingly detached from the biological body. This also leads to a *confusion of life* (生の混乱): paradoxically, the less one is biologically *one's self*, the more ontologically realized one may seem.

The desomatization argument is not a moral complaint about the body's diminishing place in Modern life; it is a structural observation about a specific configuration of the mode's organizing principles. The Modern apparatus, organized around the pursuit of life-as-willed, treats the body as one more not-as-willed obstacle to be managed, mediated, and where possible transcended. This treatment expresses itself in three registers that have intensified over the past several decades.

*The labor register.* The apparatus increasingly absorbs forms of work that require embodied presence — agricultural, craft, care-giving — and substitutes apparatus-mediated alternatives that permit disembodied or thinly-embodied participation. The remote-work configuration normalized through the early 2020s pandemic period is the most visible recent case, but the trajectory is much older: the office work that emerged in the late nineteenth century already represented a partial desomatization relative to agricultural and craft labor; the screen-mediated work of the late twentieth century represented a further step; the AI-mediated work of the early twenty-first century is a further step still. Across the trajectory, the body's role in productive labor diminishes, and with it the body's contribution to social legitimacy, identity, and meaning-making.

*The somatic-correlate register.* The not-as-willed body is increasingly addressed as an obstacle to be technically managed. Cosmetic surgery, pharmaceutical psychiatry, sleep technology, fertility technology, performance-enhancing supplementation, anti-aging research, gender-affirming interventions, prosthetic enhancement — each of these is a domain in which the apparatus's organizing principle (the body should conform to the will) takes operational expression. The point is not to judge each domain individually; many of them, considered case by case, deliver real benefits to real individuals whose suffering would otherwise be greater. The point is structural: the *aggregate* effect of the apparatus's mobilization across these domains is a progressive reframing of the body itself as material to be configured, rather than as the lived substrate of a life.

*The phenomenological register.* As the body's role in labor diminishes and as somatic correlates are increasingly technically managed, the inhabitant's lived experience of being-a-body shifts. The body becomes increasingly something one *has* — an instrument, an object of management — rather than something one *is*. The phenomenological tradition from Husserl through Merleau-Ponty has named this shift in various ways (Körper versus Leib in the German vocabulary, *corps-objet* versus *corps-vécu* in the French) and has marked it as a specifically Modern transformation. The thesis's account of desomatization integrates this phenomenological reading with the structural-mechanism account: the lived shift in the body's status reflects the apparatus's structural organizing principle, and the structural organizing principle is sustained by the cumulative shifts in lived experience. [VERIFY: Merleau-Ponty 1945 *Phenomenology of Perception*; Husserl Ideen II on Leib vs Körper.]

What the desomatization argument does *not* claim — and this distinction matters for the chapter's relation to its committee discussion — is that the somatic correlates of the apparatus are uniformly pathological from the inhabitant's lived perspective. As §IV.3 noted regarding relational pathologies, the inhabitants of the Modern world choose the apparatus-mediated alternatives over the inherited embodied practices for reasons that, from the inhabitant's lived perspective, are intelligible and often defensible. The structural cost — the cumulative thinning of the embodied substrate that the body's not-as-willedness was, on the inherited modes' organization, the texture of — is a cost that operates at a level the inhabitant's lived experience cannot easily register. The thesis names this cost; it does not denounce the choices that produce it.

## §IV.5 — Transhumanism: The Cases Differentiated

The Modern mode's trajectory toward self-finishing has produced, in its most ambitious contemporary expressions, the transhumanist program: a cluster of research, technology, and ideological commitments oriented toward overcoming the body's not-as-willed character through technical intervention. The transhumanist program is not uniform, however, and its varied cases stand in different relations to the squeeze hypothesis the thesis is developing.

*Longevity research* targets the body's metabolic decline; insofar as it succeeds, it extends the period during which the universe-side body remains a viable substrate for life-as-willed pursuits. It does not, in itself, deepen the squeeze. *Restorative prosthesis* (limb replacement after injury, cochlear implants, retinal prostheses, exoskeletal supports for the disabled) restores capacities the universe-side body has lost; it operates within the existing endosomatic-exosomatic complementarity rather than reorganizing it. The prosthesis case has been over-read in some transhumanist literature as inaugurating a new condition; the thesis reads it as continuous with the longer arc of exosomatization Chapter II named.

*Cognitive enhancement* (pharmacological, neural interface, eventually direct algorithmic augmentation) is more ambiguous. Insofar as it expands what the universe-side body can do, it extends rather than displaces. Insofar as it reorganizes cognition to meet apparatus-side demands more efficiently — to participate in increasingly fast-paced apparatus-mediated processes, to handle the increasing cognitive load of apparatus-management — it intensifies the squeeze by deepening the apparatus-side coupling. The cognitive-enhancement case differs from prosthetic restoration in that the criterion of success is set by the apparatus's demands rather than by the inhabitant's pre-existing functional capacities; this difference is structurally significant.

*Mind-uploading* — the speculative endpoint of consciousness-substrate transfer — would represent the terminal articulation of the squeeze: the abandonment of the universe-side substrate altogether in favor of pure apparatus-side existence. The mind-uploading case is, at present, technologically speculative and may remain so; its philosophical interest for the thesis is that it makes explicit what the apparatus-side trajectory implies if extrapolated. Even the speculative scenario illuminates the structural commitment: the inhabitant would no longer be coupled to the universe except through the apparatus that hosts the uploaded mind, and would therefore depend entirely on the apparatus's continued operation for continued existence. The squeeze would, at that point, have given way to a different condition altogether — total dependence on the apparatus, no longer a squeeze *between* universe and apparatus but a unilateral subordination.

*Germline editing* operates at a different level still — not as a prosthetic intervention on a living subject but as a reorganization of the developmental conditions of subsequent subjects. Its squeeze-implications are mediated by what those future subjects will inherit: an endosomatic constitution shaped to better fit the apparatus they will encounter. The case sits at the boundary between endosomatic evolution (under altered selection criteria) and exosomatic engineering (artificiality reaching back into the developmental substrate). The thesis's account of the squeeze does not predict which way any specific transhumanist program turns; it specifies the structural axis along which each case can be located. The differentiation matters because transhumanism is sometimes engaged as if it were a single program with a single squeeze-relation; the thesis's framework permits the case-by-case analysis the topic requires. [VERIFY: Ch-4-6 v2 scaffold; Bostrom 2008 for the canonical case-taxonomy; Hughes 2004 for the social-political reading.]

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

What the transhumanist program at every case-level shares is its underlying axiological commitment: that the body's not-as-willedness is an obstacle to be overcome, and that overcoming it is desirable. From the perspective of the thesis's framework, this axiological commitment is the Modern mode of artificiality's self-finishing tendency taken to its logical conclusion. Whether or not any specific transhumanist intervention succeeds, the program's commitment makes explicit the trajectory the chapter has been tracking. The squeeze hypothesis the thesis articulates does not depend on whether mind-uploading becomes feasible or whether germline editing becomes routine; it depends on the structural dynamic that the transhumanist program crystallizes — the apparatus-mediated overcoming of the body's not-as-willedness as the realization of the Modern axiological commitment.

## §IV.6 — From Self-Finishing to Self-Completion

The chapter has tracked, through Uegaki's analysis and its extensions, the trajectory by which the Modern mode of artificiality's underlying axiological commitment to life-as-willed has produced the self-finishing tendency of human life. The trajectory operates at the levels of relational pathology (§IV.3), desomatization (§IV.4), and the transhumanist crystallization of the program's logical conclusion (§IV.5). At each level, the apparatus increasingly absorbs functions that had previously been carried by the lived person and the lived body; at each level, the human portion of functionality in the world correspondingly shrinks.

The shrinkage of the human portion is the bridge to Chapter V. As humans entrust more and more of survival, relationality, and succession to the apparatus, the apparatus's portion of functionality in the world correspondingly grows. The growth is not merely additive; it is *enabling* of further self-finishing on the human side, which is enabling of further self-completion on the apparatus side, in a dialectically coupled dynamic. Chapter V examines the apparatus side of this dialectic and asks what becomes structurally possible when the apparatus achieves sufficient operational independence from the humans whose functions it has absorbed. The minimally sufficient technologies for the apparatus's self-completion — artificial intelligence, big data, the Internet of Things, and code-as-law — are already emerging; Chapter V examines how their integration produces what the thesis calls the metaverse.

Before turning to that examination, one final observation belongs here. The self-finishing tendency this chapter has tracked is not a chosen pathology nor an imposed alienation. It is the *expected* trajectory of a mode of artificiality whose underlying axiology is the pursuit of life-as-willed. The inhabitants of the Modern world have been, throughout the trajectory, doing exactly what their world's organizing principles call for: pursuing freedom, reducing burdens, extending capacities, mediating relations, transcending obstacles. The self-finishing tendency is what this pursuit looks like when its conditions are sustained across multiple generations within a totalizing apparatus. The chapter has not been a critique of individual choices; it has been a description of the structural dynamic those choices, in aggregate, produce. Whether the structural dynamic can be sustainably continued — and what happens if it cannot — is the question Chapter VI takes up.
