## §0 — The Thesis's Aim and the Subject of Inquiry

This thesis aims to conceptualize the emerging concept of *metaverse* in a manner that sheds light on the techno-social predicaments of our time and our future, especially concerning humans' perennial anxiety about artificiality's threat to humanity.

Two major factors led to the choice of the metaverse as the subject of study (C.-K. Mi, personal communication, January 7, 2022). First, the word *metaverse* was translated into Chinese as 元宇宙 (yuan yu zhou) by the People's Republic of China literature and uncritically accepted by all of the Chinese-speaking world. This translation is problematic, as the meaning of "meta-" is misrepresented by 元 — which in Chinese means "origin," "source," or "previous" — and leads to an absurd translation that literally means "original universe." Such absurdity confuses this emerging concept and overlooks its potentially significant philosophical meaning. Second, as the tech giant Facebook renames itself *Meta* and appropriates *metaverse* with its own definition and vision, we risk limiting our future to one envisioned by capitalists. Definitions and visions guide our way of thinking; our way of thinking forms our imagination; our imagination sets the boundary for possible actions. A thorough understanding of the metaverse is required to develop a suitable translation and to reclaim futures from the capitalists.

However, there are several problems with how the metaverse is currently defined, and addressing these problems is the precondition for the conceptual work that the rest of the thesis carries out.

## §1 — Current Definitions and Their Three Diagnoses

Originating in the science fiction novel *Snow Crash* (Stephenson, 1992), the term *metaverse* was first invented to refer to "a world where virtual and reality interact and create value through various social activities" (Park & Kim, 2022). The term was sparsely used in the following decades — not as common as its then-synonym "virtual worlds" or the similar concept of "cyberspace" — followed by a sudden boom from late 2021 as the tech company Meta introduced the vision of their new project called *the Metaverse*. While the vision is still a work-in-progress, we can have a taste of its blueprint in the Mark Zuckerberg-endorsed popular book *The Metaverse: And How It Will Revolutionize Everything* (Ball, 2022), in which the metaverse is defined as: "a massively scaled and interoperable network of real-time rendered 3D virtual worlds that can be experienced synchronously and persistently by an effectively unlimited number of users with an individual sense of presence, and with continuity of data, such as identity, history, entitlements, objects, communications, and payments."

The existing image of the metaverse in academia is not far from this popular one. Based on a systematic literature review of 130 papers on Elsevier and 130 papers on Google Scholar, Park and Kim (2022) define the metaverse as "a compound word of transcendence *meta* and *universe* and refers to a three-dimensional virtual world where avatars engage in political, economic, social, and cultural activities," consisting of hardware components, software components, and contents. Floridi (2022) notes that, in practice, *metaverse* has become a "catch-all" term to talk about Web 3.0, blockchain, cryptocurrencies, NFTs, DeFi, DAOs, and so on, as "the next step in the development of digital technology." Like most social-sciences and humanities researchers on this topic (e.g., Chen, 2023), Floridi refrains from this broader definition and sticks to the narrower definition of the metaverse as synonymous with or centered upon *extended reality* (XR).

Three problems with the mainstream approach to defining the metaverse render that approach inadequate for the philosophical work this thesis undertakes.

*(A) Path dependency on its arbitrary origin, failing to grasp its overall disposition.* The current mainstream definition of *metaverse* points to a specific kind of technology — XR (and sometimes the political, economic, social, and cultural activities based upon it) (Mystakidis, 2022; Smart, 2022). This definition is a byproduct of its path dependency on the term's arbitrary origin as "beyond universe" (Stephenson, 1992), and such arbitrariness has led to absurdities such as the Chinese translation. A risk of such a definition is that it renders *metaverse* an empty or confusing buzzword, as replacing it with "XR" will not significantly change its meaning, if at all. Such an attempt to be "precise," as Floridi (2022) strives for, also risks overlooking the metaverse's disposition — like that of many other modern technologies — toward becoming all-encompassing (as Floridi himself admits), and risks failing to account for the ever-blurring boundaries and ever-strengthening integration not only among specific technologies but also among the artificial and the natural. In fact, the broader sense of the metaverse — one that "catches all" about Web 3.0 and so on — that Floridi refrains from committing to is actually one that its original inventor Stephenson is building with his new project Lamina1 (Lamina1, 2022; Levy, 2022). The April 2026 retrospective bears noting: by the time of this thesis's submission, Meta's Reality Labs has accumulated cumulative losses on the order of seventy billion dollars, the consumer-XR metaverse vision has receded into industry skepticism, and the surviving vectors of "metaverse-development" have migrated to industrial digital twins, AI-spatial computing platforms, and the integration of generative AI into productivity infrastructures (Egliston & Carter, 2022; **[VERIFY: Meta Q4 2024 10-K filings; McKinsey 2025 industrial digital twin market report]**). The path-dependent XR definition has, in effect, been empirically refuted by the trajectory of the technologies it tried to encompass.

*(B) Disciplinary reductionism and fragmentation, forming closed systems that limit inquiry.* On the other side of the same coin, another limitation of the mainstream approach is disciplinary reductionism and fragmentation (Montuori, 2013). Once we reduce *metaverse* to the specimen of XR (alongside other technology specimens such as AI) and limit our inquiry within this boundary, even if we explore its intersections with various issues (ontology, epistemology, ethics, governance, security, wellbeing), we become adrift along the inertia of the disciplinarization process — which creates closed systems that "frame" knowledge and "can prevent some thoughts from being thought, and some possibilities entertained" (Montuori, 2013). While the disciplinary, specializing approach may be decisive for inquiries on issues with clear, established boundaries (e.g., chemical properties in cell biology, procedural justice in parliamentary democracy), it may prevent us from fully exploring and appreciating the still-emerging, autopoietic, and thus VUCA (volatile, uncertain, complex, ambiguous) and yet-to-be-realized characteristics of the metaverse.

*(C) Actualistic inquiry, prone to be obsolete due to the metaverse's yet-to-be-realized dispositions.* This limitation is especially significant when we situate the question concerning the nature or characteristics of the metaverse in the context of human anxiety about artificiality's threat to humanity. Tackling this challenge from the mainstream approach often implicitly entails a kind of *actualism* — addressing the question of anxiety or debating the genuineness of artificiality's threat by reference to what has been actualized, whether empirically observed or speculatively extrapolated. Critical realism (Bhaskar, 1975, 1979) has named the epistemic fallacy at the root of actualism: the conflation of what is *real* (the level of generative mechanisms, structures, and dispositions) with what is *actual* (the events those mechanisms produce) and with what is *empirical* (the events as observed). Given that we are still in a very early stage of the metaverse's emergence and that its features are still very VUCA and yet-to-be-realized, any inference contingent upon the bounded, tangible actualizations of what the metaverse is or might become can be easily rendered obsolete once the actualized premise no longer holds. For example, based on the definition of metaverse as XR, Meinlschmidt et al. (2023) raised concerns about the metaverse's adverse impact on mental health in terms of "fuzzy and incongruent identities" or "bodily discomfort associated with XR (cybersickness)." However, these threats may no longer be the case if the specific features upon which these problems are contingent do not develop further; decentralized identifier (DID) infrastructures (Reed et al., 2020) counter the threat of "fuzzy and incongruent identities," and Lamina1's "use-case agnostic" design goal (Parisi et al., 2023) counters cybersickness caused by XR devices.

The thesis's approach is to bypass these three problems by not reducing the metaverse to its currently-actualized specimens, but to characterize it in terms of its *real-level dispositions or causal powers* — the structural-environmental features that any technically distinct implementation of the same kind of background environment would need to exhibit. This is what the next sections set up. [VERIFY: Introduction-1 / Introduction-3 / Introduction-4 scaffolds; §A.3 lock 2026-05-07 on (C) Actualistic rename.]

## §2 — Preliminary Definitions: Universe, World, Human, Artificiality

Before turning to the structural argument that the body chapters develop, three working definitions must be in place. These are minimally sufficient definitions for the metaverse-definition critique above and for the structural argument that follows; Chapter I develops them at full philosophical-ecological scope and Chapter II at the analytic depth their use in the body chapters requires. The working definitions are:

The *universe* is the background environment that precedes humans, where humans are situated yet which is uncontrollable by human intention. Its laws — gravitation, thermodynamics, biology, climatology, ecology — are the substrate of all human existence. Universe-laws operate independently of human knowledge of them; humans neither make these laws nor remake them; humans adapt to them.

The *world* is the artificial environment nested within the universe that humans construct, defend, and sustain as a livable habitat against the survival challenges imposed by the universe. The world is the bounded sub-environment in which a human collectivity actually lives and to which the collectivity's organizing principles apply. A world is, in the analytic vocabulary Chapter I develops, an *adaptational operating system* — a collective strategy by which a human population mediates its existence within the universe. Different worlds — different modes of artificiality — instantiate the same operating-system type with different organizing principles, different material and nonmaterial components, and different relations to the universe-substrate.

*Humans* are not pre-existent biological subjects who subsequently adopt artificial environments; humans are constituted in the coupling between their biological substrate and the artificial environments they construct. The thesis uses *humans* to refer to this coupled biological-artificial entity; *Homo sapiens* (or ヒト in the Japanese vocabulary that Hideo Obara's work makes available) refers to the biological substrate considered apart from the coupling. The distinction matters because the metaverse-stage developments the thesis examines reorganize the coupling in structurally significant ways. [VERIFY: Introduction-2 scaffold; Obara's homo sapiens/人間 distinction in Chapter I.]

*Artificiality* names what humans make — the bounded artefacts (material and nonmaterial alike), the embodied skills, the rule-governed practices, and the institutions that together constitute a world. Artificiality has three intertwined aspects that Chapter II develops in full: *technology* as the exteriorized organ that extends biological capacities; *virtuality* as the causally efficacious nonmaterial component that coordinates social interaction and meaning-making; and the irreducibility of the *laws of artificial environments* to the laws of the universe in which those environments operate. Artificiality, in this thesis's usage, includes all three; it is not reducible to material technology alone.

A note on the thesis's deferred definition of the metaverse. The thesis does not commit to a final definition of the metaverse in the early chapters; instead, it builds up to the definition through the structural argument that runs through Chapters IV–VI. The reason is methodological: a definition committed in advance of the structural argument would have to be either (i) an actualistic definition built from the currently-actualized specimens (which §1 has shown to be inadequate), or (ii) a stipulative definition that begs the structural question. The thesis develops the structural conditions that, when satisfied at scale, constitute what it ultimately names the *metaverse*; the definition is the structural condition, not a class of technologies. ## §3 — Counterargument Engagement

Four counterarguments to the thesis's approach are worth engaging explicitly, since they identify the positions from which a reader might most reasonably resist the structural-ontological move the thesis makes.

*The XR-centric counterargument*: the metaverse simply *is* XR (extended reality, the immersive virtual-physical interface); the thesis's broader definition is a category error that conflates a specific technology with an undefined disposition. The response: the structural definition the thesis develops is not a category error but a recognition that the analytic level at which "the metaverse" becomes interesting is the structural-environmental level, not the technological-instance level. XR may be one technological implementation of metaverse-stage structural conditions, but the structural conditions are independent of any particular technical instantiation, as §1's empirical update on the XR-vision's industrial retreat illustrates.

*The existing-frameworks-suffice counterargument*: the analytical work the thesis does is already available within existing frameworks — Floridi's infosphere, Castells's network society, Stiegler's organology, Hui's cosmotechnics, Bhaskar's stratified ontology. Why a new framework? The response: the thesis does not propose a new framework; it integrates resources from these existing frameworks within a critical-realist account specifically tuned to the structural-environmental condition the metaverse stage represents. The squeeze hypothesis articulated in Chapter VI is the thesis's specific analytic contribution that no existing framework, individually, articulates; the rest of the conceptual apparatus draws on the existing frameworks and acknowledges its debts.

*The tech-determinism counterargument*: by treating the metaverse as a structural-environmental condition that has obtained or is obtaining, the thesis reproduces a technological determinism that critical-realist analysis is supposed to resist. The response: the structural condition the thesis names is *self-organized through collective human participation* via the bidirectional coupling developed in Chapter V and re-articulated in Chapter VI's productive-dimension argument. The condition is not externally imposed by technology against human will; it is the structural correlate of a coupling humans participate in. Tech determinism is precisely what the bidirectional-coupling framing refuses.

*The cultural-particularity counterargument*: the thesis presents the squeeze hypothesis as a planetary condition, but the structural conditions it names may be specific to one mode of artificiality (the Modern mode that Chapter III examines) and not to all worlds. To present the condition as a generic human predicament is to over-generalize from a culturally specific situation. The response: the thesis is explicit that the squeeze obtains within and from the metaverse-stage Modern mode of artificiality, whose universalizing trajectory Chapter III examines in detail. The thesis does not claim the squeeze is a condition all worlds face; it claims that, because the Modern mode has succeeded over five centuries in becoming the dominant mode at planetary scale, the structural conditions of the metaverse-stage squeeze are now operative for inhabitants of any world that the Modern mode has integrated. The cultural-particularity objection identifies a real risk and the thesis addresses it by tracking the universalization mechanism explicitly. ## §4 — Distinguishing from Three Mainstream Traditions on the Problem of Technology

The conceptual move that motivates the thesis is best understood by contrast with three established traditions for analyzing the techno-social predicaments of contemporary life. Each of these traditions captures real features of the situation; the thesis distinguishes its own contribution from each by specifying the structural-ontological level at which its analysis operates beneath all three.

*The Montesquieuan tradition* analyzes the problem of technology as a problem of governance through check-and-balance of power. On this reading, the predicament arises when the institutional and infrastructural apparatus accumulates power that is not adequately checked by countervailing institutions; the response is to design or reform institutions so that the apparatus is held accountable. Contemporary expressions include the European Union's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and the more recent Digital Markets Act (DMA), each of which balances the executive power of large platform corporations by imposing jurisdictional checks on them. The Montesquieuan tradition captures something real: the apparatus does accumulate power, and institutional check-and-balance is a meaningful response. What the tradition does not capture, and what the thesis specifies, is the structural-environmental level at which the apparatus has begun to operate as a phusis-like background environment that institutional check-and-balance presupposes the operative agency of (and that, when the apparatus achieves phusis-like character, becomes incommensurable with). The squeeze is a condition that obtains beneath the governance-failure analysis; institutional reform is necessary but, on the thesis's argument, structurally insufficient to address the squeeze itself. [VERIFY: Montesquieu *Spirit of Laws* 1748; current Conclusion P0177 already engages this point briefly.]

*The Marxian tradition* analyzes the problem of technology as a problem of ownership and the struggle against exploitation and oppression by inequalities in artificiality's ownership. On this reading, the predicament arises when the means of artificial production are concentrated in private hands that extract surplus from the producers; the response is to reconfigure ownership — collectivize, cooperativize, or commonify the apparatus. Contemporary expressions include data cooperatives (Hardjono & Pentland, 2019) that seek to mitigate privatized data-extraction by reclaiming data ownership. The Marxian tradition captures something real: ownership concentration is a real feature of the metaverse-stage, and ownership reconfiguration is a meaningful response. What the tradition does not capture, and what the thesis specifies, is that the metaverse-stage's environmental character produces effects on inhabitants that operate independently of who *owns* the apparatus — the architectural constraints work whether the platform is privately, publicly, or cooperatively owned; the dividuating governmentality (Chapter V §V.5) operates on data profiles regardless of who owns the data; the radical monopoly Illich names (Chapter VI §VI.5) operates environmentally, not commercially. The squeeze is a condition that obtains beneath the ownership-analysis; ownership reconfiguration is meaningful, but structurally insufficient. [VERIFY: Marx *Capital* Vol. 1, 1867; Hardjono & Pentland 2019.]

*The Mill-Berlinian liberty tradition* analyzes the problem of technology as a problem of liberty's preservation against the encroachments of governmental, social, or institutional power. The tradition runs from John Stuart Mill's *On Liberty* (1859) through Isaiah Berlin's distinction between "Two Concepts of Liberty" (1958) and forward through the contemporary liberal-democratic engagement with platform power. On this reading, the predicament arises when individuals' liberty — whether *negative liberty* (freedom from interference) or *positive liberty* (freedom to act and self-realize) — is constrained by the apparatus; the response is to preserve and extend the conditions for liberty against the constraint. The Mill-Berlinian tradition captures something real: liberty is a real value, and constraints on liberty are real harms. What the tradition does not capture, and what the thesis specifies, is that the metaverse-stage operates not by direct constraint on liberty (an external authority telling the inhabitant what they may not do) but by structural reconfiguration of the conditions under which liberty itself can be exercised. The inhabitant of the self-finishing world (Chapter IV) is formally free in both Berlin's negative and positive senses — formally unconstrained in choices, formally enabled to self-realize through the apparatus — but the structural conditions for the kinds of liberty Mill defended (the inhabitant's capacity to develop independent judgment, to engage in deliberative practices, to participate in lived collective traditions) have been progressively thinned by the same self-finishing trajectory that produced the formal freedoms. The Mill-Berlinian tradition addresses the formal-liberty register; the thesis addresses the structural-conditions register beneath it. Berlin's worry, in his Two Concepts essay, about the totalitarian potential of positive-liberty discourse anticipates a related concern; the thesis's account specifies the structural mechanism that produces the conditions in which both negative and positive liberty become formally affirmed but substantively hollowed. [VERIFY: Mill 1859 *On Liberty*; Berlin 1958 *Two Concepts of Liberty* — both **NOT in current Zotero; acquisition required**.]

The thesis's distinctive contribution, then, is to specify the *structural-ontological* condition that operates beneath the governance, ownership, and liberty registers. The condition is the squeeze; the analysis of the condition is the thesis. The three mainstream traditions each capture real features of the situation and identify legitimate response strategies; the thesis does not displace them but specifies the structural level on which their effective application depends. The Conclusion returns to this distinction explicitly. [VERIFY: Introduction-1 / Conclusion-2 / Conclusion-3 scaffolds.]

## §5 — Methodological Statement: Developmental Emergent Realism

The thesis's methodological position is what I am calling *Developmental Emergent Realism* — a working name for the integration of three substantive resources and three analytical moves that organize the inquiry.

The two substantive resources are *Continental philosophy of modernity and technology* (Bhaskar's critical realism; Stiegler's organology and tertiary retention; Hui's cosmotechnics; the Frankfurt School's critical theory) and *Eco-anthropological history and thought* (Obara's evolutionary anthropology; Brague's history of the universe in Western thought; Henrich's cumulative-cultural-evolution synthesis; Graeber and Wengrow's historical anthropology; Descola's beyond-nature-and-culture project; James Scott's analyses of legibility and refusal). The two resources are not disciplinary alternatives but mutually constitutive: the Continental tradition provides the analytic apparatus for tracking the structural-ontological level at which the thesis's argument operates; the eco-anthropological tradition provides the empirical and historical anchoring without which the structural analysis would float free.

The three analytical moves are *diachronic retroduction*, *stratified-emergent reading*, and *ideal-typical analysis*. Diachronic retroduction names the inferential move from observed phenomena back to the generative mechanisms that produced them, in the critical-realist sense developed by Bhaskar (1975) and extended by Archer's morphogenetic approach (1995). The move is *diachronic* in that it tracks mechanisms developmentally across time — through the historical trajectories of the modes of artificiality the thesis examines — rather than treating them synchronically as static structures. Stratified-emergent reading applies the critical-realist stratification of the *real* (generative mechanisms), the *actual* (events those mechanisms produce), and the *empirical* (events as observed) to the three principal entities the thesis tracks: the universe, the world, and the metaverse. The result is a 3×3 stratification matrix that organizes how the thesis distinguishes the structural-ontological level of its primary claims from the actual-empirical levels at which those claims connect to documented evidence. Ideal-typical analysis, in the Weberian sense, names the construction of analytic types — *adaptational operating system*, *mode of artificiality*, *Modern mode*, *metaverse-stage*, *squeeze* — that capture the structural features of phenomena without claiming descriptive completeness for any particular instance.

*Version A footnote translation in critical-realist vocabulary* [VERIFY: A.4 lock]: Developmental Emergent Realism is a working name for a position that integrates Bhaskar's critical realism (depth ontology, transitive/intransitive distinction, retroductive inference) with the extended evolutionary synthesis's emergentism (multilevel causation, cultural niche construction, complex adaptive systems), articulating both within the eco-anthropological-historical register where modes of artificiality are the unit of analysis. The position recognizes the priority of generative mechanisms (the *real*) over events (the *actual*) and observations (the *empirical*); it tracks the developmental dynamics of those mechanisms; and it treats the units of analysis as ideal-typical without reifying them. This is the critical-realist translation; for readers whose primary register is analytic philosophy of mind, social theory, or science and technology studies, the main-text formulation suffices.

## §6 — Structure of the Thesis and Chapter Previews

The thesis is organized into seven chapters following this introduction.

*Chapter I* develops the operating-system framework for understanding worlds as adaptational responses of human collectivities to the universe-substrate. Building on the work of Hideo Obara, Yuval Noah Harari, and the niche-construction theory of Levins and Lewontin, the chapter argues that a world is the artificial environment nested in the universe that humans construct, defend, and sustain as a livable habitat. From this view, our worlds have always been artificial. The five working criteria of an *adaptational operating system* are stated and grounded in the empirical record of the Great Oxidation Event and the niche-construction record of non-human species.

*Chapter II* unpacks the three intertwined aspects of artificiality that together constitute a world: *technology* as exosomatization (drawing on Alfred Lotka and Bernard Stiegler), *virtuality* as causally efficacious nonmaterial artificiality (against the simulationist and computer-based views of Peirce, Baudrillard, Chalmers, and Brey), and the *irreducibility of the laws of artificial environments* to those of the universe (drawing on Michael Polanyi's dual-control argument). The chapter is structured around the Brague-inspired observation that the nomos has always been tekhne — that the customary ordering by which a community lives is among the bounded artefacts the community sustains.

*Chapter III* argues that the *Modern mode of artificiality* (the configuration whose historical lineage runs from the Mediterranean of antiquity through early-modern Europe to global Modern dominance) has a structural disposition to universalize itself and overwrite the organizing principles of other modes with which it has come into contact. The disposition rests on a material basis (the production-mode triangle Joseph Needham traces), an epistemic disposition (the Platonic-Aristotelian fault line Roy Bhaskar names), and a real-level imposition mechanism (the critical-realist account of colonization). The plurality of worlds — including the Sinosphere, Islamic, Pacific, Indigenous, and other modes — is the empirical anchor against which the Modern mode's universalization can be analytically characterized.

*Chapter IV* examines, through the work of Japanese philosopher Takahide Uegaki, the internal trajectory of the Modern mode at the level of the inhabitant: the pursuit of *life-as-willed* (意のままになる生), the self-finishing tendency this pursuit produces, and the dilution of relationality and desomatization that follow. The transhumanist program is examined as the logical conclusion of the underlying axiological commitment to overcoming the body's not-as-willed character.

*Chapter V* examines, in parallel, the trajectory at the level of artificiality itself: the *self-completion* of the apparatus. Building on Brague's tekhne-becoming-phusis formulation, the chapter argues that the technical apparatus the Modern mode has constructed is acquiring the structural features of phusis — operating according to its own internal principles, forming a background environment to which subsequent human action must respond. The four minimally sufficient technologies (AI, big data, IoT, code-as-law) instantiate three real-level mechanisms (Lessig's architectural regulation, Hui's cosmotechnical mediation, Stiegler's retention-protention coupling). Stiegler's grammatization, three-stage proletarianization, and the contrast between dividuation and transindividuation are developed as the human-side correlates.

*Chapter VI* states the *squeeze hypothesis* structurally and reads its current empirical signs. The squeeze is the condition in which the world is jointly subject to two incommensurable background environments — the universe-side and the apparatus-side — neither of which can be ignored without survival cost. F1 (both sides changing dynamically) and F2 (incommensurable laws) jointly constitute the structural definition. The animal-stage Anthropocene record provides the species-scale precedent; the prior human squeezes (Obara's self-petification, Stiegler's grammatization, Frankfurt-School reification, Illich's radical monopoly) provide the anthropological-experiential precursors; the metaverse-stage squeeze proper exhibits three empirical signs. Illich's radical-monopoly and Taleb's fragility-transfer frameworks deepen the signs analytically. The four-cell typology of constraint and the symmetric/asymmetric distinction differentiate the metaverse-stage squeeze structurally from prior squeezes. The viva committee's challenge that the squeeze is not necessarily negative is engaged through the productive-dimension argument: refusal of the trade-off framing, refusal of the pathology-vs-recognition framing, articulation of the structural-exchange-via-bidirectional-coupling framing, and the recognition-and-bidirectional-reform implication.

*The Conclusion* situates the thesis's contribution relative to the three mainstream traditions (Montesquieu, Marx, Mill-Berlin) and articulates the directions toward which the analysis points: a recognition of *appropriate technology* and *tools for conviviality* in Illich's sense; the *cosmotechnics of co-becoming* and the concept of *kyōsei* (共生); Audrey Tang's *plurality* (with E. Glen Weyl); Kōmoto Hideo's *design of recovery / self-diversifying systems* (河本英夫 *復興のデザイン 自己多様化するシステム*) recontextualized as *positive niche construction*; the pluriverse movement (Escobar, Hope, Reiter); and Yuk Hui's *noodiversity* and *technodiversity*. These are not prescriptions but directions of inquiry that the thesis's structural analysis makes legible. ## §7 — A Typology of Predicaments

A final framing concept belongs in this Introduction, since it organizes the thesis's relationship to the wider literature on contemporary crises. By *techno-social predicaments* the thesis refers to a typology of registers in which the contemporary condition presents itself as crisis. Five registers are usefully distinguished, each of which the body chapters address from the structural-ontological level rather than from within the register itself.

*The adaptive register*: the predicament arises when adaptation across multiple environments produces conflict that exceeds the inhabitant's adaptational reserves. Sign A of §VI.4 (psychosomatic malfunctions and existential anxiety) is the adaptive register's contemporary expression at the level of inhabitant-experience. *The epistemic register*: the predicament arises when the inhabitant's capacity for independent judgment, deliberation, and knowledge-production is compromised by the conditions of life in the apparatus-mediated environment. Sign C of §VI.4 (disturbed lives under opaque and changing laws) and the contemporary loss of *savoir-théoriser* (Chapter V §V.4) are the epistemic register's expressions. *The governance register*: the predicament arises when collective decision-making capacity is structurally outpaced by the developments to which decision-making would need to respond. The Montesquieuan tradition's analyses of regulatory inadequacy are the governance register's expression. *The livelihood register*: the predicament arises when the means of survival become singularized to the apparatus-mediated channels, with the consequent fragility under apparatus-malfunction and the foreclosure of vernacular alternatives. Sign B of §VI.4 (singularization of livelihood) and Illich's modernized poverty are the livelihood register's expressions. *The existential register*: the predicament arises when the conditions for meaningful human existence — relational depth, embodied participation, intergenerational inheritance, ethical orientation — are structurally thinned by the trajectory the body chapters track. Uegaki's analysis of the self-contained society and its Ethics of Non-Intervention is the existential register's expression.

The five registers are not mutually exclusive; the same structural-environmental condition can manifest in multiple registers simultaneously. The thesis's analytic strategy is to identify the structural-environmental condition (the squeeze) that obtains at a level beneath the registers and to specify how the registers' different expressions are consequences of the underlying condition. The strategy is not to choose among the registers but to articulate the structural condition that makes legible why the contemporary moment presents predicaments in all five at once.
