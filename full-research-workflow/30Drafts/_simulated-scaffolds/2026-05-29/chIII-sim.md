# Chapter III — Modes of Artificiality and the Modern Mode's Disposition to Universalize

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD — friction-reducer for organizing existing scaffolds + corrections. Author rewrites in own voice before promotion. Terminology "Modern Mode of Artificiality" is provisional; see final audit §5 for the terminology analysis and decision.]

## §III.0 — From the Operating System to Its Instances

Chapter II argued that any world, considered as an adaptational operating system, depends on an artificial environment whose laws are irreducible to the laws of the universe in which the environment operates. What the present chapter must now do is move from the *type* — the world as such — to its *instances*. There have been many worlds. Some persist; some have failed. Some communicate with others; some operate by organizing principles that other worlds cannot translate without remainder. Among the surviving instances, one — the Modern mode of artificiality, whose lineage I will trace shortly — has, over the past several centuries, developed a peculiar tendency: a disposition to universalize itself and to overwrite the organizing principles of other worlds with which it has come into contact. The chapter unpacks the plurality of worlds, names the specific mode whose universalizing tendency the thesis takes as a load-bearing condition for the squeeze hypothesis, examines the material and epistemic mechanisms by which that universalization has operated, and confronts the counter-positions that would deny the analytic move.

A note on terminology before the argument proper. The mode of artificiality I will examine is the one whose origins lie in the Mediterranean of antiquity, whose institutional consolidation occurs in early-modern Europe, and whose global diffusion takes place over the past five centuries. The literature has named this configuration variously — Western Modernity, the Enlightenment project, the Modern world-system, the mechanistic worldview, the technoscientific complex. Each of these names captures something the others miss; each also carries the risk of essentializing a culturally and historically diverse arc into a single bloc. I use *Modern Mode of Artificiality* in what follows — a working term that signals the historical arc (early modern through contemporary), avoids geographic-essentialist framing of "Western" as a unitary culture, and remains analytically neutral about whether the configuration is best characterized through its philosophical (Enlightenment), economic (capitalist), technological (industrial), or epistemic (mechanistic) features. The footnote at the close of the chapter discusses the alternatives more fully and grounds the choice. ## §III.1 — The Plurality of Worlds: Goodman's Worldmaking, Re-read in Critical-Realist Terms

The thesis uses *the world* (singular) and *worlds* (plural) in different but coordinated senses. The singular is generic — it names the operating-system *type* whose criteria the previous chapter specified, in the way that "the cell" is generic in biology and "the operating system" is generic in computer science. The plural names the *instances* of that type: the Sinosphere world, the Andean world, the medieval Latin world, the modern Western world, and so on, each a different realization of the same five criteria, differing in content while sharing the same constitutive relation to the universe as their substrate.

Nelson Goodman's *Ways of Worldmaking* (1978) is the most precise philosophical articulation of this structure, and it can be re-read in critical-realist terms. Goodman's central observation — "worldmaking as we know it always starts from worlds already on hand; the making is a remaking" — positions worlds as ontologically real generative mechanisms whose products (the experienced furniture of any given culture) are the actual events and empirical observations of that culture. Worlds, in this reading, are *real* in the critical-realist sense: enduring, constitutive, generative; *actual* in their concrete instantiations; *empirical* in their lived experience. Goodman's "remaking" is the diachronic process by which worlds are reproduced and transformed across generations through what we now call cultural niche construction (Henrich, 2016). [VERIFY: Goodman 1978 *Ways of Worldmaking*, especially Ch.1 on the multiplicity of worlds and the rejection of "the world" in the singular.]

This reading also frames the empirical record this chapter and the next will mobilize. Worlds rise and fall — the Easter Island world, the Western Roman world, the late-Ming dynastic world (Brook, 2023), the Bronze Age Mediterranean world — under cosmic-ecological pressure that exceeds their adaptive range (Tainter, 1988; Diamond, 2005). The reoccurring apocalyptic collapse of the Santi civilization in *The Three-Body Problem* extrapolates from these real cases. Such collapses are empirical evidence that worlds are operating systems with adaptive limits, not eternal categories — that the *type* persists across human history while *instances* are mortal.

The plurality is not, however, an empirical curiosity. It is a structural feature of complex adaptive systems. The extended evolutionary synthesis (Laland et al., 2015) takes diversity to be both a *necessary condition* and a *necessary result* of evolutionary processes: variation is what selection acts on; differentiation is what variation produces under selection across different background environments. World-pluralism is the supra-organismal expression of this same evolutionary dynamic. Different background environments — geographic, ecological, climatic, historical — produce different organizing principles for the worlds that develop within them. Different organizing principles, in turn, produce different selection pressures on their inhabitants. The plurality of worlds is what evolutionary diversity looks like at the level of human collective survival strategies.

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

From the recognition of plurality follows a constraint on philosophical analysis that this chapter must honor. Any account of one world's mode of artificiality — including the Modern mode whose disposition this chapter examines — is necessarily *one account from one position among others*. The thesis does not claim a god's-eye view of all worlds. What it claims is that the analytical apparatus developed in Chapter I — adaptational operating system, bounded sub-environment, irreducible organizing principles, incommensurability without incompatibility — permits a comparative description that does not presuppose any particular world's organizing principles as the standard. The chapter's account of the Modern mode is offered in that spirit: a description of a specific configuration's structural features, not a denunciation of one culture by another. The chapter that follows takes Modern artificiality's *internal* trajectory toward self-finishing as its subject; this chapter examines its *external* relations with other modes.

## §III.2 — The Material Basis of the Modern Mode's Self-Reinforcement

Despite the Modern Mode of Artificiality having reigned over the globe for several centuries, its mode of artificiality was an exception rather than the commonplace among human worlds. As biochemist and Sinologist Joseph Needham concluded his magnum opus *Science and Civilisation in China* (Needham, Elvin & Huang, 2004), the historically dominant modes of production in different regions of the world produced strikingly different orientations toward the universe and the human place within it. Agriculture as the historically dominant mode of production in the Sinosphere favored a non-interventionist worldview and sought harmony with nature; the historically dominant modes of production in the Mediterranean — animal husbandry, seafaring, and slavery — favored a worldview that is metaphysically mechanistic and axiologically interventionist. As Needham himself observed, these orientations are not optional cultural preferences laid over a neutral biological substrate; they are *co-emergent* with the characteristic forms of science, technology, and institutions that the respective worlds developed. [VERIFY: Needham et al. 2004 *Science and Civilisation in China*, Vol. 7 Part 2 — the geography-mode-of-production-worldview triangle passages; the direct quote should replace the current paraphrase per dossier id=24 / Ma's "name dropping" challenge.]

The Mediterranean mode of artificiality — which eventually consolidated into what I am calling the Modern mode — began to boom at the dawn of the Renaissance, as intensified military and commercial competition backed by intergenerationally accumulated private wealth of feudal aristocrats fostered the development of mechanistic and interventionist technoscience, capital-intensive by character. Materially, this worldview justified the Modern mechanistic intervention over the globe, and the mechanistic intervention by Modern artificiality — together with the gains from such process — reinforced the worldview it embodied. Exploiting external resources from the underground and from colonies liberated the Modern mode from its organic constraints of fertility and labor, further providing the luxury to develop mechanistic and interventionist artificiality while externalizing immense costs (三浦永光, 2004; Pomeranz, 2021).

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

The contrast Needham draws between Sinosphere and Mediterranean modes is, however, only one binary within a much wider field of historical alternatives that the current literature has begun to recover. Three families of cases ought to be mentioned to mark the broader plurality the chapter's argument depends on, and to disclose that the contrast Needham works with is neither exhaustive nor binary. *Islamic astronomy and medicine* in the period from the eighth through the fourteenth centuries developed mathematical, observational, and clinical sophistication that does not map onto the mechanistic-vs-organic dichotomy: the Maragheh school's revisions to Ptolemaic astronomy and the systematic clinical observation of Ibn Sina (Avicenna) constitute a third configuration whose own philosophical anchors — Avicennan ontology, kalam theology — are neither Modern nor Sinosphere (Saliba, 2007). *Pacific wayfinding* developed across millennia a navigational science based on observation of swells, stars, cloud formations, bird behavior, and inherited cognitive cartography that delivered consistent open-ocean navigation across thousands of kilometres without recourse to mechanistic instrumentation or quantitative cartography in the Modern sense (Finney, 2003). *Indigenous land-management practices* across multiple continents — controlled burning regimes among Aboriginal Australian and California Indigenous communities, complex polyculture systems in the Amazon basin, terrace agriculture in the Andes — operated by organizing principles that explicitly refused the separation of human and non-human agency on which Modern environmental management is constituted (Gammage, 2011; Posey, 2002). [VERIFY: each of these citations against Zotero; James Poskett *Horizons* 2022 may serve as a single-source umbrella for several of these cases.]

The point of recalling these cases is not to romanticize them, nor to suggest that they constitute alternatives that the present world could simply adopt. The point is structural: each instantiates a mode of artificiality whose organizing principles are not derivable from the universe-level laws — that is, each is a real adaptational operating system in the sense of Chapter I — and whose principles are not commensurable with those of the Modern mode in the senses that matter for cross-world transfer. The plurality is real; the Modern mode is one mode among many; the universalizing tendency of the Modern mode operates against this plurality, not within it.

## §III.3 — The Epistemic Disposition: The Platonic-Aristotelian Fault Line

The Modern mode's tendency to totalize itself and to exclude others is not solely a matter of material reinforcement. It also has, in addition to its material basis, an epistemic disposition that organizes how the world is *known* and, through that knowledge, *acted upon*. The argument here is canonical and the current Chapter III's prose presents it well; I preserve its structure with light extensions.

According to Roy Bhaskar's analysis (2008: 83; 2009: 134–141, 167–174), Plato and Aristotle left a fundamentalist legacy of striving for "universal-and-necessary-certain" knowledge, and their different stances formed a *fault line* that simultaneously squeezes — flattens and excludes — the countless aspects of reality and other ways of knowing that do not comply with theirs. The Platonic strand tends to reduce the world to an "autonomized philosophy," a parthenogenetically self-generating mind, reason, spirit, or will. The Aristotelian strand tends to reduce theory to "autonomized sense-experience," which assumes the world can be read like a text or numbers on a clock. While seemingly antagonistic, the two strands are dialectically interdependent. The Platonic commits a kind of anthropomorphism that projects the human mind onto the world; the Aristotelian falls into a kind of anthropocentrism that reduces the world to what is perceivable by humans. Despite being dichotomous, they are not irreconcilable. If we recontextualize critical theories on the totalization of modernity (Adorno & Horkheimer, 2007; Marcuse, 1998) with Bhaskar's concepts, we see that the autonomized sense-experiences are being utilized for calculative, positivistic technoscience and institutions, and that this process is itself the manifestation of the autonomized Reason operating upon the principles of self-preservation and efficiency.

What this account makes available, in the present chapter's vocabulary, is a precise mechanism for the universalizing disposition. The fault line names a structural feature of the Modern mode's organizing principles by which the mode treats its own categories as universal and treats other modes' categories as either approximations of its own (Aristotelian reduction) or as failures of its own (Platonic rationalization). The fault line is not a contingent feature that better thinking would dissolve; it is the *organizing principle* of the Modern mode's epistemic apparatus, the boundary condition on the mode's intellectual operations.

The thesis takes Bhaskar's analysis to be substantially correct as a description of the Modern mode's epistemic disposition, while reserving two qualifications. First, the fault line is not the *only* organizing principle of Modern epistemology; the mode also contains traditions of immanent critique (the Frankfurt School's reflexive engagement with Reason; Bhaskar's own critical-realist project; the heterodox phenomenological tradition from Husserl through Merleau-Ponty) that interrogate the fault line from within. The thesis's own methodological position, developed in the Introduction and grounded in critical realism, draws on these internal-critical resources. Second, the fault line's empirical effects in any given setting depend on how the epistemic disposition couples with the material apparatus discussed in §III.2 and the institutional apparatus to be discussed in the universalization-mechanism subsection that follows. The fault line is the disposition; the actual universalization is what happens when the disposition is materially backed by the conditions for global diffusion. [VERIFY: Bhaskar 2008 *Dialectic*; Bhaskar 2009 *Plato Etc.* — passages cited as canonical.]

A brief methodological note belongs here, accommodating Ma's challenge from the viva that the thesis ought to justify why philosophical analysis is the appropriate register for an account that engages with biological, anthropological, and economic-historical evidence as well. The Introduction develops this at length under the heading of *Developmental Emergent Realism*. The short answer in the present context is that philosophy is the register in which *organizing principles* — including the principles that organize how disciplines themselves divide the work of inquiry — become explicitly articulable. Bhaskar's fault-line analysis is itself a philosophical move; if the mode of artificiality the thesis examines has organizing principles at the level the fault line names, then a philosophical engagement is unavoidable. The footnote that closes the chapter develops this point and engages the pragmatist and post-structuralist objections to depth-ontological inquiry that the thesis's position must answer. [VERIFY: Ch-3-7 v2 scaffold; Sayer 2000 on qualifications to critical realism; Rorty 1979 on the rejection of foundational philosophy.]

## §III.4 — Universalization in Operation: Fukuyama, Huntington, and Colonization as Real-Level Imposition

The material and epistemic dispositions just described are organizing principles; the question is what *universalization* looks like when those principles operate at planetary scale. Two large diagnoses of the contemporary world-condition operate at this level and require critical-realist correction.

Fukuyama's *end of history* (1992) is best read not as a prediction about historical termination but as an articulation, *from within*, of the disposition of one currently dominant operating system — the Modern mode of artificiality — toward global domination, what Chakrabarty (2018, 2023) names the displacement of "planet" by "globe." Fukuyama's specific failure is the linearization of evolution: he treats history as cumulative progress toward a single end-state, in the orthodox Hegelian-Marxist mould. But evolutionary theory across the synthesis from Archer's morphogenetic approach to the extended evolutionary synthesis (Laland et al., 2015; Wielecki, 2020) takes diversity to be both a *necessary condition* and a *necessary result* of evolutionary processes; world-pluralism is not a transitional inefficiency but a structural feature of complex adaptive systems. Read critically, Fukuyama's account documents the disposition; it does not establish the destiny. [VERIFY: Fukuyama 1992; Chakrabarty 2018, 2023.]

Huntington's *clash of civilizations* (1996) gets one thing importantly right: tensions between worlds at the *real* level — for instance, the deep onto-epistemic incommensurability between traditional Chinese medicine and Modern medicine — exist, cannot be resolved by normative fiat, and have led and can lead to socio-political conflict if poorly handled. But Huntington's argument fails by *conflating* the three critical-realist levels. Defining civilizations geopolitically and treating the resulting boundaries as if they tracked real-level differences, he commits cultural essentialism (precisely Sen's critique, 2006), cultural determinism (precisely Said's critique, 2001), and an internal inconsistency that confuses onto-epistemic traditions with geographic or geopolitical entities. Yuk Hui's cosmotechnical question (2016) exposes the inadequacy of the geopolitical taxonomy at the real level: is the People's Republic of China's social-credit-and-surveillance complex Western or Eastern? The components circulating in it are real-level Modern in origin, even when they are mobilized against Modern hegemony. Real-level world-tensions are real; geopolitical maps are not their mirror. The thesis preserves Huntington's recognition of real-level incommensurability and discards his cartography. [VERIFY: Huntington 1996; Sen 2006 *Identity and Violence*; Said 2001 "The Clash of Ignorance"; Hui 2016 *The Question Concerning Technology in China*.]

What both Fukuyama and Huntington gesture at, and what neither names with the precision the thesis requires, is the *mechanism* by which the Modern mode's universalizing disposition has actually operated at planetary scale over the past five centuries. Component portability across worlds is empirically obvious: technologies, concepts, institutions, foods, and forms of writing have circulated across world-boundaries throughout recorded history. Yet portability is not frictionless transfer — and here the operating-system analogy of Chapter I reaches its sharpest point. To adopt a component from another operating system is to *re-embed* it in the receiving world's stratified ontology, and the cost of re-embedding scales with the ontological depth at which the imported component operates.

At the *empirical* level, components transfer with low conversion cost: a recipe, a tool, a textual genre can be incorporated with only superficial adjustment. At the *actual* level, conversion cost rises: an institution or practice (a parliamentary form, an examination system, a market mechanism) requires functional re-coding to operate within the recipient world's existing mechanisms. At the *real* level — the level of generative mechanisms, foundational ontologies, modes of artificiality as such — re-embedding is no longer adjustment but *replacement*: the recipient world's real is overwritten by the imposing world's real. *This is the ontological structure of colonization.* Colonization, on this reading, is not best understood as a contingent moral injury layered onto neutral cultural exchange. It is the name for what occurs when real-level imposition succeeds in displacing one world's generative mechanisms with another's. The fact that the colonizing operation has been morally evaluated, contested, resisted, and partially undone in various places does not change the analytic point: the *mechanism* of universalization, at its sharpest, operates at the real level, not the empirical or the actual.

This account of colonization, framed in critical-realist terms, performs two functions for the thesis. It names the structural mechanism by which the Modern mode has, over the past five centuries, become the dominant mode of artificiality at planetary scale — and thereby grounds the analytic claim of *dominance* in a way that does not collapse into mere description of military, economic, or political superiority. It also sets up the analytic frame that Chapter VI will need: the metaverse-stage developments examined in Chapters V and VI are, on the thesis's argument, an extension of this same real-level imposition mechanism, now operating not between human collectivities but between an emerging artificial background environment and the human-collective that constructed it. The metaverse, on the analysis to come, is what colonization looks like when the colonizer is an environmental apparatus rather than another human collectivity.

## §III.5 — Counterargument Engagement

Three counter-positions to the chapter's argument deserve explicit engagement. The thesis's response to each preserves what is correct in the objection while showing why the objection does not undermine the chapter's main claim.

*Universal Technicity.* The first counter-position holds that technicity is universal to the human condition — every human collectivity engages in tool-making, language-making, institution-building — and that singling out one mode of artificiality as having a disposition to universalize amounts to a category error. Either every mode universalizes (in which case "universalization" is not a distinguishing feature), or no mode does (in which case the term names nothing real). What is correct in this objection is the recognition that technicity is, indeed, universal: every human world is an operating system in the sense Chapter I developed, and every such system has organizing principles that direct the lives of its inhabitants. What the objection misses is the distinction between a mode of artificiality and its *disposition* relative to other modes. Most modes of artificiality, historically, have organized their relations with other modes through patterns of exchange, conflict, alliance, syncretism, and coexistence that do not require the displacement of the other mode's real-level organizing principles. The Modern mode's disposition is structurally distinct: it organizes its relations with other modes as if its own organizing principles were generic, and treats other modes' principles as local approximations or failures. This dispositional feature is what §III.3's fault-line analysis names; it is what §III.4's colonization-as-real-level-imposition account formalizes. Technicity is universal; *this disposition* is one mode's. [VERIFY: Ch-3-1 scaffold.]

*Internal Western Critique.* The second counter-position holds that the chapter's account of the Modern mode is already available within the Modern mode's own self-critical traditions — Frankfurt School critical theory, post-structuralism, decolonial theory, science studies — and that the thesis is therefore producing a redundant restatement of what these traditions have argued. What is correct in this objection is that significant resources for the thesis's own critique exist within the Modern mode's reflexive traditions, and that the chapter draws on those resources (Adorno and Horkheimer at §III.3; Said and Sen at §III.4; the Frankfurt School's broader engagement with Reason throughout). What the objection misses is the analytic move the thesis makes that those traditions, individually, do not. The internal Western critique tends to operate at the actual level — diagnosing specific failures of specific institutions, specific operations of specific power-structures — without articulating the real-level organizing principles that produce the actual-level failures. The thesis's contribution is to specify the structural-ontological level at which the disposition operates and to connect that level to the squeeze hypothesis the rest of the thesis develops. The internal Western traditions are sources, not redundancies.

*Positivism-of-Anti-Positivism.* The third counter-position holds that the chapter's account is itself a positivistic generalization across heterogeneous histories — that calling "the Modern mode" a real entity with a real disposition reproduces the same totalizing operation the chapter criticizes. What is correct in this objection is that the thesis must avoid hypostatizing a configuration that is, in fact, internally diverse, multiply-contested, and historically variable. What the objection misses is the analytic distinction between a *configuration* and a *culture*. The thesis does not claim that the Modern mode is a homogeneous culture, a unified bloc, or an essentializable entity. It claims that the Modern mode is a configuration — a specific arrangement of material, epistemic, and institutional organizing principles — whose dispositional features can be analytically characterized without flattening the diversity of its instances. Hui's cosmotechnical observation is instructive again: the components of the Modern mode circulate well beyond the geographical West, and the disposition the chapter names operates wherever those components have been adopted at the real level, whether in California, Shenzhen, Bangalore, or Sao Paulo. The thesis's analytic move is to track a configuration, not to police a culture. [VERIFY: Ch-3-7 v2 scaffold on methodological grounds; Hui 2016 cosmotechnical question.]

## §III.6 — Collapse as Historical Evidence: Failed Modes of Artificiality

The chapter closes with a brief recall of the empirical record introduced in §III.1: worlds rise and fall. The point is not to belabor cases that historians have already documented but to register the analytical claim that depends on those cases.

If modes of artificiality are adaptational operating systems, they are subject to the structural constraint that any adaptational strategy is subject to: under sufficient environmental pressure, an operating system can fail to maintain its complexity, fail to reproduce its organizing principles, fail to sustain the conditions on which its inhabitants depend. The historical record across thousands of years shows that this has happened to many worlds. The reasons are typically multiple — ecological exhaustion, demographic shock, climatic shift, internal contradiction, external imposition, the conjunction of several at once. Tainter (1988) reads this record in terms of the diminishing returns on complexity-maintenance; Diamond (2005) reads it in terms of environmental constraints and policy choices; Brook (2023) reads the Ming case in terms of the Little Ice Age and the system's failure to absorb the climatic shock; the paleontological record offers the longer view, with successive mass extinctions documenting the same structural pattern at the species rather than the world scale. [VERIFY: Tainter 1988 *The Collapse of Complex Societies*; Diamond 2005 *Collapse*; Brook 2023 *The Price of Collapse*; supplemented by Schwartz & Nichols 2021 *After Collapse* for the recovery-trajectory question.]

The relevance for the present chapter is twofold. First, it grounds the chapter's claim of world-plurality in empirical evidence that worlds *are* mortal: the type persists, the instances do not. Second — and more important for the thesis as a whole — it situates the Modern mode of artificiality as one historically specific configuration whose persistence is not guaranteed by its current dominance, and whose failure modes are themselves the subject of the analytic apparatus the thesis develops. The Modern mode's universalization has, over five centuries, made the question of *its* collapse the question of a *planetary* condition rather than a regional one. The squeeze hypothesis of Chapter VI returns to this point.

A note on what this section does *not* do, and why. The Ch-3-8 scaffold develops a fuller analysis of collapse-as-evidence, integrating Tainter's complexity-maintenance framework with Holling's adaptive cycle, Perrow's normal-accidents theory, and Schwartz and Nichols's regeneration-base concept, and proposes that the Modern mode at its metaverse stage exhibits a structurally distinctive collapse-prospect (the regeneration-base is destroyed in advance through the convergent mechanisms named in Chapters IV and V). For reasons of chapter coherence and length, the full development is reserved for a future paper; the present section's brief recall of the record is sufficient for the chapter's argumentative needs. ## §III.7 — Closing and Forward Reference

The chapter has argued that the Modern mode of artificiality is one mode among many, and that its current dominance rests on a specific configuration of material conditions, epistemic dispositions, and universalization mechanisms whose internal logic the next two chapters will examine in detail. The material conditions are the production-mode foundations Needham, Pomeranz, and Miura have traced. The epistemic disposition is the Platonic-Aristotelian fault line Bhaskar's analysis has named. The universalization mechanism is the real-level imposition that critical-realist colonization-theory has formalized. These three operate together: the material conditions enable global reach; the epistemic disposition organizes how the reach is intellectually justified; the imposition mechanism is what the disposition actually does when it meets other modes.

What the chapter has not addressed, and what the next chapter must take up, is the question of where the Modern mode's universalizing disposition leads *internally* — what happens to the inhabitants of the mode whose disposition has, over five centuries, succeeded at planetary scale. Chapter IV examines the Japanese philosopher Takahide Uegaki's analysis of the pursuit of *life-as-willed* (意のままになる生) and the self-finishing tendency this pursuit produces. Chapter V then examines the parallel tendency at the level of artificiality itself: the self-completion of the apparatus. Chapter VI shows how these internal tendencies, taken together with the universalization mechanism of the present chapter, produce the structural condition the thesis calls the squeeze.
