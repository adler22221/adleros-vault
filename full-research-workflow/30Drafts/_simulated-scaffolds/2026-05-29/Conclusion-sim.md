## §0 — What the Thesis Argued

The thesis began with an emerging concept — *metaverse* — and a problem: the mainstream definitions of the metaverse, organized around its actualized technological specimens, suffer from path dependency on an arbitrary origin (Stephenson's 1992 fictional usage), disciplinary reductionism (the reduction of the metaverse to extended-reality systems and their adjacent technologies), and actualistic inquiry (the constraint of analysis to what has currently been actualized, leaving the structural-dispositional level unaddressed). The thesis proposed a different approach: to characterize the metaverse at the *real-level dispositions or causal powers* — the structural-environmental features that any technically distinct implementation of the same kind of background environment would need to exhibit. The thesis's body chapters carried out this characterization.

Chapter I argued that worlds — including those the metaverse-stage developments are transforming — are *adaptational operating systems*: bounded sub-environments by which a human collectivity mediates its existence within the universe-substrate. Five working criteria specify what makes an operating system an operating system in this sense, and the niche-construction record across the tree of life grounds the framework empirically. Chapter II argued that artificiality has three intertwined aspects — technology as exosomatization, virtuality as causally efficacious nonmaterial component, and the irreducibility of artificial-environment laws to universe laws — and grounded the irreducibility-claim in Polanyi's dual-control thesis. The chapter opened on Brague's observation that the nomos has always been tekhne: that even the customary ordering by which a community lives is among the bounded artefacts the community sustains.

Chapter III argued that the *Modern mode of artificiality* — the configuration whose historical lineage runs from the Mediterranean of antiquity through early-modern Europe to global Modern dominance — has a structural disposition to universalize itself and overwrite the organizing principles of other modes. The disposition operates through a material basis (the production-mode shifts Needham, Pomeranz, and Miura have traced), an epistemic apparatus (the Platonic-Aristotelian fault line Bhaskar's analysis names), and a real-level imposition mechanism (the critical-realist account of colonization). The plurality of worlds — Sinosphere, Islamic, Pacific, Indigenous, and other modes — is the empirical anchor against which the Modern mode's universalization is analytically characterized.

Chapter IV examined, through Uegaki's analysis, the internal trajectory of the Modern mode at the level of the inhabitant: the pursuit of *life-as-willed* (意のままになる生), the self-finishing tendency this pursuit produces, and the dilution of relationality and desomatization that follow. Chapter V examined the parallel trajectory at the level of artificiality itself: the self-completion of the apparatus, articulated through Brague's tekhne-becoming-phusis formulation, instantiated in the four minimally sufficient technologies (AI, big data, IoT, code-as-law), and operating through three real-level mechanisms (Lessig's architectural regulation, Hui's cosmotechnical mediation, Stiegler's retention-protention coupling). Stiegler's grammatization and three-stage proletarianization, alongside the dividuation-versus-transindividuation contrast, articulated the human-side correlates.

Chapter VI stated the *squeeze hypothesis* structurally: the condition in which the world is jointly subject to two incommensurable background environments — the universe-side and the apparatus-side — neither of which can be ignored without survival cost. The structural conditions F1 (both sides changing dynamically) and F2 (incommensurable laws) jointly constitute the squeeze. The Anthropocene record provides the species-scale precedent; the prior human squeezes (Obara, Stiegler, Frankfurt, Illich) provide the anthropological-experiential precursors; the metaverse-stage squeeze proper exhibits three empirical signs (psychosomatic, livelihood, governance). Illich's radical-monopoly and Taleb's fragility-transfer frameworks deepen the analysis. The four-cell typology of constraint and the symmetric/asymmetric distinction differentiate the metaverse-stage squeeze from prior squeezes structurally. The productive-dimension argument refuses the trade-off framing and the pathology-vs-recognition framing, articulating the structural-exchange-via-bidirectional-coupling framing and the recognition-and-bidirectional-reform implication.

## §1 — The Thesis's Structural-Ontological Contribution

The emerging metaverse and the evidence of the squeeze between it and the universe present us with a new perspective on understanding artificiality's threat to humanity. The thesis's central analytic claim is that this threat operates at a *structural-ontological level* beneath the registers at which the techno-social predicaments are typically diagnosed. The squeeze is not a phenomenon at the level of governance failure (the Montesquieuan register), nor at the level of ownership concentration (the Marxian register), nor at the level of liberty constraint (the Mill-Berlinian register). The squeeze is a structural-environmental condition that obtains when the world is jointly subject to two incommensurable background environments, and the registers at which the predicaments are typically diagnosed are downstream consequences of, or symptomatic expressions of, the structural-environmental condition.

This claim has implications for how the contemporary moment is analyzed and how response strategies are evaluated. The conventional registers each capture real features of the situation, and the conventional response strategies each address real symptoms. What the conventional registers do not capture, and what the thesis specifies, is the structural condition that produces the symptoms across all the registers simultaneously. The thesis does not replace the conventional analytical and prescriptive registers; it specifies the structural level beneath which those registers operate. The Montesquieuan strategy of institutional check-and-balance is meaningful; it operates on the level of governance under the structural condition the squeeze names. The Marxian strategy of ownership reconfiguration is meaningful; it operates on the level of ownership under the same structural condition. The Mill-Berlinian strategy of liberty preservation is meaningful; it operates on the level of liberty under the same structural condition. The thesis's contribution is to specify the structural-environmental level at which the conditions for all three strategies' effective application are located, and to articulate what is structurally distinctive about the contemporary condition under which the registers' typical analyses and prescriptions must operate.

The squeeze, as the thesis has articulated it, is also not a Sci-Fi scenario. While the thesis does not rule out the possibility of dramatic scenarios — AI gaining sentience, outsmarting humans, and replacing or dominating or killing us all — such anthropomorphism is not necessary for artificiality's threat. The alternative the thesis has shown is that the evolutionary or survival pressure imposed by artificiality — which is gaining its operational independence and becoming another background environment we must adapt to — is *already happening* and is *sufficient* for the squeezing of our world and lives in between. The squeeze does not require that the apparatus *complete* its self-completion; the squeeze is a causally efficacious mechanism already operative under conditions in which the apparatus's self-completion is on a trajectory of structural emergence. This is the precise sense in which the thesis answers the committee's "fully actualized?" question: the squeeze obtains in graded form *now*, and the structural mechanism is causally efficacious *now*, in advance of the full actualization that the apparatus's self-completion would eventually produce.

## §2 — Distinguishing from Three Mainstream Traditions on the Problem of Technology

The Introduction set up the distinction between the thesis's contribution and the three mainstream traditions for analyzing the techno-social predicaments. The Conclusion brings the distinction to its closing articulation, now with the body chapters' analyses in place.

*The Montesquieuan tradition: governance through check-and-balance.* The tradition that runs from Montesquieu's *Spirit of Laws* (1748) through contemporary liberal-democratic constitutional theory analyzes the problem of technology as a problem of governance failure when the institutional and infrastructural apparatus accumulates power not adequately checked by countervailing institutions. The contemporary expressions are visible in the European Union's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), the Digital Markets Act (DMA), the proposed AI Act, and the multilateral attempts to develop international AI-governance frameworks. The tradition's prescription — to design and reform institutions so that the apparatus is held accountable through check-and-balance — is meaningful and necessary. The tradition's limit, from the thesis's structural-environmental vantage, is that the institutional check-and-balance strategy presupposes the operational agency of a human-collective whose institutional reforms would address the apparatus's accumulation of power. The metaverse-stage condition the thesis has analyzed includes the structural shrinking of that operational agency itself: the inhabitants whose institutional capacity to reform the apparatus is the precondition for the Montesquieuan strategy are, on the thesis's analysis, the inhabitants whose self-finishing trajectory has thinned the very conditions for collective institutional reform. The Montesquieuan strategy requires institutional resources whose structural availability the thesis's analysis specifies as the condition that the squeeze itself partially undermines. [VERIFY: Montesquieu 1748; current Conclusion P0177 engages this in compressed form.]

*The Marxian tradition: ownership reconfiguration.* The tradition that runs from Marx's *Capital* (1867) through contemporary platform-political-economy analyses the problem of technology as a problem of ownership and exploitation: the means of artificial production are concentrated in private hands that extract surplus from producers; the response is to reconfigure ownership through collectivization, cooperativization, or commonification. Contemporary expressions include data cooperatives (Hardjono & Pentland, 2019), platform cooperatives, the political program for "digital commons," and the various proposals for collective ownership of AI infrastructure. The tradition's prescription is meaningful and necessary. The tradition's limit, from the thesis's structural-environmental vantage, is that the metaverse-stage's structural-environmental character produces effects on inhabitants that operate independently of who owns the apparatus. The architectural constraints work whether the platform is privately, publicly, or cooperatively owned; the dividuating governmentality operates on extracted data profiles regardless of who owns the data; Illich's radical monopoly is achieved environmentally rather than commercially, and ownership change does not alter the environmental character of the radical monopoly. The Marxian strategy addresses the ownership dimension; the squeeze obtains beneath the ownership dimension at the structural-environmental level. [VERIFY: Marx 1867; Hardjono & Pentland 2019.]

*The Mill-Berlinian tradition: liberty preservation.* The tradition that runs from John Stuart Mill's *On Liberty* (1859) through Isaiah Berlin's "Two Concepts of Liberty" (1958) to contemporary engagements with platform power and digital-rights analyzes the problem of technology as a problem of liberty's preservation against the encroachments of governmental, social, or institutional power. The tradition's distinction between *negative liberty* (freedom from interference) and *positive liberty* (freedom to act and self-realize) organizes the contemporary debate over digital rights, platform regulation, and the social conditions for liberty's exercise. Mill's worry about the tyranny of public opinion in mass democracies, and Berlin's worry about the totalitarian potential of positive-liberty discourse, both anticipate aspects of the contemporary condition. The tradition's prescription — to preserve and extend the conditions for liberty against the constraints — is meaningful and necessary. The tradition's limit, from the thesis's structural-environmental vantage, is that the metaverse-stage operates not by direct constraint on liberty but by structural reconfiguration of the conditions under which liberty itself can be exercised. The inhabitant of the self-finishing world is formally free in both Berlin's negative and positive senses — formally unconstrained in choices, formally enabled to self-realize through the apparatus — but the *structural conditions* for the kinds of liberty Mill defended (independent judgment, deliberative practices, lived collective traditions, the capacity for self-development through engagement with not-as-willed others) have been progressively thinned by the same self-finishing trajectory that produced the formal freedoms. The Mill-Berlinian strategy addresses the formal-liberty register; the structural-conditions register beneath it is where the squeeze obtains. [VERIFY: Mill 1859; Berlin 1958 — **NOT in current Zotero; acquisition required**.]

The thesis's positioning relative to the three traditions is not denunciatory. Each tradition captures real features of the contemporary moment; each tradition's prescription addresses real problems; the failures of the prescriptions to fully resolve the contemporary predicaments are not failures of the traditions themselves but consequences of the structural-environmental level operating beneath them, which the thesis's analysis articulates. The Conclusion's claim is that the structural-environmental level is *the level at which* the conditions for the three traditions' effective application are determined, and the thesis's distinct contribution is the articulation of that level. ## §3 — Future Research Directions: Toward Modes of Artificiality Otherwise

The relativity of the Modern mode of artificiality — the recognition that this configuration is one historically specific mode among others — offers us a space to meta-verse the emergence of the metaverse: to turn, to take a step back, to self-reflect. Would alternative modes of artificiality still be possible? How might we develop modes of artificiality that are not (as) totalizing, excluding, and self-completing? How might the trajectory of the Modern mode and its risks be mitigated, and how might its limitations be complemented by other modes of artificiality? Redirecting or circumventing the path dependence on the Modern mode is a formidable task; still, these questions may be the beginning of unfolding *other concrete possible worlds*.

The thesis does not prescribe the directions; it articulates the analytical apparatus through which the directions become legible. Six directions deserve naming.

*Appropriate technology.* Ivan Illich's framework of *tools for conviviality* (Illich, 1973) names the structural orientation toward technology that preserves the conditions for autonomous human action rather than displacing them: tools whose design parameters are limited by the criterion that they remain manageable by the inhabitants whose lives they shape. The framework was developed in the 1970s and has been substantially marginalized within the dominant technology-development discourses since; it remains a significant direction for thinking about technologies whose structural-environmental character does not produce the radical-monopoly and dividuation effects the thesis has tracked. The framework does not specify which technologies are convivial; it specifies the criterion (the second watershed beyond which the tool ceases to serve human conviviality) against which technologies are to be evaluated. Contemporary work on *appropriate technology*, *small technology*, and *de-growth-compatible technology* operates within this framework. [VERIFY: Illich 1973; current contemporary appropriate-technology literature.]

*Cosmotechnics of co-becoming and the concept of kyōsei (共生).* The cosmotechnical framework Yuk Hui has developed (Hui, 2016, 2019) names the unity, in any given mode of artificiality, between the cosmic order the mode presupposes and the technical operations the mode executes. The Modern mode's cosmotechnics — the unity between the universal-and-necessary cosmic order it presupposes and the mechanistic-interventionist technical operations it executes — has, on Hui's analysis, exhausted its productive possibilities and now operates as the apparatus the thesis has tracked. Alternative cosmotechnical orientations are visible across multiple cultural and philosophical traditions: the East Asian *kyōsei* (共生, *co-becoming* or *symbiosis*) framework that treats the human-cosmic relation as one of mutual sustenance rather than one-directional intervention; the various indigenous cosmotechnical traditions that organize technical operations around the maintenance of inter-species and inter-generational relations; the philosophical resources Japanese, Chinese, and other traditions provide for thinking technical operation as a participation in cosmic order rather than a mastery of it. The thesis does not develop these traditions; it locates them as resources for the directions toward which the analysis points.

*Plurality, in Audrey Tang's sense.* The framework Audrey Tang and E. Glen Weyl have developed under the heading of *Plurality* (Weyl, Tang, & Community, 2024) names a specifically digital-political-technical orientation toward the maintenance of meaningful diversity within and across digital infrastructures: collaborative technology and democracy organized around the preservation of multiple sites of agency, multiple sources of legitimacy, multiple registers of value, rather than the singular metric-and-algorithm convergence the dominant platform infrastructures embody. The framework is operationally articulated in Tang's work as Taiwan's Digital Minister and in the various civic-technology projects the Plurality movement has spawned. Its theoretical commitment — to the irreducible plurality of legitimate technical-political-social formations — aligns with the operating-system-pluralism the thesis has tracked across Chapter I and Chapter III; its operational program offers a concrete instance of the productive-dimension orientation Chapter VI named.

*Kōmoto Hideo's design of recovery and self-diversifying systems, recontextualized as positive niche construction.* In a Japanese-language essay that has not received the international visibility its analytical reach deserves, the philosopher 河本英夫 (Kōmoto Hideo) has developed the concept of *復興のデザイン* (*design of recovery*) under the heading of *自己多様化するシステム* (*self-diversifying systems*) (河本英夫, 2012). The concept names the design orientation toward systems that produce their own diversification — not merely tolerating diversity but generating it as the system's operational principle. The orientation can be productively recontextualized within the niche-construction framework Chapter I developed: where the Modern mode's niche-construction has tended toward singularization (the radical monopoly Illich names), the design-of-recovery orientation articulates a *positive niche construction* in which the system's design parameter is the multiplicity of niches it sustains. The recontextualization is the thesis's specific analytical move; the framework itself is Kōmoto's contribution. [VERIFY: 河本英夫 2012 *エコ・フィロソフィー研究 別冊* pp. 61-83.]

*The pluriverse movement.* Across Latin American decolonial thought (Escobar, 2015; Reiter, 2018), critical geography (Hope, 2021), and adjacent fields, the pluriverse framework articulates the recognition that the dominant Modern world is one configuration among others and that the alternatives are present in the lived practices of inheritances the Modern mode has marginalized but not eradicated. The pluriverse framework provides empirical anchoring for the operating-system-pluralism Chapter I articulated analytically; it also provides operational resources for thinking about how multiple modes of artificiality can coexist without one universalizing over the others. The thesis's account of colonization-as-real-level-imposition (Chapter III §III.4) names what the Modern mode has done; the pluriverse movement names what the conditions of recovery from that imposition look like. [VERIFY: Escobar 2015; Hope 2021; Reiter 2018.]

*Noodiversity and technodiversity.* Yuk Hui has developed, in his more recent work, the concepts of *noodiversity* (the diversity of minds and forms of thinking) and *technodiversity* (the diversity of technical systems and forms of doing) as the operative goods that pluriverse-oriented modes of artificiality preserve and the Modern mode's universalization actively destroys. The noodiversity/technodiversity framework names what is at stake in the universalization the thesis has tracked: not merely cultural variety as a sentimental value, but the operational resources for meaningful human inhabitance of the world that are progressively lost under the Modern mode's convergence onto a singular cosmotechnical order. The framework also names what the directions of future inquiry are oriented toward: the preservation and cultivation of noodiversity and technodiversity as the conditions for modes of artificiality otherwise. [VERIFY: Hui's noodiversity / technodiversity passages — locate specific Hui essays where these concepts are developed; *Recursivity and Contingency* 2019 may contain the relevant treatments.]

The six directions are not separate alternatives. They share an underlying commitment: the recognition that the planetary condition the thesis has analyzed is not the final form of human-collective inhabitance of the universe, and that the structural conditions for modes of artificiality otherwise are present and recoverable. The thesis's analytical contribution is to make the present condition legible; the future research directions name where the legibility, once achieved, points.

## §4 — Standing of Positions and the Limits of the Analytic

A methodological note belongs in the Conclusion. The thesis has drawn on a specific set of authors and frameworks — Bhaskar's critical realism, Stiegler's organology, Obara's evolutionary anthropology, Uegaki's analysis of life-as-willed, Hui's cosmotechnics, Brague's history of the universe, Illich's tools for conviviality, Taleb's fragility analysis, the niche-construction theorists' biological framework, the critical-theoretic and decolonial traditions through which the Modern mode's structural features become legible — without claiming that this set is exhaustive or that each position is uncontested within its own field. Each position the thesis relies on is one position among contested alternatives; the thesis's choices reflect what each framework permits the structural argument to articulate, not a comprehensive defense of the framework against its critics.

This methodological honesty matters for two reasons. First, the analytical apparatus the thesis has assembled is not the only apparatus that could have been assembled; alternative configurations of frameworks would have produced alternative articulations of the structural condition the thesis names. The thesis's contribution is the specific analytical articulation, not the claim that no other articulation is possible. Second, the analytic capacities of the apparatus have limits. The squeeze hypothesis articulates a structural condition; it does not predict specific outcomes; it does not prescribe specific responses; it does not exhaust the contemporary condition. The body chapters have, where the analytical apparatus reached its limits, flagged the questions and reserved them — the prospective collapse question (Chapter VI §VI.8), the productive-dimension articulation of specific reform directions (Chapter VI §VI.7 + this Conclusion §3), the relations between the structural-ontological argument and the registers of governance, ownership, and liberty (this Conclusion §2). The limits are part of the contribution: an analytic that did not flag its limits would falsely claim a completeness the contemporary condition's complexity does not permit. ## §5 — Closing: The Nonlinear Future and the Pharmakon

The nonlinear nature of open systems prevents us from ascertaining the future. The trajectories the thesis has tracked across Chapters IV, V, and VI are real and structural, but their continuation is not deterministic. The squeeze obtains structurally now; whether it reaches the collapse endpoint Chapter VI §VI.8 signaled depends on contingencies the thesis cannot resolve. What we know is that whenever we entrust some functionality to artificiality, we are reducing our portion of functionality in the world; and the way we deal with this perennial tradeoff — or, in Stiegler's reading of the older Greek vocabulary, *pharmakon*, the medicine that is at the same time a poison — will dynamically form the emergence of our future. The pharmakon is not a single substance with predetermined effects; the dosage, the practitioner, the patient, the conditions of administration all condition whether the medicine cures or poisons. The thesis's analysis articulates the structural conditions under which the contemporary pharmakon — the apparatus the Modern mode has built and continues to build — is administered. What follows from the analysis is the recognition that the conditions of administration are not fixed; they can be reorganized; the bidirectional reform Chapter VI §VI.7 named is the form that reorganization, if it is to occur, must take.

The thesis does not predict whether the reorganization will occur, nor at what scale or speed. It articulates the structural condition that makes the question legible. The question is, in the end, the inheritance the thesis hands forward: whether the structural-environmental conditions of the contemporary moment can be recognized as the structural-environmental conditions that they are, and whether the recognition can become the precondition for collective reorganization of the bidirectional coupling between the human and the apparatus that, on the thesis's analysis, has produced both the achievements of the Modern mode and the structural costs the squeeze diagnoses.

The work the thesis has done is the structural articulation. The work that remains — and that the future research directions of §3 name — is the collective work of inhabiting the recognition the structural articulation makes possible. That work belongs to inhabitants whose lives and worlds will, whether the structural condition is recognized or not, continue to unfold. The thesis offers what it can offer: an analytic that, if the recognition occurs, makes the work somewhat more legible.
