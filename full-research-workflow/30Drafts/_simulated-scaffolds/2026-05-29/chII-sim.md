## §II.0 — Tekhne as Artificiality: The Greek Inheritance the Chapter Recovers

The previous chapter argued that worlds are adaptational operating systems — bounded sub-environments that mediate a population's collective survival within the universe. What this chapter must now do is unpack the *kind* of bounded sub-environment a world is, and the *kind* of operations it sustains. The shortest way into the argument is through a Greek vocabulary the modern languages have only partly inherited.

For the Greeks, two terms bracketed the kinds of things that exist. *Phusis* named the realm of what grows of itself, what comes into being according to its own internal principle — what we now translate, imperfectly, as "nature." *Tekhne* named the realm of what is made — the bounded artefact and the embodied skill that produces it, the rule-governed practice and the institution that organizes it. Between these two poles stood a third term, *nomos*: the custom, the law, the conventional ordering by which a community lives. The Greek philosophical tradition spent itself working out the relations among these three. As Rémi Brague has shown in his study of the Western experience of the universe, the philosophical achievement of antiquity was to *separate* phusis from nomos, granting the cosmos an existence and a structure that did not depend on the polis ([VERIFY: Brague 2003, *The Wisdom of the World*, p.14] — the passage at p.14 concerning *nomos as tekhne* should be transcribed verbatim before final insertion). What Brague's analysis discloses, however, is that the philosophical separation itself rests on a prior recognition: *nomos has always been tekhne*. The custom by which a community lives, even when it is presented as eternal and divinely sanctioned, is something a community makes; the law is one of the bounded artefacts by which a people sustains itself within a background environment whose laws it does not make. The nomos has always been tekhne. [AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD] [VERIFY: Brague 2003: 14]

I take this Greek inheritance as the entry point for the present chapter for a specific reason. The thesis's central concept of *world* is, when stripped to its bones, a generalization of the nomos: a bounded ordering by which a population lives in a universe whose laws it does not make. The thesis's central concept of *artificiality* is, in the same way, a generalization of the tekhne: the bounded artefacts — material and nonmaterial alike — by which that ordering is constituted, transmitted, and adapted. The chapter's task is to show that artificiality, so understood, has three intertwined aspects each of which the existing literature has tended to name in isolation, and that taken together these three aspects ground the irreducibility-claim that the previous chapter advanced as the third criterion of any adaptational operating system. The reason for entering the chapter through the Greek vocabulary is that the recognition Brague names — nomos as tekhne — is precisely what subsequent traditions have struggled to recover. Modern reductionist accounts of social institutions repeatedly attempt to dissolve the nomos either into the phusis of natural law or into the mere convention of arbitrary choice. The thesis's position, which I begin to argue here and complete in §IIC, is that the nomos is neither; it is tekhne, and a tekhne irreducible to the materials and laws of the universe in which it operates.

To unpack how our worlds have become artificial environments aimed at — but not necessarily ensuring, as the next chapter will show — the better survival of our species within the universe, three aspects are key to understanding artificiality's role in forming a world: technology as the exteriorized organ that extends our biological capacities; virtuality as the causally efficacious nonmaterial component that coordinates social interaction and meaning-making; and the irreducibility of the laws governing these artificial environments to the laws of the universe that supports them.

## §II.1 — Technology as Exosomatization

The first aspect of artificiality is the *impetus* for developing artificial aids as extensions or enhancements of our biological functions, compensating for our weaknesses or vulnerabilities. Since the dawn of history, tools and processes of writing have served as the extension or enhancement of our memories; weapons have served the same for our blunt body and weak biological force (Stiegler, 1998). The biophysicist Alfred Lotka, in his 1945 essay "The Law of Evolution as a Maximal Principle," named this adaptation through artificial aids *exosomatic evolution* or *exosomatization*, contrasting it with the *endosomatic* evolution by which all other animals adapt through genetic mutation. The distinction is foundational. Endosomatic evolution operates within the boundary of the individual organism's body and accumulates only through reproductive transmission; exosomatic evolution operates *outside* the individual body — in tools, techniques, written records, institutions — and accumulates through forms of inheritance that do not require genetic transmission. Lotka recognized this as a difference in kind, not merely degree: humans had crossed a threshold beyond which the rate of adaptive change exceeded what biological evolution alone could deliver.

While extending or enhancing human capabilities, these artificial aids also influence humans in return. The need to master and live with artificial tools and processes has become a new kind of adaptational pressure toward this new, ever-emerging artificial environment — the self-domestication that Hideo Obara identifies as the watershed marker of human existence (小原秀雄, 2000). The affordances of these artificial aids — the range of possible usages, applications, or ways of interacting with them — also direct human emotions, thoughts, behaviors, and interactions. With new emotions, thoughts, behaviors, and interactions, new needs arise and call for new aids. The exosomatic loop closes on itself: artificiality both extends what we can do and shapes who we become in doing it.

Bernard Stiegler (1998), in *Technics and Time 1: The Fault of Epimetheus*, sees this ever-exteriorization of our organs and memories as the very nature of technology — or, on the other side of the same coin, technology as our indispensable organ. We are constituted, on Stiegler's account, not as bare biological beings who subsequently adopt tools, but as prosthetic beings from the start: there is no "natural" human anterior to the technical milieu in which the human form took shape. According to Stiegler, this process of exosomatization, while unable to change the laws of the universe, *localizes* them: it "locally trans-forms the cosmic order in unpredictable ways" (Stiegler, 2018: 42). This, in turn, tends to form *techno-geographical milieus* — a concept similar to Obara's artificial ecosystems, which are environments dependent on, but not reducible to, the universe.

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

There is a deeper layer here that Stiegler's framework allows us to recover, and that the existing prose of this chapter has so far gestured at but not unpacked. Stiegler distinguishes three orders of retention that together constitute the temporal structure of human experience. *Primary retention* is the holding of the immediately past in living perception — the still-vibrating note that lets the next note be heard as part of a melody. *Secondary retention* is individual memory, the recollection of what one has experienced, the inner archive that lets the past inform the present without being immediately perceived. *Tertiary retention* is the technical exteriorization of memory itself — the cave painting, the written record, the photograph, the recording, the database, the algorithm. Tertiary retention is what permits one generation to inherit the experience of generations it never met. It is what makes cumulative culture possible. And — this is the load-bearing point for the thesis's later chapters — tertiary retention is what subsequent industrialization can capture, organize, and commodify in ways that no other order of retention permits. [VERIFY: Stiegler 1998 *Technics and Time 1*; Stiegler 2018 *Neganthropocene*, pp. 42 + tertiary-retention sections]

Tertiary retention is, in the present chapter's vocabulary, exosomatized memory: the inheritance of experience itself outside the individual organism, into the bounded artefacts and institutions that constitute the technical milieu. It is the mechanism by which exosomatic evolution — Lotka's term — actually operates at the level of cumulative culture. It is also the mechanism by which the metaverse-stage developments of later chapters will become possible: when tertiary retention becomes industrially organized at planetary scale and algorithmically managed by systems that do not themselves inherit, the conditions of cumulative culture begin to shift in ways the present chapter only signals. The full development of that shift is deferred to Chapter V. What matters here is that exosomatization, properly understood, is not merely a story about prostheses extending the reach of the biological body. It is a story about the *temporal* exteriorization of memory itself, the externalization of inheritance, the migration of culture into the technical milieu.

This temporal dimension marks the first major departure from accounts of technology that treat artificiality as a value-neutral instrument. Artificiality, on the exosomatic account, is not an instrument the human uses; it is one of the conditions that constitutes what the human is. The point is not romantic — it is structural. To the extent that exosomatization has been the species' mode of evolutionary adaptation since paleolithic times, our species was always already coupled to the technical milieu we constructed. The squeeze argument the thesis develops in later chapters takes this coupling seriously: when the technical milieu shifts in its operative character, the species that is constitutively coupled to it must shift too, or the coupling produces survival pressures the species was not equipped to anticipate.

## §II.2 — Virtuality as Causally Efficacious Nonmaterial Artificiality

While it might be easy to picture how tangible material technologies have extensively reshaped our worlds — from cave- and stone-based tribes to AI-powered "smart cities" — artificiality is not always manifested in material forms. The nonmaterial forms of artificiality are indispensable for the development of material counterparts; the two dialectically lead to each other. As Yuval Noah Harari reminds us, artificially created nonmaterial things such as gods, money, and laws have the power to direct our thoughts and behaviors or even elicit emotions or biological reflexes (e.g., fight-or-flight responses). Without these nonmaterial things or aspects that facilitate the interactions or dynamics among components of a world, that world will not be possible — material things would just be aggregates without being integrated into functioning systems.

I call this causally efficacious nonmaterial aspect of artificiality *virtuality*.

This is different from the popular view of the term, which sees a virtual thing as a simulated replica of something real — possessing certain characteristics or features of the simulated real thing yet not being completely real. This simulationist view can be traced back to C. S. Peirce's definition (1902): "a virtual x (where x is a common noun) is something, not an x, which has the efficiency (*virtus*) of an x" (emphasis added). While my usage carries on the "efficiency (virtus)" aspect in Peirce's definition — which comes from its Latin etymon *virtus* (i.e., virtue) and *virtualis* (i.e., power or potency) — I believe the "as if" aspect in his definition is unhelpful. As Jean Baudrillard shows in *Simulacra and Simulation* (1994), many artificial things have no original or real counterparts to represent whatsoever. Seeing a virtual x as a simulation of the real x overlooks the fact that many virtual things are *novel*, and a virtual x may be original in its own right. Without a radical departure from this view, we cannot make sense of an emerging future in which we are increasingly incapable of finding the real represented or simulated by the virtual.

My view also differs from another popular view of the virtual, used colloquially nowadays and championed by David Chalmers (2022): the view of something virtual as *computer-based*. To avoid the pitfall of the illusory or fictitious implications of the previous view of the virtual as *as if*, Chalmers reasserted this recent colloquial view to emphasize realness — guaranteed by existence, causal efficacy, mind-independence, non-illusoriness, and genuineness of digital objects in computer systems. What Chalmers's substitution gains in precision against the simulationist view, it loses in historical scope: it treats a contingent feature of present-day virtuality (its computer-based instantiation) as criterial.

What Chalmers overlooks is that what he considers computer-based is no more than a temporal medium of a long historical continuum. As Massimo Durante points out (2022), humans have always dealt with the virtual ever since humans lived by the stories they collectively invented. Initially, humans told stories of gods and practiced ceremonies in the hope of bettering their lives in the vastly unknown and unpredictable universe. These orally communicated gods and embodied ceremonies formed our worlds' nonmaterial yet causally efficacious basis. Over time, in many places, gods have been replaced by science(s) and ceremonies by laws — documented and conveyed by writing. Nowadays, we are increasingly receiving guidance from AI and regulated by algorithms through computer-based interfaces and objects. Some day in the future, what we now call virtual, augmented, or mixed reality might no longer be computer-based but biologically programmed and powered. By then, the biologically programmed and powered applications that a person interacts with directly in their eyes will cease to be virtual if we follow Chalmers's definition, but still relevant if we follow mine.

Viewing virtuality as causally efficacious nonmaterial artificiality helps us navigate the ever-blurring boundaries between computer-based and non-computer-based reality. As Philip Brey notes importantly (2003), while "rocks and trees" or "screwdrivers and chairs" can only be *simulated* in digital environments, "money can literally exist" there. Money — although increasingly losing its materiality as the digital has become the default mode of existence in our time — we do not call the nonmaterial money in our banks "virtual money." Money is still money, and money has always been virtual. Shells, grain, salt, metal, paper, or digits are merely temporal mediums to operationalize money's virtuality.

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD] What I want to extract from this engagement with Peirce, Baudrillard, Chalmers, Brey, and Durante is a precise structural claim that the next subsection will need: a virtual thing is one whose causal efficacy in a human world is not reducible to the material composition of its substrate. Money in shells, money in metal, money in paper, money in a database entry — each medium is different; the money is the same. The money's causal efficacy in coordinating exchange, settling debts, motivating labor, and structuring inequality does not depend on which medium instantiates it. The medium is a condition of the money's operation, not the explanation of its efficacy. This is precisely the formal structure that Polanyi's dual-control thesis names at a more abstract level, and that the next subsection takes up.

Before turning to that argument, one further observation. The *Modernity* that Chapter III will examine has a specific relation to virtuality that is not yet visible in this chapter's general account. In all known human worlds prior to a certain stage of Western developmental trajectory, virtuality and materiality co-developed in a more or less integrated fashion: the gods inhabited the trees, the laws were embodied in the persons of judges, money was a particular kind of object that was always both material and virtual. What the Modern mode of artificiality begins to do — and what the metaverse stage of that mode, examined in Chapter V, completes — is to *separate* the virtual from the material substrate of any particular instance, treating virtuality as something that can be lifted from one substrate and reinstalled on another. This separability is the structural innovation that makes the metaverse possible, but its preconditions are already present in the Modern world: in double-entry bookkeeping, in printed money, in legal abstraction, in the bureaucratic processing of social relations as case files. The Modern mode of artificiality is, among other things, a mode that systematically prefers separable virtuality over substrate-bound virtuality. The deeper historical argument for that claim is reserved for Chapter III.

## §II.3 — Laws of Artificial Environments Are Not Reducible to Those of the Universe

The first two aspects — exosomatized technology, causally efficacious virtuality — bring us to the third, which is the chapter's load-bearing claim. The laws of artificial environments are not reducible to those of the universe in which those environments operate. In other words, the artificial environments have laws of their own.

This claim is implicit throughout the preceding sections and explicit in the third criterion of an adaptational operating system stated in Chapter I. What this section provides is the philosophical-biological warrant for the claim, drawing on Michael Polanyi's argument for the dual control of complex systems.

Polanyi's argument can be stated as follows. A complex system organized hierarchically — say, a machine, a living cell, a sentence, a social institution — is subject to two controlling orders simultaneously. It is controlled, first, by the laws governing the elements at the lower level taken in themselves: the physics of materials in a machine, the chemistry of reactions in a cell, the phonetics of sounds in a sentence, the psychology of individuals in an institution. It is also controlled, second, by the principles operating at the higher level — the design of the machine, the organization of the cell, the grammar of the sentence, the institutional rules of the social organization — and these higher-level principles are not derivable from the lower-level laws. Polanyi states the structural claim with characteristic precision: "Each level is subject to dual control: (i) control in accordance with the laws that apply to its elements in themselves, and (ii) control in accordance with the laws of the powers that control the comprehensive entity formed by these elements" [VERIFY: Polanyi 1968 "Life's Irreducible Structure" *Science* 160 — exact page; the user has also marked *The Tacit Dimension* 1966 as a candidate source, both contain the dual-control argument].

The mechanism by which dual control is possible is what Polanyi calls the *boundary condition*. "Such multiple control is made possible by the fact that the principles governing the isolated particulars of a lower level leave indeterminate conditions to be controlled by a higher principle" [VERIFY: same passage]. Consider a machine. The parts of the machine — gears, levers, circuits — obey the laws of physics. But the physical laws governing the parts do not specify which configurations of those parts will perform what function. A gear can rotate clockwise or counterclockwise; physics is indifferent to the direction. The design of the machine — the higher-level principle — selects one direction as the functional one and arranges the parts so that the physically-permitted indeterminate degrees of freedom in the parts are *closed* by the design. The design imposes a *boundary condition* on the lower-level physics. The physics remains the substrate; the design remains irreducible to that substrate.

Polanyi extends the argument through the full hierarchy of natural and human realities. "Each level relies for its operations on all the levels below it. Each reduces the scope of the one immediately below it by imposing on it a boundary that harnesses it to the service of the next higher level, and this control is transmitted stage by stage, down to the basic inanimate level" [VERIFY: same passage]. From physics to chemistry to biology to psychology to sociology to culture: at each step, the higher-level organizing principle harnesses the lower without being reducible to it. The biological function of a cell is not derivable from the chemistry of its molecules; the meaning of a sentence is not derivable from the phonetics of its sounds; the institutional structure of a society is not derivable from the psychology of its members.

The argument extends to artificial environments by the same logic. An artificial environment — a world, in the sense Chapter I developed — is a hierarchically organized complex system whose operating principles harness the universe-level laws (gravitation, thermodynamics, biology) on which its inhabitants depend, without being reducible to those laws. The materials and the bodies remain subject to universe-level laws; the *organizing principles* of the artificial environment are not. The chair I am sitting on obeys the universe's laws of statics — but whether I am the *right kind of person* to sit on this particular chair, in this particular social setting, depends on organizing principles that statics cannot specify. The grammar of a language obeys nothing in the larynx or the air-pressure waves it produces; the structure of the parliamentary procedure obeys nothing in the biology of the legislators; the constitution of money obeys nothing in the chemistry of paper or the physics of silicon. In each case, the universe-level laws are the substrate that *enables* the operation of the artificial environment; the artificial environment's *own* laws are the boundary conditions that *use* the substrate without being derivable from it.

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD] The use-lessness of many material objects in digital environments — which the previous section's discussion of screwdrivers and chairs in computer-based environments illustrated — exemplifies another key feature of artificial environments. While artificial environments are dependent and constrained by the materiality and laws of the universe, they are not reducible to the latter. In other words, they have laws of their own. In purely biological terms, the physically fittest person has the best odds of surviving in the wilderness, yet in a world — an artificial environment — this may no longer be the case. A physically strong individual might have a higher mortality rate as they are designated to the role of hunting, war, or heavy labor, while a physically unfit person might utilize their charisma or wit to concentrate power and resources, which in turn benefits their survival. Despite all humans being subject to the materiality and laws of the universe, the local materiality and laws of the artificial environments — namely, the mode of artificiality, or worlds — that direct their members' adaptation or domesticate them are not reducible to the former.

The irreducibility claim has limits that the literature has explored and that this section ought to acknowledge briefly. Polanyi's own framework was developed primarily with biological and physico-chemical hierarchies in mind, and his treatment of supra-organismal systems — the social, the cultural, the institutional — is less developed than his treatment of organisms and machines. There are at least three places where the analogy from biology to artificial environments must be handled carefully. First, the higher level can interfere with the lower in ways that violate the dual-control logic — genetic modification is the clearest contemporary case in biology; intentional reshaping of physical substrates to better serve artificial-environment laws is the artificial-environment analogue. Second, Polanyi tends to focus on the individual organism as the relevant case, and the move to populations or collectivities introduces complications about how boundary conditions are reproduced across individuals. Third, the boundary conditions of an artificial environment are not, in any straightforward sense, *given*: they are made, transmitted, contested, and sometimes broken. None of these complications undermines the core claim; all of them constrain how the claim should be cashed out in particular applications. The thesis takes the core claim — irreducibility of artificial-environment laws to universe laws — as the load-bearing premise; the complications are returned to in later chapters as they become argument-relevant.

To close the section's argument, I want to mark a feature of artificiality that the previous two sections have prepared but only this section can name precisely. Artificiality is double-edged. The same exosomatization that extends our reach also produces dependency on the extensions; the same virtuality that coordinates social life also constrains what kinds of social life become possible; the same irreducibility that grants worlds their integrity also produces the incommensurability between worlds that Chapter I's fourth criterion named. Ivan Illich, writing in *Tools for Conviviality* (1973), called this double-edged structure the *threshold* character of technology: every technology has two watersheds, one beyond which it ceases to serve human conviviality and begins to organize humans for its own perpetuation. Bernard Stiegler, drawing on the older Greek vocabulary, calls the same structure *pharmakon*: every artificial means is at once a remedy and a poison, a curative and a venom, depending on the dosage, the practitioner, and the patient ([VERIFY: Stiegler 2018 *Neganthropocene*]; cf. Illich [VERIFY: 1973 *Tools for Conviviality*, the "two watersheds" passage]).

The pharmakon framing is more than rhetorically convenient. It captures the structural-ontological feature of artificiality that the chapter has been working toward: the irreducibility of artificial-environment laws to universe laws does not mean that artificiality is autonomous from the universe, nor that its effects are neutral. The artificial environment depends on the universe for its substrate at every moment; its effects on inhabitants are real precisely *because* of that dependence. What the irreducibility-claim establishes is that the *direction* of those effects — toward survival or toward maladaptation, toward conviviality or toward coercion, toward extension or toward dependency — is not determined by universe-level laws and cannot be predicted from them. The direction is set by the artificial environment's own organizing principles. Which is why understanding *which* organizing principles are operative in a given mode of artificiality, and *how* those principles shape the adaptational pressures on the population that inhabits the world, becomes the question the rest of the thesis pursues.

The next chapter examines one specific mode of artificiality whose organizing principles have, over the past several centuries, exhibited a peculiar tendency: the tendency to universalize itself and to exclude or overwrite the organizing principles of other modes of artificiality with which it has come into contact. The chapter that follows develops that case.
