# Chapter V — Self-Completion of Artificiality and the Emergence of the Metaverse

## §V.1 — From Self-Finishing to Self-Completion: The Brague Inheritance Continues

Chapter II opened on a passage from Rémi Brague's study of the universe in Western thought: the recognition that the nomos has always been tekhne — that the customary ordering by which a community lives is among the bounded artefacts the community sustains. The recognition grounded Chapter II's account of artificiality. The present chapter takes up the second half of the same inheritance. If the nomos has always been tekhne, what is now occurring under the Modern mode's self-completion trajectory is something Brague named with equal precision: *the tekhne is now becoming phusis* — the made is becoming the given, the artefact is becoming the background environment, the constructed is becoming the substrate to which subsequent constructing must respond. [VERIFY: Brague 2003 *The Wisdom of the World*, p. 14 — the tekhne-becoming-phusis passage; the user has emphasized that this is the second movement of the same Brague chapter from which §II.0 draws the nomos-as-tekhne formulation.]

The phrase requires careful unpacking, since it can sound metaphorical when it is in fact structural. *Phusis*, in the Greek vocabulary Chapter II recovered, names what grows of itself, what comes into being according to its own internal principle, what was *not* made by the community within which it operates. The universe — gravitation, thermodynamics, biology, climate — is phusis in the Greek sense: the background environment to which a community must adapt because the community did not make it and cannot directly remake it. *Tekhne*, by contrast, is what the community makes: the bounded artefact, the embodied skill, the rule-governed practice, the institution. The classical separation between phusis and tekhne organized millennia of philosophical and practical thought: humans dwell in a phusis they did not make and construct a tekhne by which they adapt to that phusis.

What is now occurring under the Modern mode's self-completion trajectory is the *crossing* of this classical distinction. The technical apparatus — the institutional, infrastructural, informational, computational complex that the Modern mode has built and continues to build — is acquiring the structural features of phusis. It is operating according to its own internal principles, principles whose generation is no longer traceable to any human-collective decision. It is forming a background environment to which subsequent human action must respond, rather than a foreground artefact that human action makes and remakes. It has, in the precise structural sense, *become*, or is becoming, *phusis* — not metaphorically, not poetically, but as a matter of the kind of relation the human inhabitant must take to it.

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

This is the formulation Chapter VI will sharpen into the squeeze hypothesis. The squeeze obtains when a human collectivity is jointly subject to two incommensurable background environments — the universe-phusis that has always been the substrate, and the apparatus-phusis that is in the process of completing itself. The present chapter examines the apparatus side of this dialectic: how the apparatus comes to operate as a phusis-like background environment, and what becomes structurally possible at the stage of self-completion. The previous chapter examined the human side: the self-finishing tendency that entrusts more and more of human functionality to the apparatus. The two sides are dialectically coupled, as Chapter IV concluded: human self-finishing enables apparatus self-completion, and apparatus self-completion enables further human self-finishing. The squeeze is what the coupling produces when both sides reach the stage at which the apparatus has become sufficiently phusis-like that the human inhabitant must adapt to it as to an environment, rather than directing it as to a tool.

Building upon Uegaki's analysis of self-finishing, I see this tendency emerging in parallel with the self-finishing of human life: the *self-completion* (自足完成化) of artificiality. If the routine operation and maintenance of artificiality are considered "soul-sucking" or "alienating" — hindrances to the actualization of one's life-as-willed — the self-finishing tendency of human life will strive to make artificiality self-complete, capable of operating and maintaining itself on its own. On the flip side of the same coin, as humans increasingly entrust their tasks and responsibilities to artificiality, the *portion of functionality* (役割) humans play in their world also shrinks. This shrinkage of human's portion of functionality is exacerbated by the dilution of relationality and the desomatization of life examined in Chapter IV: decreased social cohesion and solidarity reduce sheer human capacity at the collective level, whereas decreased physicality reduces it at the individual level. Such shrinkage, in turn, reinforces the growth of artificiality's portion of functionality in a world.

## §V.2 — Minimally Sufficient Technologies for Self-Completion

Despite artificiality being yet to be self-complete, the minimally sufficient technologies for such self-completion are already emerging. These technologies at least include:

*Artificial intelligence (AI)* — an artificially created system that is capable of making decisions on its own.

*Big data* — the aggregate of exponentially growing information collected from almost every aspect of every single individual's day-to-day life.

*Internet of Things (IoT)* — a network of everyday objects that enables data collection, communication, and operation by and throughout the network.

*Code is law* (Lessig, 2006) — codes that function as laws without the need for third-party jurisdictions, unstoppable once executed by pre-programmed algorithms.

In Bhaskar's language, big data is becoming the new autonomized sense-experience that feeds AI as the new autonomized philosophy. This synthesis of the historically antagonistic yet dialectically interdependent Platonic-Aristotelian fault line seems to emerge as a reified version of the Hegelian Spirit or the Nietzschean Will — an all-encompassing whole that subsumes all experiences and dynamically directs collective progress. For such dynamic directing of collective progress to occur, this directing whole needs the means of control over materials and social behaviors. When connected to autonomized big data and AI, the IoT can provide the material infrastructure for the former to control anything connected to it. On the other hand, code is law can serve as the virtual shaping and propelling force under the command of the autonomized dual.

If we improve these technologies and make them work together seamlessly, we can reasonably expect the following cybernetic system to become a possibility: every object that people use has become *smart*, meaning that it is part of the IoT; IoT automates the operation of objects and collects data from all interactions with them; big data collected by the IoT is aggregated and delivered to AI, which utilizes such an enormous amount of data to make decisions; the decisions made by AI are programmed into codes-as-law that have regulatory and commanding power; codes-as-law, in turn, direct the behaviors of AI and all things in the IoT.

If humans cease to — whether voluntarily or involuntarily — participate in the operation and decision-making process in a cybernetic system like this, humans will become a mere source of data, and have our lives directed by AI, regulated by codes, and have our thought and behavioral patterns bounded by the affordances of IoT. If this becomes the case, such artificiality can no longer be considered merely a critical component of our world as a human-made suitable habitat that supports our survival in the universe as a greater background environment in which we are situated. Rather, it has become another background environment comparable to the universe — we reside in it, are not in charge of its workings, and must adapt to survive.

This emerging artificial background environment, I propose, is what the *metaverse* should be understood to name: a realm (-verse) that emerges from the human attempt to go beyond the given (meta-) and turns into (-verse) a self-referential (meta-) underlying order (meta-) (Oxford English Dictionary Online, 2022).

## §V.3 — The Architecture of the Metaverse: Lessig, Hui, and Retention-Protention

The four minimally sufficient technologies of §V.2 are the *actual-level* historical instantiation of the metaverse. The *real-level* mechanisms — the structural-architectural features that any technically distinct implementation of the same kind of background environment would need to exhibit — can be specified more precisely. Three real-level mechanisms together constitute what the thesis calls the architecture of the metaverse. Each is medium-independent in principle; the contemporary technologies happen to instantiate them, but the real-level claim is structural.

*The architecture-as-regulation mechanism.* The first real-level feature is the structural property identified by Lawrence Lessig and generalized: any sufficiently rich architectural mediation can operate as a regulator on both physical and virtual constraints, *automatically*, without enforcement parties. Walls regulate physical movement; code regulates digital action; the structural form is the same. As Lessig writes, *architectural constraints work whether or not the subject knows they are working* (Lessig, 2006: 344) — the constraint operates by determining what is possible, not by being followed as a rule. In the metaverse-stage, this architectural-regulation mechanism crosses the physical-virtual divide that prior architectures held separate. Smart locks implement code-as-law in physical space; automated traffic systems coordinate physical movement via algorithmic decision; predictive risk-scoring in criminal justice operates code-as-law in legal-institutional space; algorithmic credit allocation operates code-as-law in financial space. The architectural constraint is everywhere, and everywhere it operates automatically. [VERIFY: Lessig 2006 *Code: And Other Laws of Cyberspace, Version 2.0*, p. 344 and related passages.]

*The cosmotechnical-mediation mechanism.* The second real-level feature is the property Yuk Hui has identified under the heading of *cosmotechnics*: the unity, in any given mode of artificiality, between the cosmic order the mode presupposes and the technical operations the mode executes (Hui, 2016). The metaverse-stage cosmotechnical mediation is distinctive in that its operative cosmic order is no longer one inherited from a specific cultural tradition; it is the order of computational decidability itself, the order according to which what can be represented, manipulated, and exchanged within the apparatus has the structure of computable information. The metaverse's cosmotechnical mediation generalizes the computational order to all domains of life — relational, embodied, economic, political — by rendering each domain into computable terms. The rendering is the cosmotechnical move; the computational order is the operative cosmos. [VERIFY: Hui 2016 *Question Concerning Technology in China*; *Recursivity and Contingency* 2019 for the recursive-contingency framing.]

*The retention-protention coupling.* The third real-level feature draws on the phenomenological vocabulary of internal time-consciousness. Husserl's analysis of temporal experience identifies *retention* as the structure by which the immediately past is held in present perception, and *protention* as the structure by which the immediately future is anticipated. Stiegler's extension, developed under the heading of tertiary retention (Chapter II §IV.1), names the technical exteriorization of retention itself — the apparatus's holding of past data, the algorithmic anticipation of future behavior. The metaverse-stage couples technical retention (massive data collection) with technical protention (predictive algorithmic decision) into a single closed-loop dynamic: the past is captured and held; the held past trains the predictive apparatus; the predictive apparatus anticipates the future and prepares the apparatus's response; the response is enacted; the enactment is captured as new past data; the loop closes. The closed loop is the temporal architecture of the metaverse; it is what permits the apparatus to operate as a phusis-like background environment, since the loop's operation does not require human decision at any point along the cycle. [VERIFY: Husserl on retention/protention; Stiegler 1998 *Technics and Time 1*; Stiegler 2018 on industrialized tertiary retention.]

The three real-level mechanisms — architecture-as-regulation, cosmotechnical-mediation, retention-protention coupling — together constitute the structural conditions for the metaverse's emergence as a phusis-like background environment. Each is necessary; together they are sufficient. The four minimally sufficient technologies of §V.2 (AI, big data, IoT, code-as-law) are the contemporary actual-level instantiation; alternative actual-level instantiations are conceivable but would need to satisfy the same three real-level features. The thesis's account of the metaverse is, in this respect, *substrate-neutral*: the metaverse-stage is defined by the real-level architectural features, not by the specific technologies that currently realize them. This substrate-neutrality is important for the squeeze hypothesis Chapter VI develops, since the hypothesis must remain valid under technological developments the thesis cannot foresee.

## §V.4 — Stiegler's Grammatization and the Shrinking Human

Across the trajectory by which the apparatus comes to operate as a phusis-like background environment, a parallel process operates on the human side. Bernard Stiegler, drawing on the linguistic vocabulary of grammar, names this process *grammatization*: the progressive discretization of continuous human flows — speech, gesture, emotion, attention, judgment — into calculable units that the apparatus can store, process, and operate on. Grammatization is older than the metaverse stage; the alphabet was already a grammatization of speech, double-entry bookkeeping was already a grammatization of commercial activity, the chronometer was already a grammatization of time. What is distinctive about the metaverse-stage grammatization is its *scope*: it extends to domains of human flow that prior grammatization had left in continuous form. Affect is grammatized into measured emotional response. Attention is grammatized into engagement metrics. Relational labor is grammatized into communication-platform interaction. Judgment is grammatized into algorithmic recommendation acceptance or rejection. [VERIFY: Stiegler 2010 *For a New Critique of Political Economy*; Stiegler 2018 *Neganthropocene*.]

Grammatization, Stiegler argues, produces a *three-stage generalized proletarianization* whose stages mark successive losses of human capacity to the apparatus. *Loss of savoir-faire* (knowing-how, manual deskilling) is the first stage: industrialization grammatized the artisan's hand-skills into machine-executable operations, leaving the worker as the machine's appendage rather than the practice's bearer. *Loss of savoir-vivre* (knowing-how-to-live, the deskilling of cultural and existential practice) is the second stage: twentieth-century mass culture grammatized the practices of family life, leisure, neighborliness, and moral-emotional response into commodified standard products, leaving the consumer as the consumer of practices rather than the practitioner of them. *Loss of savoir-théoriser* (knowing-how-to-theorize, the automation of intellectual labor) is the emerging third stage: contemporary AI is grammatizing the practices of analysis, synthesis, articulation, and judgment that intellectual labor had reserved to itself, leaving the analyst as the verifier of machine output rather than the producer of analysis. [VERIFY: Stiegler 2010, 2018; *States of Shock* 2015 also relevant.]

The three-stage proletarianization is the human-side correlate of the apparatus's self-completion. Each stage transfers a domain of human capacity to the apparatus; each transfer enables the apparatus to operate more autonomously in that domain; each enabling permits further transfers. The cumulative effect is what the thesis names the *shrinking human*: the inhabitant of the metaverse-stage whose portion of functionality in the world has progressively diminished, not because of any specific imposed deprivation but because of the cumulative consequences of the self-finishing axiology playing out over multiple generations. The shrinking is real and structural; it is also experienced, from the inhabitant's lived perspective, as comfort, as opportunity, as the disappearance of certain kinds of burden. The lived experience does not contradict the structural diagnosis; the two are complementary.

## §V.5 — Algorithmic Governmentality and the Transindividuation Counter

The third register in which the metaverse-stage's effects on human life can be specified is that of *governmentality*: the operations by which the apparatus organizes the conduct of populations whose individual subjects are addressed as data profiles rather than as deliberating persons. Following Foucault's framework, governmentality names not the use of force on subjects but the structuration of the field of possible actions within which subjects act, the indirect organization of conduct through the configuration of environments rather than the direct imposition of commands. *Algorithmic governmentality*, the term Antoinette Rouvroy and Thomas Berns have developed for the metaverse-stage variant (Rouvroy & Berns, 2013), names the operation of governmentality through algorithmic processing of behavioral data, without the mediation of explicit norms, deliberative subjects, or institutional discourse. [VERIFY: Rouvroy & Berns 2013 "Algorithmic Governmentality and Prospects of Emancipation."]

Algorithmic governmentality addresses subjects not as individuals but as *dividuals* — data fragments that can be assembled, recombined, and operated on without ever requiring the unification of the data subject as a deliberating person. Deleuze's brief but influential discussion of "control societies" introduced this vocabulary (Deleuze, 1992); Stiegler develops it as the precise dynamic the metaverse-stage extends. The dividuation operates below the level at which classical political subjectivity is constituted: the dividuated subject is not a citizen, not a worker, not a deliberating moral agent, but a profile that can be predicted, primed, sorted, served, or denied without the subject's deliberation entering the loop.

[AI-SIMULATED-SCAFFOLD]

Against algorithmic governmentality's dividuation, Stiegler proposes the counter-concept of *transindividuation*: the psychic-and-collective individuation by which subjects come to themselves through shared technical-symbolic mediation. Drawing on Gilbert Simondon's 1958 work on individuation, transindividuation names the structural condition under which individual psychic life and collective social life develop *through* one another — neither prior to the other, both produced through the technical milieu that mediates them. The contrast with dividuation is precise. Dividuation operates on extracted data profiles below the level at which psychic-and-collective individuation is constituted; transindividuation, where it occurs, operates at and above that level by sustaining the conditions of inheritance, deliberation, and meaning-making across generations. [VERIFY: Simondon 1958/2020 *L'individuation à la lumière des notions de forme et d'information*; Stiegler's late-period works 2015, 2018, 2020 on transindividuation as the prescriptive counter to dividuation.]

The contrast is not a slogan against a counter-slogan but a structural difference at the level of operative time-horizons and aggregation-scales. Algorithmic governmentality operates at the time-horizon of the data-cycle (seconds to days) and at the scale of the individual profile, never aggregating into the longer-term, deliberative, intergenerationally-inherited structures within which psychic-and-collective individuation occurs. Transindividuation, by contrast, requires the longer time-horizons of cultural retention and the deliberative-relational scales at which psychic-collective formations consolidate. Stiegler's prescriptive register, where it appears in his late work, is the call to develop technical and institutional forms that *sustain* the transindividuating function under conditions where the dividuating apparatus has captured most of the technical milieu. The call is real and the dynamic it names is structural; whether the contemporary technical-institutional landscape contains sufficient resources to sustain transindividuation against an increasingly dominant dividuating apparatus is an open question the thesis registers without resolving.

What the dividuation-versus-transindividuation distinction makes available, for the thesis's argument, is a precise specification of one of the dimensions along which the metaverse-stage's effects on inhabitants are operative. The shrinking human of §V.4 is not merely deskilled in any specific domain; the shrinking human is dividuated — addressed by the apparatus at the sub-individuation level — and to that extent partly excluded from the conditions under which psychic-and-collective individuation has historically been sustained. The squeeze hypothesis the next chapter develops will draw on this specification: the universe-side of the squeeze includes the inherited conditions for psychic-and-collective individuation that the dividuating apparatus, structurally, cannot reproduce.

## §V.6 — The Bridge: Toward the Squeeze

The chapter has tracked, through three registers — Lessig's architecture-as-regulation, Hui's cosmotechnical mediation, Stiegler's retention-protention coupling and grammatization, the dividuation-versus-transindividuation contrast — the structural conditions under which the apparatus comes to operate as a phusis-like background environment. The minimally sufficient technologies are emerging; the real-level mechanisms are operative; the human-side correlates are visible. The metaverse-stage is not, in the thesis's account, a future to be predicted; it is a present condition whose structural character can be specified now.

What remains for Chapter VI is to articulate what the structural condition the present chapter has named *means* for the inhabitants of the world it is transforming. The world, in Chapter I's vocabulary, is the adaptational operating system the human collectivity has constructed within the universe. When the artificial apparatus the world has built becomes phusis-like — when the tekhne becomes phusis — the world finds itself between two background environments: the universe-phusis it has always inhabited, and the apparatus-phusis it has produced. Both background environments operate by their own laws; both impose adaptational pressures on the inhabitant; their laws are not commensurable in any direct sense, since they have different generative principles and different selection criteria. The inhabitant is squeezed between them. The squeeze hypothesis, articulated at structural-ontological precision, is the subject of Chapter VI.
