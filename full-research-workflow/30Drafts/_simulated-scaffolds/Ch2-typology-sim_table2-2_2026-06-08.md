# A Typology of the Made

The two distinctions I have drawn so far — the register in which an artificiality compels, conventional or structural, and the manner in which it works, through material arrangement or through virtuality — do not merely describe two unrelated facts about the made. Crossed against each other they generate a single map, a typology with four cells, into which every artificiality I will treat in the rest of the thesis can be placed. I set the typology out here, in its own right, because it is the apparatus the later chapters will lean on most heavily, and because the map repays scrutiny: it shows not merely *that* the world is irreducible to the universe but *how* the irreducibility varies, and it locates, in one of its corners, the precise configuration the whole thesis is built to reach. The two axes apply across the board — material *and* nonmaterial alike are sorted by register, structural *and* conventional alike are sorted by manner of working — so that the grid is genuinely a two-by-two and not a list of special cases. Table 2-2 sets it out, and the paragraphs that follow read it across, down, and into depth.

The columns separate the two manners of working. A *material* artificiality compels by its physical substrate: it borrows the universe's own matter and runs on the universe's own physics, foreclosing or affording not by shape and mass alone but by the whole range of physical principles its build puts into play — aerodynamics, thermodynamics, friction, leverage, conductivity, and countless more — so that to obey a material artificiality is at the same time to obey the universe's own laws operating through it. A *nonmaterial* artificiality compels by meaning rather than by any such physics — it works through the causally efficacious nonmaterial aspect of the made that I have called virtuality, a status or rule or relation that does real work in the world without being any of the matter through which it works.[^meaning-semiotic] "Meaning" here I take in a deliberately broad *semiotic* key — meaning as sign-relation, the operation of and over signs in roughly the sense of Peirce's general theory of the sign — not in any narrowly human or linguistic one. The semiotic is the genus, and it has a living register and a non-living one. Its living register is the *biosemiotic*, which I take in Jakob von Uexküll's sense: the sign-relations out of which an organism's surrounding-world (*Umwelt*) is built, the carriers of meaning (*Bedeutungsträger*) by which a creature's world is furnished and addressed. I invoke the biosemiotic for one precise purpose — to de-anthropomorphize meaning at the level of the living: meaning is not the proprietary achievement of human minds, for the tick's world is woven of it no less than ours, and it is not reducible to the universe's own *real* and *actual*. The same physical stem is a path to the ant, building material to the cicada larva, and feed to the cow, and what differs across these is not the matter but the meaning impressed upon it. The semiotic also has a non-living register, and it is there that the formal occupants belong: mathematics, code, algorithms, and Stiegler's tertiary protention are sign-systems and operations over signs, not living *Umwelten*. They are semiotic — they are not biosemiotic. What unites the living and the non-living registers, and what matters for the column, is that both work by sign and relation rather than by mass and force. It is in this register — the semiotic — that nonmaterial artificiality does its work, which is why it belongs to the field of virtuality rather than to the physics of the substrate. I state the point plainly for its hardest-looking cases: mathematics, code, algorithms, and tertiary protention compel as sign-systems, not as physical principles — they are formal, technical occupants of the semiotic, and that is precisely why they fall in the nonmaterial column and not in the physics of the substrate.[^semiotic-occupants] The rows separate the two registers. In the *conventional* register the made holds the way *nomos* holds: it compels because, and only so long as, humans keep enacting it — sustained by enforcement, by sanction, by belief, and lapsing the moment the enacting practice lapses. In the *structural* register the made compels not because anyone keeps accepting it but because it has been built into the arrangement of the environment itself, so that it conditions action whether or not it is acknowledged; here violation is not deterred but foreclosed, removed from the space of available actions rather than punished after the fact. Crossing the two axes yields four cells. Cell 1 is material and structural: the steam engine, the wall, the lock, the server and its devices — a made physical form that forecloses by its very build. Cell 2 is material and conventional: the fork that calls for etiquette, the ceremonial object, the vehicle answerable to a traffic-rule — a material thing whose force lies not in its substance but in a use-norm that humans must keep enacting around it. Cell 3 is nonmaterial and conventional: speech, fallible knowledge, the gods, money, the institution, customary law, narrative, the scientific paradigm — pure made-meaning, sustained in being by nothing but collective enactment. Cell 4 is nonmaterial and structural: the formal order — mathematics, codified grammar, the algorithm — and, at its limit, code-as-law and artificial intelligence, made-meaning that has been built into operation so that it binds without an enforcer.

The single most important thing the table shows is not visible in the cells taken one at a time but in the path that runs *through* them, from the bottom-left of the nonmaterial column to the top-right — for the conventional and the structural are not a partition but the two ends of a continuum, and the made migrates along it. The pure case of the conventional sits at one extreme: spontaneous human speech, the institutions, the gods, the money, the customary law of Cell 3 — each compels only by being kept up, and each may at any moment be violated, defied, or simply allowed to lapse. The first step toward the structural is taken by *codified grammar*. Once a grammar has been written down and established, it is followed without anyone enforcing it — no constable stands over a sentence — and in this it has begun to harden out of the purely conventional; yet it remains, decisively, *violable*: one can break it, and be understood breaking it, which is why I would file it within Cell 3 as a *conventional-becoming-structural* case rather than promote it across the line. (I label this case a′ rather than give it a new letter precisely to mark that it is a developed *form of* the conventional — a-prime, a stage within (a) — and not a fresh category alongside it; the lettering tracks the migration continuum itself, a conventional → a′ conventional-becoming-structural → … → the structural cells, so that the prime records movement *within* a register rather than a crossing out of it.) The next step is taken by *mathematical formulas and algorithms*. These too are followed without enforcement, but they differ from grammar in a way that matters: they *cannot be violated* — there is no breaking the rule that two and two make four, only erring in the attempt to follow it — and so they have crossed into the structural register and belong in Cell 4. And yet they are *structural-still-conventional*: their structurality is real but their origin is conventional, a human formalism, and, crucially, they *do not propel themselves*. A formula sits inert until a human (or a machine a human has set running) takes it up; it constrains without compelling, foreclosing error without forcing anything to happen. The continuum's far end is reached only by *code-as-law and artificial intelligence*. Here the made-meaning does not merely refuse violation; it *propels and limits against contingency* on its own — the locked transaction that will not execute, the model that decides and acts where no operator intervenes. It is essential, at just this point, not to mistake *structural* for *deterministic*. To compel structurally is to bind by the arrangement of the situation rather than by ongoing enactment; it is not to fix every outcome in advance. There are forces that propel and limit without determining — that narrow the field of the possible, weight it, foreclose some paths and incline toward others, yet leave the particular issue underdetermined — and artificial intelligence is the exemplary case. A generative or probabilistic model does not compute a single fated output the way a falling stone computes its trajectory; it is non-deterministic in operation, its products contingent on sampling, on training data, on a prompt it could not have foreseen. And yet it is, in the strongest sense, structurally compelling: it propels action and bounds it, against contingency, on its own initiative, without any party keeping it up. Non-determinism, then, is no objection to placing it in the structural register; it is precisely the kind of structural compulsion the metaverse stage introduces — propulsion-and-limitation without fixed determination.

Yuk Hui's account of *preemption* names this combination exactly. The algorithmic system does not coerce by foreclosing choice outright; it appears, on the contrary, to offer choices — and that is the point. "Such a determination is always preemptive," Hui writes, "in the sense that the machine has already anticipated what the options will be: In this case, freedom means choice"; what looks like an open field of options is in truth "a reduction of the contingent to the most probable," the menu itself already drawn up by the structural conditions of the algorithms and their training data.[^hui-preemption] The choices are real, and they are non-deterministic — which option the user takes is not fixed in advance — yet the *space* of choice has been pre-structured by the recursive evaluation of past data that "anticipate[s] the future, which in turn determines the present." This is structural compulsion that works *through* apparent freedom rather than against it, and it is why I locate code-as-law and AI at the far, real-compelling end of the continuum: they propel and limit against contingency precisely by pre-empting the field within which contingency would otherwise play. This is the *real-compelling* occupant of Cell 4, and it is genuinely new. It is what I name, with a term the later chapters will carry, *self-completion* (自足完成化, *jiko kanseika*, "the made completing itself"): the made acquiring, at the level of its enduring organizing principles, the capacity to drive and bound action against the contingent — and it is here, and only here, that the provocation "technē & nomos becoming phusis" earns its keep, for this is the cell that was empty until the metaverse stage and that the metaverse fully occupies. The conjunction is deliberate: the structural-nonmaterial corner takes in both the made-*technical* (the formalism, the model, the executing system) and the made-*normative* (code-as-law, the smart contract that *is* the rule it enforces), so that what migrates into the structural register is at once technē and *nomos* — code-as-law is exactly *nomos* becoming phusis, the made norm acquiring the unanswerable bite of the given.

Reading the typology in depth — across its three layers rather than its four cells — guards against a confusion that the migration just traced makes tempting, and it is here that the stratification does its sharpest work. Take Cell 1's lock. At the layer of *context-specific phenomena* the lock is this bolt, thrown now, in this door. At the layer of *structures or dynamics* it is the locked door as a standing arrangement — the configured constraint that forecloses passage. But what compels at the deepest layer, the layer of *enduring conditions or principles*, is not the door at all; it is the law beneath the structure — the rigidity of the metal, the friction and resistance that no act of will can talk its way past, the universe's own organizing principles to which the door merely gives a human shape. The structure (the locked door) is the *arrangement*; the *law* — the organizing conditions and principles that make the arrangement bite — is the real beneath it. The same threefold reading disciplines the new cell. For code-as-law it is essential not to mistake the layer at which the novelty lies. A particular artificial intelligence, a particular smart contract, is a *structure or dynamic* — a configuration, one made thing among others, which could have been built otherwise. What is genuinely new sits one layer deeper: the *gaining* of a real-level capacity to forcibly propel and limit against contingency, the organizing principle that any such configuration now embodies. Keeping the configuration (a structure) distinct from the capacity (an enduring principle) is what lets me say that the metaverse occupies a cell the formal sciences never did, without the absurdity of claiming that this or that particular algorithm is itself the deep principle. The kind — "the structural-nonmaterial that compels at the level of its organizing principles, against contingency" — is the enduring feature; any given system is only its passing instance.

Two cautions keep the typology from hardening into something it is not, and both are needed before the later chapters put it to work. The first is that the cells name a dominant tendency, not an absolute partition: almost every real artificiality mixes registers, and the honest way to read the table is as a field of gravitational centres rather than a set of sealed boxes. The railway makes the mixing plain. Its rails, its electrical circuits, its signalling interlocks belong to the *structural* register — they foreclose, by their build, what a train can physically do, and a points-lock will not pass a train onto an occupied line whatever the driver intends. But its design specifications, its operating protocols, its published timetable belong to the *conventional* register — they hold by being enacted, are revisable in principle, and can be, and routinely are, departed from. A railway is neither a structural nor a conventional artificiality; it is a working composite of both, and to ask which cell "the railway" occupies is to ask the wrong question of the table. The second caution concerns the continuum itself: the migration from convention toward structure is a directional tendency the later chapters will track, not a ladder every artificiality is fated to climb. Codified grammar need not become a formula, nor a formula become code; the point of arranging the cases along a single line is to make legible the *drift* — the steady conversion of what was negotiable into what is merely operative — that I will argue defines the metaverse stage, in which the conventional margin shrinks and the structural-nonmaterial, real-compelling corner fills out. That corner, the deepest cell of the grid, is where the made comes to foreclose with the unanswerable authority of the given while remaining wholly, visibly authored — as hard as a cliff and as made as a promise — and the account of what it is to live squeezed inside it is the work of the chapters to come.
