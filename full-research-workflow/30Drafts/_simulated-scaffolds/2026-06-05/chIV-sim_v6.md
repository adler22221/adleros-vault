# Chapter IV — The Inward Drive of the Universalizing Controllability Mode

> **Scaffold note (read first).** Chapter III examined how the mode *expands*: how, as one actual world among many, it overwrites the organizing conditions of the worlds it meets and carries its own made world as though that world were the given universe. This chapter turns *inward*: it asks how the same mode drives the internal dynamics of the world it has made — what it does to its members, their others, and their bodies. The argument proceeds dialectically, through two diagnoses of the *same* mode that read it in opposite valences: Adorno and Horkheimer, who see only its darkness, and Uegaki Takahide, who lets us see its brightness without illusion. I take Habermas as the hinge between them. The chapter's own claim is that neither valence is the truth of the mode; both are *actual-empirical manifestations* of one *real* mechanism — universalizing controllability — whose final form is the metaverse, and whose terminus is the squeeze.

---

## §IV.1 — A view from its darkness: Adorno and Horkheimer

The most unsparing account of what the mode does to its own world is the oldest of the three this chapter will use. In *Dialectic of Enlightenment*, Horkheimer and Adorno give the inward drive a single name: **self-preservation** (*Selbsterhaltung*). For them this is not one motive among others but the constitutive principle of reason as such. "Self-preservation is the constitutive principle of science, the soul of the table of categories" (DoE, p.68); reason is "the organ of calculation, of planning" whose "principles are those of self-preservation" (p.65). They locate the maxim at the head of the whole tradition, in Spinoza's *conatus*: the "endeavour of preserving oneself is the first and only basis of virtue," which they call "the true maxim of all Western civilization" (p.22).

From this principle the dark account unfolds in three movements.

First, **the man/nature divide makes reason instrumental.** What human beings "seek to learn from nature is how to use it to dominate wholly both it and human beings. Nothing else counts" (p.2). Once nature is disenchanted into a field of mere objects — "industrialism makes souls into things" (p.21) — reason is reduced to the calculation of means for mastering it. The price of dominating outer nature is the domination of inner nature: the history of civilization becomes "the history of the introversion of sacrifice — in other words, the history of renunciation" (p.43), the bourgeois self mutilating its own impulses to survive (the bound Odysseus).

Second, **instrumental reason totalizes and congeals into a second nature.** The drive to render everything calculable admits no remainder — "enlightenment is totalitarian as only a system can be" (p.4) — because "nothing is allowed to remain outside, since the mere idea of the 'outside' is the real source of fear" (p.11). What the made order then produces is a *zweite Natur*: the historically-produced confronts its makers as a fate-like, natural-seeming totality. Adorno's *Naturgeschichte* names exactly this — the moment at which "nothing [is left] outside any more, nothing unaffected by mediation, which is total," and "nature turns into an irresistible parable of imprisonment" (*Negative Dialectics*, pp.354–361). The made becomes the given; enlightenment "reverts to mythology" (DoE, p.xv). `[Scaffold: this is the dark-valence statement of the thesis's own tekhnē→phusis / second-nature claim; cross-ref §IV.4 and Ch V.]`

Third, **the second nature escapes its makers and turns against the self it was to preserve.** Self-preservation, absolutized, "proved to be a destructive natural force no longer distinguishable from self-destruction. The two principles combined in a murky fusion" (p.71). The reified totality, having "made the control of internal and external nature the absolute purpose of life" (p.24), destroys the very subject in whose service it was undertaken.

### The genuine limitation of the dark view

The power of this account is also its defect. To make self-preservation the *whole* of enlightenment reason, A&H must read the entire canon — not only the empiricists and political realists (Hobbes's terror of violent death; Locke's preservation-of-property), not only the scientific program (Bacon's "knowledge is power"), but the rationalists and even the idealist zenith — as variations on one engine. Spinoza's *conatus*, Descartes' reduction of nature to *res extensa* to be calculated and mastered, and even Kant's categorical imperative (the autonomous subject ruthlessly suppressing its inner nature; the first *Critique*'s synthesis as the cognitive mirror of a society subsuming nature) are, for them, the same self-preservation in different dress. `[VERIFY: the Kant reading — DoE Excursus II "Juliette" + Adorno, Problems of Moral Philosophy — and a Descartes locus; T0 pending. The whole-canon scope is the user's NLM-confirmed point; see §IV.2.]`

This is a *monism*, and its cost is precisely what the thesis cannot give up. By essentializing enlightenment and reason to their dark, **instrumental** side, A&H cannot account for the flourishing the enlightenment has *genuinely* pursued and, in part, *genuinely* brought. If reason is nothing but domination, then the freedoms, equalities, and forms of autonomy and conviviality that modern people actually enjoy must be illusion — and so must the critique that says so. The dark view sees the second nature truly; it cannot see the brightness that the second nature also, and in the same motion, delivers.

## §IV.2 — The hinge: Habermas `[PLACEHOLDER — verify primary page-refs before prose; see [[ch4-habermas-bridge-AH-uegaki-NLM-2026-06-05]]]`

> **Scaffold note.** This section is the *bridge* between the two valences and is drafted from NLM-retrieved Habermas passages that currently reach Habermas *through* secondary literature (Zuidervaart, Cook, Wilson, Hearfield). **Do not set to prose until the primary *Philosophical Discourse of Modernity* / *Theory of Communicative Action* page-references are confirmed.** The argument, however, is structurally load-bearing and is sketched here.

Habermas gives the limitation of §IV.1 its name. A&H, he argues, "expand instrumental reason into a category of the world-historical process of civilization as a whole… project[ing] the process of reification back behind the capitalist beginnings of the modern age into the very beginnings of hominization" (*TCA* 1:366) — reducing the whole of history to "a reason that is instrumentalized for the purpose of self-preservation, which is posed as an absolute end" (*TCA* 1:380). This is the monism named from outside: Habermas confirms that A&H's self-preservation runs through the *entire* canon. And the totalization is self-undermining — A&H "fall into a 'performative contradiction': they use reason to critique the ideological corruption of all reason" (*PDM* 127/154), an "ideology critique that outstrips itself."

Habermas's rescue is the hinge into the brightness. A&H reach their pessimism, he holds, "due to their one-sided view" of reason: they "failed to pursue the inner logics of different complexes of rationality" (*TCA* 1:382) and so missed the dimension of reason that is not domination at all — **communicative rationality**, oriented to mutual understanding rather than to mastery. *Dialectic of Enlightenment* "does not do justice to the progressive potential latent within modern rationality." In short: the genuine value the dark view cannot see is real, and it has its own logic.

But Habermas also supplies the dark thread's continuation into the present, and this is what makes him the bridge to Uegaki specifically. His diagnosis of modernity is the **colonization of the lifeworld** (*Kolonialisierung der Lebenswelt*): "the rationalization of the lifeworld makes possible a heightening of systemic complexity, which becomes so hypertrophied that it unleashes system imperatives that burst the capacity of the life-world they instrumentalize" (*TCA* 2:155). The system — money and power — uncouples from, and then invades, the communicatively-structured lifeworld, producing "social pathologies… from distorted self-identities to cultural impoverishment and social anomie." Habermas thus holds both halves at once: the genuine value (communicative reason) *and* its strangulation by the system.

Uegaki, as the next section shows, takes the second half directly — he says so. The hinge, then, runs: Habermas **rescues the genuine value from A&H's reduction**, and Uegaki **takes Habermas's colonization-of-the-lifeworld to its terminus** — but re-reads that very uncoupling as the user's *liberation*, not only as pathology.

## §IV.3 — A view from its brightness: Uegaki

### §IV.3.1 — The political problematic, ontologically grounded

Uegaki lets us see what A&H cannot: that the mode's values are *genuinely pursued and genuinely realised*. To see how, we must reconstruct the lineage he addresses — a specific, structurally **soteriological** thread (in Bhaskar's terms, the *Platonic* pole), whose genealogy he traces from the ancient Greek elevation of spirit over earthly life through Renaissance Christianity's belief in a perfect human created by God. What unites this thread is not a shared doctrine but a shared *form*: the great modern value-ideals — 自由 (freedom), 平等 (equality), 自律 (autonomy), 共生 (conviviality), all of them matters of **value rationality** rather than instrumental rationality — are grounded **ontologically**, despite the thinkers' fierce disagreements about their actual content.

The structure is, term for term, a secularised salvation-history (see [[uegaki-soteriological-reading-2026-06-05]]):

- **存在論的自由 (ontological freedom) ≈ *imago Dei* and the covenant.** The Enlightenment pursuits are held to be inherent in the human as an *endowed nature* — the 本来の人間 ("the human as it ought to be") with its 約束された本来性 ("the human's true endowed nature"), whose essence is the teleological あるべき人間 — regardless of whether the source is named God, Reason, or Nature. Uegaki grounds this in his own words: the conviction that a 本来の人間 exists "probably derives from Renaissance Christianity," from "the belief that a special creature made in God's image cannot but have a special plan and purpose given to it by God" (glossary 16-05/16-07).
- **存在論的抑圧 (ontological oppression) ≈ the Fall.** The present is read as alienation or corruption of the human, or as the brutality and chaos of nature — attributed differently by each thinker (society's chains in Rousseau, estrangement in Marx, self-incurred immaturity in Kant).
- **解放 (emancipation) ≈ redemption.** Remove the oppression, and the 本来の人間 with its 約束された本来性 can actualise toward あるべき人間.

This narrative does not end with secularisation; it continues to the present — and, crucially, **A&H themselves belong to this lineage**, which is why their critique reads as enlightenment turning on itself.

### §IV.3.2 — The slippage from a political into an ontological programme

The problems these thinkers set out to solve were largely *political* — the critique of despotic power, the claim to rights against unjust subjection. The decisive move is that, by grounding the political programme **onto-teleologically**, they converted it into something else. Uegaki's term for the result is 「現実を否定する理想」, the **reality-negating ideal**: an ideal that "begins from an imagined ideal and seeks to overcome the very reality that does not fit it" (glossary 16-11), as against the 「現実に寄り添う理想」 that begins from the reality one is given and seeks a better way of living within it.

Read through the thesis, the slippage is an instance of what Bhaskar calls — on the Platonic pole — the **ontic fallacy**: the assumption that socially-produced knowledge is "the compulsive determination of knowledge by being," knowledge "naturalised" as a direct reflection of the world, in the form of "reified facts or hypostatised ideas." Here the hypostatised idea is *the anthropic ideal itself*: the projection of "what the human really is" (本来の人間) is taken *as* the real, and the non-ideal present — "the Fall" — is condemned in its light, with no consideration given to the emergence of that non-ideal across the full range of a stratified ontology. `[Scaffold: this is the v6 correction — ONTIC fallacy = Platonic/idealist pole (hypostatised ideal), per the author's assignment; the empiricist/instrumental engine commits the EPISTEMIC fallacy, §IV.3.5. Stipulate the pole↔fallacy mapping as the author's reading. Both fallacies foreclose the intransitive real → the squeeze, §IV.4.]`

It is this slippage that licenses Uegaki's central narrative: "the story of individuals being liberated from 'ontological oppression' and acquiring the 'ontological freedom' that ought to be theirs — that is, the very story of 〈無限の生〉 = 'the as-willed life' (意のままになる生)" (glossary 16-09). Note the precise sense of 無限 ("limitless"): not eternal life, but "the limitless *actualisation* of an imagined ideal upon reality" (16-02) — the conviction that reality can be remade without end to match the projected ideal.

### §IV.3.3 — The problem of others

The as-willed life founders on a permanent fact: others are, finally, **意のままにならない** — not as one wills them. The soteriologists' solution is an axiological **universalisation**: the freedom, equality, autonomy, and conviviality of *one* are made to entail those of *all* — a solidary liberation from man-made oppressions so that the endowed good may revive in everyone.

But this Aufhebung is, by itself, naïve, because it leaves the not-as-willed unaddressed. *Who* is to carry the not-as-willed burdens? What if one person's as-willed life is interfered with by the not-as-willed other? And — the sharp form — what if the *right not to bear* the not-as-willed burdens comes to be counted as an inalienable part of the かけがえのないこの私 / ありのままの私, the irreplaceable self endowed with 存在論的自由? As the political programme turns onto-teleological, the not-as-willed condition of others is read as 存在論的抑圧 to be lifted — Uegaki states this explicitly: "ultimately the very 〈concatenation of existence〉 becomes an evil oppression, and the 'not-as-willed other' itself comes to be regarded as… a high-risk, high-burden being that oppresses the irreplaceable self's spontaneity and self-determination" (glossary 16-09). `[VERIFY: confirm the chain is Uegaki's, not over-read — 16-09 supports it directly.]`

### §IV.3.4 — The problem of the body

The same logic reaches the body. Since the ancient Greeks the soul, spirit, reason, or mind has been held superior, divine, essential; the body inferior, mortal, contingent — a piece of baggage. As the programme turns onto-teleological, the not-as-willed condition of one's *own* body is likewise read as 存在論的抑圧, the 意のままにならない身体 interfering with the self-actualisation of the 本来の人間. Its limit-form is the 思念体 — the "thought-entity… that has lost even the body" (16-04/06), the secular-Gnostic terminus at which "salvation" means liberation *from* the body. `[Scaffold: the body-chain is entailed by Uegaki's 脱身体化 / 時空間的自立性 logic rather than stated as a syllogism; flag as the thesis's reconstruction.]`

### §IV.3.5 — Confluence with instrumental reason

Here the two engines meet, and here is the chapter's spine. As Needham, Pomeranz, and Uegaki each show in their own register, the toll of the not-as-willed has historically been **externalised** — offloaded onto those held *not* to possess the 約束された本来性: animals, slaves, women, colonies. And 存在論的自由 has been, genuinely, the major pillar that has sought to *correct* these externalisations — the liberation of women, of the enslaved, of colonised peoples, and now, increasingly, of animals (or, at least, the driving of the externalisation underground, into the disguised).

But — and this is the decisive point — the universal emancipation the value-engine promises is *self-contradictory* so long as the not-as-willed burden must be borne by *someone*. It is **only through the maturation of the apparatuses of instrumental reason** that the liberation of the previously-offloaded-to can, *in practice*, occur *simultaneously* with their own 存在論的自由 — because only then can the burden be offloaded onto **non-persons (artificiality)** rather than onto other persons. Without that maturity, 政治的自由 inevitably arrives bound to 存在論的不自由＝抑圧, since someone must still live as a 担い手としての〈生〉, a *bearer* of life who cannot offload his own not-as-willed conditions onto other politically-free beings. By the same token, it is only through instrumental reason's maturity that 意のままになる身体 becomes increasingly possible at all; absent it, longevity and health have meant attuning oneself — one's very 意 — to harmonise with the universe, not the reverse.

So the soteriological value-engine (the Platonic pole; the *ontic* fallacy) and the empiricist/instrumental engine (the Aristotelian pole; the **epistemic fallacy** — being reduced to what can be known, calculated, controlled) are not rivals. They are two genuine expressions of one root — the human posited as prior to and over the disenchanted given (時空間的自立性) — and the value-engine's promise *cannot be kept* without the instrumental engine's apparatus. This is *why* the two converge: not by accident of history, but by the internal logic of the promise itself. `[Scaffold: this directly answers the long-standing "why do the engines converge" question; it is the chapter's load-bearing argument.]`

### §IV.3.6 — The offloading to artificiality, and the Enlightenment values achieved "as users"

What the mature apparatus offers is, precisely, **entrustment** — not domination. Uegaki's 〈社会的装置〉 (social apparatus) is what one *entrusts* (委ねる) one's living to, and it *alleviates* the burdens of 関係性 and 〈共同〉. The offloading runs in two directions:

- **Horizontally**, the apparatus takes over portions of *function* (役割), *trust* (信頼), and the loosening-and-forgiveness (許し) that relationship and common life used to demand of persons.
- **Vertically**, it takes over the "realisation of survival" (〈生存〉の実現, roughly livelihood), the "realisation of existence-in-society" (〈現実存在〉の実現, one's standing as a member of a society or culture), and the "realisation of succession" (〈継承〉の実現, the inheritance, reproduction, and transformation of things for the lives to come).

The underlying directionality, in the thesis's own typology, is a migration **from the conventional to the structural cells** of artificiality (Ch II): the burden moves from forms that compel by ongoing human enactment to forms that compel by the structure of the apparatus itself. `[Scaffold: tie to Ch-2-C typology; this is where Stiegler's exteriorisation/exosomatisation naturally sits if invoked.]`

The result is the genuine, increasing — "universalising" — achievement of the Enlightenment values, but achieved by people *as ぶら下がっている 〈users〉 hanging from* the social apparatus, the ever-more-omnipotent artificiality system: 「〈ユーザー〉としての 自由・平等・自律・共生」 (glossary 16-10, 17-04). Uegaki's point, and ours against A&H, is that these are *not* illusion. Set beside the lives of people a hundred years ago, the freedom, equality, autonomy, and conviviality of the 〈user〉 are real — even if not the rose-coloured things the ideals imagined (16-10).

### §IV.3.7 — The completion of the Enlightenment ideal

This is why **self-finishing (自己完結化) is not an aberration of the mode but its immanent completion** — the sole form in which the mode's own demand, the reconciliation of total individual freedom with a working common life, can in fact be met. The values are realised; they are realised *as the apparatus delivers them*; and the apparatus can deliver them universally only by taking the not-as-willed onto itself. In the thesis's reading, this is **instrumental rationality guided by value rationality** — the Aristotelian engine made to serve the Platonic end.

The emerging future this projects is the techno-utopian vision in which everyone is equal and free to do or become whatever they want (自己実現) with the aid of artificiality, while everything unwanted is done *for* us by artificiality. Its entailed consequences are real but, strikingly, *none of them runs against the Enlightenment values*: the self-finishing of life (生の自己完結化) brings the thinning of relationality (関係性の希薄化) and the disembodiment of life (生の脱身体化).

> **Scaffold note on normativity.** Uegaki judges these to issue in 関係性の病理 (the pathology of relationality) and 生の混乱 (the disarray of life). The chapter should present these as *Uegaki's normative reading*, contextualised as the actual of his moment — and should NOT adopt the moral verdict as the thesis's own. The thesis's verdict is the *ontological* squeeze (§IV.4), not the moral pathology. This keeps the brightness genuinely bright and the chapter non-declinist. `[VERIFY: this resolves the user's open flag.]`

> **Scaffold note on the Geist.** The technological *realisation* of the two engines' confluence — AI as autonomised reason and big data as autonomised sense-data converging into a new "Hegelian Geist" (cf. 総合人間学 18) — belongs to **Chapter V** (the metaverse / apparatus realised), not here. Ch IV establishes the two engines and their convergence *in principle*; the autonomised Geist is their consummation.

## §IV.4 — Real-world index, and A&H vs Uegaki as two sides of one coin

### §IV.4.1 — The case and the present

Uegaki writes from late-modern Japan — the *site* at which the mature apparatus and its inward dynamics are most fully observable, not (as in Ch III) the story of the mode's *imposition* upon Japan as a once-separate world. `[Scaffold: keep the "five-stage assimilation of Japan" in Ch III (outward); here Japan is the vantage, not the assimilation narrative. Per Han/Rosa, the soft/entrustment face is a matter of phase, not of West-vs-Japan geography — so do not over-geographise the contrast below.]` What can be read off the present is the as-willed life pursued across the cells of artificiality at once.

### §IV.4.2 — Two sides of one coin

Adorno-and-Horkheimer and Uegaki are not two competing theories to be adjudicated. Their works are, in the main, *actual* — each a faithful rendering of the actual structures and dynamics of its own moment (the industrial, world-war, modernist moment; the digital, "globalised", postmodernist moment). And the charge of essentialism that each invites can be understood, in critical-realist terms, as a **conflation of the actual with the real** — an essentialising of the real *by* the actual: A&H take the actual face of domination for the whole, and a reader of Uegaki could take the actual face of liberation for the whole.

The **real** beneath both is one mechanism: **universalising controllability**, legitimated by the supremacy of the subject and made possible by the subject/object — culture-man/nature — rupture. `[VERIFY: "supremacy of the subject" must be read as the subject/object rupture + the self posited prior-to/over the disenchanted given (時空間的自立性), NOT the narrowly transcendental subject — so as to be inclusive of the empiricists, whose materialism still makes the rupture and still posits the individual prior to society. A&H's "subject" here is the self-preserving instrumental ego set over disenchanted object-nature, not Kant's transcendental subject.]` Domination and liberation are *not* properties of this real mechanism; they are its two actual-empirical manifestations — the same offloading-onto-an-autonomised-apparatus read once as a cage (A&H) and once as relief (Uegaki). The discipline of the given does not vanish in the bright reading; it is *outsourced* — borne now by the apparatus rather than by the self, which is exactly why the self experiences as freedom what the system enacts as control.

### §IV.4.3 — The metaverse, where the two sides are revealed as one — and the squeeze

The metaverse is the point at which the coin can be seen whole. There, universalising controllability does not *necessarily* dominate or liberate — for those are the actual-empirical readings — but it does something more fundamental: it forms a **real, new background environment**, a second nature (A&H) and an autonomised 社会的装置 (Uegaki) at once, parallel to the universe rather than nested within the world.

And here the dialectic recoils. The controllability that realises the values is the same second nature that hollows them (A&H's *Eclipse of Reason*: value-reason swallowed by instrumental reason); the entrustment that delivers freedom is the same uncoupling that pathologises relation (Habermas's colonisation; Uegaki's 無間地獄, the no-respite hell of an ideal that can never be reached). But the thesis's verdict is neither A&H's domination nor Uegaki's pathology. It is **ontological**: as long as we are human, "there always remains a not-as-willed" (Uegaki, 2022b:120) — the intransitive, stratified **real** that no controllability can subsume, the *ontic* and *epistemic* fallacies' common remainder. The universalising-controllability mode, perfected in the metaverse, collides with the given it declared independence from. That collision — the universe reasserting against the metaverse, between which the human is caught — is **the squeeze**, and it is where Chapter IV hands off to the chapters that follow.
