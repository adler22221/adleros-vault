## §C — "Why the Enlightenment mode" (recalibrated)

**¶ (opening — retained, but reframed):** The section still opens by stating that the name must be *earned* rather than assumed. What changes is what the earning delivers: not, in the first instance, a verdict of domination, but the identification of a *stance* — the elevation of a particular kind of self.

**¶ (NEW — the spine; replaces the A&H-led entry at current l.275–280):**
By the Enlightenment mode I mean a mode of artificiality whose defining characteristic is the universalization of a single figure of the self: the *sovereign subject*. Adorno and Horkheimer gave the widest version of the idea when they spoke of enlightenment not as a century but as a stance that "has always aimed at liberating human beings from fear and installing them as masters of a disenchanted world" (Horkheimer and Adorno, 2002: 1); and I will return to them, because they saw something real. But mastery is not where the mode begins. It begins one step earlier, with what the contemporary Japanese philosopher Uegaki Takahide calls the human's *spatio-temporal self-subsistence* (時空間的自立性, jikūkanteki jiritsusei): the assumption that the human can stand as "a single individual substance, a subject, prior to the others and the systems of meaning that surround it" — a self that has been, in his exact word, *dis-embedded* (脱埋め込み, datsu-umekomi) from the connectedness of existence that had always constituted it (Uegaki, 2022a). The clearest philosophical portrait of this self is the one Uegaki sets against his own position: Max Scheler's *world-open* (*weltoffen*) human, the being that, alone among the living, is "not bound to drives and environment, but 'environment-free' and … 'world-open': such a being *has* 'world'" (*Ein solches Wesen ist also nicht mehr trieb- und umweltgebunden, sondern 'umweltfrei' und … 'weltoffen': Ein solches Wesen hat 'Welt'*; Scheler, *Die Stellung des Menschen im Kosmos*, 1928, [VERIFY printed page — Frings 2009 / Meyerhoff 1961]). For Scheler the human, possessed of Spirit (精神, *Geist*), suspends its reflexive drives and so is "freed from the fetters of the environing world (環世界, kankyō-sekai — Uexküll's *Umwelt*)," becoming a free being raised above it (Uegaki, 2022a: 90). I cite Scheler not as a claim I endorse but as the purest expression of the very stance this thesis is written to correct — the picture of a human who has *overcome* the Umwelt, escaping the given into transcendence, a face of the dis-embedded sovereign subject. Uegaki rebuts it on the page (2022a: 90–91): world-openness is no transcendence *from* the Umwelt but a peculiar structure *within* the human's own environment, and that own environment is an artificial one. The thesis follows Uegaki, and joins to him Obara's account of the human's artificial ecosystem: the human does not escape the Umwelt; it builds a peculiar, made one. Scheler's "world-open" subject is thus the sovereign subject in its most flattering self-description — the dis-embedded self mistaking the environment it has rebuilt for an environment it has left behind. This is also the figure Charles Taylor, tracing its modern emergence, calls the *buffered self*, sealed against a world to which an earlier self had been porous (Taylor, 2007); and the one Michael Sandel names the *unencumbered self*, the self imagined as prior to its ends and attachments. To picture such a self fully, Sandel observes, "is not to conceive an ideally free and rational agent, but to imagine a person wholly without character, without moral depth" (Sandel, 1998: 179; first stated in Sandel, 1984: 90) — a self Uegaki himself invokes in just this connection (Uegaki, 2022b: 112). The mode's first move is not to dominate the given but to *raise a self above it*.

**¶ (NEW — the reversal of answerability):**
With that elevation comes a reversal of the direction of answerability, and it is the reversal, more than any particular act of mastery, that the rest of this thesis tracks. The embedded human of the older worlds stood answerable *to* its surroundings: it attuned itself to a given it did not author, reading what the world asked of it. The sovereign subject inverts the relation. It takes up what Uegaki describes as a kind of "God's-eye view" (神の視点, kami no shiten) over an environment, a body, and other persons now reduced to objects to be known (Uegaki, 2022a) — the very standpoint Scheler's *weltoffen* human claimed in overcoming the Umwelt, here redescribed not as a liberation from the given but as a posture *toward* it. And from that height the given no longer makes demands but *owes* them — it is the world that must answer to the subject, furnishing what the subject takes to be its due. Artificiality is the instrument of this reversal. It is the means by which the recalcitrant given is offloaded and entrusted — Uegaki's *to entrust* (委ねる, yudaneru) — to a made order that will be made to deliver what the subject intends, so that the subject need no longer bend itself to the world. *(The phrase "reversal of answerability" is my own redescription; the structure it names — the dis-embedded subject, the God's-eye standpoint, the entrusting of the work of living to the made — is Uegaki's. See Scaffold-notes.)*

**¶ (NEW — domination relativized to an endowment):**
Here the most important clarification of the chapter must be entered, because the familiar critical vocabulary of "domination" and "control" tends to swallow it. To install the sovereign subject is to confer an *entitlement*: the given is now the subject's to dispose of as it sees fit. Domination — the active mastering of the world, the bending of the given to the intended — is one way the subject may exercise that entitlement. It is not the entitlement itself. The endowment may be claimed, and then the mode wears its mastering face: the nature-subduing, tradition-displacing, world-overwriting reason that Adorno and Horkheimer, Bacon,[^bacon] and Descartes,[^descartes] all in their different registers describe. But the same endowment may equally be *declined* — and that it can be declined, without the subject ceasing for a moment to be sovereign, is the surest sign that sovereignty, not mastery, is the root. Controllability, then, is not the mode's essence but the operative principle of one of its faces; I borrow the term from Hartmut Rosa, who shows the modern relation rendering the world *verfügbar* — which can be rendered not only "controllable" but "available," "disposable," "at one's disposal," or "accessible"[^verfugbar] — along four dimensions,[^rosa-dims] and I subordinate it deliberately: it names what the sovereign subject does when it chooses to master, not what makes it sovereign.

**¶ (NEW — the withdrawn face + the false-consciousness point; carries C-b):**
For the sovereign subject has more than the mastering face, and the others are just what a diagnosis pitched entirely at "domination" cannot see. One is *stewardship* — dominion exercised as care. The endowment may be discharged not by subduing the given but by tending it, and this benevolent disposal is now widely taken as the very premise of environmental protection. Its charter text is the same one that licenses mastery: the human is told to "have dominion … and subdue" the earth (Genesis 1:28) and also "to till — to work — and keep" the garden (Genesis 2:15). Lynn White Jr. read the first as the root of the crisis — "Christianity … is the most anthropocentric religion the world has seen," teaching "the rightful mastery of [man] over nature" — and proposed Francis of Assisi, who would unseat "man's limitless rule of creation," as its corrective (White, 1967: 1205, 1206–1207). But stewardship, for all its gentleness, does not leave the structure. The steward still receives the given as *his to keep*; answerability still runs from the given up to the subject who answers for it; the disposal is benevolent, but it remains a disposal. Stewardship is therefore not an exit from sovereignty but a third way of exercising it — alongside mastery and the withdrawal to which I now turn. When mastery fails, or wearies of itself, the same dis-embedded self does not return to embeddedness; it withdraws. It turns to the disengagement Uegaki anatomizes as listlessness (無気力, mukiryoku), resignation (諦め, akirame), and finally the void (虚無, kyomu) — the condition of a self "cut off from the connectedness of being" (Uegaki, 2022a). In the contemporary Sinosphere this face has found its own name in the refusal called *lying flat* (躺平, tǎngpíng): the individual, taking its life to be answerable to no one, declining to strive at all. Mastery and withdrawal are not two moods but two exercises of one entitlement — the sovereign subject disposing of a given it takes to be wholly its own, in the one case by remaking it, in the other by refusing it. And it is exactly this second face that marks the limit of the great critical-theoretical accounts. For a theory that has defined the mode as domination, the withdrawn, nihilistic, lying-flat self is an embarrassment it can absorb only by declaring it deceived: Adorno and Horkheimer must read it as the work of a culture industry that manufactures consent, and Habermas as a "fragmented consciousness" that "blocks enlightenment by the mechanism of reification" (Habermas, 1987: 355). Each must treat the withdrawal as a *failure to see*. The sovereign-subject diagnosis needs no such auxiliary: it reads mastery and withdrawal as the two native dispositions of one elevated self, neither of them a mistake about the self's true interest, both of them the self exercising precisely the sovereignty the mode conferred. (Uegaki's *ethic of non-intervention* (不介入, fukainyū) belongs not here, among the pathological symptoms, but downstream: it is an ethical principle he derives from the as-intended axiom — one must not intervene in another's life-as-intended, each bearing full responsibility for its own (Uegaki, 2022a: 77, 278). I carry it forward to Chapters IV and VI as the self-completing regime's live-and-let-live ethic, of which the listless, lying-flat withdrawal is the two-sides-of-one-body (表裏一体, hyōri-ittai) underside.) *(躺平/nihilism are the thesis's own extension of Uegaki's verified withdrawal-structure, not Uegaki's terms — see Scaffold-notes.)*

**¶ (A&H as one witness — reframed from current l.278–280):**
It is now possible to take from Adorno and Horkheimer exactly what they offer and no more. Their verdict that "enlightenment is totalitarian" (Horkheimer and Adorno, 2002: 4), that the mode seeks to "use [nature] to dominate wholly both it and human beings" (2002: 2), is the truest description we have of the *mastering face* — of what the sovereign subject does when it claims its endowment to the full. I keep it for that. What I do not take is its sufficiency: read as the whole of the mode, the verdict can accommodate neither the withdrawn face nor the stewardship face, and so must dismiss half of what the present plainly shows as false consciousness. Adorno and Horkheimer are, in this chapter, the indispensable witness to one of sovereignty's faces — not the name of the disposition itself. (The full reckoning with their account, and with Habermas's correction of it, belongs to Chapter IV, where the three thinkers are set side by side.)

**¶ (Western vs modern — retained):** The argument for preferring "Enlightenment" to "Western" and to "modern" (current l.285–289) is retained unchanged: "Western" essentializes and mislocates; "modern" names only recency. The eponymous Platonic/Aristotelian strands remain *eponyms, not histories* (retained).

## §H — "From the mastery of worlds to the mastery of life" (recalibrated)

**¶ (summary — reweighted, replaces current l.358):**
The argument of this chapter can now be gathered, and gathered around the right centre. I began by establishing that human worlds are genuinely plural, plural down to the ontologies on which they rest, and that beneath every one of them runs the synchronic squeeze between a made world and the given universe it never escapes. I read collapse as that squeeze in its extreme — a mismatch, not a verdict of unfitness — and found in its survivors the regeneration base, the plurality of worlds itself. I then named the mode whose disposition is this chapter's subject: the Enlightenment mode, identified not by a date and not, first of all, by domination, but by the *elevation of the sovereign subject* — the dis-embedded self that takes the given to owe it answer, and to which mastery (controllability), stewardship, and withdrawal are three exercises of one entitlement. I traced its conditions of emergence, material and nonmaterial, and showed why its universalization, far from proving it the fittest, manufactures a planetary monoculture and a single point of failure.

**¶ (the inward turn — recalibrated, replaces current l.359):**
One movement remains, and it carries the chapter into the next. A mode founded on the sovereignty of the subject does not, in the end, confine the subject's entitlement to the world outside the human. The same self that takes the given world to owe it answer turns, eventually, upon the one stretch of the given that is nearest and least tractable — its own life. For the human life is not lived as intended: the body sickens and ages against one's intention, others resist being made over to one's design, the given in oneself declines to be merely disposable. Uegaki gives this recalcitrance its name in the negative — the reality that no life is lived entirely as intended (意のままにならない生, i no mama ni naranai sei), the *anti-intended* in the self as in the world — and the sovereign subject, having taken its life to be its own to dispose of, cannot meet that recalcitrance as a condition to be lived with. It meets it as a hindrance to be overcome, or, failing that, as a void to be borne. The pursuit of a life lived wholly as intended (意のままになる生, i no mama ni naru sei) is the form the sovereign subject's entitlement takes when it turns upon the human itself. *(This recalibrates the current "demand for control inward" to "the sovereign subject's claim upon its own anti-intended life" — Uegaki's subject-pole, not the object-pole of controllability.)*

**¶ (hand-off to Ch IV — retained):** The closing hand-off (current l.360) is retained: the mastery of worlds and the mastery of life are one disposition at two scales, and it is the inward turn that drives toward the terminal form of the squeeze. (Ch IV then opens with the three-thinker arc — A&H / Habermas / Uegaki — now *back-referencing* the sovereign subject seeded here, rather than introducing it cold; see the coordinated Ch IV trim in Scaffold-notes.)

## Footnotes

[^bacon]: Francis Bacon supplies the mastering register at its most programmatic. The given is to be commanded only through obedience to its own laws — "Nature, to be commanded, must be obeyed" (*Novum Organum* I, aph. 3) — yet the aim is unambiguously to extend "the empire of man over things" (*Novum Organum* I, closing aphorisms), and to do so the inquirer must press nature under "the vexations of art," constraining and moulding it rather than letting it run its course (*Novum Organum* I, aph. 98). I do not attribute to Bacon the notorious figures of nature as a slave to be "tortured" or put "on the rack": those phrases derive from Benjamin Farrington's translation of *Temporis Partus Masculus* and were popularized by Carolyn Merchant, and their fidelity to Bacon's Latin is contested; I therefore leave them aside, noting only that the dispute exists. [VERIFY exact wording of the closing-aphorism phrase against a standard edition, e.g., Jardine & Silverthorne 2000.]

[^descartes]: Descartes states the project in the *Discourse on Method* VI: the new philosophy would "render ourselves, as it were, the masters and possessors of nature" (*nous rendre comme maîtres et possesseurs de la nature*) — where the hedging *comme* ("as it were") is worth preserving, since it qualifies the mastery even as it asserts it. The disposal extends to the living: in *Discourse* V he advances the beast-machine thesis, treating animals as automata, mechanisms without reason or soul whose motions are wholly explicable as those of a machine. [VERIFY against a standard edition/translation, e.g., Cottingham, Stoothoff & Murdoch, *Philosophical Writings* I.]

[^verfugbar]: Rosa's *verfügbar* admits a range of English renderings beyond the "controllable" of the standard translation: "available," "disposable," "at one's disposal," and "accessible" each catch part of the sense — the world rendered reachable, manipulable, and on call to the subject's will.

[^rosa-dims]: Rosa specifies four dimensions along which the modern subject renders the world controllable: making it (1) *visible* or knowable, (2) *reachable* or accessible, (3) *manageable* or masterable, and (4) *usable* or instrumentally available (Rosa, 2020: 14–17).
