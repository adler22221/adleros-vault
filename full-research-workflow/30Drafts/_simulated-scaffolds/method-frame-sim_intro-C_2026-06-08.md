> ## 1. The approach, in one paragraph

> I adopt **an ecological and critical-realist approach**. It is *realist* about generative structures and dispositions, *stratified* about the modes by which those structures are accessed (real / actual / empirical), *emergentist* about the irreducibility of higher-level organizations to lower ones, and *developmental* about the deep-historical trajectories by which dispositions have come to operate. To these critical-realist commitments I join two further lenses that the object demands: an *ecological-evolutionary* lens, which treats its objects — organisms, worlds, civilizations, the metaverse — as organisms-in-environments and environments-within-the-universe, co-adapting across deep time; and a *complex-adaptive-systems* lens, which treats their characteristic dynamics — self-organization, emergence at higher levels of organization, scale-recurrent patterns — as features of the phenomena themselves and not artifacts of description. The argumentative engine is *retroduction* — the inference, akin to but not identical with what the philosophy of science calls inference to the best explanation, by which one *posits* the generative structures that must exist for a given phenomenon to be possible, rather than merely *selecting* the most plausible among already-given explanations.[^retroduction-ibe] I reason from observable phenomena (the catalogued signs of human malaise under late artificiality, set out in Chapter VI) to the underlying generative structure that, were it real, would account for them. Because the hypothesized structure is itself a developmental process, the retroduction is *diachronic*: the trajectory by which a mechanism has come to operate is part of what must be reconstructed, since the direction of that trajectory grounds the diagnostic claims about where it is heading. The central concepts — universe, world, metaverse, squeeze, self-completing artificiality — are *ideal-typical constructions* in Weber's sense, accentuated analytical constructs designed to illuminate the empirical multiplicity from which they are drawn, not to exhaust it. And the whole is unapologetically *transdisciplinary*, because the squeeze is the object of no inherited discipline: nothing in the standing division of intellectual labour takes the universe–world–metaverse relation as its subject. It therefore draws on substantive bodies of thought that no single field encloses. The first is a pair of Continental lineages. The one is **the philosophy of technology** — the post-Heideggerian line running from Heidegger through Stiegler to Hui — which supplies the diagnostic vocabulary for treating the technological not as a stock of devices but as a mode of world-disclosure and exteriorization; I take up this lineage's *diagnosis* of the modern condition while declining its governing ontology.[^continental] The other is **the critical theory of modernity** — from Weber through Adorno and Horkheimer to Habermas and Rosa — which supplies the diagnostic grammar of disenchantment, instrumental reason, and the modern compulsion toward controllability.[^critical-theory] These Continental diagnoses are carried over to the second body of thought, **an eco-anthropological history of the human–environment relation**, by two distinct bridges: a *postcolonial* critique (Mignolo; de Sousa Santos; Chakrabarty) that opens the diagnosis onto the pluriverse and the standing of other worlds, and **the post-anthropocentric "ontological turn" in anthropology** (Descola; Viveiros de Castro) that dissolves the nature/culture partition and unseats the sovereign human — the two kept distinct, since the pluriversal and the post-anthropocentric are not the same move. I take this second bridge from the anthropology the thesis already uses rather than from a separate posthumanist apparatus: Descola and Viveiros de Castro are not "posthumanism" in the philosophical-school sense (Braidotti, Wolfe, Haraway), but the ontological turn does the same decentering work — it dissolves the nature/culture partition and unseats the sovereign human — and I would rather draw the bridge from figures the thesis engages directly (Descola's four ontologies in Chapter III §A; Viveiros de Castro's perspectivism and multinaturalism in §IIC) than import a posthumanist apparatus the argument does not otherwise need. > [^retroduction-ibe]: The two inferences are kin but distinct, and the difference matters for a stratified ontology. *Retroduction* (Peircean abduction as taken up in critical realism) is a *generative* move belonging to the discovery phase: it conjectures a previously unposited mechanism, asking in its transcendental form "what must the world be like — what must exist at the level of the real — for this phenomenon to be possible?" Peirce held abduction to be "the only logical operation which introduces any new idea," yielding grounds only to *suspect* that something may be the case. *Inference to the best explanation* (Harman; Lipton) is by contrast a *justificatory* move belonging to the warrant phase: it presupposes an assembled set of candidate explanations and ranks them by comparative explanatory virtue (simplicity, scope, coherence), inferring that the best of them is probably true — which is why it carries the structural risk of "the best of a bad lot," the best *available* explanation need not be the best *possible* one (SEP, "Abduction"; "Scientific Realism"). To equate the two silently imports IBE's comparative-selection logic, and its actualist habit of reasoning to the best explanation *of the evidence*, into a move that is doing different work: positing depth ontology rather than choosing among surface theories. CR-aligned scholarship is explicit that the two are "different, but complementary, modes of inference" (Ritz, 2020 — *[VERIFY: pull verbatim from Zotero/NLM before final; full text returned 403/cert errors, claim rests on abstract + convergent SEP framing]*; see also Sayer, *Method in Social Science*). One enlarges the hypothesis space; the other ranks within it.
>
> [^continental]: This lineage furnishes the thesis's critique of the merely instrumental-and-anthropological definition of technology and its account of the technological as a mode of revealing; it is engaged as a *diagnosis* and not adopted as an ontology, since the thesis's own diagnosis of the modern condition is Uegakian (the universalizing elevation of the sovereign subject) rather than Heideggerian (enframing/*Gestell*). > [^synthetic-anthropology]: Synthetic anthropology (総合人間学, *sōgō ningengaku*) is the program of the Japan Association of Synthetic Anthropology (総合人間学会, *Sōgō Ningen Gakkai*), which since its 2006 founding has pursued the single question "what does it mean to be human?" by "reconsidering humanity as a whole" against the reductive fragmentation of the specialist sciences, holding that the contradictions of contemporary civilization "have their roots in the self-alienation of the human being" — being dominated by the very tools and systems humanity has made [VERIFY: synthetic-anthropology.org, "About this Association," statement of purpose, page_id=379, accessed 2026-06; corroborated in the 2019 Japanese revision 「総合人間学会趣旨 新版（2019年）」 (*Sōgō Ningen Gakkai shushi shinpan*, "Statement of Purpose, New Edition"), page_id=1932]. The movement traces to the animal ethologist Obara Hideo (小原秀雄, *Obara Hideo*), whose theory of self-domestication (自己家畜化論, *jiko kachikuka-ron*) holds that humans, alone among animals, evolve under an environment of their own making: through the recursive production of "things" (モノ, *mono* — encompassing not only material tools but language, institutions, and worldview), humanity replaces the natural ecosystem with a self-made "artificial ecosystem" (人工生態系, *jinkō seitaikei*), so that biological "Homo" (ヒト, *hito*) becomes socio-cultural "Human" (人間, *ningen*) by being imprinted upon it, reshaped not by natural selection but by "auto-artificial selection" (自己人為淘汰, *jiko jin'i tōta*) [VERIFY: the descriptive-model account is in 「自己家畜化論」 ("The Theory of Self-Domestication"), *Synthetic Anthropology Keyword Collection* (総合人間学キーワード集), KW2021, pp. 23–24, co-authored by Anami Shin'ichi, Uegaki Takahide 上柿崇英, and Haseba Takeshi; page numbers TBD against the Zotero PDF]. The environmental philosopher Uegaki Takahide (上柿崇英, *Uegaki Takahide*) reframes this systematically: between "Homo" (ヒト, *hito*) and the natural ecosystem stands "society" (社会, *shakai*), itself an artificial ecosystem mediating the two; once industrialization freed this artificial layer from ecological limits, its "boundless expansion of 'things' now conflicts not only with the global natural ecosystem but also with 'Homo,' the inner nature of humans, triggering a 'crisis of humanity' simultaneously with the 'environmental crisis'" [VERIFY: uegakiSogoNingenGaku2017, 『「自己家畜化論」から「総合人間学的本性論・文明論」へ』 (*"From the Theory of Self-Domestication to a Synthetic-Anthropological Theory of Human Nature and Civilization"*), pp. 254–257 — page citations from NLM Uegaki notebook, confirm against Zotero PDF before final]. ### A.4 — In §3, replace the **Heidegger** disposition note (line 63) with the T0-corrected formulation: 

> **Heidegger** is the genealogical ancestor of Stiegler and Hui, and the thesis engages his *diagnosis* of modern technology — the critique of the merely instrumental-and-anthropological definition, and the account of the technological mode as a way of *revealing* that sets the world up as standing-reserve (*Gestell*, enframing). But Heidegger does not serve as the thesis's governing *ontology or method*, and the reasons are now stated precisely (see §6.2). His "history of Being" makes the *Being* of entities — their intelligibility and significance — dependent on a historical mode of disclosure; the thesis instead needs an intransitive, mind-independent universe whose structures and dispositions are real prior to and apart from any disclosure of them. (Heidegger grants that *entities* exist independently — "Nature does not need disclosedness … to be as it is" — but it is the dependence of their *Being* on revealing that the thesis cannot adopt.) His account of the technological epoch is a single "destining of Being" (*Geschick*); the thesis instead gives a stratified, multi-causal, ecological-evolutionary account. And his response — *Gelassenheit*, releasement, the "saving power" that grows where the danger is — is a meditative comportment rather than the analytic, actionable diagnosis the thesis pursues. Heidegger is thus engaged as a diagnostician and declined as a system.

### A.5 — Replace §6 item 2 ("Heideggerian methodology") with a tightened, T0-anchored version:

> 2. **Heideggerian ontology and method (declined, with the diagnosis retained).** The thesis takes up Heidegger's *diagnosis* — the inadequacy of the instrumental-anthropological definition of technology, and enframing (*Gestell*) as the modern mode of revealing — but declines his ontology and method as governing, for three reasons that the T0 verification of the primary texts bears out:
>     - **(i) Anti-realism about Being.** For Heidegger, entities exist mind-independently, but their *Being* — their intelligibility and significance — does not: it "is dependent upon the understanding of Being," and is disclosed only where there is Dasein. The thesis requires the opposite for the universe: a substrate whose generative structures are *real* independent of any disclosure, the intransitive object of an ecological and critical-realist inquiry. This is a difference in ontology, not emphasis.
>     - **(ii) A single destining versus a stratified account.** Heidegger presents the technological essence as one epochal "destining of revealing" (*Geschick des Seins*) and expressly refuses to explain it "historiographically" or as a "sequence belonging to a process of history." The thesis's explanation is the reverse: multi-causal, stratified across real/actual/empirical, and ecological-evolutionary in its reconstruction of how the modern mode of artificiality came to predominate.
>     - **(iii) Releasement versus an actionable diagnosis.** Heidegger's response is *Gelassenheit* — a meditative "releasement toward things," "resignation and passivity … rather than an active program of reform." The thesis is forward-looking and diagnostic: it aims to identify the disposition's likely yield in order to inform deliberate counter-construction (the world that might be built between the universe and the metaverse), not to await a saving power.
>     Heidegger therefore appears as background to Stiegler's lineage in Chapter II and as a diagnostic resource, never as a methodological co-equal.

## Deliverable B — Introduction §C, naming the method 

> **Placement note (v2).** This is the paragraph that sits where the reconstructed Introduction names its method — at the close of I.2 ("The stance of the inquiry"), after the real (道, *dào*) / the actual (理, *lǐ*) / the empirical (相, *xiàng*) stratification sentence and before I.3's working definitions. **Per the author's instruction, it now *leads* with the more elaborated Deliverable A §1 statement** (rendered in the first person to match the Introduction's voice), and folds into it what the earlier B paragraph carried but A did not: the complex-adaptive-systems reinterpretation of the stratified ontology, the explicit Bhaskar citation, and the real/actual/empirical mapping. It is worth naming, plainly, the kind of inquiry this is. The approach is **ecological and critical-realist**. I am *realist* about generative structures and dispositions, *stratified* about the modes by which those structures are accessed (the real, the actual, the empirical), *emergentist* about the irreducibility of higher-level organizations to lower ones, and *developmental* about the deep-historical trajectories by which dispositions have come to operate. To these critical-realist commitments I join two further lenses that the object demands: an *ecological-evolutionary* lens, which reads my objects — organisms, worlds, civilizations, the metaverse — as beings-in-environments and environments-within-the-universe, co-adapting across deep time; and a *complex-adaptive-systems* lens, which treats their characteristic dynamics — self-organization, emergence at higher levels of organization, scale-recurrent patterns — as features of the phenomena themselves and not artifacts of description. On this reading I take the three strata to name, at every level of being, the **real** as the organizing conditions and principles, the **actual** as the structures and dynamics these conditions formulate or in which they are manifested, and the **empirical** as the observable or experienceable context-specific phenomena to which those structures and dynamics in turn give rise.[^bhaskar-cas] This is the same stratification I have just given in the classical vocabulary of the real (道, *dào*), the actual (理, *lǐ*), and the empirical (相, *xiàng*).

My argumentative engine is *retroduction* — the inference, akin to but not identical with what the philosophy of science calls inference to the best explanation, by which one *posits* the generative structures that must exist for a phenomenon to be possible at all, rather than merely *selecting* the most plausible among already-given explanations.[^retroduction-ibe-b] I reason from the observable signs of our predicament — and in particular from the *seemingly contradictory* symptoms and diagnoses that the catalogued signs of human malaise present across Chapters III–V, where the evidence pulls in opposite directions at once — toward the underlying disposition that, were it real, would account for them. The retroductive question is therefore not "which available explanation best fits the surface data?" but "*what underlying organizing conditions or principles could make these contradictory symptoms and diagnoses simultaneously true?*" And because that disposition is itself a historical process, the inference runs further still — from its reconstructed trajectory to the condition it continues to generate. My central terms — universe, world, metaverse, squeeze — are *ideal types* in Weber's sense, analytical constructions to be judged by what they illuminate rather than by their fit to any single specimen, and, as such, *not exhaustive of the empirical specificities* they organize. This is no concession but a corollary of the retroductive stance: an inquiry trained on the underlying conditions rather than their surface manifestations does not require — and should not expect — its constructs to capture every empirical particular. And the inquiry is necessarily transdisciplinary, for the squeeze falls within the borders of no established discipline. It draws, first, on a pair of Continental lineages. The one is **the philosophy of technology** — the post-Heideggerian line running from Heidegger through Stiegler to Hui — which furnishes the vocabulary for treating the technological not as a stock of devices but as a mode of world-disclosure and exteriorization.[^continental-b] The other is **the critical theory of modernity** — from Weber through Adorno and Horkheimer to Habermas and Rosa — which supplies the diagnostic grammar of disenchantment, instrumental reason, and the modern compulsion toward controllability.[^critical-theory-b] These Continental diagnoses are carried into the second body of thought, **an eco-anthropological history of the human–environment relation**, by two distinct bridges: a *postcolonial* critique (Mignolo; de Sousa Santos; Chakrabarty) that opens the diagnosis onto the pluriverse and the standing of other worlds, and **the post-anthropocentric "ontological turn" in anthropology** (Descola; Viveiros de Castro) that dissolves the nature/culture partition and unseats the sovereign human — the two kept distinct, since the pluriversal and the post-anthropocentric are not the same move. I draw this second bridge from the anthropology I already use — Descola's four ontologies (Chapter III §A), Viveiros de Castro's perspectivism and multinaturalism (§IIC) — rather than importing a separate posthumanist apparatus: the ontological turn does the decentering work that posthumanism would (Braidotti, Wolfe, Haraway), but it is already inside the thesis's evidence base.[^bridges-b] The eco-anthropological tradition reconstructs across deep time how humans came to interpose a self-made layer between themselves and nature — and here, in particular, the inquiry leans on the Japanese tradition of **synthetic anthropology** (総合人間学, *sōgō ningengaku*), which diagnoses modern crisis as a "self-alienation" in which humanity is dominated by the artificial world (the "things," モノ, *mono*) it has itself made, and frames the human as the one animal which interposes a self-produced "artificial ecosystem" (人工生態系, *jinkō seitaikei*) between itself and nature.[^synthetic-anthropology-b] The inquiry holds these Western and East-Asian diagnoses together by design: where they converge it reads them as mutual corroboration, and where they diverge as mutual correction — a triangulation whose method I make explicit below.[^triangulation-b] Neither body of thought alone reaches the squeeze; together they make it visible.

[^bhaskar-cas]: I depart here, deliberately, from Bhaskar's original folio of the three domains, and the departure should be stated. In *A Realist Theory of Science* the **real** is the domain of generative structures and mechanisms — "Structures and mechanisms then are real and distinct from the patterns of events that they generate" — the **actual** is the domain of those events (the patterns the mechanisms produce), and the **empirical** is the domain of *experiences*, the events as observed; the three are nested, not partitioned (Dr ≥ Da ≥ De), and empirical realism's error is to collapse them into one (Bhaskar, 2008, p. 46) [VERIFY: folio — 2008 Routledge edition; the 1975 first edition paginates differently]. My reinterpretation — real as *organizing conditions and principles*, actual as *structures and dynamics*, empirical as *observable phenomena* — is offered not as exegesis but as a generalization, so that one stratification can serve the natural and the social sciences alike: it is not intuitive to call gravitation or the laws of thermodynamics "structures" in Bhaskar's sense, yet they are unquestionably real, and they sit more naturally under "organizing conditions and principles." The warrant for the recasting is the logic of complex adaptive systems, in which a set of generative *rules* (the real) is the condition out of which components *self-organize* into structures and dynamics (the actual), from which, in turn, *emergent properties* arise as the system's observable behaviour (the empirical). I note for precision that this is a redescription and not Bhaskar's own scheme: it relocates "structures" from his real to my actual and recasts his intransitive mechanisms as "principles." A strict critical realist might worry that naming the real "organizing conditions and principles" makes it sound idea-like, eroding Bhaskar's insistence that the real is structured and mechanism-laden quite independently of any thought about it. The answer is that these conditions and principles are *generative constraints* — causally efficacious, not at anyone's disposal, and in no sense mental contents. I keep the realist commitment — the organizing conditions and principles are independent of the subject as a vantage point and causally efficacious whether or not activated or observed (a formulation that also forecloses the misreading that social and cultural structures, being collective, are therefore individually mind-dependent, and that thereby reinforces the thesis of the hypostatized subject) — while taking the generalization to be worth the cost, because it lets the same stratified ontology read a galaxy and an institution without equivocation.

[^retroduction-ibe-b]: Retroduction and inference to the best explanation are kin but not the same, and the difference bears on a stratified ontology. *Retroduction* is a *generative* move: it conjectures a previously unposited mechanism, asking in its transcendental form what must exist at the level of the real for a phenomenon to be possible. *Inference to the best explanation* (Harman; Lipton) is a *justificatory* move: it presupposes an assembled set of candidate explanations and ranks them by comparative virtue, with the attendant risk of "the best of a bad lot." To equate them silently imports IBE's comparative-selection logic, and its actualist reasoning to the best explanation *of the evidence*, into a move whose work is to posit depth ontology rather than to choose among surface theories (SEP, "Abduction"; "Scientific Realism"; Ritz, 2020 — *[VERIFY: confirm verbatim from Zotero/NLM before final]*). One enlarges the hypothesis space; the other ranks within it.

[^continental-b]: I engage this lineage as a *diagnosis* and decline its governing ontology: my own diagnosis of the modern condition is Uegakian — the universalizing elevation of the sovereign subject — not Heideggerian (enframing/*Gestell*); the precise grounds on which Heidegger's ontology and method are declined are set out with the method (method-frame §6.2). I treat Stiegler and Hui as *post-Heideggerian* — heirs who rework Heidegger's account of technics (Stiegler's exteriorization and tertiary retention; Hui's cosmotechnics) — rather than as "poststructuralists," a label that would misdescribe the specific philosophy-of-technology inheritance at issue here. [VERIFY: Heidegger citekey + texts — QCT (1977) and, for *Gelassenheit*, *Discourse on Thinking*; Stiegler `YPJ94B8W`; Hui 2016 citekey pending.]

[^critical-theory-b]: This second Continental lineage is the critical theory of modernity, distinct from the philosophy of technology though intersecting it: Weber on disenchantment (*Entzauberung*) and rationalization; Adorno and Horkheimer on the dialectic of enlightenment and instrumental reason; Habermas on the colonization of the lifeworld by system imperatives; and Rosa on acceleration and the modern drive toward *controllability* (*Verfügbarkeit*) of the world. The thesis draws on this tradition's diagnosis of modernity's characteristic disposition while, again, declining to take any one of its frameworks as the governing ontology. [VERIFY: citekeys — Weber, Adorno & Horkheimer *Dialectic of Enlightenment*, Habermas *Theory of Communicative Action*, Rosa *The Uncontrollability of the World* / *Resonance*; resolve against Zotero before final.]

[^bridges-b]: The two bridges are kept analytically distinct because they do different work. The *postcolonial* critique (Mignolo's decoloniality and the colonial matrix of power; de Sousa Santos's epistemologies of the South and the "pluriverse"; Chakrabarty's provincializing of Europe and, latterly, the planetary) carries the critique of modernity outward into the standing of *other worlds* — the multiplicity of meaning-spaces that a single modern world would override. The **post-anthropocentric "ontological turn" in anthropology** (Descola; Viveiros de Castro) makes a different move: it dissolves the nature/culture partition and unseats the sovereign human as the privileged term. I draw this second bridge from the anthropology the thesis already engages — Descola's four ontologies, which reduce the modern nature/culture partition to one cosmology among several (Chapter III §A), and Viveiros de Castro's perspectivism and multinaturalism, which invert the modern settlement of one nature and many cultures (§IIC) — rather than from a separate posthumanist apparatus. Descola and Viveiros de Castro are not "posthumanism" in the philosophical-school sense (Braidotti, Wolfe, Haraway), but the ontological turn does the same decentering work while staying inside the thesis's evidence base. Collapsing the second bridge into the first would lose exactly what each contributes: one can de-provincialize the world's plurality without dissolving the nature/culture partition, and one can dissolve that partition without addressing colonial difference. The thesis needs both, separately. [^synthetic-anthropology-b]: Synthetic anthropology (総合人間学, *sōgō ningengaku*) is the program of the Japan Association of Synthetic Anthropology (総合人間学会, *Sōgō Ningen Gakkai*, founded 2006), which pursues the single question "what does it mean to be human?" against the fragmentation of the specialist sciences, holding that the contradictions of contemporary civilization "have their roots in the self-alienation of the human being" [VERIFY: synthetic-anthropology.org, statement of purpose, page_id=379 / 2019 revision page_id=1932, accessed 2026-06]. It traces to the animal ethologist Obara Hideo (小原秀雄, *Obara Hideo*), whose theory of self-domestication (自己家畜化論, *jiko kachikuka-ron*) holds that humans alone evolve under an environment of their own making: through the recursive production of "things" (モノ, *mono* — material tools, but also language, institutions, and worldview) humanity replaces the natural ecosystem with a self-made "artificial ecosystem" (人工生態系, *jinkō seitaikei*), so that biological "Homo" (ヒト, *hito*) becomes socio-cultural "Human" (人間, *ningen*), reshaped by "auto-artificial selection" (自己人為淘汰, *jiko jin'i tōta*) rather than natural selection [VERIFY: obaraDakaraGishikiWa / obaraKyoikuWaNingen, Obara NLM notebook; and the model account in 「自己家畜化論」, *Synthetic Anthropology Keyword Collection* (KW2021), pp. 23–24, co-authored Anami Shin'ichi, Uegaki Takahide 上柿崇英, Haseba Takeshi — page numbers TBD]. Uegaki Takahide (上柿崇英, *Uegaki Takahide*) systematizes it: between "Homo" (ヒト) and the natural ecosystem stands "society" (社会, *shakai*) as an artificial ecosystem, and once industrialization freed this layer from ecological limits its "boundless expansion of 'things' now conflicts not only with the global natural ecosystem but also with 'Homo,' the inner nature of humans, triggering a 'crisis of humanity' simultaneously with the 'environmental crisis'" [VERIFY: uegakiSogoNingenGaku2017, pp. 254–257; confirm against Zotero PDF]. The tradition urges not a return to wild nature but a recalibration toward a "higher-order naturalness" (高次の自然さ, *kōji no shizensa*) [VERIFY: Obara NLM notebook, pages TBD]. *I note, per the locked glossary, that this movement's "domination-by-モノ" register must not be flattened onto Uegaki's own normative position, where the apparatus figures as* entrustment *— delivering freedom, equality, autonomy, and conviviality — rather than domination.*

[^triangulation-b]: The East–West reading is itself a method commitment, not an incidental comparison. The thesis triangulates the Western diagnoses (the critical theory of modernity and the philosophy of technology) against the East-Asian ones (synthetic anthropology, the real / actual / empirical stratification rendered as 道理相 (*dào-lǐ-xiàng*), and the ideal of 和合 (*héhé*, harmonious union)): where the two traditions *converge* on the same diagnosis from independent starting points, that convergence counts as mutual *corroboration*; where they *diverge*, each is read as a *correction or mitigation* of the other's blind spots. The full warrant for reasoning this way — the claim that standing at the seam between the traditions affords a vantage neither commands alone — is developed in Chapter IV ("the vantage from the seam"). > Source: NLM **Martin Heidegger** notebook, id `5bcb5a9c-d010-4338-8401-9562dc581b76` (45 sources), queried 2026-06-08. Excerpts are verbatim from the notebook's cited_text (which quotes the primary texts — *Question Concerning Technology* / *Discourse on Thinking* / *Being and Time* — and the secondary literature: Sheehan, Polt/Wrathall-type commentary, etc.). **Claim (i): Heidegger's "history of Being" dissolves the given's mind-independent independence into modes of revealing/disclosure (i.e., he is not a realist about an intransitive universe in Bhaskar's sense).**
- **Verdict: OVERSTATED → corrected.** As stated, the claim is too strong: Heidegger is a realist *about entities*. He affirms that "What is … is never of our making or even merely a product of our minds"; that "entities … are as the entities they always were before Dasein encountered them"; and, most pointedly, that "**Nature does not need disclosedness, revealedness … to be as it is**." Sheehan: "things are quite independent of the experience, knowledge, and grasping by which they are disclosed."
- **What *does* hold (the corrected, narrower claim used in the draft):** it is the *Being* of entities — their intelligibility, significance, *aletheia* — that Heidegger makes disclosure-dependent and Dasein-dependent: "**Being (not entities) is dependent upon the understanding of Being**"; "entities are uncovered only when Dasein is." Sheehan: Heidegger's topic "was not *Sein* but *Sinn* … the intelligibility and meaningfulness of things." This is precisely the dimension a critical-realist intransitive universe needs to keep *non*-disclosure-dependent. So the thesis's reason for declining Heidegger's ontology stands — but it must be stated as anti-realism about *Being/intelligibility*, not about entities. **The draft (A.4, A.5(i), B) is written to this corrected formulation.**

**Claim (ii): his account of the technological mode is a single, epochal "destining of Being" (Geschick) rather than a stratified/multi-causal one.**
- **Verdict: ACCURATE, with one qualification.** Confirmed verbatim: "**Enframing belongs within the destining of revealing**"; "The essence of modern technology … We shall call that sending-that-gathers … which first starts man upon a way of revealing, **destining [Geschick]**. It is from out of this destining that the essence of all history is determined." Heidegger expressly refuses multi-causal / historiographical explanation: "never does the historical — let alone happening itself as represented historiographically — determine destining." So "single epochal destining, not a multi-causal/stratified explanation" is correct.
- **Qualification (folded in, lightly):** the single sending is not a *neat deterministic sequence* — "In the destiny of Being there is never a mere sequence one after another … there is always a passing by and simultaneity of the early and late," and "destining is never a fate that compels." This does not rescue it as *stratified/multi-causal* in the thesis's sense (it remains one onto-historical sending, not a plurality of co-acting mechanisms across levels); the draft therefore keeps the contrast but does not caricature it as crude determinism.

**Claim (iii): his response — Gelassenheit / the "saving power" — is quietist rather than an analytic, actionable program.**
- **Verdict: ACCURATE (strongest-confirmed of the three).** Verbatim: "**Human activity can never directly counter this danger. Human achievement alone can never banish it. But human reflection can ponder …**"; the Hölderlin line "**But where danger is, grows / The saving power also**"; *Gelassenheit* as "**releasement toward things**" — a comportment that says "yes" and "no" to devices. A secondary source states it flatly: "Heidegger calls for resignation and passivity (*Gelassenheit*) **rather than an active program of reform** which would simply constitute a further extension of modern technology." "Quietist / meditative rather than an actionable program" is correct as stated. (The draft uses "meditative releasement … awaited rather than built," which is faithful and slightly less polemical than "quietist.")
