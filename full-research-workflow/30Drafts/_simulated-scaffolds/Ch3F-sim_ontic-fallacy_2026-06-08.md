## §F (recalibrated / expanded)

**¶ (opening — retained):** The movement still opens as the current draft does: the material conditions explain how the mode could arise and entrench, but not the feature that makes it *dangerous*; what converts intervention into universalization is "something at the level of how the mode knows," a structure in its way of relating thought to being, whose spine is Bhaskar's critical realism (current l.335).

**¶ (epistemic fallacy — retained, lightly extended):** The epistemic fallacy is stated as in the current draft (Bhaskar, 2008: 25): the reduction of *what is there?* to *what can we know?*, so that being is silently rewritten as the knowable (retained l.339). Stated plainly, the epistemic fallacy *de-realizes the world*: it lets our knowledge stand in for what there is, so that whatever does not fit our way of knowing is denied the status of the real. The one addition is to name the move in Bhaskar's own terms: it collapses the *intransitive* dimension — the real structures and mechanisms that exist and act independently of us — into the *transitive*, the historically given materials of inquiry.[^epistemic-transitive]

**¶ (NEW — the ontic fallacy as the dialectical pair):**
But the epistemic fallacy never travels alone, and naming its companion is what this section adds to the current draft, because the companion is the deeper form of the disposition this whole thesis tracks. Bhaskar gives it a name of its own: the *ontic fallacy*. If the epistemic fallacy reduces being to knowledge, the ontic fallacy runs the move in reverse — Bhaskar's compact phrase for it is the "compulsive determination of knowledge by being" (Bhaskar, 2008a). Stated plainly: our made knowledge is projected onto the universe as the universe's own structure — the map mistaken for the territory. Where the epistemic fallacy lets our way of knowing swallow what is independently there, the ontic fallacy takes our historically produced, fallible knowing and installs it as the world's own eternal furniture.[^ontic-symmetric]

**¶ (NEW — the complicity, and the recurrence theme):**
The two are not alternatives but accomplices. Bhaskar speaks of the "tacit complicity" of the epistemic and ontic fallacies — "twinned fallacies" that "characteristically presuppose each other" (Bhaskar, 2009: 16): a way of knowing that has first reduced being to its own knowable then projects that knowable back onto the world as its very furniture, fusing world and knowledge into a single closed loop in which the human's construction confronts the human as the given itself. This loop is the recurrent figure of the whole thesis, named here in its philosophical form. It is the *hypostatization* — treating something made (a concept, an idea) as if it were a free-standing thing in the world — that returns at every level of the argument: the dis-embedded subject of the previous movements, hypostatized from a useful abstraction into a metaphysical fact; the made order that, in the chapters on the metaverse, comes to stand with the unanswerable authority of a given — a *tekhnē* that has come to function as a *phusis*, a made order operating with the authority of the given. The ontic fallacy is that movement caught at the level of knowledge: the made mistaken for, and installed in the place of, the given.

**¶ (NEW — the ontic fallacy IS enlightenment-centrism; bridges to the retained l.342):**
This is why the diagnosis the current draft already gives — the *anthropic fallacy* and its variant *enlightenment-centrism*, "the reading of all reality in the image of the Enlightenment mode's way of knowing, and the placing of that mode at the center of the real" (retained l.342) — is best understood as the ontic fallacy run in one particular key. To universalize is, at the level of metaphysics, to commit the ontic fallacy with a specific content: to take *this* mode's made knowing and hypostatize it as the structure of being as such, so that every other world's way of knowing appears not as another fallible purchase on the same universe but as so much error scattered around the one reality. The mode does not merely reduce being to the knowable (epistemic); it crowns its own knowable as being (ontic). That is the precise mechanism by which a local way of knowing forgets its own locality — the pathology the current draft names (retained l.343).

**¶ (NEW — the primal squeeze; carries F-a in the body):**
And here Bhaskar supplies a figure whose name is not accidental to this thesis. He locates the twin fallacies on what he calls the *Platonic/Aristotelian fault-line*, and argues that thought trapped there is subjected to a "primal squeeze" (Bhaskar, 2016).[^primal-squeeze] Stated plainly: thought that lacks any sense of real depth is forced to choose between two ways of closing the gap between knowledge and reality — remaking the world in the image of the mind (rationalism) or dissolving it into sense (empiricism) — and between these two the independent real, the natural necessity of a mind-independent universe, is squeezed out. I take the resonance with this thesis's central figure to be more than verbal, and it is worth being exact about how. Bhaskar's primal squeeze is a squeeze *of thought*: it operates at the level of the relation between knowledge and being, and what it crushes is the real's independence. This thesis's squeeze is a squeeze *of the human*: it operates at the level of existence, and what it crushes is the human pressed between two givens. But the link is constitutive, not coincidental — and the ontic fallacy is what links them. The very operation Bhaskar diagnoses — the hypostatizing of made knowledge until it usurps the place of the given and the genuine given is squeezed out — is, run not in a seminar but across a civilization, the operation by which an artificial order installs itself as a second given and presses the human between it and the universe. The primal squeeze is the epistemological ancestor of the squeeze this thesis is finally about: the same disposition, diagnosed first in the theory of knowledge, then traced into existence. *(I reserve the bare word "squeeze" for the existential figure; Bhaskar's is always the attributed "primal squeeze." See Scaffold-notes — this is the lineage you flagged as part of the term's origin.)*

**¶ (the consequence — retained):** "Here, at last, is the mechanism of universalization stated in its most general form… to know, for this mode, is already to totalize and to exclude" (retained l.340–341), now standing as the *joint* work of the two fallacies rather than the epistemic alone.

## Footnotes

[^epistemic-transitive]: The epistemic fallacy is "the view that statements about being can be reduced to or analyzed in terms of statements about knowledge… that ontological questions can always be transposed into epistemological terms" (Bhaskar, 2008: 25). The intransitive dimension comprises the real structures and mechanisms "that exist and act quite independently of men and the conditions which allow men access" to them (Bhaskar, 2008b: 5); the transitive dimension comprises "the raw materials of science… the antecedently established facts and theories… available to a particular scientific school" (Bhaskar, 2008b: 11). The epistemic fallacy collapses the former into the latter.

[^ontic-symmetric]: Bhaskar names the ontic fallacy the "compulsive determination of knowledge by being," in which knowledge appears "in the guise of reified facts or hypostatized ideas" (Bhaskar, 2008a). He sets the two fallacies out symmetrically: "On the epistemic fallacy, being either just is (constituted by) knowledge or is analysable in terms of the knowledge of objects. On the ontic fallacy, knowledge either just is being simpliciter or is analysable in terms of the being of its objects" (Bhaskar, 2009: 16).

[^primal-squeeze]: The primal squeeze is "the squeeze… exerted within Western philosophy by metaphysics, on the one hand, and empiricism, on the other," a pressure that eliminates between them "empirically controlled scientific theory and its intransitive object and ontological counterpart, natural necessity" (Bhaskar, 2016).
