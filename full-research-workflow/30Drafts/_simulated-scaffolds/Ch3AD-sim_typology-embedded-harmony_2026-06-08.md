## §A — the dyssynchrony, deepened (resolves #A1, #A2)

**¶ (Obara/Uegaki dyssynchrony — replaces current l.260):**
Obara's ecological vocabulary lets us state the tension precisely, and more substantially than a single word can carry. The human lives within an *artificial ecosystem* (人工生態系, jinkō seitaikei) — a made web of provision, built of tools and their derivatives, that has displaced the natural niche so thoroughly that "even soil and air are, in our time, no longer primal nature" (Obara, in Obara and Hani, 1995). Obara's image for our predicament is the coral: the human is the polyp that has built the reef of the city and can no longer live outside it. Within that reef a *dis-synchrony* (ズレ, zure) opens, and Obara is exact about its two dimensions — it is a gap of *speed and of kind* at once: "the change of the system and of the things produced (モノ), and the change of the human as an organism, differ in both speed and quality, and a zure arises between them" (Obara, in Obara and Hani, 1995). The made web races; the biological human barely moves; and the gap between an exponential artifice and a near-static animal is the standing condition of the artificial ecosystem.

Uegaki hardens this slippage into something sharper, and names its architecture. Where Obara sees a *zure* that strains, Uegaki describes a *severance* (setsudan, 切断) — a cutting-apart of what was once continuous — and stages it as a sequence of historical "singularities," each cutting a tie the previous age had taken for granted. The first is the *mediation of the human and nature* (〈人間〉と〈自然〉の間接化): the made world inserts itself between the human and the given, so that the relation between them ceases to be direct and becomes routed through artifice. This is not yet a cut but an interposition — the very work this thesis has named world-construction as an *adaptational operating system* (AOS) in Chapter I, the layer through which the human henceforth meets the universe. The second singularity is the *severance of society from nature* (〈社会〉と〈自然〉の切断): once fossil energy releases the social order from the carrying capacity of its territory, society "continues to expand itself by the logic of society alone, ignoring any consistency with nature." Here interposition becomes severance proper — the made order no longer answers to the given but follows its own momentum, which is precisely the diagnosis of the Western rise that Pomeranz and Miura supply below (§E). The third, and the one that defines our own moment, is the *severance of society from the human* (〈社会〉と〈人間〉の切断): the social order so fully provisions existence that, "for the first time in seven million years of human history, living no longer requires the existence of flesh-and-blood others" (Uegaki, 2022a). This last severance is the condition that the type-3 and type-4 squeeze will name in Chapter VI — the human held apart not only from the natural given but from the fellow humans through whom a world was always made. The three are thus not a flat list but a deepening: an interposition that becomes a severance from nature and then a severance from one another, each stage pulling the made world further from the substrate it was built upon. That progressive severance is the squeeze in its severe form, and it is the form toward which, this thesis will argue, the Enlightenment mode tends; Uegaki's diagnosis is revisited, and pressed harder, in Chapter VI. *(Terminology fix: this is 切断/severance, Uegaki's term for the world↔environment rupture; his 亀裂/fracture names a different gap — ideal vs. reality — and belongs to the later chapters. See Scaffold-notes.)*

**¶ (the qualification — embeddedness + answerability as the non-Enlightenment baseline; #A2):**
One qualification must be entered with care, because the whole moral of the chapter depends on it: to say that a world *can* be squeezed against the universe is not to say that it *must* be. The squeeze is a danger built into world-making, not a fate sealed by it — and human traditions have known the danger and built against it. What such traditions hold in common is not a doctrine but a default orientation: the made world is understood as *embedded* within the given, and the human as *answerable to* it. The relation runs in that direction — the human answers to the universe and to the world it sustains — and it is precisely this direction that the Enlightenment mode reverses, setting the subject over the given and demanding that the given answer to it instead. Embeddedness and answerability are the baseline; sovereignty is the departure from it.

The Sinic figure of *he/ho* (harmony, 和/合) is one mode of that baseline, and worth marking precisely because the English "harmony" misleads (see note). In its own idiom it names not a settled, finished state but a processual attunement — a never-completed effort to be in accord with a counterpart, to co-become with it. Classical formulations make the point: *harmony without sameness* (和而不同, hé ér bù tóng), in which accord is achieved *between* differences rather than by erasing them; and *harmony generates the myriad things* (和實生物, hé shí shēng wù), in which accord is generative, the very source of plurality rather than its dissolution. So understood, the Confucian unity of Heaven and humanity (天人合一, tiānrén héyī) names a world deliberately tuned to keep its made order answerable to the given one, and the Daoist *wu wei* (無為) — not inaction but, in Needham's rendering, "refraining from activity contrary to Nature" — describes a stance that "produces without possessing, acts without self-assertion, and develops without dominating" (Needham, 1956). Yet *he/ho* is only *one* approach to embedding the made world in the given. The impulse is not uniquely Chinese: Descola's animism is another such settlement — the Achuar and Makuna of the Upper Amazon meet animals and plants as persons with souls like their own, a relation of negotiation rather than dominion (Descola, 2013) — and the Andean ideal of *sumak kawsay* (buen vivir) and the Māori principle of *kaitiakitanga* (guardianship of land and water held in trust across generations) each articulate, in their own idiom, the same binding of the made order to the given one. These are not claims that such worlds were ever perfectly realized, nor that they were superior; the point is only that traditions have *articulated* embeddedness and answerability as constitutive values, and in doing so held the squeeze at bay. A mode of artificiality that abandons that answerability for mastery does not merely differ in style from them; it courts the very squeeze they were built to prevent. *(The Andean/Māori examples are LOOKUP-pending — see Scaffold-notes.)*

> **[Footnote]** The English word *harmony* is an imperfect carrier for the Sinic *he/ho* (和/合). In ordinary English usage "harmony with nature" tends to connote a static, Edenic condition — a passive, pre-corrupted innocence — and it is exactly this romanticized image that Graeber and Wengrow (2021) demolish, showing it to be neither historically accurate nor politically innocent. That critique is real and is taken up where it belongs: in Chapter I's rejection of the nature/culture split, where Graeber and Wengrow's counter-evidence against the romanticized, nostalgic image of "nature" is cited directly. It does not bear on the present point, and *he/ho* must not be conflated with the static "harmony" they dismantle: the Sinic figure is processual and generative — an ongoing attunement, *he ér bù tóng* — not a frozen Eden. Graeber and Wengrow are therefore **not** cited here in support of harmony; the argument of this section concerns embeddedness and answerability, of which *he/ho* is one processual mode, not "harmony" in the sense their critique targets.

## §D — the sovereign subject as one configuration among others (resolves #D1, #D2)

**¶ (soften the typology — replaces current l.293 "Three configurations can be distinguished"):**
To name the mode by the sovereignty of the subject is to make a claim the mode itself cannot easily hear: that this sovereignty is a *configuration* — one settlement of what a self is — rather than the shape of the human as such. The point does not require an exhaustive taxonomy of selves, and I do not attempt one; I have already drawn the distinctions the argument needs, and a comprehensive typology here would make empirical claims the argument can do without. It is enough to set the sovereign subject beside *some* ways of being a self that do not posit it, so that it is seen as a selection and not a given. Consider three, offered as illustrations rather than as a closed grid. There is the self-subsistent self that *masters* the given — the sovereign subject this chapter studies. There is the self-subsistent self that *withdraws* from it — the renouncer who keeps his separateness but turns from mastery to release: the contemplative of the desert, the yogin of classical Sāṃkhya, for whom liberation (*kaivalya*) is the discernment that pure consciousness (*puruṣa*) is not, and never truly was, entangled in nature (*prakṛti*) (Larson, 1979); the soul of Advaita Vedānta seeking to recognize its non-difference from Brahman behind the veil of a world deemed *māyā* (Deutsch, 1969). *(This withdrawing-but-still-separate self is the ancient cousin of the modern nihilist/lying-flat refusal noted in §C — both decline mastery without dissolving the separate self; I cross-link them lightly.)*

**¶ (the embedded self — replaces current l.293's "not separate" + #D2 fix):**
And there is a third settlement that does the decisive work, because it declines at the root the very separateness the first two share. This self is not *un-separated* — collapsed into an undifferentiated continuum — but *embedded*: nested within a given whose layers of irreducibility it does not erase. Embeddedness keeps the strata (the person within a world, the world within the universe); what it refuses is the *elevation* of the self above them. The Buddhist teaching of no-self (anātman, 無我) holds that there is no abiding, self-subsistent self to be sovereign in the first place — "this is not mine, this I am not, this is not my self" (SN 22.59, in Bodhi, 2000). The Daoist equalizing of things (qí wù, 齊物, in the Zhuangzi) levels the self with the myriad things, refusing it the privileged standpoint from which sovereignty could be claimed: "Heaven and earth were born at the same time I was, and the ten thousand things are one with me" (Zhuangzi, ch. 2, trans. Watson, 2013). Where the sovereign subject begins from a self set over a world, the embedded self begins from no such elevation — and from layered nestedness, not flat fusion.

**¶ (no East/West romance — retained, with verified Western lineages):**
The contrast must be drawn with one discipline above all: this is not a contrast between a sovereign West and a self-dissolving East, a romance this thesis refuses. The declension of the separate self has its own Western lineages — Meister Eckhart's apophatic *Gelassenheit*, the releasement in which the willing self is emptied so that God may be born in the soul (Walshe, 2009); and Schopenhauer, who read the *principium individuationis* as a veil of Māyā over a deeper undivided reality, drawing the thought, by his own account, from Indian sources (Schopenhauer, WWR I, §63). One nuance marks a genuine difference rather than a likeness to be smoothed over: the Western mystical self-emptying is an emptying *into* God, a transcendent ground that receives the dissolved self, whereas the Buddhist no-self dissolves into *no* ground at all. Both decline the sovereign subject; they do not decline it identically. What is at issue, then, is not a geography but distinct settlements of what a self is — and the embedded settlements show that the separateness on which the sovereign subject is built is something some traditions decline outright (retained, with cites supplied).

**¶ (the anti-prescriptive discipline — retained):** "These traditions are invoked here for one purpose only… not as a program to be adopted… what these configurations offer is not an exit but a vantage… the one vantage from which the sovereignty of the subject can be seen at all" (retained l.295).
