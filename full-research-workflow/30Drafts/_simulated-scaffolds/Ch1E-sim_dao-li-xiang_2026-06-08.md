# Ch I §E — Introducing the three strata, the real / actual / empirical (道 / 理 / 相), once (simulated prose)

> Docking point: Chapter I §E, "World as AOS in the universe," at the paragraph that currently begins "The relationships among universe, world, and operating system can be set out as three ontological layers…" (manuscript line ~148), immediately before **Table 1-1**. This is where Adler's inline note asks for the triad to be introduced once: *"the East Asian (don't reduce it to Chinese) 道, 理, 相 perhaps should be introduced once here, and don't appear again in the later chapters unless necessary for the argument. you can harvest the introduction from where it currently locates in this draft and use it here."*
>
> The prose below is the section as it would read. It replaces the existing two-sentence lead-in to Table 1-1 and threads the romanized-and-glossed triad into it. The naming of the made (人為, *rénwéi*) — the prefix *wěi* (偽) — is held back to a single forward-pointing clause, since the 偽-prefixed terms do not appear until Chapter V; the point here is only to license the bare script later.

---

The relationships among universe, world, and operating system can be set out as three ontological layers — the layer of (1) the enduring conditions or principles that organize things, (2) the structures or dynamics those principles organize, and (3) the context-specific phenomena, experienceable or observable to a subject, in which those structures are manifested. This stratification is the point at which two vocabularies this thesis draws on converge. It integrates the language of complex adaptive systems and self-organization — its talk of levels, emergent order, and dynamics that play out across multiple scales — with critical realism's stratified ontology, mapping the systems vocabulary of levels onto the critical-realist strata of the real, the actual, and the empirical: the multi-scale conditions that organize a system answer to the layer of the real, the structures and dynamics they generate to the actual, and the observable behavior in which they surface to the empirical.[^dlx-cas] And at the same time it articulates the very same stratification in East Asian terms, which had already drawn the distinction with economy. This is not a quaint parallel to a Western scheme but a third articulation of one stratified ontology. I name the first layer for the enduring conditions or principles that organize things — the real (道, *dào*); the second for the structures and dynamics those principles organize — the actual (理, *lǐ*); and the third for the context-specific phenomena experienceable to a subject — the empirical (相, *xiàng*) — 道 the way things follow, 理 their constitutive pattern (the grain in jade or wood), 相 the aspect they present to a looking eye.[^dlx-strata] The critical-realist terms — real, actual, empirical — I keep as handles for cross-reference and let the three characters carry the argument, since the relation among the three — a contingent surface seen through to a more enduring ground — is exactly the one the East Asian usage already names, and one that, as later chapters will show, the made can counterfeit but not escape.[^dlx-cr] The table below maps the universe, an adaptational operating system, and a world each onto these three layers, so that the same stratified structure can be read across all three at a glance; read down a column, a domain falls into its three layers, and read across a row, the three domains share a layer. The same three layers will return when, in Chapter V, I come to the metaverse, where each is marked with the prefix for the humanly-made — *wěi* (偽), in Xunzi's old sense of the made (人為, *rénwéi*): "made," not "fake"[^dlx-wei]; but the triad itself I introduce only here.

[^dlx-strata]: The three-layer gloss: enduring conditions/principles (the real — 道, *dào*); structures/dynamics (the actual — 理, *lǐ*); context-specific phenomena observable to a subject (the empirical — 相, *xiàng*). "Real / actual / empirical" are critical-realist handles only — parenthetical at first mention and nowhere in the running body. Each character is chosen for its classical, not modern, sense, and each is meant to do the work its stratum requires. **道** in its root sense is a road or way — the path things follow and the generative source-principle from which an order issues; it sits at the layer of the real because it names not a particular structure but the enduring principle by which structures come to be at all. **理** originally names the veins or grain in jade and wood (the lines along which a thing rightly splits), and from there the immanent, constitutive pattern of a thing — later the structuring principle of Neo-Confucian metaphysics; it sits at the actual because it names the structures and dynamics through which a principle is articulated, not the principle's bare ground. 韓非子 「理者，成物之文也」 ("理 is the pattern that completes a thing") makes the structural sense explicit, and Xunzi's defining of the made through 文理 ("偽者，文理隆盛也") is what later licenses the made counterpart 偽理. **相** has the eye at its root — 《說文》「相，省視也，从目从木」 ("相: to look closely at; from *eye* and *tree*") — so its core sense is the look or aspect a thing presents to an observing subject; it sits at the empirical because that is exactly what the empirical layer is: the contingent appearance available to perception, as against the 性/essence behind it. The 相 of the Buddhist 體‑相‑用 triad (substance / appearance / function) and of 性相/法相 (the *lakṣaṇa*, the manifest mark) carries this phenomenal sense; the thesis borrows the *epistemology* it implies — that the observed aspect is contingent and must be seen *through* to a more enduring ground — without taking on the 唯識 idealism with which 相 is sometimes paired, since the thesis remains realist about the universe (see `[^dlx-cr]`). 相 is preferred over the near-synonym 象 (image/likeness) because 象 is etymologically representational — 韓非子's 「案其圖以想其生…謂之象」 makes 象 a *conceived image of an absent thing* — so its made form, 偽象, would denote a *simulacrum*, which clashes with this thesis's causally-real, non-simulationist virtuality, whereas 相's only unwanted association (the 金剛經's 「凡所有相皆是虛妄」) is a famous-text resonance a single gloss can quarantine. *(The character-by-character rationale is the author's own, set out in `30Drafts/_notes/three-layer-chinese-ontology-2026-06-02.md` and confirmed as a tentative-pending-finalization decision; the classical loci — 《說文》, 韓非子《解老》, 荀子《禮論》 — are the author's vetted citations.
