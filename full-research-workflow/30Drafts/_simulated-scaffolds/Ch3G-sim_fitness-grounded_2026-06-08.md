## §G (recalibrated / grounded)

**¶ (the objection + the distinction — retained):** The movement still opens with the natural objection (if the mode spread across the planet, is that not proof it is the fittest?) and the distinction that answers it: between *fitness to a world* and *fitness to the universe* (retained l.348–349).

**¶ (the circular reading — grounded with Crosby + Amazonia):**
On the easier reading — that to be fittest is to prevail within the world the mode itself has made — the claim is a circle wearing the costume of an argument: the Enlightenment mode sets the very standard of prevailing (productivity, growth, measurable control), so to say it best achieves those is to say nothing. Alfred Crosby, the historian of ecological imperialism, supplies the principle that exposes the circle. A "weed," he observes, "is not a scientific word"; it "refers not to plants of any specific species… but to whatever plants spring up where humans do not want them" (Crosby, 1986: ch. 7) — superiority is meaningless except relative to a particular environment and a particular standard of fit. Change the standard and the ranking changes with it. And the change is not hypothetical. Flip the standard from expansion to *biodiversity-facilitation*, and a different mode of world-making rises to the top. There is now substantial evidence that the long Indigenous management of Amazonia did not deplete its forests but helped compose them: domesticated species are, on one large study, "five times more likely than nondomesticated species to be hyperdominant," their abundance rising on and around ancient settlement (Levis et al., 2017), while the anthropogenic dark earths (terra preta) record a deliberate enrichment of soil itself (Clement et al., 2015). On a ledger that counted the fostering of biodiversity as success, an Amazonian mode of world-making would rank among the fittest, and the European mode — which Crosby shows succeeding precisely by *simplifying* and *replacing* the biota it met — among the least. The mode is fittest by its own lights; but its own lights are not the universe's, and there is no neutral scale on which its kind of success counts as success as such. *(On the scale-dependence of "fittest," the circular objection collapses into a tautology.)*

**¶ (the harder reading + the resilience cluster, grounded by sheep/grass/wolves):**
The harder reading is the one worth answering: granting that the internal standard proves nothing, is the mode not fitter *to the universe* — by the one standard not of any world's making, endurance under real constraint? Asked in the universe's own coin, the verdict reverses. A mode that has universalized has, by that fact, converted a plural biosphere of world-making strategies into a *monoculture* of one — and monoculture, by everything the science of resilience has learned, is the fragile form, not the fit one. The simplest model makes the danger vivid. In a classical predator–prey ecology, let the sheep gain solidarity and drive off every wolf: released from predation, the flock grazes the grass faster than it can regenerate, the pasture fails, and the sheep that had mastered their environment starve in it. Dominance over an environment is not fitness but the manufacture of fragility — and the resilience literature names why at each turn, each result with its own visible case. C. S. Holling separated *engineering stability* (fast return to one equilibrium) from *resilience proper* (the capacity to persist and reorganize through large disturbance), and showed that a system optimized for the first is brittle to the second (Gunderson and Holling, 2002); his own forests make it concrete — a stand held in a single tidy state by fire suppression looks stable for decades while it quietly accumulates the fuel, or the spruce-budworm pressure, that one disturbance then releases catastrophically — the flock tuned to grow is the flock that cannot survive the shock. Brian Walker and David Salt draw the management lesson: a system cannot maximize persistence, adaptability, and transformability at once (Walker and Salt, 2006); an over-optimized rangeland, stocked and grazed for maximum present yield, is precisely the one that fails when the drought it was never built to absorb arrives — the sheep bought present performance with future adaptive capacity. Nassim Taleb sharpens it: suppressing volatility does not remove risk but stores it, converting many survivable shocks into one unsurvivable one (Taleb, 2012); the decades of damped volatility before 2008 did not abolish financial risk but concentrated it into a single systemic break, just as a levee that spares a valley every small flood is building toward the one flood it cannot hold — no wolves, no small corrections, until the one large correction is starvation. And Tainter's diminishing returns on complexity complete the picture (Tainter, 1988): like the late Roman Empire or the Classic Maya, each buying ever-costlier layers of administration and infrastructure until the marginal return went negative, the universalized mode keeps buying control at a rising marginal cost until the cost of holding the monoculture together exceeds what it can pay.

**¶ (the inversion — retained):** "Put together, these results invert the objection exactly… Universalization is not fitness. It is the manufacture of a single point of failure on a planetary scale" (retained l.353).

**¶ (externalization + Ponting — retained, with the micro-edit):**
The fragility has a particular source, and naming it precisely matters, because it is easy to mistake the argument for a moral one it is not making. The mode appears to succeed only by continually externalizing its costs — onto a colony, a future, an atmosphere, a buffer somewhere outside its own ledger. I do not raise this as a moral indictment. **Evolution is impartial**, and the universe keeps no fairness-ledger; the charge against externalization is not that it is unjust but that it is structurally self-undermining, because the buffers it draws on are finite and it has no plan for their exhaustion. Clive Ponting found the recurring pattern: "a succession of ever more complex and environmentally damaging ways of meeting the same basic human needs," each escalation buying time by reaching for a larger buffer than the last (Ponting, 1991) — and the buffers deplete (retained l.354, with "built on cruelty" struck per your edit).

**¶ (depth-of-overwriting + monoculture + colonization-as-ontology-replacement — expanded):**
The deepest form of the cost is what universalization does at the level of a world's *enduring organizing principles*, and here the monoculture is not only agricultural but ontological. The agricultural case names the mechanism plainly: when the United States planted some eighty-five per cent of its corn on a single cytoplasmic source, a single blight in 1970 took roughly a sixth of the crop — uniformity is vulnerability (National Research Council, 1972); the Irish dependence on one clonal potato a century earlier had taught the same lesson at the cost of a famine. What holds for a crop holds for a world. To overwrite another world is not equally costly at every depth, and the cost scales with the layer overwritten. At the surface — particular tools and recipes — transfer is nearly free; worlds have always borrowed there. One layer down, at structures and dynamics, transfer means a functional re-coding, exacting a real but bounded price. But at the deepest layer — the enduring organizing principle, the Dao (道), of a world, its basic settlement of what there is and how a life is to be lived — there is no re-fitting, only replacement. This is what recasts colonization in the terms of this thesis: not, at its deepest, the seizure of land or labour, but the *replacement of one world's ontology by another* — the abolition, at the Dao-layer, of an entire alternative way of organizing a world at all. The Enlightenment mode's universalization reaches past the phenomena and the structures to the principles, and there it does not exchange but erase; and each such erasure is one more strain of the planetary monoculture, one more alternative way-of-being subtracted from the species' reserve. *(This connects #G4's monoculture point to the stratified-ontology recontextualization of colonization we discussed; the Dao/Li/Xiang (道/理/相) depth-of-transfer is the same triad introduced once in Ch I §E.)*

**¶ (coherence with the collapse movement — retained):** "And this coheres exactly with the lesson of the second movement… Dominance is not fitness, and collapse is not unfitness; both are phenomena of the squeeze between a world and the universe" (retained l.356).
