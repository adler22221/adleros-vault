# Political Economy of Education Resource Allocation Under Crisis — Literature Brief

Brief for Routledge *World Yearbook of Education* Vol 29 (*Education and Planetary Crises*) chapter proposal. Maps the literatures, names anchor authors, surfaces the specific gap that an **allocation-dependence** lens can fill.

## 1. State of the Field — Fragmented Across Three Subfields

The literature on education funding under planetary crisis exists but is split across three weakly communicating subfields:

- **Education-in-emergencies / humanitarian** (UNHCR, INEE, Education Cannot Wait, Geneva Global Hub): operational, displacement-focused, framed as humanitarian shortfall. Headline figures: 117.3M forcibly displaced (end-2023), ~14.8M school-age refugees, ~half out of school; UNHCR 2023 refugee-education budget 56% unmet (US$211M of US$478M) (UNHCR 2024; INEE 2024).
- **Climate–education finance** (UNICEF, World Bank GPSS, GPE, Green Climate Fund/BRACE): the "1.5% problem" — education receives <1.5% of global climate finance and education is unmentioned in the COP28 US$700M Loss & Damage fund (UNICEF Innocenti 2023; World Bank 2024). 242M students in 85 countries had schooling disrupted by extreme climate events in 2024 (UNICEF 2025).
- **Critical political economy of education** (Ball, Robertson, Verger): treats funding as a governance, marketisation, and privatisation question — but largely *not* climate-keyed.

These three rarely cite each other in the same chapter. **That gap is the chapter's opening.**

## 2. Conceptual Frameworks and Anchor Authors

### 2.1 Distributive justice (Rawlsian and post-Rawlsian)
- **Anchors:** Caney (2014, *Distributive Justice and Climate Change*); Moellendorf; Shue (climate justice as distributive ethics).
- **Education-keyed application:** "Justice in climate change education: a systematic review" (Tandfonline 2023) — but the dominant register is curricular/pedagogical justice rather than allocative. The *funding* side of distributive climate justice is still underdeveloped for the education sector.

### 2.2 Capability approach
- **Anchors:** Sen; Nussbaum; **Walker** (Univ. Free State) — *Higher Education Pedagogies: A Capabilities Approach*; Walker & Unterhalter (eds.), *Amartya Sen's Capability Approach and Social Justice in Education* (Palgrave).
- Walker's frame redirects from input-equality to capability-equality — useful for arguing that climate-displaced learners need *non-equivalent* resource bundles, not equal ones. Climate-specific capability work is thin; that's a hook.

### 2.3 Critical political economy of education
- **Ball** — *The Education Debate*; with Collet-Sabé (2024), "The School Is Irredeemable" (Foucauldian critique of school-as-institution). Ball's three contexts of policy-making (influence, text, practice) translate cleanly to climate-finance flows.
- **Robertson** (Cambridge; *Globalisation, Societies and Education*) — Critical Cultural Political Economy of Education (CCPEE); global governance of teachers' work; risk industry and universities.
- **Verger** (Autonomous Univ. Barcelona) — *The Privatization of Education: A Political Economy of Global Education Reform* (with Fontdevila & Zancajo, 2017). **Crucially identifies "privatization via disaster" as one of six privatisation paths** — direct bridge to crisis-allocation analysis.

### 2.4 Loss-and-damage and climate finance applied to education
- **Anchors:** UNICEF Innocenti (2023), *Loss and Damage Finance for Children*; Adrienne Robertson and the Loss & Damage Collaboration; World Bank *Choosing Our Future: Education for Climate Action* (2024); GPE Climate-Smart Education Systems.
- Empirical headline: Eastern & Southern Africa losses already US$1.3B in direct damage to schools; global $120–140B in lost lifetime earnings since 2005 from climate-disrupted schooling (UNICEF ESA 2024; World Bank 2024). The L&D literature is policy-vibrant but theoretically thin on **why** education keeps getting allocatively excluded.

### 2.5 Path dependence
- **Anchors:** Pierson (2000), *Increasing Returns, Path Dependence, and the Study of Politics*; Mahoney; recent applications: Beyer (2026, *Review of Policy Research*) "On a branching route"; *History of Education Quarterly* "From Path Dependence to Alternative Paths"; Chinese welfare-policy applications (Education Insights 2024); European higher-ed harmonisation (Robertson & Komljenovic).
- Education-policy applications exist but tend to be retrospective/institutional rather than allocative.

## 3. Empirical Cases for Crisis-Allocation

- **Pakistan 2022 floods:** ~20,000 schools destroyed/damaged; only 1 in 5 rebuilt by Nov 2024; children lost 97 school days Jan-2022–Jun-2024 (Save the Children 2024; World Bank). One Sindh climate-resilient school (high ceilings, ventilation, solar) shows what re-allocated infrastructure looks like — but at trivial scale.
- **Ukraine:** World Bank LEARN; school-safety, transport, teacher training continuing under wartime allocation rules — reconstruction begins before war ends.
- **Syria:** protracted-refugee education in neighbouring host systems (Lebanon, Jordan, Türkiye) — the durable test case for allocative inclusion in national systems vs. parallel humanitarian provision.
- **US wildfires / school-district debt:** Climate and Community Institute (*Healthy School Finance*, 2024) on districts trapped in "debt-conditioned austerity" cycles that reproduce racial/class inequities and compound climate costs.
- **Heatwave closures (April 2024, 118M students):** the slow-onset allocative invisibility — no single "disaster" event, but cumulative schooling loss with no finance instrument keyed to it.

## 4. The Specific Gap "Allocation Dependence" Can Fill

The "allocation dependence" search returns **zero** hits in education scholarship. The phrase is unclaimed. Three positioning hooks:

- **Hook 1 — Beyond the flow framing.** Most education-finance literature treats the problem as *insufficient flow* (raise the 1.5%, fill the US$267M refugee-education gap). Allocation dependence reframes: existing distributive patterns are **structurally locked in** — climate-finance architectures route to mitigation/infrastructure not education; humanitarian appeals route to food/shelter not schooling; national budgets route to credentialed mainstream pupils not displaced learners. More money in does not redirect; it reinforces. This is a *Pierson-style increasing returns* claim applied to allocation channels rather than to institutions.
- **Hook 2 — Why "privatisation via disaster" is the wrong default.** Verger's sixth path (privatisation-via-disaster) describes what happens when systems are crisis-loaded *and* allocatively brittle. Allocation dependence explains *why* disaster reliably produces privatisation rather than reconstruction-in-kind: the public allocative channel was already path-dependent in directions incompatible with rapid scaling to displaced populations. The framing repositions privatisation-via-disaster as a *predictable allocative failure*, not an opportunistic ideological capture.
- **Hook 3 — Capability-allocation mismatch.** Capability theorists (Walker, Unterhalter) have shown that equal resources do not produce equal capabilities. Allocation dependence adds the structural counterpart: even when systems *want* to allocate to capability-expansion for displaced/disrupted learners, the *channels* through which money moves (per-pupil formulas tied to enrolment registers, infrastructure grants tied to land tenure, teacher salary scales tied to civil-service categories) are dependent on prior allocative architectures that cannot route to climate-displaced learners without structural redesign.

## 5. Adjacent Fields the Chapter Would Bridge

- **Education finance** (Ball, Robertson, Verger, GPE, UNESCO IIEP) ↔
- **Political ecology** (loss-and-damage scholarship; climate justice; Caney; UCS framings) ↔
- **Disaster studies / humanitarian governance** (INEE, ECW, UNHCR; reconstruction literature) ↔
- **STS / historical institutionalism / path dependence** (Pierson, Mahoney; resource-dependence theory — Pfeffer & Salancik via Frooman; "sticky policies" funding literature).

The chapter's contribution is to make **allocation dependence** the connective tissue: a single analytic that travels across the four bodies of work and explains a pattern none of them, alone, fully accounts for — *the persistence of allocative architectures that the planetary crisis has already rendered structurally inadequate*.
