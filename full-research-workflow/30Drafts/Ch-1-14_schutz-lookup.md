## Key Passages

### Passage 1 — Definition of finite provinces of meaning (core passage)
> "We speak of provinces of meaning and not of sub-universes because it is the meaning of our experiences and not the ontological structure of the objects which constitutes reality. Hence we call a certain set of our experiences a finite province of meaning if all of them show a specific cognitive style and are — with respect to this style — not only consistent in themselves but also compatible with one another."

— Schutz, Alfred. "On Multiple Realities." In *Collected Papers I: The Problem of Social Reality*. The Hague: Martinus Nijhoff, 1962. p. 230.

**Relevance:** This is the foundational definition. "Finite province of meaning" — not an ontological category but an experiential one. Reality is constituted by the *meaning* of experiences, not by independent objects. This is categorically different from Leibnizian possible worlds (which are ontological alternatives) and directly supports the thesis's "world" as a human-constructed meaning-space.

---

### Passage 2 — The paramount reality and its sub-universes
> "William James's subuniverses; finite provinces of meaning [...] The paramount reality [...] The transition from the paramount reality to other finite provinces of meaning, experienced through a shock."

— Schutz, *Collected Papers I*, Table of Contents entry for "On Multiple Realities" (pp. 340–343).

**Relevance:** Schutz inherits James's notion of "sub-universes of reality" but reframes it phenomenologically: what James called "sub-universes," Schutz prefers to call "finite provinces of meaning" — emphasizing that the accent of reality is bestowed by the experiencer, not given ontologically. The "shock" of transition between provinces (from everyday life to art, dream, theoretical science) is the mechanism by which we experience reality as multiple.

### Passage 3 — Transition between provinces via shock
From the essay structure: the transition from the "paramount reality" (everyday waking life) to other provinces (art, dream, play, science) is marked by a *shock* — a leap that requires a shift in cognitive style. This is not modal accessibility between possible worlds but phenomenological rupture in lived experience.
