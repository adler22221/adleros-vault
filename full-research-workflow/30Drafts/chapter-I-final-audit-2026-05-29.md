## §2 — Ch I Style Fingerprint + Argumentative Structure

### §2.1 — Style fingerprint (replicable voice features)

**Sentence rhythm**: median ~18–22 words; strong variance — punchy 9–12w empirical openers alternating with 25–35w subordinate-rich expositions. Em-dashes liberal as thinking-aloud pause-markers; commas modest; semicolons rare.

**Paragraph rhythm**: median ~280w. Opening short claim (~100–150w), middle anchor with author or case (~200–300w), closing principle-extraction (~100–150w). Long paragraphs (~400w+) in framework-building zones; punchy paragraphs at case-openings and transitions.

**Voice features**: First-person singular explicit at conceptual moves ("I argue", "I consider", "I do not use the term X because", "I will unpack further"); third-person "this thesis / the chapter" for structural attributions. First-person plural ("we now see") rare and strategic for shared-reasoning hand-offs. Hedged conditionals minimal in framework exposition — assertive, non-tentative tone.

**Citation conventions**: 2–3 parenthetical citations per major section. Block-quotes restrained — typically one extended quote per chapter at the conceptual fulcrum (Heraclitus Fragment 30 in Ch I); medium-length embedded quotes (15–25w) mid-paragraph, never opening a paragraph.

**Signature transitions**: "However, [inversion]" is THE signature pivot (8+ instances in Ch I); "In other words, [restatement]" (3 instances); "Rather, [counter-assertion]" used after "not X" constructions; "As [case/author has shown]" bridges case to principle. Avoids "furthermore"/"moreover" — author favors inversion over accumulation.

**Register switches**: Japanese 〈〉 quotation when English lacks precision (Obara's 自己人為淘汰, 人間/ヒト distinction; Uegaki's 意のままになる生); Latin etymologies for semantic history (virtus/virtualis, ūniversum); German technical philosophy in translation but named for precision (Umwelt/Umgebung). Multilingual is taxonomic, not ornamental.

**Voice tells** (load-bearing constructions repeated as conceptual glue): *adaptational [X]*; *[X] is irreducible to*; *mode of artificiality*; *collective [survival/adaptational] strategies*; *bounded [X]*; *background environment*; *operating system / operate upon*; *incommensurability / commensurability*; *population-dependent totality*; *self-organizing / autopoietic*.

**Avoidance patterns**: chooses narrative over philosophical jargon where the jargon would be available; defines technical terms inline rather than relying on footnoted glossaries; structurally signals what concepts are *not* (negative definition by rejection) as well as what they are.

### §5.2 — My recommendation: Option (6) "Modern Mode of Artificiality"

The strongest single-term candidate is **Option 6: "Modern Mode of Artificiality"** — dropping "Western" from the current DOCX phrasing. Rationale:

1. **Temporal scope matches the thesis**. The thesis's argument runs from Greek philosophical roots (Bhaskar's Platonic-Aristotelian fault line at §III.3) through early-modern Europe (Renaissance, Needham's Mediterranean mode-of-production) to global Modern dominance (post-five-centuries) to contemporary metaverse-stage developments (Chapters V-VI). "Enlightenment" is too narrow temporally; "Modern" covers the full arc; "Mechanistic-Interventionist" is structurally more precise but loses the temporal-lineage signal.

2. **Anti-essentialism is partial but defensible**. Dropping "Western" addresses the strongest geographic-essentialism worry (Sen 2006, Said 2001). What remains — "Modern" — is a temporal-historical descriptor, not a cultural-essentialist one. Per Hui's cosmotechnical observation (the social-credit complex is real-level Modern in origin even when mobilized against Western hegemony), the configuration is now globally diffused; "Modern" captures this better than "Modern Western."

3. **Minimal change to existing prose**. The current DOCX uses "Western Modernity" / "Modern Western mode of artificiality" throughout Ch III and the body chapters. A find-replace of "Western Modernity" → "Modern Mode of Artificiality" (and "Modern Western mode" → "Modern Mode") is mechanical and produces consistent terminology across chapters.

4. **Supported by anti-essentialism footnote**. A single explanatory footnote at first occurrence (Ch III §III.0 or Ch I where the term is first invoked) can do the analytic work: "I use *Modern Mode of Artificiality* to refer to the configuration whose origins lie in the Mediterranean of antiquity, whose institutional consolidation occurs in early-modern Europe, and whose global diffusion takes place over the past five centuries. I deliberately drop 'Western' to avoid the geographic-essentialist reading the term invites; the configuration is now globally diffused, and the structural features the thesis tracks operate wherever those features are adopted at the real level, regardless of geographic location (cf. Hui 2016 on the social-credit complex as Western-origin components mobilized against Western hegemony)."

### §5.3 — Why not Enlightenment Mode

Your specific worry — that "Modern Western" essentializes the West — is well-grounded. But "Enlightenment Mode" creates a different problem: it pins the configuration to a specific philosophical movement (17th–18th c. European Enlightenment) whose temporal scope is narrower than the configuration the thesis actually tracks. The thesis tracks a configuration with:
- Roots in Greek antiquity (Plato-Aristotle fault line, per Bhaskar at §III.3)
- Consolidation in Renaissance / early-modern Europe (Needham's geography-mode-of-production at §III.2)
- Global diffusion across five centuries (the colonization-as-real-level-imposition mechanism at §III.4)
- Contemporary metaverse-stage developments (Chapters V-VI)

"Enlightenment" covers roughly two centuries of this arc, and even within that period, the Enlightenment was internally contested (Romantic counter-Enlightenment, Catholic-Reformation counter-currents, etc.). Using "Enlightenment Mode" for the full configuration would require either stretching the term beyond its conventional dating (with a footnote-explanation that would do the same work as the "Modern Mode" footnote but with more rhetorical strain), or restricting the analytical claim to the Enlightenment-period instance (which would lose the full arc).

### §5.4 — If you reject "Modern Mode": fallback recommendation

If "Modern Mode" feels insufficiently specific, my secondary recommendation is **Option 7: dual-track vocabulary**:
- **"Mechanistic-Interventionist Mode of Artificiality"** for the structural-functional configuration (the disposition itself)
- **"Modern Western mode"** OR **"Western Modernity"** for the historically-specific instantiation when discussing concrete cases (already in current DOCX usage)
- **"Enlightenment lineage"** for the specific philosophical thread when discussing intellectual-history grounding

This preserves the analytic precision Mechanistic-Interventionist offers, the historical-narrative familiarity of Modern Western, and the lineage-traceability you want from Enlightenment, by reserving each for a distinct level of analysis. The cost is terminological proliferation, but the precision gain may justify it for the final-draft pass.
