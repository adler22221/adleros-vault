# Ch-3-7 v2: Philosophy's Role — A Methodological Note for §III

## 1. The Question and Why It Cannot Be Deferred

Ma's challenge during the viva — *哲學是不是對社會發展這麼重要? 用複雜的哲學論述...* — articulates a methodological worry that any thesis claiming a philosophical "fault line" at the root of a social-historical process must answer before the substantive argument can proceed. The history of attempts to explain social development through philosophical ideas has produced some of the worst social science: crude Hegelianism in which "Spirit" actualises itself in institutions, vulgar Weberian idealism in which "the Protestant Ethic" causes capitalism, intellectual history that treats books as the prime movers of material processes. If the thesis's claim that a Platonic-Aristotelian fault line conditions Western Modernity's disposition to domination is to be defensible, it must be clear about *what kind of explanatory claim this is* — and about *why philosophy, rather than (or alongside) sociology, history, and science studies, is the appropriate register for making it*.

This note responds to A1 in two parts. The first part defends the substantive claim that culture has ontological standing as a real-level stratum — the claim that licenses talking about the "world," in the universe / world / metaverse triad, as a real ontological layer rather than as a merely subjective or merely intersubjective overlay on the universe-substrate. The defence draws on Margaret Archer's analytical dualism (*Cultural and Agency*, 1996), and engages three substantial counter-positions: Andrew Sayer's qualifications on stratification within critical realism itself; the pragmatist objection to depth-ontology associated with Rorty, Putnam, and Brandom; and the post-structuralist critique of depth-ontology as logocentric, associated with Derrida and Foucault. The second part defends philosophy as the appropriate register for articulating a real-level mechanism that empirical sociology, history, and STS each touch from a different angle but none can articulate without philosophical resources.

## 2. The Affirmative Case: Archer on Culture as a Real-Level Stratum

The thesis's central ontological commitment is that the "world" — the human-constructed meaning-space nested within the universe — is *real*. It is not a useful fiction, not a projection of individual consciousnesses, not an aggregate of intersubjective beliefs, not a "social construction" in the deflationary sense in which the construction is ontologically less serious than what it constructs. It is a stratum of the real with its own emergent causal powers, irreducible to either the physical mechanisms of the universe-substrate or to the psychological states of the agents who inhabit it.

Margaret Archer's *Culture and Agency: The Place of Culture in Social Theory* (1996, revised edition; orig. 1988) provides the most rigorous extant defence of precisely this position. Archer's analytical dualism distinguishes the **Cultural System (CS)** from **Socio-Cultural (S-C) interaction**. The Cultural System is the set of intelligible items — propositions, theories, doctrines, beliefs, frameworks — standing in *logical relations of contradiction or complementarity* with one another. Socio-Cultural interaction is the empirical layer of who-believes-what-when-with-whom: the actual circulation of beliefs among agents, in real time, in particular settings. The two are *analytically* separable even though they are *empirically* always co-implicated [VERIFY: confirmed | archerCultureAgencyPlace1996 | p.TBD (approx. xv–xviii) | nlm-excerpt:"the degree of logical consistency was labelled 'Cultural System integration' (hereafter CS) and the extent of causal cohesion was termed 'Socio-Cultural integration' (hereafter S-C)" — NLM source UUID 9353c836; locate page in Zotero PDF].

The crucial move is that the CS, on Archer's account, is not an aggregate of S-C beliefs and not reducible to them. It is an emergent stratum whose internal contradictions and complementarities *condition* — without determining — the situations agents face and the strategies available to them. A logical contradiction between two doctrines that no living person currently entertains can still constrain the future intellectual options of agents who later encounter the doctrines. A complementarity between two frameworks can make their joint adoption strategically attractive even where no agent has explicitly conjoined them. The CS, in other words, exhibits the criterial mark of a real-level stratum in Bhaskar's sense: it has causal powers that operate *transfactually* — they exist and condition outcomes whether or not any given agent activates them in any given moment [VERIFY: confirmed | bhaskarRealistTheoryScience2008 | p.TBD (approx. p.13) | nlm-excerpt:"Mechanisms, events and experiences thus constitute three overlapping domains of reality, viz. the domains of the real, the actual and the empirical" — NLM source UUID 15591783; locate page in Zotero PDF].

This is what licenses the thesis's central ontological vocabulary. The "world," in the universe / world / metaverse triad, occupies the same explanatory position that Archer's Cultural System occupies: an emergent cultural-symbolic stratum, irreducible to the physical mechanisms it presupposes (universe) and irreducible to the psychological states of the agents it conditions. The "metaverse," in turn, is a further emergent stratum — a neo-artificial layer of computational-infrastructural mechanisms — that in the thesis's argument increasingly displaces and re-engineers the world-stratum. The squeeze hypothesis is therefore intelligible as a *real-level causal claim about the interaction of three real-level strata*, not as a generalisation over empirical observations or a phenomenological description of contemporary experience. Without the Archerian defence of culture as real, the squeeze hypothesis would collapse either into physicalism (only the universe-stratum is real; the world is epiphenomenal) or into a constructivist idealism (the world is "what we make it"; the metaverse is just another make-believe). Archer's analytical dualism is what holds the middle ground that the thesis requires.

## 3. Counter-Argument 1 — Sayer's Qualifications

The first counter-position to engage is internal to critical realism. Andrew Sayer's *Method in Social Science: A Realist Approach* (2010, revised ed.; orig. 1984/1992) accepts Bhaskar's stratified ontology but qualifies it in ways that have to be acknowledged before the Archerian defence can be deployed cleanly [VERIFY: pending-T0-retrieval | sayerMethodSocialScience2010 — no NLM query run for Sayer; check if in A-Sources and run targeted T0 retrieval, or locate in Zotero PDF].

Sayer's chief qualifications are three. First, he insists on the distinction between *necessary* and *contingent* relations among objects. Mechanisms exist in real-level structures, but their activation in concrete situations is conditioned by contingent conjunctures, so the inference from "mechanism M exists" to "outcome O follows" is never deductive. Second, Sayer presses a lighter touch on emergence-claims than the Bhaskar–Archer mainstream: emergence is a methodological warrant for treating certain phenomena as analytically irreducible, not a metaphysical thesis about ontologically novel substances. Third, Sayer is wary of the temptation — frequent in CR-influenced cultural theory — to multiply real-level strata beyond explanatory necessity, and he insists that the proliferation of "real" levels must be disciplined by demonstrating in each case that the stratum exhibits genuine causal powers.

The thesis acknowledges all three qualifications without conceding the core stratification. The squeeze hypothesis is precisely the kind of claim that needs the necessary/contingent distinction: the squeeze is a *necessary tendency* in the interaction of universe, world, and metaverse strata, but its concrete activation in any given society is conditioned by contingent conjunctures (the institutional density of the social formation, the depth of pre-metaverse cultural commitments, the availability of resistance vocabularies, and so on). This is exactly why the §IV chapter treats Japan as one *legible actualisation* of the squeeze rather than as the squeeze itself. The lighter-touch reading of emergence is also acceptable: the thesis does not need to claim that the world-stratum is an ontologically novel substance, only that it is analytically irreducible to either physical mechanisms or psychological states for the purpose of explaining the historical-social phenomena the thesis addresses. And the disciplinary worry about multiplying strata is met head-on: the thesis posits exactly three strata (universe, world, metaverse), each of which is shown to exhibit causal powers that the others cannot supply — the universe-stratum's biophysical constraints, the world-stratum's symbolic-normative conditioning, the metaverse-stratum's algorithmic-infrastructural mediation.

Sayer's qualifications, in short, *strengthen* rather than undermine the thesis's ontology when its claims are formulated with appropriate care.

A brief extension of this critical-realist framing is warranted before the next counter-argument is engaged. The thesis's argument in §III deploys empirical-historical evidence — civilizational collapse (Tainter, Diamond, Ponting, Brook, Schwartz & Nichols) and natural extinction (the Big Five mass-extinction events and the background-extinction record) — to support the worlds-end-of-the-OS-spectrum framework developed in §III-8. The methodological status of that empirical evidence requires the same treatment Sayer's necessary/contingent distinction underwrites. The empirical evidence operates at Bhaskar's *domain of the empirical* (`de` — observed events and patterns recorded in human and paleontological knowledge); it does not directly establish the real-level claims about modes of artificiality or about Western Modernity's disposition. Rather, the evidence functions as *consistency requirement*: what would have to be the case empirically for the real-level claims to be defensible — what the real-level mechanisms predict at the empirical level, and what the empirical record either confirms, complicates, or constrains. The civilizational-collapse and natural-extinction records satisfy the consistency requirement for the OS-spectrum-as-adaptational-strategies framework §III-8 develops; they do not constitute that framework's grounds in any reductive sense. This is the standard critical-realist relationship between real-level claims and empirical evidence, and it preserves the methodological priority of philosophical analysis defended above without insulating the analysis from empirical accountability.

## 4. Counter-Argument 2 — The Pragmatist Objection

The second counter-position is more radical. Pragmatist philosophy, in the lineage from William James through John Dewey to Richard Rorty, Hilary Putnam, and Robert Brandom, rejects depth-ontology as a category mistake. On the strong Rortyan version, ontology is just a vocabulary; what matters is what the vocabulary *does* — what inferential patterns it licenses, what practical engagements it supports, what forms of life it sustains. The very question "is the cultural system real?" already concedes too much to the realist framing: it presupposes a fact-of-the-matter that the pragmatist denies is meaningful apart from the linguistic-practical context in which the question is posed.

This is a serious objection, and the thesis cannot dismiss it by reasserting the realist vocabulary it is supposed to defend. Three responses, however, allow the thesis to acknowledge the pragmatist insight while preserving the substantive claim.

First, even on a pragmatist construal, vocabularies are not all on a par. Some vocabularies are robust across many possible practical contexts; others are local, context-bound, and inert outside their original setting. The biophysical vocabulary — gravity, energy, biochemistry — is robust across virtually all human practical contexts; abandoning it produces immediate practical failure. The metaverse vocabulary — engagement, attention, recommendation — is local to a particular techno-economic configuration; it is intelligible only within the institutional formations that built it. The thesis's distinction between universe and metaverse can be reformulated in pragmatist-friendly terms as a distinction between *vocabulary-robust* and *vocabulary-contingent* domains. The universe-substrate is what every plausible vocabulary must accommodate; the metaverse is one contingent vocabulary among many possible techno-cultural formations.

Second, the pragmatist's "what matters is what it does" cuts both ways. If the question is what a vocabulary does, then the question of whether to treat culture as real becomes the question of *what gets done* by the realist-cultural vocabulary versus its alternatives. The realist-cultural vocabulary — Archer's CS as conditioning S-C interaction — *does* a great deal that its alternatives cannot do: it makes intelligible the conditioning of agents by inherited intellectual frameworks they did not choose, the persistence of cultural patterns across generations of agents, and (crucially for the thesis) the asymmetry between the world-stratum that humans inhabit and the metaverse-stratum that increasingly conditions them. A pure-pragmatist alternative that refused to grant culture its conditioning power would have to redescribe these phenomena in vocabulary that would, by the pragmatist's own criterion, be doing less work.

Third, and following on from the second, the practical difference between treating the metaverse as a contingent vocabulary and treating it as a real-level stratum is itself substantial — and the thesis's diagnostic and normative argument depends on the difference. If the metaverse were merely a contingent vocabulary, its expansion would be a vocabulary-shift to be evaluated by its practical fruits. But the thesis's argument is precisely that the metaverse expansion is *not* under the control of those whose practical engagements it reshapes; it imposes conditions transfactually, whether or not any given user adopts the metaverse vocabulary, by reorganising the infrastructural environment in which any vocabulary at all has to operate. To capture this, even a pragmatist-friendly account must concede something close to the realist-cultural conditioning that Archer defends.

The pragmatist objection is therefore preserved as a methodological caution against ontological hypostasis; it is not preserved as a refutation of the thesis's stratified ontology.

## 5. Counter-Argument 3 — The Post-Structuralist Objection

The third counter-position is, in some respects, the most demanding. Post-structuralist philosophy in the Derridean and Foucauldian lineages reads depth-ontology as an instance of the Western metaphysics-of-presence — *logocentrism*, in Derrida's terminology — that organises Western thought toward mastery, hierarchy, and the suppression of difference. To posit "real strata" beneath the surface play of differences is, on this reading, to repeat the gesture by which Plato subordinated the sensible to the eidetic, by which the metaphysical tradition has consistently privileged depth over surface, presence over trace, substance over relation. The objection is not merely that depth-ontology is theoretically mistaken; it is that depth-ontology is *complicit* with the very disposition to domination that the thesis is trying to diagnose. Derrida's *Of Grammatology* and *Dissemination* — particularly "Plato's Pharmacy" — develop this reading in detail [VERIFY: confirmed | derridaOfGrammatology2016 (KE3YPPJ2) | p.TBD | nlm-excerpt:"the history of the metaphysics…has always assigned the origin of truth in general to the logos: the history of truth…has always been…the debasement of writing, and its repression outside 'full' speech" — NLM source UUID c774be6d (A-Sources); locate page in Zotero KE3YPPJ2 PDF] [VERIFY: absent-from-A-Sources | derridaDissemination1981 (VYNZTA5S) — Dissemination PDF absent from A-Sources; pharmakon concept partially available via Spivak Preface to Grammatology (source UUID 27610fcb); add Dissemination PDF to A-Sources for T0 retrieval]. Foucault's archaeological and genealogical work, while tonally distinct, converges on the same suspicion of any depth-claim that purports to identify structures more real than the discursive practices that articulate them.

The thesis's response to this objection turns on a distinction that the post-structuralist critique itself acknowledges, even if it is not always foregrounded: the distinction between *metaphysics-as-presence* and *ontology-as-stratification*. Heidegger, on whose critique of metaphysics both Derrida and Foucault build, was not opposed to ontological inquiry as such — *Being and Time* is, after all, a project in fundamental ontology — but to a particular *form* of ontology in which Being is reduced to the constant presence of substantial entities. The post-structuralist generalisation of Heidegger's critique extends the worry to any depth-claim that posits fixed natures or essences. But ontology-as-stratification, in the critical-realist sense the thesis adopts, does not posit fixed natures. Critical-realist strata are not essences; they are *levels of contingent emergence*, each conditioned by the strata beneath and conditioning the strata above, none reducible to the others, none metaphysically prior. The CR ontology is, in this respect, thoroughly anti-essentialist and historically open. The world-stratum is what it is because of the contingent history of human meaning-making; the metaverse-stratum is what it is because of the contingent history of digital-infrastructural development; neither is a fixed nature awaiting discovery, both are emergent products of contingent processes that could have unfolded otherwise.

This compatibility between CR stratification and post-structuralist anti-essentialism is not an ad-hoc reconciliation but a structural feature of the position. Bhaskar's argument against the *epistemic fallacy* — the reduction of ontological questions to epistemological ones — is itself a critique of the metaphysics-of-presence, on the grounds that the metaphysics-of-presence privileges a particular epistemological stance (the immediate intuition of substantial entities) and treats it as the measure of what can be real. Critical realism's insistence that the real exceeds the empirical, and that mechanisms operate transfactually whether or not they are observed, is in this respect *closer to* the post-structuralist suspicion of presence than to the metaphysics that the post-structuralist critique opposes. The strand-genealogical position the thesis develops in §III-4 v2 (regarding the Platonic-Aristotelian heritage) can be deployed here in a parallel register: post-structuralism diagnoses one strand of ontology — the substantialist-presence strand — as logocentric, and CR ontology operates on a different strand that is compatible with the post-structuralist diagnosis rather than refuted by it.

The thesis can therefore acknowledge the post-structuralist worry, refuse the conflation of all ontology with metaphysics-of-presence, and proceed with a stratified-emergent ontology that does the substantive work the squeeze hypothesis requires.

## 6. Why Philosophy — The Methodological Case for the Register

The three counter-arguments above all bear on the substantive ontological claim. The remaining methodological question is the one Ma raised most directly: even granting that the squeeze hypothesis can be defended ontologically, why is *philosophy* the appropriate register for making the argument? Why not sociology alone, or history alone, or science and technology studies alone? The thesis's answer is that each of these disciplines provides indispensable angles of approach to the underlying mechanism, but none of them can articulate the mechanism itself without philosophical resources — and that this is precisely Bhaskar's point about the relation between science and the philosophy of science.

Sociology shows the *empirical patterns* through which the squeeze actualises in particular societies. Uegaki's five periods of post-war Japanese cultural transformation, the literature on recognition anxiety and the attention economy, comparative work on social media use across institutional settings — these are sociological achievements that the thesis depends upon and could not produce by philosophical means alone. But sociology, on its own, leaves the underlying *mechanism* under-articulated. Sociological description tells us *that* certain patterns recur, *how* they correlate with institutional and economic variables, and *what* their statistical structure is. It does not, by itself, tell us *why* these patterns are what they are at the level of generative tendency — what the structural logic is that all the empirical instances are different actualisations of.

History shows the *contingent trajectory* through which the underlying mechanism came to be actualised in the particular historical configuration we now inhabit. The Great Divergence literature, the historiography of the Industrial Revolution, the genealogy of digital capitalism — these are historical achievements that the thesis depends upon and could not produce by philosophical means alone. But history, on its own, also leaves the underlying mechanism under-articulated. Historical narrative tells us *how* a particular configuration came about and *what sequence* of contingencies produced it. It does not, by itself, identify *which* of the many features of the resulting configuration belong to the configuration's structural logic and which are local accidents of its trajectory.

STS shows the *technical-material networks* through which the metaverse-stratum is materially constituted: the algorithms, the data centres, the protocols, the user-interface design choices, the labour practices that build and maintain the infrastructure. These are STS achievements that the thesis depends upon and could not produce by philosophical means alone. But STS, on its own, has its own characteristic limit. The flat-ontological commitments of much actor-network theory leave the discipline well-equipped to describe the surface networks but ill-equipped to articulate the asymmetries of conditioning power between strata that the squeeze hypothesis names. STS can show *what* the metaverse is materially made of; it does not, by itself, articulate the structural logic by which the metaverse-stratum reorganises the world-stratum.

Philosophy is needed, in this constellation, because it is the register in which the underlying *real-level mechanism* — that all three empirical disciplines are different angles of approach to — can be articulated *as such*. Bhaskar's argument in *A Realist Theory of Science* is that the natural sciences need a philosophy of science not because the sciences are inadequate but because the question of what kind of objects they investigate, and in what sense those objects are real, is a properly philosophical question whose answer conditions how the empirical findings are to be understood [VERIFY: confirmed | bhaskarRealistTheoryScience2008 | p.TBD (approx. p.13) | nlm-excerpt:"Mechanisms, events and experiences thus constitute three overlapping domains of reality, viz. the domains of the real, the actual and the empirical" — NLM source UUID 15591783; locate page in Zotero PDF]. His argument in *The Possibility of Naturalism* extends the point: the social sciences need a philosophy of social science for the same reason — to articulate the ontology of the objects they investigate (social structures, cultural systems, institutional formations) in a way that allows the empirical findings to be assessed for what they show about underlying mechanisms rather than merely for what they show about surface correlations [VERIFY: confirmed | bhaskarPossibilityNaturalismPhilosophical1998 | p.TBD | nlm-excerpt:"The positivist tradition is correct to stress that there are causal laws, generalities, at work in social life... Where it errs is in the reduction of these laws to empirical regularities, and in the account that it is thereby committed to giving of the process of their identification. For in the absence of spontaneously occurring, and given the impossibility of artificially creating, closed systems, the human sciences must confront the problem of the direct scientific study of phenomena that only ever manifest themselves in open systems." — NLM source UUID 6c1a3202-620a-4112-9a41-88bdd353365d (A-Sources); locate page in Zotero PDF].

The thesis's use of philosophical analysis is precisely this Bhaskarian use. The philosophical articulation does not substitute for the sociological, historical, and STS analyses; it provides the ontological register in which the convergent findings of those disciplines can be brought together as evidence for a single underlying mechanism. Without the philosophical articulation, the convergence would not be visible *as* convergence; it would appear as a coincidence of patterns across three independent literatures. With the philosophical articulation, the patterns become intelligible as different empirical traces of the same real-level tendency.

There is a further methodological reason for the philosophical register that bears specifically on the thesis's interdisciplinary ambition. Each of sociology, history, and STS speaks in a discipline-specific vocabulary that is partially incommensurable with the others. Sociological talk about "social structure," historical talk about "trajectory" and "contingency," and STS talk about "actor-networks" and "assemblages" do not translate into one another without remainder. A philosophical articulation at the right level of abstraction provides the *interdisciplinary translation layer* in which the convergent findings can be discussed across disciplinary boundaries without each discipline having to abandon its native vocabulary. The squeeze hypothesis, articulated philosophically, is a claim that sociologists, historians, and STS scholars can each engage with on their own terms and either corroborate or challenge from their respective evidentiary bases. A claim formulated in the native vocabulary of any one discipline could not function as such a translation layer; it would be heard, in the other disciplines, as a parochial overreach by an outside discipline.

Philosophy is, in this constellation, the register that articulates the real-level mechanism in a way that survives interdisciplinary translation. That is why the thesis is philosophical even as it depends substantively on sociological, historical, and STS findings.

## 7. Closing Methodological Note

The position defended in this section can be summarised in three propositions. (1) Culture has ontological standing as a real-level stratum, irreducible to physical mechanisms or psychological states, with emergent causal powers that condition the situations agents face — this is what licenses talking about the "world," in the universe / world / metaverse triad, as a real stratum rather than a useful fiction or an intersubjective overlay. (2) The principal counter-positions to this stratified-cultural ontology — Sayer's qualifications, the pragmatist objection, the post-structuralist critique — are acknowledged and absorbed: Sayer's necessary/contingent distinction strengthens rather than undermines the position when claims are formulated with appropriate care; the pragmatist's "what matters is what it does" is met by showing that the realist-cultural vocabulary does indispensable explanatory work; the post-structuralist worry about logocentrism is met by distinguishing metaphysics-as-presence (which CR also rejects) from ontology-as-stratification (which is compatible with anti-essentialism). (3) Philosophy is the appropriate register for the thesis not because philosophy is causally primary but because the convergence of sociological, historical, and STS findings on a single underlying mechanism cannot be articulated *as such* in any of the three disciplinary vocabularies alone, and because Bhaskar's general argument that science needs a philosophy of science applies *a fortiori* to a transdisciplinary argument that crosses three sciences.

These three propositions are sufficient to answer Ma's challenge. The thesis is not claiming that ideas cause social development; it is not claiming that philosophical analysis is the only or primary register for understanding social development; it is not claiming a privileged epistemic standpoint for philosophy over against the empirical sciences. It is claiming that the squeeze hypothesis names a real-level mechanism whose articulation requires a philosophical register, that the articulation is consistent with and supported by the empirical findings of sociology, history, and STS, and that the ontology that licenses the articulation can be defended against the principal counter-positions in contemporary philosophy of social science.

If a footnote version of this methodological note is required for placement at the first occurrence of the Platonic-Aristotelian reference in §III, the substance can be compressed as follows:

> [Footnote]: The thesis's philosophical analysis does not claim that ideas cause social development. The squeeze hypothesis names a real-level generative mechanism — in the critical-realist sense developed by Bhaskar (*A Realist Theory of Science*, 2008) and applied to culture by Archer (*Culture and Agency*, 1996) — whose actualisation in particular historical configurations is conditioned by contingent material, institutional, and technical factors that sociology, history, and STS investigate in their own registers. Philosophy is the appropriate register for articulating the underlying mechanism *as such* because the convergent empirical findings of these disciplines cannot be brought together as evidence for a single mechanism in any of their native vocabularies alone. The position defended is Archer's analytical dualism — the cultural system as a real-level stratum with emergent causal powers, irreducible to but not independent of the agents who inhabit it — qualified by Sayer's necessary/contingent distinction, compatible with pragmatist concerns about ontological hypostasis once the realist-cultural vocabulary's explanatory work is acknowledged, and distinguishable from the metaphysics-of-presence that post-structuralism critiques because the strata posited are levels of contingent emergence rather than fixed natures.
