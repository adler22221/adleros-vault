# Ch II — subsection: *Why "artificiality," not "technics"* (Stiegler's equivalent and where the thesis departs)

The choice of *artificiality* over *technics* must answer an etymological objection before it can answer a philosophical one. "Artificial" descends from the Latin *artificium* — *ars* ("art, skill, craft," the standard Latin rendering of Greek *technē*) compounded with *facere* ("to make"). The word is therefore no less Greek-by-descent than "technics" itself; both run back to *technē*, and any claim that "artificiality" were the more culturally neutral term *on etymological grounds* would be false. The justification lies elsewhere — in which half of the compound bears the weight. *Artificium* is built on *facere*: its operative element is the *factum*, the *made*, not the *ars*, the craft-skill. This is why the living antonym of "artificial" is *natural* — it tracks the made/given distinction (*factum* against *natum*, what holds *thesei* against *phusei*) — whereas "technical" tracks the narrower craft/instrument axis. The thesis stipulates the *factum* sense and brackets the *ars* sense; and on that reading the genus is wide enough to hold not only made artefacts (*technē*) but instituted laws and conventions (*nomos*) and articulated meaning (*logos*), since each is *posited by the human against the given* rather than grown. The same result arrives from the Chinese side, where Xunzi's 偽 = 人為 names precisely "what is done by the human" — the deed, the instituted — and not craft-skill: the Latin *facere* and the Chinese 人為 converge on *made-by-human* as the genus marker.[^wei]

That this genus is real, and not the thesis's invention, can be shown from the very thinker whose word the thesis declines to adopt. Stiegler has no single noun for "the made as such"; his concept is distributed across a cluster — *technics* as the broadest domain, *exteriorization* (later *exosomatization*) as its process, the *"inorganic organized being"* as its ontological register, *originary technicity* as the human's constitutive condition. Yet the work that the thesis assigns to "artificiality" is performed, in his vocabulary, by "the technical" — and he stretches it exactly as far: language, religion, and politics are, he writes, "wholly technical, fruit of an originary incompleteness of technical being."[^tech] What the thesis calls the artificiality of *nomos* and *logos* is what Stiegler calls their technicity. The difference is one of intension, not extension: he names the genus by the producing faculty (technics), the thesis by the made result (artificiality). And his own text reaches for the thesis's noun without promoting it — *tekhnē*, he says, "is prosthetic; that is, it is entirely artifice."[^artifice] The thesis does only what that sentence invites: it lifts "artifice" from an adjective beneath "the technical" to the name of the genus.

Three departures follow, and they are the reasons the rename is not cosmetic. *First*, the thesis takes artificiality as an *ontological status* — the made as a kind of being — rather than as a faculty (technics) or a class of objects (Stiegler's "inorganic organized beings," his tertiary retentions); only the status-reading lets *nomos* and *logos* fall under one genus without strain. *Second*, the thesis de-Hellenizes the concept: Stiegler derives the whole structure from a single mythic-Greek seam — Prometheus, Epimetheus, the stolen *tekhnai* — whereas the thesis cross-validates the made/given cut against 偽 = 人為, claiming a cross-cultural reach his derivation neither seeks nor licenses.[^hui] *Third*, and most consequentially, Stiegler's artificiality is *anthropogenic*: it explains how the human comes to be, and his "the what invents the who" describes a *co-constitution* at the origin.[^who] The thesis carries the same concept past the origin to a terminus he does not narrate — artificiality *self-completing* (自足完成化), the *what* outrunning the *who*, the made acquiring the self-arising character the tradition reserved for *phusis*. Beneath that terminus the thesis keeps a thick realist *universe*, where Stiegler's "the interior and the exterior are the same thing"[^interior] tends to dissolve the given into the made at the very origin. The thesis inherits Stiegler's ground floor — artificiality as originary and constitutive — and builds upon it the storey he leaves unbuilt.

[^wei]: On 偽 = 人為 see [[Ch-2-0_why-artificiality-not-technology_2026-06-02]] and the Chinese-ontology note; 楊倞 glosses 「偽，人為也」. The convergence is the warrant for reading "artificiality" as *factum* (made-by-human) rather than *ars* (craft-skill).
[^tech]: Stiegler, *Technics and Time 1*, 194–95.
[^artifice]: *Technics and Time 1*, 193.
[^hui]: Cf. Hui's *cosmotechnics* — there is no single universal "technics" but plural, cosmologically-indexed cosmotechnics; the thesis places "artificiality" as the genus *above* the plural cosmotechnics, each a *mode* of artificiality, and is wary (per the Ch III critique of universalization) of letting the genus over-claim.
[^who]: *Technics and Time 1*, 141: "it is the tool, that is, *tekhnê*, that invents the human, not the human who invents the technical."
[^interior]: *Technics and Time 1*, 142.

## Post-verification correction (2026-06-02) — the Stiegler reading, refined
Two NLM re-verifications *after* this note was drafted tightened the Stiegler engagement; apply when merging:
1. **"An exclusively human solution" is Leroi-Gourhan's, not Stiegler's.** It is a quotation (Leroi-Gourhan 1993, p. 228) at *T&T1* **p. 172**, predicated of "breaking the link between species and memory" (epiphylogenesis), and Stiegler *corrects* the exclusivity in his own voice (the process "occurred before the human… in ape societies"; "'the human' is precisely this hegemony"). On the human/animal continuum Stiegler is an **ally**, not a target. ("Exosomatization" is likewise **not** in *T&T1* — it enters only ~2014; so the ledger's "exteriorization (later exosomatization)" should not be read as one continuous *T&T1* term.)
2. **"Without qualities, without predestination" is a *transcendental* claim (no fixed instinctual essence), not a denial of biology.** Stiegler explicitly affirms the persisting body: the human "is also animal," "a significant part of human activity is instinctual," the noetic soul "unswervingly dependent" on its biological "in-itself" (*T&T1* p. 50; *Symbolic Misery*). So departure 3's realist-floor contrast (the p. 142 "interior = exterior" *tendency*) marks Stiegler's **exteriorising tendency** — the human's qualities "may rather become those of technics" (p. 194), the human "outside itself" (p. 193) — **not** a flat blank-slate. The thesis's quarrel is over the **weight and direction** given to the biological, not whether it persists.
