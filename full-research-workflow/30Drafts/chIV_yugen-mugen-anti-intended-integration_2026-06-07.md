## PART 1 — NEW SUBSECTION (insert after P0626; new "(B)")

### (B) Two views of the anti-intended, and the particularity of the limitless

The two terms just set down — limitless life and finite life — are easily heard as a matched pair, two abstract options a mind might weigh. They are not symmetric, and the asymmetry is the hinge of the whole chapter. Finite life is not the exotic member of the pair but the ordinary one: the worldview-and-view-of-the-human that takes the anti-intended as an integral feature of being a creature embedded among others has been, Uegaki argues, the traditional inheritance of most cultures since antiquity, so that it is the limitless-life view — the conviction that the recalcitrance of the given is a wrong to be abolished — that is the historical peculiarity, the one settlement that has to be explained (Uegaki, 2022b: 110–111). The point is not parochial to East and West; Uegaki is explicit that even Christianity, which he treats as the proximate origin of the limitless-life worldview, was rooted before the Renaissance in the finite-life view: it imagined a Pure Land or a Heaven elsewhere, but placed its doctrinal weight on living out the given finite life to the full, not on convicting the here-and-now of falling short (Uegaki, 2022b: 110–111). The demand to be liberated from, and to subjugate, the anti-intended "is by no means universal to humanity"; that worldview "was given concrete form by Western modern philosophy alone" (Uegaki, 2022b: 113–114).

I should be candid about a weakness in how that claim is argued, because the thesis must repair it rather than borrow it. Uegaki asserts the particularity more than he demonstrates it; writing from inside a culture for which the finite-life view is still the unmarked default, he can take as self-evident what a reader formed wholly within the limitless-life view — for whom the demand that reality answer to the self is simply what it is to have ideals at all — will rightly require to be shown. This is the converse of the vantage argued earlier (P0620): the seam that lets a thinker see the elevated subject as one particular shape is also what lets him pass over its justification as obvious. Supplying that justification — grounding the particularity not in cultural assertion but in the stratified, evolved embeddedness established in Chapter I and the squeeze it will issue in — is part of the work of the chapters that remain. What Uegaki names, the thesis must earn.

The two views matter here because they do not merely value the anti-intended differently; they constitute it differently — they make it show up as a different kind of thing. To the limitless-life view, whatever will not answer to the intending mind is read as ontological oppression (存在論的抑圧): an aberration, a violation, the secular Fall the next section will anatomize, and therefore a ground for overcoming. To the finite-life view, the same recalcitrance is not a violation of anything, because nothing limitless was ever owed; it is a constitutive condition of living as an embedded, embodied, mortal being among other such beings — what Uegaki sets out as the five principles of finite life, each naming something "from which, so long as we are human, we cannot escape" (Uegaki, 2022b: 130–135). Whether any given instance of it can or should be eased is then a real question, answered differently by each culture and each life; but it is not posed as the righting of a wrong against the self. To affirm finite life is not to resign oneself to whatever is the case — Uegaki is emphatic that affirmation is not acquiescence — but to face the sorrow and cruelty of existence once, squarely, and still seek a better life *within* the given rather than against it: the disposition he calls world-acceptance (世界了解), the willingness to receive the as-it-is world, including the other who will not be as one intends, as it is (Uegaki, 2022b: 130).

The difference is sharpest at the two sites the rest of the chapter turns on, and Uegaki's own treatment of each furnishes the contrast exactly. Take the body. Its stink and dirt and pain, its liability to injury, illness, disability, ageing, and decline, are for Uegaki the first principle of finite life, the principle of biological existence — the plain fact that one is born, lives, and dies as a member of the biological world, from whose cruelty "we can never escape" however much we automate the slaughterhouse or sterilise the room (Uegaki, 2022b: 130). To the finite-life view this body is the form a creaturely life takes, met not by escape but by the search for meaning in it. To the limitless-life view it is the most intimate hindrance, to be overcome — and the overcoming runs, in Uegaki's hot-iron fable, into the structure of the no-respite hell: the man who wins a machine arm finds he now cannot bear that his left arm, or his leg, still cannot grasp the hot iron, so that each conquest of the body only exposes the next unconquered margin, until the body is to be shed altogether for the disembodied thought-being (Uegaki, 2022b: 123). Now take the other. That other people are essentially not-as-intended — that no relationship answers to the shape one wished, that one's life is forever being intervened upon and asked to bend — is the third principle of finite life; and because "no burdenless relationship can be supposed," the recalcitrance of the other is, for the finite-life view, not a defect of relation but its very condition, the thing without which there is no meaningful relation and so no meaningful self, borne through the hard-won arts of distance, role, trust, and forgiveness (Uegaki, 2022b: 132–133). To the limitless-life view the same other is a high-risk, high-burden being who oppresses the irreplaceable self (Uegaki, 2022b: 114), to be managed at last by replacement — the compliant android who "conveniently plays the as-intended other," or, short of that, the block, the mute, the unmatch (Uegaki, 2022b: 125; and §[present (E), P0641]). One body, one other; two worlds, because two views. The sections that follow trace what the limitless-life view does with this material; the present one has only fixed the fork at which it departs from the older default.

## PART 2 — ANCHORED INSERTIONS

### Insertion 1 — (A) Three terms · into P0624 · the definitional guard

> Add after the sentence defining the anti-intended ("…whatever does not answer to the intending mind, whatever resists.").

A guard against an easy misreading belongs here, because the anti-intended is constantly confused with two things it is not. It is not the merely difficult: a burden freely taken up — a hard climb, a fast, a chosen ordeal, even a courted pain — is not anti-intended at all but one of the purest figures of the as-intended, the will meeting exactly the resistance it sought. And it is not the merely uncontrolled: much of what answers to one's whole intending mind is precisely *not* at one's disposal, and is wanted that way. This is why, as the next sections will formalise against Rosa, the as-intended is the broader category — it annexes much of what a controllability-centred account files under the uncontrollable, the surprise of an artwork, the open result of a game, the otherness of a beloved (P0619). What makes something anti-intended is neither its hardness nor its unavailability but its standing *against* the intending mind when one would have it otherwise: not the resistance one sought, but the resistance one refused.

### Insertion 2 — (B→C) Soteriological structure · into P0628 · the debt gloss + the appeal

> Add after "…secularized into a this-worldly entitlement." (the Renaissance-Christianity sentence), before "There is ontological oppression."

It is worth rendering the structure of this entitlement in a sharper, frankly juridical register than Uegaki's own, on the understanding that the rendering is the thesis's gloss and not his term — as with the thought-being above. If a true nature is *promised* to the self (約束された本来性, the promised authenticity), then it is *owed*; and a debt that is owed but unpaid puts the debtor in default. On this reading the given stands, before the elevated subject, as a debtor in permanent default: the anti-intended is not first a misfortune but an *unpaid balance*, the difference between the nature that was promised and the nature reality has so far delivered. This is precisely why ontological freedom is not a cold axiom but an intoxicating one — its appeal is the appeal of being told that one *deserves* to be free of the recalcitrant, that the body's decline and the other's resistance are not simply one's lot but a wrong done to one, a withholding one is entitled to see redressed. And it is why the debt is, decisively, *optional to claim*: the same structure that licenses the demand also leaves open the other path, of releasing the claim and receiving the given as given — the fork the finite-life view takes and the limitless-life view forecloses.

> `[VERIFY]` the metaphor before locking it: 約束された本来性 is grounded as a *promise* of an as-yet-unrealised "true human" (Uegaki, 2022b: 112; glossary 16‑05). The "debt/default" rendering is the thesis's juridico-economic gloss of that promise-structure — mark it as such in the prose, not as Uegaki's vocabulary.

### Insertion 3 — (B→C) Soteriological structure · into P0629 · the CR sharpening (hypostatised-as-transitive)

> Add into the ontic-fallacy paragraph, after "…the taking of a hypostatized ideal for a fact of the world (Bhaskar, 1993: 4)."

Put in the stratified vocabulary of Chapter I, the error has a precise shape. The teleology of the human — the nature it is "meant" to realise — is a piece of transitive, socially produced knowledge, a human-made ideal; the ontic fallacy consists in *hypostatising that transitive ideal as though it were intransitive*, a standing fact of being that reality is failing to deliver. Once the made ideal is mistaken for a real to which the world is answerable, experienced reality is convicted of default — held liable to be bent until it discharges what the hypostatised nature is taken to be owed. The slide the next paragraphs trace, from a right against unjust power to a claim against the given as such, is this hypostatisation working itself out: the transitive promise, frozen into an intransitive entitlement, turns every limit of the given into a withholding.

### Insertion 4 — (B→C) Soteriological structure · into P0630 · Aufhebung is institutional, hence artificiality

> You confirmed *Offenbarung → Aufheben*. Add after "…the freedom claimed for oneself is extended, in principle, to all… But this is, Uegaki argues, naïve, because it leaves the anti-intended unaddressed rather than resolved."

It is worth being fair to the tradition at exactly this point, because the charge of naïveté can be made to sound like a charge of mere hand-waving, and it is not. The Enlightenment did not simply *wish* the tension between the freedom of each and the freedom of all away; it laboured, with enormous seriousness, to *sublate* it — to build the laws, rights, constitutions, and institutions in which individual and collective freedom might be reconciled rather than traded off. Uegaki himself grants that the social apparatus was developed precisely for this, to discharge the inherited inequality the ideal could not abide (Uegaki, 2022b: 131). But this does not rescue the synthesis from the naïveté Uegaki charges; it relocates it. For laws and institutions are not a transcendence of the apparatus — they *are* artificiality, the made order in its conventional register. The proposed sublation of the burden of the other is therefore not its resolution but its *offloading* onto a built system, which is exactly the movement the next sections analyse under the name of the social apparatus. The synthesis is naïve not because it did nothing, but because what it did was to entrust the unbearable remainder to an apparatus and call the entrustment a reconciliation.

### Insertion 5 — (D→E) The migration · into P0637 · the material gate on offloading

> Add into the migration argument (after the Japan-as-legible-case material, or wherever the "why now" is in view).

One condition of this migration deserves to be made explicit, because it guards against reading the offloading as a pure act of will. Whether a given strand of the anti-intended is offloaded at all depends not only on the disposition to offload it but on whether the material artificiality capable of absorbing it yet exists. Much that an earlier age simply bore was borne not because it was affirmed but because no apparatus could take it; the demand was unthinkable for want of the means, and so was never even posed. As the material conditions mature — as the platform, the device, the procedure come into being — strands of the anti-intended that were structurally un-offloadable become, in principle, offloadable, and the disposition that was always latent finds its instrument. The offloading is thus over-determined from the start: it requires both the elevated subject who reads the anti-intended as a debt in default *and* the material artificiality that makes the discharge possible. Uegaki's own fable concedes the point — the hot-iron tale "is no longer a joke" only once technology has made cyborg limbs ordinary (Uegaki, 2022b: 123). Disposition supplies the demand; material artificiality supplies the occasion; neither alone suffices.

### Insertion 6 — D. Two sides of one coin · into P0651 · the distributional asymmetry (footnote, beyond-scope)

> Attach as a footnote to "…freedom-for-the-user and control-by-the-system are the same event described from its two faces."

**Footnote.** That the two faces are one event does not entail that they fall evenly on one population. The same offloading that registers as liberation for those it relieves registers as the anti-intended for those onto whom the discharged burden is displaced — the worker who *is* the gig platform's flexibility, the body that bears the externalised cost of another's optimisation. The unevenness of this distribution is, I suspect, one of the underlying mechanisms of contemporary inequality, the liberation of some being materially continuous with the new unfreedom of others. The claim is large and would require its own apparatus to establish; I record it here only to mark that the "two sides of one coin" is, distributionally as well as conceptually, a single structure, and to flag the question as beyond this thesis's scope.

### Insertion 7 — D. Two sides of one coin · into P0652–P0654 · the second face of the no-respite hell (the recoil-spiral)

> Add after the 無間地獄 paragraph (P0653), which gives the *ideal's* receding terminus. This names a *second*, material engine of the same hell — the A&H recoil — so the two readings converge here.

There is a second road to the same no-respite hell, and it runs not through the ideal's receding terminus but through the apparatus's recoil — which is why the dark reading and the bright one meet exactly at this point. The first road is the one just traced: because the ideal defines oppression as whatever holds the self back from a limitless nature, no finite gain can satisfy it, and the terminus recedes as fast as it is approached. The second road is the one Adorno and Horkheimer saw from their side: the artificiality deployed to discharge the anti-intended does not simply discharge it but *recoils*, throwing up new anti-intended of its own — the second nature that hardens against its makers. Uegaki's own fables already contain this recoil: the ideal is, in part, *realised* — the machine arm is won, the killing is in part averted — and it is precisely the partial realisation that breeds the fresh suffering, the next margin of the body that will not comply, the microbe that the synthetic food must still kill (Uegaki, 2022b: 123). Offloading begets a remainder; the remainder demands a further offloading; and the spiral — choice hardening into compulsion — is the no-respite hell read materially rather than ideally. This is the precise sense in which the two readings of this chapter are two sides of one coin: the value-engine's terminus recedes from in front while the apparatus's recoil presses from behind, and the same subject is wound between them.

### Insertion 8 — D. Two sides of one coin · seam after P0654 · the squeeze as a *driver* of offloading (brief note → Ch VI)

> Add a short paragraph at the seam, before the chapter pivots to its ontological verdict (P0655). Keep it brief; Ch VI carries the full account.

One driver of the offloading remains to be named, and it must be named lightly here and developed where it belongs, in the analysis of the squeeze. Beyond the disposition to offload and the material means to do so, there is the plain fact that some of the anti-intended becomes *unbearable* — and the threshold of the bearable is not fixed, but varies from life to life and, decisively, from age to age. For the thresholds are themselves raised by the very process this thesis is tracing: the world-squeeze named in Chapter III, and the metaverse-squeeze the final chapters will unfold, are among the forces that drive strands of the anti-intended past what a given life can bear, so that offloading ceases to be a disposition one might or might not act on and becomes a pressure one cannot refuse. The squeeze, in other words, is not only the *consequence* of offloading but, in its turn, a *cause* of it — the loop by which a stance that began as one culture's wager is forced upon lives that never made it. That this turns choice into necessity, and so universalises the mode without making everyone a sovereign subject, is the burden of Chapter VI; here it is enough to mark that the offloading the present chapter has anatomised is, at its far edge, no longer simply chosen.
