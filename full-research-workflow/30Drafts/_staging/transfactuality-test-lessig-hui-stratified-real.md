# The transfactuality test for Lessig and Hui

Bhaskar's test for the real level: **a claim is at the real level if it specifies a causal power that holds transfactually — that would still be true under counterfactual conditions where the specific actual mechanism does not hold.** Translated for the thesis: a claim is real-level if it holds for *any* technically-mediated environment, including quantum or biocomputing futures. A claim is actual-level if it depends on the specific current digital substrate (silicon, TCP/IP, HTML, current databases).

Applied to Lessig and Hui, the answer is more interesting than either "they are purely actual-level" or "they belong at the same real-level as 自足完成化." Both have **stratified contributions**: each has a structurally-real core that holds across mediums, and each has medium-dependent applications that don't.

Below the primary text is laid out, then the stratified-real argument the thesis can adopt.

---

## Lessig — what is medium-independent vs. medium-dependent

### Lessig's medium-INDEPENDENT structural claims (real-level candidates)

#### Claim 1: Architecture is a fourth modality of regulation, alongside law, norms, market

> "This is the constraint of architecture—the way the world is, or the ways specific aspects of it are. Architects call it the built environment; those who don't give out names just recognize it as the world around them. Plainly some of the constraints of architecture are constraints we have made (hence the sense of 'architecture') and some are not. A door closes off a room. When locked, the door keeps you out. The constraint functions not as law or norms do—you cannot ignore the constraint and suffer the consequence later." (*Code 2.0*, p. 341)

> "Some architectural constraints, however, are absolute. Star Trek notwithstanding, we cannot travel at warp speed. We can travel fast, and technology has enabled us to travel faster than we used to. Nonetheless, we have good reason (or at least physicists do) for believing that there is a limit to the speed at which we can travel. As a T-shirt I saw at MIT put it, '186,282 miles per second. It's not just a good idea. It's the law.'" (p. 341)

> "Thus, four constraints regulate this pathetic dot—the law, social norms, the market, and architecture—and the 'regulation' of this dot is the sum of these four constraints. Changes in any one will affect the regulation of the whole." (p. 122)

**Why this is medium-independent:** Lessig's "pathetic dot" is *any* agent in *any* environment with regulatory modalities. Architecture as a regulator includes locked doors, the speed of light, town planning, gravity. Code is the *cyberspace instance* of architecture; architecture itself is the structural-real category.

#### Claim 2: Architectural regulation operates by physical/structural constraint, not by command

> "The question here is not how the architecture of the Net will make it easier for traditional regulation to happen. The issue here is how the architecture of the Net—or its 'code'—itself becomes a regulator. In this context, the rule applied to an individual does not find its force from the threat of consequences enforced by the law—fines, jail, or even shame. Instead, **the rule is applied to an individual through a kind of physics. A locked door is not a command 'do not enter' backed up with the threat of punishment by the state. A locked door is a physical constraint** on the liberty of someone to enter some space." (p. 82)

> "**Architectural constraints, then, work whether or not the subject knows they are working**, while law and norms work only if the subject knows something about them." (p. 344)

**Why this is medium-independent:** the structural-physical regulation mechanism (constraint that operates without requiring the subject's knowledge) is medium-neutral. It would hold under quantum or biocomputing — wherever architecture has affordances.

#### Claim 3: Regulation-of-code-regulates-behavior — the indirect-regulation principle

> "It may well be difficult for the government to regulate behavior directly, given the architecture of the Internet as it is. But that doesn't mean it is difficult for the government to regulate the architecture of the Internet as it is. **The trick, then, is for the government to take steps that induce the development of an architecture that makes behavior more regulable.**" (p. 61)

> "In each case, the government directs an intermediary that has some power over code to change that code to effect a change in behavior." (p. 118)

**Why this is medium-independent:** the principle that *whoever-has-power-over-the-architectural-medium has indirect regulatory power over the regulated* is structurally about layered governance, not about silicon specifically. It would hold for any architecturally-mediated environment.

#### Claim 4: Lessig's own meta-claim that the analysis is medium-independent

This is the strongest evidence. Lessig himself signals that his framework is general:

> "**Regulation in cyberspace can help us see something important about how all regulation works.** That's the lesson of the first theme, 'regulability.'" (p. 27)

> "These stories suggest a technique, and once we see the idea, we'll recognize the technique in many different contexts of regulation. If Second Life can use code to better control behavior, what about first-life? If AOL can use code to better control fraud, what about America off-line? … **The answer is that policy makers have done the same in real space for a long time.**" (pp. 113–114)

> "Like Michael Sorkin, I believe that 'meanings inhere in forms, and that the settings for social life can aid its fulfillment.' … Whatever the source of the content of these codes, he writes, 'their consequences are built.' This is the feature to focus on." (p. 344)

> "This chapter is a step toward that more general understanding. It is an invitation to think beyond the threat to liberty from government power. It is a map for this more general understanding." (p. 121)

**Lessig is explicit:** the cyberspace analysis is meant to illuminate "how all regulation works." This is a **transfactual real-level claim** by Lessig's own frame.

### Lessig's medium-DEPENDENT claims (actual-level)

- The specific architecture of Net95 / TCP/IP
- East Coast Code (Washington statutory) vs. West Coast Code (Silicon Valley software) — geographically tied
- Specific platform examples (AOL, Second Life, Facebook)
- DRM / encryption / specific examples of West Coast Code regulation

These are *applications* of the structural-real claims to current-medium specifics.

## Hui — what is medium-independent vs. medium-dependent

### Hui's medium-INDEPENDENT structural claims (real-level candidates)

#### Claim 1: Productive ontologies — schemas MAKE digital objects objects (Kantian-categorial structural claim)

> "Ontologies are what make digital objects objects rather than merely data. **Ontologies are productive, like Kant's categories.** Categories capture data and organize them in an order that unifies the manifold elements of their presence. Analogically, ontologies give a machine the ability to recognize and operate the object as a unity instead of as random data."

> "We can compare this with Kant's schemata, as the fusion of the pure concepts or categories that gives rise to phenomena from sense-data."

**Why this is medium-independent:** the *Kantian-categorial structure* (productive schemas constitute objects from raw data) is a transcendental claim — it would hold under quantum-data or bio-data; what would change is the specific schema, not the structural fact that schemas are required and productive.

#### Claim 2: Theory of relations vs. theory of substance — Hui's explicit medium-independence

This is Hui's most explicit anti-silicon-reductionist statement:

> "Digital objects appear to human users as colourful and visible beings. At the level of programming they are text files; further down the operating system they are binary codes; finally, at the level of circuit boards they are nothing but signals generated by the values of voltage and the operation of logic gates. How, then, can we think about the voltage differences as being the substance of a digital object? **Searching downward we may end up with the mediation of silicon and metal. And finally we could go into particles and fields. But this kind of reductionism doesn't tell us much about the world.**"

> "The transcendence of the object thus totally collapses in digital objects… objects fall into the field of total immanence. **A new theory must therefore move away from the question of substance, and that for me is a theory of relations.**"

> "**The digital object is utterly relational.**"

**Why this is medium-independent:** Hui *explicitly* refuses to identify digital being with silicon. The relational ontology is what holds; the medium is contingent. Quantum or bio computing would change the *medium* of the relations, not the *fact* that digital being is relational.

#### Claim 3: Associated milieu is constitutive of technical individuation (Simondonian structural claim)

> "A technical individual is a technical object that supports the functioning of its inner structure, at the same time as it is able to incorporate an external milieu into its functioning. **This external milieu is what Simondon calls the associated milieu**, which provides a stabilizing function that restores the equilibrium of the system itself."

Hui himself signals the cross-medium generality with the **Guimbal turbine** (a *physical*, non-digital technical individual):

> "Simondon often spoke of the Guimbal turbine (named after the engineer who invented it), which… uses oil to lubricate the engine and at the same time isolate it from water; it is then also possible to **integrate a river as the drive and the cooling agent of the turbine. The river here is the external milieu for the engine**…"

The current-Web instantiation is then a specific application:

> "We can see that databases, algorithms, and network protocols become the associated milieux of digital objects."

**Why this is medium-independent:** the structural fact (technical individuals require associated milieus for stability and individuation) holds for turbines, digital objects, and would hold for quantum or bio-computational individuals. What varies is *what serves as* the associated milieu.

#### Claim 4: Algorithmic governmentality — modulation independent of processing medium

> "Rouvroy goes deeper into the subject and proposes that the neoliberal mode of governmentality… has been already overtaken by what she calls algorithmic governmentality. **The logic of modulation does not only operate through infrastructures such as networks, but is rather embedded in all types of apparatus** (for the purpose of data collection, recommendation, restriction)."

> "In such a modulative process, however, each individual is finally divided into what Deleuze calls the 'dividual'. Rouvroy observes that this form of governmentality does not address the subject, but rather the **infra-individual** (which are numerised relations) and the **supra-individual** (meaning the profile automatically constructed by machines through pattern analysis)."

**Why this is medium-independent:** the logic of *modulation* (continuous adjustment based on pattern analysis) and the *dividual* fragmentation are claims about the structure of governmentality, not about the silicon medium specifically. Quantum or bio-computing apparatuses would only accelerate this transfactual logic.

#### Claim 5: Cosmotechnics — the most fundamentally medium-independent claim

> "**Technics is not anthropologically universal**; technologies in different cultures are affected by the cosmological understandings of these cultures, and have autonomy only within a certain cosmological setting—**technics is always cosmotechnics**."

> "I will give a preliminary definition of cosmotechnics here: **it means the unification between the cosmic order and the moral order through technical activities**."

> "For our purpose here, cosmotechnics should be understood as an Urtechnik, **it challenges our current understanding of technology and therefore also its future**."

**Why this is medium-independent:** cosmotechnics is a meta-claim about the *plurality* of technical orders. It would hold under any future medium: each new medium would be embedded in a particular cosmological-moral order, and there is no universal medium-independent "Technology."

### Hui's medium-DEPENDENT claims (actual-level)

- Specific HTML / XML / SGML / GML history
- The Berners-Lee semantic-web vision
- The current "objectification of data" / "dataification of objects" movements (specific to current Web)
- The current state of databases, algorithms, network protocols as associated milieux

These are *applications* of the structural-real claims to the current Web.

## The argument: stratified real

Both thinkers fail the simple "purely actual-level" reading because **each has explicitly transfactual structural claims that pass the Bhaskar test**. Both also fail the "same-stratum-as-自足完成化" reading because the thesis's meta-law operates at the *whole-environment* stratum, while Lessig's and Hui's claims operate at the *architectural-mechanism* stratum.

The clean resolution is **stratified real** — a Bhaskarian laminated ontology:

```
Real level (transfactual)
  ╞══ META-LAW STRATUM
  │     自足完成化 — disposition of artificiality-as-environment 
  │                     to become self-sufficient
  │     (Yang's named real-level meta-law; viva-passed §V)
  │
  ╞══ ARCHITECTURAL-MECHANISM STRATUM
  │     ├─ Lessig: architecture as fourth modality of regulation
  │     │     — regulatory grammar, medium-independent
  │     │     — "regulation in cyberspace can help us see 
  │     │        something important about how all regulation works"
  │     │
  │     └─ Hui: productive ontologies via associated milieus
  │           — ontological-constitution grammar, medium-independent
  │           — relations not substance; cosmotechnics
  │           — Rouvroy/dividual algorithmic-governmentality logic
  
Actual level (current-medium mechanisms)
  ├─ Specific code-as-law applications (TCP/IP, content moderation,
  │   DRM, platform algorithms — all in current silicon)
  └─ Specific digital-object/milieu instantiations 
      (HTML, XML, current databases, semantic web — all in current Web)

Empirical level (specific events)
  ├─ Specific algorithmic suppressions
  ├─ Specific platform incidents
  └─ Specific user experiences of opacity, mutability, etc.
```

### Why this stratification is correct (and defensible to Mi)

**(a) It honors what each author explicitly says.** Lessig: "all regulation works" this way — explicit transfactual self-claim. Hui: silicon-reductionism "doesn't tell us much about the world" — explicit medium-independence.

**(b) It preserves the structural priority of 自足完成化.** The meta-law remains the superordinate real-level disposition; Lessig's and Hui's architectural-real claims are *what the meta-law works through* — they specify the structural conditions of any technically-mediated environment that, when intensified, enables self-completion.

**(c) It matches Bhaskar's laminated ontology.** Bhaskar (1979/2014) explicitly endorses stratified-real claims at different ontological strata: a "real" mechanism at one stratum can be embedded within (and operate through) a "real" mechanism at a higher stratum. The thesis already uses Bhaskar's stratification; this is its proper extension.

**(d) It clarifies where Lessig's and Hui's contributions go.** Specifically: Lessig and Hui are NOT competitors with 自足完成化 (they don't propose alternative meta-laws). They are CONDITIONS OF POSSIBILITY for the meta-law's actualization — the architectural-grammatical facts about how any technically-mediated environment could produce a self-completing tendency.

### What this means for Ch-5-3 v2 §1

If the stratified-real solution is accepted, Ch-5-3 v2 §1 should be structured:

1. **Real level — meta-law stratum**: state 自足完成化 as the superordinate disposition (as in viva-passed §V).
2. **Real level — architectural-mechanism stratum**: state Lessig's and Hui's structural-real claims as the conditions through which the meta-law operates. *Both are medium-independent (real); both are subordinate to 自足完成化.*
3. **Actual level**: the four "minimally sufficient technologies" (AI / big data / IoT / code-as-law) become instantiations of the architectural-real grammar in current silicon. Plus the five operational signatures (opacity, mutability, etc.).
4. **Empirical level**: specific platform examples.

### What this means for the user's intuition (Group 1 / Group 2)

**Slight refinement.** Group 1 (Obara/Uegaki/CT/Stiegler) describe the *generative momentum* at the real-level meta-law stratum. Group 2 (Lessig/Hui) describe the *structural conditions* at the real-level architectural-mechanism stratum AND their actual-level current-Web applications. The Group 2 thinkers are *not entirely* at the actual level — they are layered, with both real and actual contributions.

This refinement strengthens the user's overall thesis architecture by giving Lessig and Hui their proper philosophical depth.

## Honest assessment of the argument's validity

**Strong points:**
- Both Lessig and Hui *explicitly* signal the medium-independence of their structural claims (Lessig: "all regulation works"; Hui: anti-silicon-reductionism)
- The Bhaskarian laminated-ontology framework already in the thesis methodology supports stratified-real claims at different strata
- The proposed stratification is internally consistent and parsimonious
- It does not weaken the priority of 自足完成化; if anything, it strengthens it by specifying the structural conditions through which it operates

**Vulnerable points / objections to anticipate:**
- Mi might object that adding a second real-level stratum complicates the analysis. **Defense**: Bhaskar's laminated ontology explicitly supports this; the alternatives (collapsing Lessig/Hui to actual-level despite their explicit transfactual claims, or treating them as competing meta-laws) both have worse philosophical costs.
- Liu might object that this expands the thesis's scope. **Defense**: it does not add new content — it relocates existing content (Lessig in §V.A, Hui in proposed §V.A.b) at a more accurate ontological stratum. The thesis prose changes are minimal; the architectural clarification is significant.
- A determined critic might argue Hui's claims are still tied to the "digital" specifically. **Defense**: Hui himself extends the framework to physical technical individuals (Guimbal turbine), and the cosmotechnics claim is meta-medium.

**Limit of the argument:**
- The stratified-real solution assumes Bhaskar's laminated ontology is the right philosophical scaffolding; if a critic rejects that scaffolding, the argument needs to be rebuilt on different grounds.
- The current revision draft's §V architecture should be checked to determine whether this stratification can be added cleanly, or whether it would require substantial restructuring.

---

## Recommendation

**Stratified-real solution**, with Lessig's and Hui's structural-medium-independent cores promoted to a real-level *architectural-mechanism* stratum, subordinate to 自足完成化 at the meta-law stratum.

Alternative: a *single*-real solution (where only 自足完成化 is at real and Lessig/Hui are entirely at actual, with the medium-independent claims explicitly bracketed) — simpler but requires the thesis to *not engage* the medium-independence question, weakening the thesis if a critic raises it.
