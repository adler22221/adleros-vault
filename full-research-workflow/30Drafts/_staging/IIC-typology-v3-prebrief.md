The author cannot afford to defend these in thesis scope.

**Result — Refined Framing X (endorsed in dialogue)**:
- Artificiality is the umbrella category (humanly-produced organizing principles). The thesis takes religion, ideology, morals, legal codes, etc. as artificiality without prejudging revelation-claims or means-end reductions.
- Technology and virtuality are *cross-cutting analytical lenses*, not axes of the typology:
  - **Technology lens** (§IIA, Stiegler-broad): "how does this artificiality exteriorize biological/cognitive function?" Applied where illuminating, not as blanket categorization.
  - **Virtuality lens** (§IIB, Giddens/Bhaskar-anchored): "what is this artificiality's nonmaterial-causally-efficacious dimension?" Applied where illuminating.
- The typology's axes are **substrate × register**:
  - Axis 1: material / nonmaterial substrate
  - Axis 2: conventional / structural register `[TERMINOLOGY-PENDING]`
- Cells 1-4 are populated by *artificialities*; technology and virtuality are concepts we *apply to analyze* the cells, not labels for whole cells.

## §2 — Virtuality terminology economy

The question "if virtuality is co-extensive with nonmaterial artificiality under §IIB, is the term 'virtuality' still necessary?" was raised in the 2026-05-13 dialogue.

**Author's preliminary view (endorsed)**: Yes, virtuality is necessary.

**Reasons** (from dialogue):
1. **Rhetorical / strategic**: thesis title is *Between the Universe and the Metaverse*. The popular metaverse = XR/VR/computer-based virtuality. The thesis must engage and reframe this; "nonmaterial artificiality" cannot do the rhetorical work at the title/introduction/§V level.
2. **Lineage redefinition**: §IIB's load-bearing move is redefining virtuality against Pierce's "as if," Chalmers' "computer-based," Baudrillard's simulationism. The redefinition only works if "virtuality" is the term being redefined.
3. **Historical-continuum**: §IIB's continuum from animal proto-virtuality (Obara: gorilla chest-beating, crane courtship) through gods/money/narrative (Durante, Harari) to algorithmic systems requires "virtuality" as the through-line.
4. **Cross-disciplinary engagement**: virtuality has currency in philosophy (Deleuze, Bergson, Giddens, Bhaskar), media studies (Lévy, Manovich), STS, digital studies. The thesis's contribution is partly a redefinition that engages these literatures.
5. **Etymological substance**: virtuality's Latin root *virtus* (potency/power) is part of the thesis's argument (per §IIB's reading of Pierce: keep "efficiency (virtus)," drop "as if"). The term carries substantive meaning; "nonmaterial artificiality" doesn't.

**Author's concern**: "we need to avoid confusing readers' with too many concepts."

**Proposed handling (deployment strategy)**:
- *§IIB, §V, §VI, introduction*: use **"virtuality"** — where conceptual lineage, redefinition, historical continuum, and metaverse-argument-bridge all need the term.
- *§IIC typology*: use **"nonmaterial artificiality"** as the substrate-axis label — where the analytical work is substrate × register, not the philosophical-cultural concept of the virtual.
- *Defensive sentence* in §IIC.2 stipulating: "nonmaterial artificiality" in the typology axis = "virtuality" per §IIB's definition; the two are co-extensive but rhetorically deployed in different contexts.

Two terms, one population, stipulated equivalence — manageable for readers.

## §3 — Technology and virtuality as cross-cutting lenses (not as axis values)

**The "religion as technology" problem** (the load-bearing reason for refined Framing X):

The author raised: technology, in colloquial AND much philosophical usage, has a *means-to-end* structure (Stiegler's compensation-for-biological-weakness; tools as means; survival as end). Religions, however, present themselves as *revealers of truth* (theogenic / revelation traditions). Calling religion "technology" imposes the means-end framing from outside, contradicting the tradition's self-understanding and attracting predictable theological pushback. Similarly, "science is technology" inverts the common-sense direction (science → technology becomes technology = science) and attracts scientific-realist pushback. Both fights are out-of-scope.

**Resolution**: technology and virtuality are *analytical lenses* the thesis applies to artificialities where illuminating, not ontological categories the thesis assigns to all artificiality.

- Religion → artificiality (humanly-produced civilizational organizing system); analyzable via the *virtuality lens* (it's nonmaterial-causally-efficacious; cell 3 dominant); *also* analyzable via the technology lens *where applicable* (e.g., when analyzing the technology of writing-mediated religious tradition; or specific functional aspects of religious institutions). The thesis does NOT blanket-categorize religion as technology.
- Science → similarly artificiality (the institution + the method + the body of knowledge); analyzable via various lenses; not blanket-reduced to "technology."
- Algorithms / code → artificiality (engineered organizing principles); analyzable as *technology* (clearly: they are exteriorized computational mechanisms) AND as *virtuality* (clearly: they are nonmaterial-causally-efficacious). The two lenses CONVERGE on cell 4 phenomena.
- A wall → artificiality (engineered physical structure); analyzable as *technology* (clearly); NOT analyzable as virtuality (it's material, not §IIB virtuality). Cell 1 phenomenon under the technology lens; the virtuality lens doesn't apply.

This preserves §IIA's technology concept and §IIB's virtuality concept intact while letting the typology classify artificialities by structural-substrate-register without imposing lens-categorizations on whole cells.
