## Recommended Unified Passage (to replace both occurrences)

### Version A — Full (~450 words, for the §I canonical definition location at lines 184–188)

The nested view of dual ecosystems — an artificial ecosystem within a natural ecosystem — maps directly onto two concepts we use to denote totality: the world and the universe.

The word "world" carries its meaning in its etymology. With Germanic origin — *Welt* or *vereld*, combining *vir* ("man") and *alt* ("age") — its root meaning is "age of man" (*World, n.*, Oxford English Dictionary Online, 2022). As the historian of philosophy Rémi Brague has shown in his comparative historical survey (2003), this etymology reflects a deeper semantic commitment: "world" almost never designates a physical reality as such. It is anthropological rather than cosmological — its "worldliness" derives not from the totality it names but from the human beings for whom it is a totality. A world is a totality-for-us: it is bounded, organized, and constituted by the fact of human habitation and the meaning-structures humans bring to it.

By contrast, "universe" derives from the Latin *ūniversum* — a compound of *uni-* ("one") and *vertere* ("to turn") — meaning "the whole, the sum of existing things, complete in all its parts, whole, regarded as a whole, affecting everyone or everything" (*Universe, n.*, Oxford English Dictionary Online, 2022; *Encyclopædia Britannica*, 2017). Unlike "world," the universe is not constituted by human presence. Its boundaries, if it has any, are set by physical reality, not by human occupation or recognition. It is a totality-in-itself.

Drawing on this distinction, I define the two concepts as follows:

**A world** is a totality dependent on human existence. Its contents are the material constructions, nonmaterial institutions, shared meanings, and causally efficacious practices through which a human community organizes its survival within — and against — the universe. A world contains the built environment, the legal and economic systems, the symbolic and communicative practices, the tools and techniques: in short, everything humans have produced and collectively maintain to render existence livable. Worlds are bounded; they can fail; they are historically specific.

**The universe** is a totality independent of human existence. Its contents include everything that would obtain whether or not humans existed: physical matter, energy, biological life, geological processes, the laws of thermodynamics and quantum mechanics. In relation to humanity, the universe is the background environment that provides the material substrate for human survival while simultaneously threatening, challenging, or pressuring it — indifferently, without regard for human purposes.

Crucially, the world's totality is *smaller in portion* than the universe's — not in functionality, but in scope. A world is nested within the universe; it does not contain the universe. The world has always been a human-made enclosure within a larger environment it can shape at the margins but cannot master in whole. In this sense, our worlds have always been artificial: they are what humans have built against, within, and in response to a universe they did not create and cannot fully control.

### Version B — Condensed (~200 words, for contexts where brevity is required)

The words "world" and "universe" carry their distinction in their etymology. "World" — from Germanic *Welt* or *vereld*, combining "man" and "age" — means "age of man": it is anthropological, designating a totality constituted by human habitation. As Rémi Brague (2003) has shown, a world's "worldliness" derives not from physical extent but from human presence and meaning-making. "Universe" — from Latin *ūniversum*, "one" + "to turn" — designates the whole sum of existing things, independent of human recognition or occupation.

On this basis, I define **world** as a totality *dependent* on human existence: its contents are the built environments, institutions, symbolic systems, and collective practices through which humans organize survival within the universe. Its contents are human-made and historically specific. I define **universe** as a totality *independent* of human existence: its contents — physical matter, biological life, natural laws — would obtain without us. A world is nested within the universe; its scope is smaller, its contents artificial. In this sense, our worlds have always been artificial: they are what humans build in response to a universe they did not create.

## Answer to Mi's "totality包含什麼?"

The unified passage answers Mi's question directly:

| Totality | Contents | How constituted |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| World | Built environments, institutions, laws, economic systems, symbolic systems, communicative practices, tools, techniques — everything humans produce and maintain to render survival possible | Dependent on human existence; would not obtain without human world-making; historically and culturally specific |
| Universe | Physical matter, energy, biological life, geological processes, thermodynamic laws, quantum mechanics — all that would obtain with or without humans | Independent of human existence; not bounded by human recognition or purpose |

The key clarification for Mi: the world's totality is not *smaller* in the sense of *less functional* — it is smaller in *scope*. A world is a bounded human-made enclosure nested within a larger, unbounded universe. The world *works* within the universe; it does not encompass it.
