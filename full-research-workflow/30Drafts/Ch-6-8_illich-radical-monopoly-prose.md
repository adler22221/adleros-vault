## Prose Draft — Full Version

The structural threat this thesis describes is not that the emerging metaverse will simply be very large, or that one corporation will become very powerful. These are familiar forms of dominance — the kind against which antitrust law, data regulation, and platform governance are designed to provide protection. The deeper threat is of a different order, and it has been named with precision by Ivan Illich.

In *Tools for Conviviality* (1973), Illich distinguishes between two kinds of monopoly. *Commercial monopoly* occurs when one firm gains exclusive control over a market: Coca-Cola controls the soft-drink market in a given region; one television channel is licensed to broadcast. This is familiar territory for regulation. *Radical monopoly* is of a different kind:

> "By radical monopoly I mean a kind of dominance by one product that goes far beyond what the concept of monopoly usually implies. [...] I speak about radical monopoly when one industrial production process exercises an exclusive control over the satisfaction of a pressing need, and excludes nonindustrial activities from competition."

— Illich, *Tools for Conviviality*, pp. 64–65

The clearest illustration is the automobile. Cars do not achieve radical monopoly by being the only cars on the market, or even by being cheaper or better than alternatives. They achieve it by *reshaping the environment* in which the need for transportation arises:

> "Cars can thus monopolize traffic. They can shape a city into their image — practically ruling out locomotion on foot or by bicycle in Los Angeles. [...] That motor traffic curtails the right to walk, not that more people drive Chevies than Fords, constitutes radical monopoly."

— Illich, *Tools for Conviviality*, p. 65

The critical feature of radical monopoly is that it is *environmentally achieved* rather than commercially imposed. Once a city has been structured around the automobile — highways, distances, suburban dispersal — the capacity for independent foot travel is not merely inconvenient; it becomes practically impossible. No one has compelled anyone to use a car; the environment has been transformed so that not using a car becomes a form of incapacity. The monopoly is held not by any particular corporation but by a *mode of production* — the automobile-structured world — over the entire domain of transportation needs.

Illich extends the analysis beyond transport:

> "Radical monopoly exists where a major tool rules out natural competence. Radical monopoly imposes compulsory consumption and thereby restricts personal autonomy. It constitutes a special kind of social control because it is enforced by means of the imposed consumption of a standard product that only large institutions can provide."

— Illich, *Tools for Conviviality*, p. 66

Schools, medicine, social work, civil engineering: each, when it crosses what Illich calls the "second watershed" of industrial development, establishes a radical monopoly over the domain it was built to serve. Schools monopolize not the market for educational goods but the very definition of *learning* — the unschooled person is not merely economically disadvantaged but officially illiterate, regardless of what they know. Medical institutions monopolize not a drug market but the definition of *health* — the patient who does not comply with professional management becomes, in the eyes of the system, delinquent to their own condition.

The result is what Illich calls *modernized poverty*: "Unchecked industrialization modernizes poverty. [...] The radical monopoly of industries has created new types of demeaning poverty in societies of sometimes profligate affluence" (p. 84). Modernized poverty is not material destitution but *deprivation of the power to act*. When institutional systems eliminate indigenous competence, people lose not access to goods but the capacity to satisfy their own needs through their own means. The subsistence farmer who is drawn into the wage economy and the cash-crop system may eat less well than before; more precisely, he has lost the competence and the infrastructure to feed himself by the means he previously used. The deprivation is real and radical, but it shows up in no poverty metric calibrated to commodity access.

---

Applied to the metaverse, the radical monopoly concept describes the structural dynamic of the squeeze with a precision that commercial and regulatory frameworks miss.

The digital-bureaucratic apparatus that constitutes the emerging metaverse does not coerce participation by law. It does not forbid walking to work, farming one's own land, or trading face-to-face in a local market. What it does — gradually, systemically, and largely without explicit design — is restructure the environment of livelihood such that the competencies, credentials, and capacities required for effective social and economic participation increasingly presuppose platform-mediated existence. The radical monopoly is not held by any firm (not even Meta, Google, or the Chinese state individually) but by *the digital mode of production as such*, over the entire domain of livelihood.

Consider the cascading singularization: employment platforms (Upwork, LinkedIn, national job-posting systems) restructure labor markets such that digital presence becomes prerequisite for economic access — the unregistered worker is not merely disadvantaged but invisible to the hiring infrastructure. Financial platforms restructure payment and credit such that the unbanked and un-digitized cannot participate in the formal economy. Administrative platforms restructure civic life such that welfare payments, healthcare registration, visa applications, and tax compliance increasingly require digital access and digital literacy — the unequipped citizen is not merely inconvenienced but structurally excluded from services nominally guaranteed to all. Educational platforms, accelerated by the COVID-19 pandemic, restructured learning such that the school without a learning management system, and the student without a device and connection, was not merely slow but non-participatory. Each of these restructurings is partial, incremental, and nominally optional; together, they constitute a radical monopoly of the digital mode of production over the conditions of livelihood itself.

This is not merely a question of access or inequality — though it is also those things. It is a question of *world*. The thesis has argued throughout that the world, understood as the human-made meaning-space through which life is organized and given direction, is constituted through the diversity of practices, competencies, and modes of inhabitation that a given civilization sustains. When radical monopoly eliminates vernacular pluralism — when the diversity of ways of working, learning, healing, trading, and belonging is replaced by a single institutionally managed mode — it does not merely restrict choices. It narrows the world. The lived world loses the plurality of practices through which human beings maintain multiple orientations to the universe; it contracts toward a single institutionally designed space whose structure reflects the requirements of platforms rather than the requirements of human life.

Ivan Illich identified this dynamic in 1973, with the automobile and the hospital as his examples. What this thesis argues is that the emerging metaverse represents the same dynamic at the civilizational scale — not a sector-level radical monopoly over transport or medicine, but a *total-environment* radical monopoly over the conditions under which any need can be satisfied, any competence can be recognized, any livelihood can be sustained. The squeeze, in this register, is not a metaphor for discomfort; it is the structural experience of living in a world that is being reorganized around a mode of production to which one's inherited capacities, languages, and practices are becoming progressively incommensurable.

## Condensed Version (if space is limited)

Ivan Illich distinguished two kinds of monopoly. *Commercial monopoly* restricts which firm provides a good; *radical monopoly* — his more serious concern — occurs "when one industrial production process exercises an exclusive control over the satisfaction of a pressing need, and excludes nonindustrial activities from competition" (Illich 1973, p. 65). Cars achieve radical monopoly not by being the only cars but by restructuring cities so that walking becomes impossible: the monopoly is environmental, not commercial. The result is what Illich called "modernized poverty" — not material destitution but deprivation of the power to act, the systematic elimination of indigenous competence by institutional design (p. 84).

The digital-bureaucratic apparatus constituting the metaverse imposes radical monopoly at a civilizational scale. It does not forbid alternative modes of livelihood; it restructures the environment of economic and civic life such that platform-mediated existence becomes prerequisite for participation — invisible to hiring systems without a digital profile, excluded from welfare without digital literacy, unable to compete educationally without connectivity. No single corporation holds this monopoly; it is held by the *digital mode of production as such*. What the thesis calls the singularization of livelihood is the experiential correlate of this structural transformation: the progressive narrowing of the world to a single institutionally managed space, displacing the plurality of practices through which diverse human orientations to the universe were maintained.
