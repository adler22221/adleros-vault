# Ch-3-9 LOOKUP/PROSE: Nelson Goodman — Plurality of Actual Worlds (Chapter III)

## Recalibration note (2026-05-30)

The original Ch-1-13 recruited Goodman to support the §I claim that "the world is a human construction." The finished DOCX no longer needs Goodman for that: Chapter I establishes world-as-construction through niche-construction theory, Obara's dual ecosystems, and Brague's *kosmos*, and it already draws the singular/plural (kind-vs-instance) distinction and disambiguates from modal-logic "possible worlds" in §E ([P0108], plus the Ch-1-11 footnote ^2).

Goodman's live work in the thesis is now in **Chapter III**. The DOCX Ch3 opens its argument by relativizing the Modern world:

> "By putting worlds into perspective, we find the world (and the mode of artificiality and survival it represents) we live in as relative instead of universal and absolute. This opens the possibility of a new, concrete sense of **other possible worlds (in contrast to the abstract Leibnizian one)**, which is a key for later discussion." (DOCX Ch3, [P0132])

This is exactly Goodman's distinctive contribution: the move from "a world fixed and found" to "a diversity of right and even conflicting versions or worlds in the making," and the explicit refusal of Leibnizian/modal "possible worlds" in favor of *multiple actual worlds*. So Goodman belongs precisely where the DOCX says "concrete sense of other possible worlds (in contrast to the abstract Leibnizian one)" — supplying the philosophical warrant for treating worlds as plural-and-actual, which is the premise Ch3 needs in order to present Western Modernity as **one mode of artificiality among others** rather than the universal endpoint.

No change to the canonical stance is required (Goodman concerns the *world* concept, not the artificiality/virtuality distinction). The only recalibration is the relocation and the re-pointing of the citation's argumentative job: from "worlds are constructed" (Ch1, now redundant) to "worlds are plural and actual, hence the Modern world is relative" (Ch3, load-bearing).

## Key passages (carried from the verified Ch-1-13 lookup; Zotero WZEEWC4D, PDF attached)

### Passage 1 — Foreword (the headline statement for Ch3 relativization)
> "The movement is from unique truth and a world fixed and found to a diversity of right and even conflicting versions or worlds in the making."
> — Goodman, *Ways of Worldmaking* (Hackett, 1978), Foreword, p. ix.

**Ch3 relevance:** Directly underwrites [P0132]'s relativization — the Modern world ceases to be "unique truth … fixed and found" and becomes one of a "diversity of … versions or worlds."

### Passage 2 — Distinguishing from "possible worlds" (Ch. I §2, pp. 2–3) — the load-bearing passage for [P0132]
> "Let it be clear that the question here is not of the possible worlds that many of my contemporaries, especially those near Disneyland, are busy making and manipulating. We are not speaking in terms of multiple possible alternatives to a single actual world but of multiple [actual worlds]."
> — Goodman, *Ways of Worldmaking*, Ch. I §2, pp. 2–3.

**Ch3 relevance:** This is the exact disambiguation the DOCX flags ("in contrast to the abstract Leibnizian one"). Goodman's worlds are *multiple actual*, not modal alternatives to one actual world. This is what licenses Ch3 to treat the Sinosphere world, the Mediterranean-Modern world, etc., as plural actual modes of artificiality.

### Passage 3 — Worldmaking as remaking (Ch. I §4, p. 7)
> "Worldmaking as we know it always starts from worlds already on hand; the making is a remaking."
> — Goodman, *Ways of Worldmaking*, Ch. I §4, p. 7.

**Ch3 relevance:** Supports the historical-sedimentation reading Ch3 needs — the Modern (Mediterranean) mode is itself a *remaking* of prior worlds, and (anticipating Ch5) the metaverse is a remaking of the Modern world. NB: this passage is also used in Ch-1-11 §B; see Coordination below to avoid double-quoting.

### Passage 4 — Versions as worlds / irrealism (Ch. I §2)
> "Right world-descriptions and world-depictions and world-perceptions, the ways-the-world-is, or just versions, can be treated as our worlds."

**Ch3 relevance:** Use with caution. Goodman's full irrealism ("no single underlying world independent of our symbol systems") is *stronger* than the thesis's position, which is realist about the **universe** (the substrate "neither a god nor a man made") and constructivist only about **worlds** nested within it. In Ch3, cite Goodman for plurality-of-worlds, but do **not** import his denial of an observer-independent substrate — that would contradict the universe/world ontology. A one-line footnote should mark this divergence (see below).

### Passage 5 — Cassirer framing (Ch. I §1, p. 1)
> "Countless worlds made from nothing by use of symbols… the multiplicity of worlds, the speciousness of 'the given', the creative power of the understanding, the variety and formative function of symbols."

**Ch3 relevance:** Optional. The "multiplicity of worlds" theme is useful; "the speciousness of 'the given'" again overshoots the thesis's realism about the universe and should not be endorsed without the divergence footnote.

## Recommended Chapter III citation form (drop-in near [P0132])

> This relativization has a precise philosophical articulation in Nelson Goodman's *Ways of Worldmaking* (1978). Goodman describes "the movement … from unique truth and a world fixed and found to a diversity of right and even conflicting versions or worlds in the making" (p. ix). Crucially — and this is what the present argument requires — Goodman is explicit that these are not Leibnizian *possible* worlds: "we are not speaking in terms of multiple possible alternatives to a single actual world but of multiple [actual worlds]" (pp. 2–3). The plurality at issue is not a space of modal alternatives to one real world but a plurality of *actual* worlds, each a different mode of artificiality realized under different conditions, none of which is the world "fixed and found." Western Modernity is, in this light, one such actual world — a remaking of worlds "already on hand" (p. 7), not the universal terminus that its own self-understanding takes it to be.[^goodman-realism]
>
> [^goodman-realism]: This thesis adopts Goodman's plurality of *actual* worlds but not his full irrealism. Goodman denies any single world independent of symbol systems; this thesis is realist about the **universe** — the substrate "neither a god nor a man made" (Ch1, Heraclitus via Brague 2003) — and constructivist only about **worlds** nested within it. The borrowing is therefore selective: Goodman's plurality, not his anti-realism about the given.
