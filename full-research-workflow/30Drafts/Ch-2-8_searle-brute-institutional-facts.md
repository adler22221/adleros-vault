# Ch-2-8 GENERATIVE: Searle's Brute/Institutional Facts — Chapter II footnote (virtuality section)

## Recalibration note (2026-05-30)

The finished DOCX develops the world/universe distinction in Chapter I entirely through Obara (dual ecosystems), Uexküll (Umwelt / Umgebung), and Brague (the Greek *kosmos* as the totality "neither a god nor a man" made). It does **not** route that distinction through Searle, and does not need to. Searle's contribution is therefore moved to Chapter II, where the live question is no longer *what is a world* but *how can a nonmaterial artificiality be causally real*.

Chapter II's §II.B ("Virtuality as causally efficacious nonmaterial artificiality") makes the central claim that nonmaterial artificialities — Harari's "gods, money, and laws" — "have the power to direct our thoughts and behaviors or even elicit emotions or biological reflexes." Searle is the natural philosophical interlocutor for exactly this claim: his institutional facts are precisely nonmaterial facts that nonetheless do real causal work in the social world. Recruiting Searle here strengthens §II.B's realism; the author's critique then shows where the thesis must go *beyond* Searle.

This is consistent with the canonical stance: **artificiality** is the umbrella (Stiegler's broad *technē* + Obara's 「モノ」), and **virtuality** is the §II.B-specific lens on the *nonmaterial, causally-efficacious* subset of artificiality. Searle's institutional facts fall squarely inside that subset — they are nonmaterial artificialities whose compelling power is given by collective meaning, not by physical instantiation.

## Searle's distinction — summary

John Searle distinguishes *brute facts* from *institutional facts* [VERIFY: Searle, *The Construction of Social Reality*, 1995; also *Making the Social World*, 2010 — check Zotero / acquire before citing]:

- **Brute facts** obtain independently of human opinion, convention, or collective recognition. A mountain's height, a rock's mineral composition, the wavelength of light — these would obtain even if no humans existed. In the thesis's vocabulary they belong to the **universe**: the substrate "neither a god nor a man" made.

- **Institutional facts** obtain only within human institutional practices and collective recognition. *This paper is money; this person is the President; this move is checkmate.* Such facts require collectively accepted **status functions** — the agreement that *X counts as Y in context C* — and they carry **deontic powers**: obligations, rights, and prohibitions with genuine normative force.

The decisive point for Chapter II is Searle's insistence that institutional facts are *not less real* for being nonmaterial, and that they are *causally efficacious*: status functions generate reasons for action that actually move people, allocate resources, and structure behavior. This is independent corroboration, from analytic social ontology, of the thesis's §II.B claim that virtuality — causally efficacious nonmaterial artificiality — is real and causal rather than "merely" symbolic or "as if." Money's power in Searle is not a simulation of some prior real money (cf. the thesis's rejection of Pierce's "as if" and Baudrillard's pure simulation in §II.B); it is constituted by, and exhausted in, collective acceptance. *Money is money, and money has always been virtual* (DOCX §II.B): Searle furnishes the social-ontological mechanism — status function plus deontic power — by which that virtuality binds.

## The author's critique — Searle's framework is too anthropocentric

The author's DOCX note identifies the limitation precisely: "Searle's dichotomy still too anthropocentric — wouldn't say that rituals in animals have deontic powers."

For Searle, deontic powers require language: status functions must be (at least implicitly) linguistically articulable as "X counts as Y in C," and they depend on human collective recognition. On this account an animal display can only ever be a *brute* fact — a behavior produced by neurobiological mechanisms — never an institutional one.

But this leaves Searle unable to accommodate the proto-virtual phenomena that Chapter II must reach in order to establish virtuality as a deep feature of artificiality rather than a recent human (still less digital) invention. Obara's ethological cases — the gorilla's chest-beating display, the crane's courtship dance, the management of collective excitement — function as nonmaterial, representational, behavior-structuring systems: the chest-beat *stands for* fighting capacity and produces real deference and conflict-avoidance without enacting combat [VERIFY: Obara's framing; cross-reference the proto-virtuality draft, now Ch-2-9]. Their *functional role* — producing socially structured constraints through a representational layer that refers rather than directly enacts — is structurally the same work that institutional facts do in human life, even though the mechanism (evolutionary-neurobiological rather than linguistic-conventional) differs.

Searle would deny these the status of institutional facts, because he draws the line at human language. The thesis draws the line elsewhere: at the presence of a causally-efficacious nonmaterial (representational) layer mediating between organism and environment. That is exactly the move §II.B requires, and it is why the thesis prefers its own concept — **virtuality as the causally-efficacious nonmaterial dimension of artificiality** — over Searle's narrower "institutional fact."

## The thesis's position relative to Searle

- **Searle's institutional facts** require: human collective recognition, language, status functions ("X counts as Y in C"), and deontic powers arising from them.
- **The thesis's virtuality** requires only: a nonmaterial artificiality whose compelling power is given by its meaning (not its physical instantiation), and which is causally efficacious in directing behavior.

Every Searlean institutional fact is therefore a virtuality in the thesis's sense — a *paradigm* virtuality, indeed, in the human case (money, law, office). But not every virtuality is a Searlean institutional fact: the animal proto-virtualities are causally-efficacious nonmaterial representational systems that fall *outside* Searle's language-gated category. Virtuality is thus the broader concept, and the broadening is precisely what frees Chapter II from anthropocentrism: virtuality is not the unique possession of language-using humans but the most-developed point on an evolutionary continuum of nonmaterial artificiality. Searle gives a precise account of the human apex; he does not account for the trajectory that the human case culminates.

This relation mirrors the thesis's own internal architecture: just as Searle's "institutional fact" is a language-gated special case of virtuality, virtuality is itself the §II.B lens on the *nonmaterial* subset of the umbrella concept, **artificiality** (Stiegler's broad *technē* / Obara's 「モノ」). The thesis does not adopt Searle's vocabulary as load-bearing; it uses Searle as a corroborating interlocutor whose category, suitably de-anthropocentrized, points at the same causal reality the thesis names virtuality.

## Recommended footnote text (attach to Chapter II §II.B)

> [Footnote]: The claim that nonmaterial artificialities are genuinely causal finds independent support in John Searle's social ontology. Searle distinguishes brute facts — which obtain independently of human opinion — from institutional facts, which obtain only through collectively accepted status functions ("X counts as Y in context C") and carry deontic powers: obligations and entitlements with real normative force [VERIFY: Searle, *The Construction of Social Reality*, 1995]. Money, on Searle's account, is causally efficacious precisely as a nonmaterial institutional fact rather than as a simulation of some prior "real" money — which corroborates this thesis's redefinition of the virtual against Pierce's "as if." Searle's framework is nonetheless too anthropocentric for present purposes: by gating deontic powers behind human language, it cannot recognize the proto-virtual status functions that Obara identifies in animal behavioral repertoires — the gorilla chest-beat that structures deference in the gorilla group without arising from any linguistic convention. This thesis's concept of virtuality is deliberately broader than Searle's institutional fact: it requires only a causally-efficacious nonmaterial (representational) artificiality, of which Searlean institutional facts are the language-using human apex and animal proto-virtualities are evolutionary precursors. Searle precisely describes the human case; virtuality names the wider trajectory it culminates.
