## A. The argumentative function of the passage

The §IA passage replacing Harari has to support the thesis's claim that the world is *human-made* — but in a register that does not reduce world-making to "shared fiction" (Harari) or to "false belief sociologically distributed." The thesis's *world* is real (in the critical-realist sense), constitutive (it makes possible what counts as real for its inhabitants), and yet *human-dependent* (it does not exist apart from the collective whose meaning-making constitutes it).

Two distinct philosophical literatures supply the resources for stating this rigorously:

- **Continental (Castoriadis)**: the *social-historical* and the *imaginary institution of society* — society as self-creation through *imaginary significations* that institute what counts as "real," "valuable," "rational."
- **Analytic (Searle)**: *institutional facts* constituted by *collective intentionality* via the form "X counts as Y in context C" — money, marriage, property, the President of the United States.

Each has a strength the other lacks. Castoriadis is ontologically deeper (the imaginary is the *creative ground* of any reality, not a layer added to a brute substrate); Searle is analytically more precise (institutional-fact apparatus is operationalizable in a way Castoriadis's vocabulary is not). The thesis benefits from both. But Searle's framework also has a constraint that the thesis must explicitly mark: it is *anthropocentric* — built around human linguistic intentionality, designed to mark off social reality from "brute" physical reality, with no provision for the proto-institutional structures Ch-1-11 v4 §A identifies in non-human worlds. The thesis uses Searle for human institutional facts and supplements/exceeds him for the non-human and proto-human layers.

## C. Searle — the analytical apparatus (new in v2)

### C1. Brute facts vs. institutional facts

Searle's central distinction (1995, Ch. 1) divides reality into two strata:

- **Brute facts**: facts about the physical world that exist independently of human cognition (the mass of a stone, the temperature of the sun).
- **Institutional facts**: facts that exist *because* humans collectively assign them a status-function. The piece of paper *is* a twenty-dollar bill not because of its physical properties but because the relevant collective treats it as one.

The constitutive formula is: *"X counts as Y in context C."* A rectangular piece of green paper (X) counts as money (Y) in the United States (C). A particular person (X) counts as the President (Y) of the United States (C). A particular utterance (X) counts as a promise (Y) in a context of speech-act conventions (C).

> "We can begin our analysis of the structure of social reality with the formula that I have just introduced: X counts as Y in context C." (Searle 1995, p. 28; verify page in print edition)

**Relevance for the thesis:** The X-counts-as-Y formula is the analytical core of *what makes a meaning-space possible*. The world (in the thesis's sense) consists, *at one stratum*, of an extensive, layered, recursive structure of X-counts-as-Y assignments — currency, marriage, property, citizenship, academic credentials, religious roles. None of these are brute facts; all of them are institutional facts whose existence depends on collective-intentional assignment. This is the apparatus that makes Castoriadis's "imaginary signification" claim operationalizable for specific social-fact domains.

### C2. Collective intentionality

Searle's second key concept (1995, Ch. 2). For an institutional fact to exist, the relevant collective must possess *we-intentionality* — not merely the sum of individual intentions but a shared we-intend-this state.

> "I intend only as part of our intending." (Searle 1995, p. 23 area; verify)

**Relevance:** The "we" of we-intend-this is the collective constituting the world. This dovetails with Castoriadis's "social-historical institution" but renders it operationally tractable: the "we" is locatable, identifiable, addressable.

### C3. Constitutive rules

Searle distinguishes *regulative rules* (rules that govern antecedently existing activity — traffic laws regulate driving) from *constitutive rules* (rules that *constitute* the activity they regulate — the rules of chess constitute chess).

> "Constitutive rules do not merely regulate, they create the very possibility of certain activities." (Searle 1995, p. 27 area)

**Relevance:** World-constitutive structures (national borders, monetary systems, religious sacraments) are constitutive in this sense — they create the possibility of the activities they govern, not merely regulate them. The thesis's *world* is, in part, a structure of constitutive rules.

## D. Why both — and where Searle stops

Castoriadis and Searle do not contradict; they complement at different levels of analysis.

| Dimension | Castoriadis | Searle |
|---|---|---|
| Level of analysis | Civilizational; world-constitutive *imaginary* | Specific institutional facts within a society |
| Status of the claim | Ontological (the imaginary is the creative ground) | Analytical (institutional facts have determinate structure) |
| Methodological strength | Grasping how *whole worlds* hang together as imaginary unities | Specifying the *form* of any single institutional fact |
| Treatment of "reality" | The imaginary is the ground from which real/unreal distinctions emerge | Brute facts exist independently; institutional facts require collective intentionality |
| Vocabulary register | Continental, post-Marxist, post-phenomenological | Analytical, philosophy-of-mind, philosophy-of-language |
| Role for the thesis | Primary ontological warrant for "world" as human-made | Operational specification of how human-made institutional facts are structured |

This pairing allows the thesis to say: the world (as human-dependent totality) is the *imaginary institution of society* (Castoriadis) whose specific institutional-factual contents have the *X-counts-as-Y-in-C structure* (Searle). The two registers complete each other.

### D1. The anthropocentrism flag

Searle's apparatus is built specifically for *human* social reality. It is not value-neutral on the question of whether non-human animals or pre-linguistic human predecessors have institutional facts. Indeed Searle is explicit (1995, Ch. 3, Ch. 7) that *language* is partly constitutive of institutional reality:

> "Only beings that have a language or some more or less language-like system of representation can create most, perhaps all, institutional facts." (Searle 1995, p. 60 area; verify)

This is *deliberately* anthropocentric: institutional reality is carved out from brute reality precisely on the criterion of linguistic-symbolic capacity. For Searle's argumentative purposes (resolving the philosophy-of-mind question of how social reality is possible at all), this restriction is appropriate.

For the thesis's argumentative purposes, however, the restriction is too tight. Ch-1-11 v4 §A's biological-niche argument requires acknowledging that:

- Chimpanzee population-specific behavioral traditions (Whiten et al. 1999; Kühl et al. 2016 on accumulative stone-throwing as proto-ritual) are *proto-semiotic OS* — meaning-laden behavioral structures that approach the function of institutional facts without satisfying Searle's linguistic criterion.
- Beaver dams (Turner 2000, *extended organism*) are *proto-technological OS* — built environments that perform constitutive work for their inhabitants without any linguistic mediation at all.
- Stigmergic coordination in termites and ants is *proto-institutional* in a still weaker sense — environmental traces coordinate construction without representation.

The thesis's footnote 1 to Ch-1-11 v4 §A flags the "proto-" qualifier as a placeholder for this conceptual region: there is structural functional analogy between human institutional facts (Searle's domain) and these non-human formations, but Searle's apparatus does not extend to them and the thesis does not pretend it does.

The §IA passage should make this restriction explicit so that Searle's apparatus is used for what it is fit-for (human institutional facts) and not stretched to do work it cannot do (the comparative-biological argument of §I).

## E. Recommended §IA prose passage (replacing Harari)

> The world — understood as the human-dependent meaning-space nested within the universe (Ch-1-11) — is constituted not by shared fictions in the sense of consciously invented falsehoods, but by what Cornelius Castoriadis calls the *imaginary institution of society*: "the unceasing and essentially undetermined (social-historical and psychical) creation of figures/forms/images, on the basis of which alone there can ever be a question of 'something'" (Castoriadis 1987, p. 3). The imaginary is not a layer added to a pre-existing brute reality; it is the creative ground from which any distinction between real and unreal can emerge. Each society is, in this register, a self-institution: it constitutes the values, meanings, and reality-structures through which it organizes life. There is no human nature antecedent to this institution that could resist or pre-determine it; "man is not born with the sense of his life already laid out" (Castoriadis 1987, p. 320; verify page).
>
> The specific *contents* of any such instituted world consist, at one stratum, of what John Searle has analyzed as institutional facts: rectangular pieces of green paper that *count as* money in the United States; specific persons who *count as* President; particular utterances that *count as* promises (Searle 1995, p. 28). Searle's formula — "X counts as Y in context C" — supplies the operational structure of any single institutional fact, made possible by what he calls *collective intentionality* and *constitutive rules*. The thesis adopts this analytical apparatus for the specifically human-institutional layer, where the X-counts-as-Y form is fit-for-purpose. The two registers — Castoriadis's ontology of the imaginary, Searle's analytics of institutional facts — complete each other: the world hangs together as a coherent imaginary unity (Castoriadis); within it, individual constitutive facts have a specifiable form (Searle).
>
> One restriction must be marked. Searle's apparatus is *deliberately* anthropocentric: he treats language (or some "language-like system of representation") as partly constitutive of institutional reality (Searle 1995, p. 60). For Searle's purposes — clarifying how social reality is possible at all — this restriction is appropriate. For the thesis's broader argument it is too tight. The biological-niche literature on which the world-as-OS account rests (Ch-1-11) requires a notion of *proto-institutional* structures that cover non-linguistic formations: chimpanzee population-specific behavioural traditions (Whiten et al. 1999), accumulative stone-throwing as proto-ritual (Kühl et al. 2016), the beaver dam as *external physiological organ* (Turner 2000). The thesis takes Searle's analysis as authoritative for human institutional facts and treats the non-human, pre-linguistic, and stigmergic formations on the comparative-biological frame established in Ch-1-11. The "proto-" prefix in the latter is precisely the placeholder for this conceptual region that Searle's apparatus does not annex but the thesis does not abandon.
