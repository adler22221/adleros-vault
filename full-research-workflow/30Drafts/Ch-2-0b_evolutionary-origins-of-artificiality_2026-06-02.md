## The radically immature animal

Before artificiality can be shown to form a world, it is worth asking why a creature should need to form one at all. The answer is biological, and it is not flattering. The human is born unfinished. Among mammals the newborn arrives along a spectrum that runs from the *altricial* — born blind, naked, and helpless, like a kitten or a songbird hatchling — to the *precocial* — born furred, sighted, and mobile, like a foal that stands within the hour; and these are not two boxes but a graded series, "a continuum, without distinct gaps between them."[^1] The human sits at a peculiar place on that series. By the measure of brain and motor development at birth we should, like other large primates, be precocial; instead we are delivered in a state of helplessness more typical of the altricial nest. The zoologist Adolf Portmann named this paradox *secondary altriciality*: the human is born, in effect, about a year too early, requiring an "extra-uterine year" in which the development that other primates complete in the womb is instead completed outside it, in arms, in speech, in the company of others.[^2] We are the animal whose gestation finishes in the world rather than in the body.

This is the biological fact on which the whole apparatus of artificiality rests. A creature born into an instinct-complete niche needs no world: its environment is given to it from within, in the form of an inherited behavioural repertoire fitted in advance to the conditions of its life. The secondarily altricial human inherits no such fit. Lacking a niche given by instinct, it must have one *made* — and made not once but continuously, from the outside, by the very generation that bore it. What we will go on to call artificiality is, in the first instance, the name for whatever does this making. It is not a tool that an already-complete creature picks up; it is what completes the creature. The human does not *have* a world the way a mole has its burrow, ready-dug by its body; lacking a given niche, it must build one, because it has no other way to be at all.

The biological literature already has two names for what this necessity demands. The biophysicist Alfred Lotka observed that the human species evolves along "an entirely new path": where other organisms adapt by the "slow adaptation of anatomical structure and physiological function in successive generations by selective survival," the human achieves adaptation "by the incomparably more rapid development of 'artificial' aids to our native receptor-effector apparatus, in a process that might be termed *exosomatic evolution*" — adaptation *outside* the body, in tools, records, and institutions, rather than within it.[^3] Bernard Stiegler gives the same fact a philosophical name. Reading the myth of Epimetheus, who distributes every natural power to the animals but forgets the human and leaves it "naked... without fur and means of defense," Stiegler argues that "there will have been nothing at the origin but the fault, a fault that is nothing but the de-fault of origin."[^4] Where the animals are "positively endowed with qualities," "humanity is without qualities, without predestination: it must invent, realize, produce qualities."[^5] Exosomatic evolution and the default-of-origin are a biophysicist's term and a philosopher's term for one and the same condition — that the human is the animal which must be completed from outside itself. Artificiality is therefore constitutive, not supplementary: there is no pre-artificial human waiting to be equipped.

## A spectrum, not a split

It would be easy to convert this into a story of human exceptionalism — the one animal that broke from nature into artifice. That is not the thesis's commitment, and the biology does not support it. Immaturity at birth is graded, not binary; the human is an *extreme* on the altricial–precocial continuum, not an exception to it. And the same gradedness holds for the response to immaturity. The capacity to compensate for what the body does not supply — to extend a function outward, to construct the conditions of one's own survival — does not begin with us. It is distributed, in lesser degrees and narrower forms, across the animal world. The thesis therefore approaches artificiality as Chapter I approached world-building: as "one of the many coexisting evolutionary strategies in a continuum," not "a disconnected split from other animals."[^6] What the human does at full scale, other animals do in proto-form. The argument of this section is that artificiality has *evolutionary precursors*, and that the difference between the precursor and the full case is one of degree along identifiable dimensions, not a metaphysical break.

## Proto-artificiality: the material side

On its material side, the proto-form of artificiality has already been established in Chapter I under the name *niche construction*: organisms that do not merely occupy an environment but build it — the beaver that engineers a river's hydrology, the ant colony that tunnels a micro-climate, the coral that secretes the reef — and so become, in Lewontin's phrase quoted there, "both the object of natural selection and the creator of the conditions of that selection."[^7] Animals make material aids and material environments; the dam, the burrow, and the nest are the evolutionary kin of the wall and the city. This much the thesis takes as settled and does not re-argue here. What remains, and what matters for an account of artificiality as such, is the *nonmaterial* side — for it is the nonmaterial that the philosophy-of-technology tradition, fixated on tools, most often misses.

[^7]: [VERIFY: cross-reference Ch1 §B "Niche Construction," DOCX [P0060]–[P0070]; Odling-Smee, Laland & Feldman 2003, *Niche Construction: The Neglected Process in Evolution* (Zotero TJ7XAN6V); Levins & Lewontin 1985 quoted at [P0064]: organisms "influence [their] own evolution, by being both the object of natural selection and the creator of the conditions of that selection." KEEP BRIEF — do not re-argue niche construction.]

## Proto-artificiality: the nonmaterial side, or proto-virtuality

Animals coordinate not only by force but by *meaning*. The ethologist Obara Hideo's studies of animal ritual furnish the clearest cases. When two gorilla groups meet, the leading males drum their chests, and "in this case it fulfils the function of avoiding a fight": the display does real causal work — it produces deference and averts costly combat — yet it works not by the physical force of the beat but by what the beat *signifies* within the group.[^8] The courtship dance of the crane and the kneeling, horn-flaunting display of the ibex follow the same logic in the reproductive register: "even if this series of actions appears to be a grandiose, theatrical performance, it thereby achieves its intended purpose."[^9] A nonmaterial principle — a posture, a sound, a sequence — compels behaviour through its meaning rather than its mass. This is the evolutionary floor of what the next section will call *virtuality*: the causally efficacious nonmaterial aspect of artificiality. Long before there were humans to live by collectively invented stories, the nonmaterial-and-causal had already organized animal life. (The fuller treatment of virtuality, and of why these animal cases are *proto*-virtual rather than virtual outright, belongs to §IIB; here they establish only that the nonmaterial dimension of artificiality, too, has a biological origin.)

The reach of this nonmaterial coordination is wider than ritual. Ethology has documented, across the animal kingdom, traditions transmitted not genetically but socially — "cultural differences (often known as 'traditions' in ethology) are well established phenomena in the animal kingdom and are maintained through a variety of social transmission mechanisms," from the dialects of songbirds to the sweet-potato washing of Japanese macaques.[^10] These are nonmaterial coordinating principles — learned, shared, inherited outside the genome. They are not yet a world; but they are the raw material from which a world is built.

[^10]: [VERIFY: confirmed verbatim | Whiten, Goodall, McGrew, Nishida, Reynolds, Sugiyama, Tutin, Wrangham & Boesch (1999), "Cultures in chimpanzees," *Nature* 399:682–685, at p.682 (Zotero IJNH4UP5; PDF F84SSJZD): "By this definition, cultural differences (often known as 'traditions' in ethology) are well established phenomena in the animal kingdom and are maintained through a variety of social transmission mechanisms. ## Recursivity as the (graded) criterion

If the proto-form and the full form lie on a continuum, the continuum needs a measure. What distinguishes proto-artificiality from artificiality proper is *recursivity*: the degree to which the exteriorized supports feed back upon, accumulate within, and reorganize the very life that produced them. Stiegler names this loop *epiphylogenesis* — "the conservation, accumulation, and sedimentation of successive epigeneses" in the inherited technical object, such that the artificial support "is as much the result as the condition of its production."[^11] The made thing carries forward the experience that made it, and the next generation is born into and formed by that deposit; in Stiegler's compressed formula, "the *what* invents the *who* just as much as it is invented by it."[^12] Obara describes the same self-referential loop in biological terms as *self-domestication*: the artificial environment humans build "imposes selective pressure so that one survives within that society," so that "human selection has changed humans themselves" — a feedback he calls *auto-artificial selection* (自己人為淘汰).[^13] The exteriorized world acts back on its makers and remakes them. Where the loop is rich, cumulative, and self-reorganizing, we have artificiality; where it is thin and non-cumulative, proto-artificiality. That is the criterion.

But — and the thesis must hold this line carefully — recursivity is itself graded, and the boundary it draws is not clean. The same ethology that grounds the proto-forms also supplies evidence that the rudiments of recursivity reach below the human line. Animals manipulate and modify objects: "tool use is quite widespread in the animal kingdom," and "the budding forms [萌芽形態] of the various traits and abilities of humans can be seen within animals," Obara insists.[^14] Chimpanzee tool-use is not uniform but varies by community as a transmitted *tradition*, the combined repertoire of each group being "itself highly distinctive, a phenomenon characteristic of human cultures... but previously unrecognised in non-human species."[^15] The reach of such animal culture, on recent surveys, is "burgeoning" — found to "permeate the lives of a diversity of animals, including all major classes of vertebrates," resting on "a suite of social learning processes."[^16] And recursion in the strict structural sense — long held to be the signature of the human language faculty — has been demonstrated in birdsong: European starlings "accurately recognize acoustic patterns defined by a recursive, self-embedding, context-free grammar," so that "the capacity to classify sequences from recursive, centre-embedded grammars is not uniquely human."[^17] None of this collapses the proto/full distinction — the *degree* of cumulative self-reorganization in human artificiality is incomparably greater, and some researchers reserve "cumulative culture" for humans altogether.[^18] But it does mean that recursivity, like immaturity-at-birth, reads as a graded dimension along which the human is an extreme, not a faculty the human alone possesses. The continuity survives at the criterion that was meant to mark the difference.

## The anthropocentric-bias caveat

One qualification governs this entire section, and it must be stated in the first person, because it concerns the limits of the person stating it. As a human author writing about animals, I cannot fully escape anthropocentric bias; the very categories I reach for — ritual, meaning, recursivity — are drawn from the human case and projected outward. The distinction I have drawn between proto-artificiality and artificiality is an attempt to honour the *continuity* between animal and human life without smuggling in a verdict I have no warrant to give: a verdict on animal *subjectivity*. Whether there is "something that it is like to be" a gorilla performing its display, or a starling parsing its grammar, is a question this thesis deliberately does not answer. Thomas Nagel's formulation marks the boundary precisely — "an organism has conscious mental states if and only if there is something that it is like to be that organism" — and his conclusion marks why I stay on my side of it: even with complete knowledge of the animal's physiology, "it would still be impossible to know what it is like for a bat to be a bat."[^19] The proto/full distinction is a heuristic for tracing continuity in *function* — in what artificiality and its precursors *do* — not a claim about animal experience.

[^19]: [VERIFY: confirmed verbatim | Nagel, T. (1974), "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?", *The Philosophical Review* 83(4):435–450: "an organism has conscious mental states if and only if there is something that it is like to be that organism—something it is like for the organism"; "it would still be impossible to know what it is like for a bat to be a bat." NOT in Zotero — acquire. Scope-discipline (Liu): keep at this weight; the animal-minds debate is explicitly out of scope.]

This restraint cuts in both directions, and Frans de Waal named the opposite error. Against anthropomorphism — projecting human minds onto animals — he set *anthropodenial*: "a blindness to the human-like characteristics of other animals, or the animal-like characteristics of ourselves."[^20] To deny continuity wholesale, to insist on an unbridgeable break between the human and everything below it, would be its own bias — the very human exceptionalism the spectrum claim rejects. The thesis steers between the two: it affirms continuity of function while bracketing the question of inner experience. Jakob von Uexküll's lesson stands behind this caution — that each creature inhabits its own *Umwelt*, a perceptual-meaningful world built to the measure of its own body, neither reducible to ours nor transparently knowable from outside.[^21] We can trace the evolutionary lineage of artificiality through the animal world without claiming to occupy any animal's point of view. That the human is the animal squeezed hardest by its own constructions is the thesis of what follows; that it is continuous with the animals it is squeezed apart from is the commitment established here.
