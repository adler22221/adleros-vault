# Ch III — The nonmaterial driver: the mode's disposition to totalize and exclude

The material story is only half of it. A mode of artificiality spreads not only because it has found, for a time, somewhere to externalise its costs, but because it carries within its very way of knowing a disposition to **totalise itself and exclude whatever will not conform**. The deepest account of that disposition is Roy Bhaskar's. Western philosophy, he argues, has been organised since its Greek beginnings around a demand for knowledge that is *universal-and-necessarily-certain* — a demand he reads as a fear of change and error, a wish for "an unhypothetical starting-point."[^bhk-certainty] That demand exacts a price. Certainty can be had from only one of two sources — pure reason, which needs no experience, or pure sense-experience, which needs no reason — and each, to deliver it, must squeeze out the messy middle on which actual science depends: the real, structured, only-fallibly-knowable world, what Bhaskar calls *natural necessity*. He names the operation the **"primal squeeze"**: "the elimination of the middle term … its intransitive object and ontological counterpart, natural necessity," crushed "between the domains of metaphysics … and those of experience."[^bhk-squeeze] What remains, once the real middle is gone, is a knowledge-stance that can register only what answers to its own criterion of certainty — and so, in Bhaskar's words, it "inevitably splits reality into two: that which conforms to its criterion and that which does not."[^bhk-split] That split is the engine of universalisation: a way of knowing that mistakes its own measure for the measure of being *must* project itself as universal and refuse whatever it cannot register.

It is worth being clear why this is a more explanatory account than the better-known critiques of modernity, since the argument leans on it. The point is not that those critiques are wrong; each captures a facet, while Bhaskar supplies the spine. Derrida's "logocentrism" diagnoses the *form* — being determined as presence, "an ethnocentric metaphysics … imposing itself upon the planet"[^derrida] — but it dissolves the referent, leaving no real world to be squeezed. Adorno and Horkheimer catch the *pathos* — "Enlightenment is totalitarian"; "anything which cannot be resolved into numbers … is illusion"[^ah] — but, in the key of negative dialectics, they decline the positive ontology this thesis needs. Habermas names the *institutional vector* — money and power "colonising" the communicative lifeworld[^habermas] — yet he treats that colonisation as a contingent encroachment to be resisted, where Bhaskar shows the totalising drive to be *structurally necessary* to certainty-seeking reason itself. Only Bhaskar names the **real** thing excluded — a stratified, mind-independent world — which is exactly what a thesis realist about the universe requires. And the diagnosis is not his alone: Heidegger independently describes the modern "mathematical project" of nature as a grid "stipulated in advance," into which every entity "must be fitted" and which "skips over the things";[^heidegger] Husserl traces it to the Galilean "mathematisation of nature" that substitutes an idealised mathematical world for the lived one;[^husserl] Dewey traces the same craving to philosophy's "quest for certainty."[^dewey] Several traditions, one founding move.

Bhaskar gives this move its general name, the **anthropic fallacy** — "the analysis or definition of being in terms of human being," in its two faces: *anthropocentrism*, "taking man as the centre … of the cosmos," and *anthropomorphism*, "painting or interpreting the cosmos in the image of man."[^anthropic] I want to extend it. What the Enlightenment mode projects is not the human as such; it is *itself* — its own particular way of being and knowing — as the measure of all being: call it **enlightenment-centrism** and **enlightenment-morphism**. In the vocabulary of this thesis, that is just what universalisation *is*: the projection of the made *world* — one mode's artificiality — as though it were the given *universe*. The extension is less a departure from Bhaskar than a historicisation of him: he himself treats "any regional geo-historical variant of anthropocentrism (e.g., Eurocentrism)" as an ethnocentrism that "take[s] on an extreme form … in post/modernity."[^eurocentrism] The Enlightenment mode is that extreme form — the anthropic fallacy not merely committed (every mode is tempted by it) but *universalised*, pressed upon the whole earth.[^extension-tensions]

Seen from the other side — from the worlds upon which it was pressed — the same disposition appears as the exclusion of other ways of knowing. Walter Mignolo describes how the modern/colonial matrix "generated the image of its own totality," which "authorised its promoter … to disregard, marginalise, ignore, deprecate … attack all knowledge that didn't obey" — and he roots that totality, tellingly, in "Greek and Latin categories,"[^mignolo] the very Greek inheritance Bhaskar's fault-line names. What Bhaskar describes as an *epistemic* mechanism, Mignolo describes as its *planetary* enactment; they point to the same root at two scales. Boaventura de Sousa Santos gives the result its name — **epistemicide**, the destruction of subordinated knowledges along an "abyssal line"[^santos] — and Yuk Hui, as we have seen, reads the same homogenisation as its technological face: "epistemicide leads to homogeneity."[^hui-epistemicide] The mode does not merely out-compete other worlds; by projecting its own being as Being, it cannot *count* them as knowledge at all.

The nonmaterial and the material drivers are two faces of one movement. The mode that *thinks* its own way of being is being-as-such is the same mode that *acts* to remake the planet in its image while exporting the cost; the disposition Bhaskar finds in the theory of knowledge is realised in the mechanistic, interventionist, cost-externalising artificiality that Needham and Pomeranz find in the history of production. And there is a deeper symmetry. Bhaskar's primal squeeze is the squeezing of the real middle out of *knowledge*; the squeeze this thesis is named for is the squeezing of the world — the made middle realm — out of *being*, between the universe and the metaverse. The first is the epistemic ancestor of the second: a way of knowing that has already squeezed the real out of thought is fit to build a world that squeezes the given out of existence.
