**Working title:** *Between the Universe and the Metaverse: Surviving the Adaptational Squeeze*
**Adler Yang (楊逸帆)** · adler.yang@thesouledu.org · ~80,000-word manuscript (developed from an MA philosophy thesis, Soochow University, 2026) · **scholarly trade / crossover** · embargoed, pre-publication

> **In one line:** Beneath our quarrels over AI lies a structural squeeze — the human-built world pressed between the universe and a second given of our own making: artificiality self-completing into an order that runs, maintains, and rewrites itself without us.

---

We are quarreling about AI — who governs it, who owns it, and whether we can keep it aligned. Adler Yang argues that beneath all of it lies a floor almost no one is watching, and that this floor is the thing each of those quarrels silently stands on: settle it wrong, and the rules of governance, ownership, and alignment keep slipping out from under you.

We adapt to the universe because we must — it is simply there, indifferent to us, and its rules hold still long enough to learn; the referents behind gravity and thermodynamics do not change. The artificiality we've built — our tools, systems, infrastructures — is now self-completing into a second given of the same weight: not a set of gadgets but a **real stratum with causal powers of its own**, an order that operates, maintains, and renews itself without us, with laws that afford, constrain, and ground what is possible. Yang characterizes it by those mechanisms and tendencies, *not by its current devices* — its leading edge is already visible in agentic AI, big data, the Internet of Things, and code-as-law (algorithms that make normative nudges, judgments, and sanctions automatic — not rules you follow or appeal, but conditions you act within, the way a physical law sets what is possible), but the claim is about what artificiality *becomes*, not which machines carry it now. And here is the asymmetry that changes everything: these laws are as binding as the universe's, yet — unlike gravity — they never hold still. This second ground rewrites its own rules as fast as we build it, so the very ground we'd study in order to live well will not stand still to be studied – Yang calls this our anthropogenic three-body problem, after the novelist Liu Cixin. Which is why the headset "metaverse" was a category error — and why the thing Yang means is not, in the end, "AI" either: it is the self-running and mutating order those names only begin to point to.

And the name fits — not the headset rebrand, but the plain sense of its parts. *Meta-* means both "beyond" and "about its own kind" (as in *metadata* — data about data); *-verse* means a realm, as in *universe*. The metaverse is just that: the beyond-realm we build in reaching past the given — one that then begins to run itself, a realm standing alongside the universe rather than nested inside our world.

This is the **adaptational squeeze**. Caught between two givens whose rules don't translate, the world we built to live in — the niche that has always buffered us from the universe — is hollowed from within, its mediating work handed off, piece by piece, to the very artificiality that is becoming the second given.

To map it, Yang corrects his sources as much as he borrows them. Critical theory taught us to fear the modern subject as a will to domination. Yang argues it saw only one face: it fails to explain the nihilist and stewardship faces, with the former only explainable as "false consciousness" and the latter as the only "right" version of being "Enlightened." Behind the seemingly contradictory faces is the flight from dependence — the sovereign subject who masters, withdraws from, or benevolently governs the given by entrusting/offloading the world to artificiality until artificiality runs itself. Around that correction he builds a wide frame held to one rule — the universe stays real: critical realism, joined to niche construction, the East-West concepts of the stratified ontology and the made, a disciplined ontological-turn and decolonial critique (bridging the ecological to the human, and grounding the plurality of modes of artificiality and worlds), and the Needham/Great Divergence question — Europe's escape ran on offloading its dependencies to colonies and coal, not evolutionary fitness of its mode of artificiality. The book does not end in alarm: the way out is to recompose our dependence, attunement (he 和) as the prevention of the squeeze.

---

**Comparable books.** For readers of David Chalmers's *Reality+*, Yanis Varoufakis's *Technofeudalism*, and Byung-Chul Han's *Non-Things* — and, in the scholarly lineage, Yuk Hui, Hartmut Rosa, and Luciano Floridi. It sits squarely in the Stiegler-Hui and Frankfurt School-Rosa lineage but differentiates itself through a genuinely cross-tradition synthesis — critical realism and niche construction welded to East Asian thought — that engages and corrects critical theory rather than merely citing it. Where each of these books pulls on one thread — the reality of virtual worlds, platform economics, and the felt hollowing of the world of things — this one asks a simpler, prior question: what do we actually depend on to live? Its answer keeps two things in view at once — the universe we didn't make, and the self-running order we're now building — rather than collapsing them into one.

**Readership.** For intelligent general readers following the AI debates who sense something deeper is at stake, and for the philosophy-of-technology and critical-theory community looking for a synthesis that is genuinely cross-tradition and genuinely realist.

**Author.** Adler Yang (楊逸帆) is a systems researcher, practitioner, and educator who has spent his life, since adolescence, searching for the common mechanisms beneath the polycrisis — the interlocking social, ecological, and civilizational crises of our time. At the Awakening Cooperative Lab (青醒人共生文化智庫) he founded, longitudinal study and workshops with thousands produced his theory of *Allocation Dependence* — how dependence on allocated resources breeds social pathologies — which he widened into a broader *modes of dependency* framework: his account of how disparate predicaments share generative roots. His debut documentary, *If There Is a Reason to Study* (學習的理由, 2016), directed from the age of fourteen over seven years, received recognition from more than ten international festivals and helped spark a national reckoning with credentialism. His work brings critical realism into dialogue with East Asian thought, and now extends to artificiality and the metaverse. He serves on the council of the International Association for Critical Realism and the board of the Japan Association of Synthetic Anthropology, is an inaugural fellow of The Institutional Architecture Lab (UCL), and has worked with Taiwan's National Development Council and Ministry of Digital Affairs.
