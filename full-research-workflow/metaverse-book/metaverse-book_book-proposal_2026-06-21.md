**Author:** Adler Yang (楊逸帆) · adler.yang@thesouledu.org

## Overview

We are quarreling about artificial intelligence — who should govern it, who should own it, whether we can keep it aligned with human ends. These are the right questions, and this book takes them seriously. But it argues that beneath every one of them lies a floor almost no one is watching: the ground each quarrel silently stands on. Settle governance, ownership, and alignment however you like, and the rules keep slipping out from under you — because the ground beneath them is being remade. *Between the Universe and the Metaverse* is about that ground.

Start with what the debates take for granted. We adapt to the universe because we must: it is the given that answers to no intention of ours, indifferent to us, and its organizing principles — gravitational, thermodynamic, biological — hold still long enough for organisms to learn them across deep time. Between ourselves and those raw pressures, human collectivities have always built a buffer: not a set of gadgets but a *world* — a habitat made of artificiality, an adaptational operating system (AOS) that buffers us from the universe without ever insulating us from it. This is the book's first move, grounded in niche construction across the tree of life and in the emergence of *Homo*, the animal that met a mismatch between its biology and its habitat and built a world to live in between the two. We have always lived nested: a made world inside an unmade universe.

The second move is the central claim. The artificiality we have built is now self-completing into a *second* given of equal weight — and here the argument turns hard against the reigning definition of the metaverse. The fashion that passed in 2021, the one Meta staked tens of billions on, was never the metaverse; it was one mistaken definition of it, the equation of the metaverse with headsets and extended reality. To fix the term by such specimens commits three errors: a path dependency on an accidental coinage, a disciplinary reductionism that files the metaverse beside other gadgets, and an actualism that mistakes what has so far been actualized for what is real. Against these, the book characterizes the metaverse not by its devices but by the conditions from which any such environment emerges — reading dispositions rather than actualities. Its leading edge is already visible in agentic AI, big data, the Internet of Things, and code-as-law — but these are the current *instance* of a tendency, not the thing's definition. The argument's hook runs from *not headsets* to *not just "AI."* The claim is about what artificiality *becomes* once it can anticipate, sustain, and rewrite itself without us: a real stratum with causal powers of its own, an order that runs and renews itself.

To name the thing rightly, the book recovers Xunzi's *wei* 偽 (the made, not the merely fake) and renders the metaverse not as meta-universe (*yuanyuzhou* 元宇宙) — the mistranslation it rejects — but as *forged realm* (*weijing* 偽境): a background environment with its own forged Dao, structures, and phenomena. The book borrows three terms — *Dao* 道, structure (*li* 理), and phenomena (*xiang* 相) — for critical realism's strata of the real, the actual, and the empirical, read through complex adaptive systems in which organism and world co-evolve. And here is the asymmetry that changes everything. Where the universe's *Dao* is relatively constant and open to long calibration — as binding as gravity and, like gravity, holding still long enough to be learned — the forged *Dao* is opaque, perpetually self-rewriting, and tethered to a substrate it disavows: a made principle entrusted with the causal powers of the given, as binding yet, unlike gravity, never holding still. Because this second ground rewrites the very floor one would study in order to live well, the book names it an *anthropogenic three-body problem*, after Liu Cixin — except that here we are the ones unsettling the constant: every science for living turns in vain as the studied "constant" mutates beneath the inquiry.

The result is what the book calls the **adaptational squeeze**. A world is a kind of standing science-for-living that works only because the ground it studies stays put long enough to be studied; the forged realm removes that floor. The squeeze is driven by two coupled tendencies, the faces of one coin. On the human side, the Enlightenment Mode — not a period but a disposition — overspread other worlds by universalizing the sovereignty of the subject: a pursuit of life-as-intended that condemns whatever resists it, until life turns self-finishing. On the apparatus side, the artificiality so entrusted self-completes, gaining the capacity to sustain and renew itself without us. The more thoroughly we delegate, the more the apparatus completes itself; the more it completes itself, the smaller our part in the world becomes. For the first time, the human is pressed between two parallel givens, while the world that once buffered it is hollowed from within. Earlier forms of this squeeze — organismic, civilizational, global — shared the universe's *Dao* as a common floor that kept recovery possible. The metaverse stage removes it.

Why this book, and why now. The moment that looked like the metaverse's failure is in fact its arrival under other names, and the inquiry built to read it was on record before the verdict came in. The book thus turns the apparent obsolescence of its subject into a vindication of its method: a realist, retroductive inquiry that reads tendencies rather than actualities. The threat it diagnoses lies *beneath* governance, ownership, liberty, and AI alignment: each presupposes a subject already standing over against the apparatus and asks how to secure its position, while the squeeze is a transformation in what the subject depends on in order to be a subject at all. It needs none of the apparatus of science fiction — no machine awakening to outwit us; that the apparatus is becoming a second environment we must adapt to, while we lose the means to adapt to the first, is threat enough, and is underway already. To see it, the book triangulates three bodies of thought that no discipline owns together — philosophy of technology (Stiegler, Hui), the critique of modernity (Adorno and Horkheimer, Habermas, Rosa), and Japan's synthetic anthropology (*sōgō ningengaku* 総合人間学: Obara Hideo 小原秀雄, Uegaki Takahide 上柿崇英, Miura Nagamitsu 三浦永光) — across an East–West comparison disciplined by the postcolonial and ontological turns, welded along the spine of Bhaskar's critical realism, with niche construction as its evolutionary floor and Needham's question on the table (Europe's "escape" being a contingent offloading of dependencies to colonies and coal — not evolutionary fitness). It corrects its sources as much as it borrows them: critical theory taught us to fear the modern subject as a will to domination, but that is only one face; the other is the flight from dependence — the subject who masters the given by handing it off, until artificiality runs itself. Uegaki's entrustment, by contrast, names a burden-alleviation meant to deliver freedom, not domination — a resource the book draws on rather than a target.

The book does not end in alarm. As a *pharmakon* — the term Derrida revived and Stiegler extended — the metaverse admits more than one end. The fork it leaves is neither full migration into the metaverse nor a nostalgic refusal of artificiality, but a *re-embedded world* that recomposes what life depends on: a turn from asking how to free ourselves from the chains we are born into toward asking how to attune ourselves to what we depend on. The openings lie in symbiosis (*gongsheng* 共生), technological diversity, and the plurality of digital democracy — what the book gathers under attunement (*he* 和), understood as the prevention of the squeeze.

The contribution is this. Beneath the familiar readings of our technological predicament — power to be checked, property to be redistributed, freedom and survival to be defended — lies a structural condition they do not name: the squeeze. Seeing it requires refusing to define the metaverse by its passing devices and asking instead the prior question — what an embedded being depends on in order to be a subject at all — and it requires a genuinely cross-traditional synthesis that keeps two givens real where its closest neighbors collapse them into a single informational or technical order. By welding critical realism and niche construction to a still little-read East Asian thought, by reclaiming Xunzi's *wei* to render the metaverse as forged realm, and by engaging and *correcting* critical theory rather than merely citing it, the book offers the AI debates the floor they have been standing on without looking down — and a constructive horizon for rebuilding, between the universe and the metaverse, a world worth living in.

## Why "metaverse"?
Not the headset rebrand, but the plain sense of the word's parts. *Meta-* means both "beyond" and "about its own kind" (as in *metadata* — data about data); *-verse* means a realm, as in *universe*. The metaverse is just that: the beyond-realm we build in reaching past the given — one that then begins to run itself, a realm standing alongside the universe rather than nested inside our world. The book renders it, against the *yuanyuzhou* 元宇宙 ("meta-universe") mistranslation, as *forged realm* (*weijing* 偽境): Xunzi's *wei* 偽 — the made, not the merely fake.

## Key features

- **Defines the metaverse by the real, not the actual** — escaping the headset/XR category error *and* the reflex to fix it as "today's AI." Through a realist, retroductive method, the metaverse is characterized by the conditions from which any such environment emerges, treating agentic AI, big data, the Internet of Things, and code-as-law as the current instance of a tendency — not its definition.
- **A new ontological vocabulary: universe / world / metaverse.** The book distinguishes the universe (the given that answers to no intention of ours), the world (not a given but a habitat human collectivities build within it — an adaptational operating system that buffers, never insulates), and the metaverse (the "second given" that artificiality becomes once it self-completes) — a three-term map that reorganizes how we think about technology and human survival.
- **Names the predicament: the adaptational squeeze and the anthropogenic three-body problem.** Once a second given emerges, the human is pressed for the first time between two parallel givens as the world that buffered it is hollowed from within. Because the metaverse rewrites the very ground one studies — its forged Dao as binding as gravity yet, unlike gravity, never holding still — every science for living turns in vain, an anthropogenic three-body problem after Liu Cixin.
- **A genuine East–West synthesis welded to critical realism.** Borrowing Dao (*dao* 道), structure (*li* 理), and phenomena (*xiang* 相) for critical realism's strata of the real, the actual, and the empirical — and recovering Xunzi's *wei* 偽 (the made, not the merely fake) to render the metaverse not as meta-universe (*yuanyuzhou* 元宇宙) but as forged realm (*weijing* 偽境) — the book triangulates philosophy of technology (Stiegler, Hui), the critique of modernity (Adorno & Horkheimer, Habermas, Rosa), and Japan's synthetic anthropology (Obara, Uegaki, Miura) on Bhaskar's spine.
- **A corrective reading of critical theory.** Domination is only one face of modernity's coin; the other is liberation. Drawing on Uegaki's account of entrustment — burden-alleviation that genuinely delivers freedom — the book reframes the deep driver as a flight from dependence, the offloading of what life relies on, rather than mastery alone, and shows why both faces become visible at once only in the metaverse.
- **A constructive exit, not an alarm.** Read as a pharmakon (revived by Derrida, extended by Stiegler), the condition admits more than one end. Against both full migration into the metaverse and a nostalgic refusal of artificiality, the book argues for a re-embedded world — recomposing what life depends on through symbiosis (*gongsheng* 共生), technological diversity, plurality and digital democracy, and attunement (*he* 和).

## Method and contribution

The book proceeds by a realist, retroductive method. It does not ask what the metaverse looks like now, but what conditions must hold for any such environment to emerge at all — reasoning back from observable tendencies to the generative mechanisms beneath them. This is what lets the inquiry survive the obsolescence of its examples: headsets and extended reality were never the thing, and neither is today's agentic AI. They are the current instance of a deeper tendency, not its definition.

The realist apparatus is critical realism, the spine of the argument. The book borrows three terms from Chinese thought for its three strata: Dao (*dao* 道), structure (*li* 理), and phenomena (*xiang* 相), mapped to critical realism's real, actual, and empirical, and read through complex adaptive systems in which organism and world co-evolve. The payoff is a precise diagnosis: the metaverse's *forged Dao* — its opaque, perpetually self-rewriting organizing principle — is what makes it a new kind of environment, not its devices.

Three bodies of work are triangulated: philosophy of technology (Stiegler, Hui), the critique of modernity (Adorno and Horkheimer, Habermas, Rosa), and Japanese synthetic anthropology — human–environment studies (Obara Hideo 小原秀雄, Uegaki Takahide 上柿崇英, Miura Nagamitsu 三浦永光). The comparison is genuinely East–West, alert to the postcolonial and ontological turn: where the traditions converge, they corroborate; where they diverge, they correct one another.

The contribution is to name and specify a condition that current debate keeps describing only in pieces. Governance, ownership, liberty, and AI alignment each register a symptom; none reaches the level at which the environment itself is being rearranged. By recovering Xunzi's *wei* 偽 (the made, not the merely fake) and rendering the metaverse as *forged realm* (*weijing* 偽境) rather than meta-universe (*yuanyuzhou* 元宇宙), the book offers a framework — the adaptational squeeze between two parallel givens — that holds whatever the next device turns out to be.

## Annotated table of contents

### Introduction (p.3)
The Introduction turns the metaverse's apparent demise into its central wager: what failed was not the metaverse but one definition of it — the equation with headsets and extended reality. Yang diagnoses three faults in that view (path-dependency on Stephenson's accidental coinage and the Chinese mistranslation as meta-universe (*yuanyuzhou* 元宇宙), disciplinary reductionism, and an actualism that, after Bhaskar, conflates the real with the merely actual). Against them he sets a realist, retroductive method that reads dispositions rather than devices. The guiding question is reframed: not how to govern, own, or be free of the apparatus, but what an embedded being depends on — recovering East Asian threads of dependence (dependent origination, *yuanqi* 緣起; and the Daoist dependence-on-something, *yousuodai* 有所待). Working definitions of universe, world (an adaptational operating system built of artificiality), and metaverse (the self-completing second given) are set out, and the squeeze named: the human pressed between two givens by coupled self-finishing and self-completing tendencies. A six-chapter map follows, establishing the book's critical-realist spine.

### I. Worlds as Human-made Habitats in the Universe (p.19)
The opening chapter builds the book's foundational vocabulary from the ground up. Starting with the Great Oxidation Event, it shows that organisms do not merely adapt to environments but remake them, producing self-organizing feedback that can turn a creature's own inherited traits maladaptive — the first form of the "adaptational squeeze." Drawing on niche construction theory, it establishes that organisms routinely inhabit two environments at once: a self-built niche and its background. Through Japanese synthetic anthropologist Obara Hideo's (小原秀雄) dual ecosystems and auto-artificial selection, it argues humans uniquely build an artificial ecosystem incommensurable with its substrate. From these, Adler defines the adaptational operating system (AOS) — a bounded, irreducible, incommensurable, population-relative totality — and recasts a world as a special human AOS nested within, never split from, the universe. Here he introduces the Dao/structure/phenomena triad (道/理/相) for critical realism's real, actual, and empirical, anchoring the book's spine.

### II. Artificiality's Role in Forming a World (p.40)
This chapter establishes the book's central claim: artificiality is the human mode of survival, not one activity among others. Drawing on Stiegler's "default of origin" (corrected to an ecological misfit, not a blank), Portmann's secondary altriciality, and Obara's "doubling of making," it shows how recursive tool-making and tertiary retention carry the human out of the food web into a built world. It then defends the term "artificiality" over "technology," recovering Xunzi's *wei* 偽 (the made, not the fake) against the nature/artifice divide. A key move introduces virtuality — artificiality's causally efficacious nonmaterial aspect (the semiotic, via Giddens, Bhaskar, Searle), distinguished from "as-if" and computational readings. A two-by-two typology (material/nonmaterial × conventional/structural) maps how the made compels, tracing migration toward self-completion (自足完成化, *jiko kanseika*) — "technē and nomos becoming phusis." Five independent traditions (pharmakon, the cultivated person who does not become an instrument (*junzi bu qi* 君子不器), Illich, Obara, Adorno & Horkheimer) converge on the made's tendency-toward-inversion, preparing the squeeze.

### III. The Enlightenment's Disposition to Universalization (p.78)
Worlds are plural all the way down to their ontologies (Descola's four schemas [AUTHOR-INPUT: confirm the manuscript cites Descola]), and every world stands in a synchronic universe-world squeeze between the given and what it builds. Traditions long held that squeeze at bay through embeddedness and answerability — attunement (*he* 和), non-action (*wuwei* 無為), sumak kawsay, kaitiakitanga. The Enlightenment mode breaks the baseline. Not a period but a metaphysical stance, it universalizes one figure — the sovereign subject, a self raised above the given — reversing answerability. Its root is sovereignty, not domination: mastery, stewardship, and withdrawal (lying flat, *tangping* 躺平) are three faces of one entitlement, which is why Adorno and Horkheimer capture only part. It spreads by appeal as much as conquest, consuming the species' regeneration base of plural worlds. Materially, it arose contingently (Needham's command-stance; Pomeranz's coal and colonies; Miura's self-entrenchment), not by fitness; non-materially, Bhaskar's epistemic and ontic fallacies make universalization (enlightenment-centrism) intrinsic to its knowing — the "primal squeeze" that is the epistemological ancestor of the book's existential squeeze.

### IV. The Pursuit of Life-as-Intended and the Self-Finishing Tendency of Human Life (p.119)
Following the Enlightenment mode's inward turn onto life itself, this chapter names the self-finishing tendency (*jiko kanketsuka* 自己完結化): the drive to close every gap between the life one has and the life one intends — not a pathology of the mode but its immanent completion. It reads three thinkers as one mechanism seen from three moments: Adorno and Horkheimer (domination), Habermas at the hinge (the colonization of the lifeworld, plus communicative reason loosening the dark view's monism), and Uegaki Takahide, who sees the bright face — the "as-intended life" (*i no mama ni naru sei* 意のままになる生) pursued by entrusting the anti-intended to a "social apparatus" that delivers real freedoms. Two engines converge: a soteriological value-engine (ontic fallacy) and an instrumental one (epistemic fallacy), both foreclosing the intransitive real. Read through Bhaskar's critical realism, domination and liberation prove two faces of one coin — and at the metaverse's edge, the squeeze itself: a made order colliding with a given universe that reasserts itself.

### V. Enlightenment's Self-Completion and the Emergence of the Metaverse (p.151)
Chapter IV explained why the sovereign subject offloads the anti-intended to artificiality; this chapter explains how the apparatus, once handed the work, comes to run it alone. Reading offloading as Stiegler's exosomatization — growing organs (Uexküll's receptor-effector cycle) outside the body — it traces ancient moves (grammatization, tertiary retention, Simondon's concretization) that the metaverse merely runs at its most advanced stage. The one genuinely new thing arrives with Hui's tertiary protention: anticipation exosomatized, so the loop closes (past evaluated, future anticipated, present determined) and the apparatus reaches self-completion (*zizu wanchenghua* 自足完成化) — running without the hand that built it. This closure installs the forged Dao (*weidao* 偽道): an opaque, perpetually self-rewriting organizing principle that, unlike gravity's constancy, never holds still, sheds activity- and concept-dependence yet stays tethered to a disavowed substrate. Tracking the organized-to-organizing-inorganic shift and the minimal organs through which self-completion is presently realized — the Internet of Things, big data, AI, and code-as-law, named here as a tendency rather than as today's machines — the chapter names the metaverse the second given pressing the human between two incommensurable grounds.

### VI. The Squeeze Between the Universe and the Metaverse (p.171)
The book's culminating diagnosis. The squeeze is not new but a recurring figure with a latest, severest mutation; the chapter scores four accumulating types against five criteria. Type 1 (organismic mismatch, e.g. the Great Oxidation Event) births the world as a buffer; Type 2 (civilizational, in Uegaki's embedded mode) keeps a regeneration base; Type 3 (the Anthropocene, the Enlightenment's dis-embedding mode) reverses the adaptational arrow as the sovereign subject offloads the anti-intended. Type 4 is different in kind: artificiality self-completes into the metaverse, a second given whose forged Dao (偽道)—opaque, self-rewriting, substrate-dependent yet disavowing it—is "as binding, yet, unlike gravity, never holding still," making an anthropogenic three-body problem (after Liu Cixin). The divergence now cuts at the organizing principle, removing the common floor recovery needs—a disposition toward irreversibility, not a prophecy. Refusing alarm, it closes on the fork: full migration, or a re-embedded world rebuilt through attunement (*he* 和).

### Conclusion (p.198)
The Conclusion gathers the argument and states what the diagnosis is for. Recapping the six chapters, it shows the squeeze already obtains in graded form — a disposition is efficacious before fully exercised — surfacing at three levels: the body's malfunctions, livelihood's singularization (Illich's radical monopoly), and life under the opaque, self-rewriting forged Dao. It then locates the threat beneath governance, ownership, and liberty, and beneath AI alignment: each presupposes the subject, while the squeeze concerns what the subject depends on. A perfectly aligned apparatus would tighten, not loosen, the squeeze. Invoking Laozi's impartial heaven and earth (*tiandi bu ren* 天地不仁), it names the danger as structural erosion, not a villain's deed. The turn it asks is a recovery of dependence (dependent origination, *yuanqi* 緣起; non-self, *wuwo* 無我); openings lie in claiming less, re-embedding through symbiosis (*gongsheng* 共生), and re-opening contingency, framed as attunement (*he* 和) — squeeze-prevention. The pharmakon keeps the future open; the book ends in diagnosis, not alarm.

## Comparable titles

*Comps do two jobs; this list keeps them apart — the book is pitched at the **accessibility** of the first group and engaged in the **lineage** of the second.*

**— Accessibility / register (the lane the book targets) —**

**Reality+: Virtual Worlds and the Problems of Philosophy** by David J. Chalmers (W. W. Norton / Allen Lane, 2022) — A leading philosopher's accessible, wide-ranging case that virtual worlds are genuine realities and a lens on classic problems of mind and reality. *[accessibility + foil]* Both a register model and a productive foil: the book shares Chalmers's seriousness about virtual worlds for a general readership, but reframes the metaverse *away* from VR and simulation toward a structural *second given* — what artificiality becomes when it self-completes, not a world we log into.

**Technofeudalism: What Killed Capitalism** by Yanis Varoufakis (Bodley Head / Melville House, 2023) — A bestselling, polemically accessible argument that platform "cloud capital" has displaced markets with a new feudal order. *[accessibility / sales]* It shares the conviction that a new ordering layer is overtaking the old, and the gift for making a structural claim legible to a wide readership; it differs by going *beneath* political economy to ontology — the squeeze is about what life depends on in order to be a subject at all, not only who collects the rent.

**Non-Things: Upheaval in the Lifeworld** by Byung-Chul Han (trans. Daniel Steuer; Polity, 2022) — A short, widely read meditation on how the digital order is dissolving the world of tangible *things* and the lived presence they anchored. *[accessibility]* The nearest companion to the book's picture of a world hollowed from within — but Han renders mainly the *dim* side, the felt, melancholic experience of that loss. This book registers that dimness without being captured by it: its focus is the ontological and structural *causes* working behind the predicament's seemingly contradictory faces, and it keeps the universe real, ending not in elegy but in a way out.

**— Intellectual lineage (engaged directly in the book) —**

**The Question Concerning Technology in China: An Essay in Cosmotechnics** by Yuk Hui (Urbanomic, 2016) — A philosophical argument that technology is never universal but always cosmologically situated, recovering the Chinese Way–instrument (*Dao–Qi* 道器) relation to pluralize a Western-derived philosophy of technology. *[editorial]* This book shares Hui's core conviction that East Asian thought can do philosophical (not merely illustrative) work on technics, and his cosmotechnical pluralism is a direct interlocutor. It differs by grounding that comparison in Bhaskar's critical realism rather than Heidegger and Simondon, by recovering Xunzi's *wei* 偽 (the made) rather than the *Dao–Qi* couplet, and by keeping the universe and the metaverse as two irreducibly real poles rather than dissolving technics into cosmology.

**The Fourth Revolution: How the Infosphere Is Reshaping Human Reality** by Luciano Floridi (Oxford University Press, 2014) — Argues that we increasingly live "onlife" inside an *infosphere*, a fourth revolution in our self-understanding after Copernicus, Darwin, and Freud. *[editorial]* It engages Floridi's claim that a new informational environment is reshaping the human condition, and the "second given" is in dialogue with the infosphere. The decisive difference is realist: where Floridi tends toward an informational ontology that collapses the real and the informational into one order, this book keeps two poles real — the universe's given and the metaverse's forged given — and refuses the single informational substrate.

**The Uncontrollability of the World** by Hartmut Rosa (trans. James C. Wagner; Polity Press, 2020) — A short, widely read essay arguing that modernity's drive to render the world controllable hollows out our capacity for *resonance* with what we cannot master. *[editorial]* This is the closest sibling on the critique-of-modernity side: Rosa's "uncontrollability" names from the object-pole what the book names from the subject-pole as the sovereignty of the subject and life-as-intended. It differs by diagnosing the deeper mechanism — offloading the given to artificiality until it becomes a *second* given — and by carrying the argument past resonance into a structural account of the squeeze between two givens.

**Differentiation.** Each of these books pulls on one thread of the predicament — the reality of virtual worlds, platform economics, the felt hollowing of the world of things, the loss of resonance — while this book asks a prior question: what does an embedded being depend on? It keeps two givens in view at once — the universe and the self-completing metaverse, incommensurable at the level of their organizing Dao — and looks for the structural causes behind the predicament's contradictory faces rather than settling into any single mood or master-system. It is a genuine cross-tradition synthesis, welding Bhaskar's critical realism and niche construction to East Asian thought (Xunzi's *wei* 偽, the *Dao/Li/Xiang* strata) and to Japan's synthetic anthropology, rather than importing East Asian concepts as illustration. And it engages *and corrects* the critical-theory lineage it inherits — reading Adorno and Horkheimer, Habermas, and Rosa through Uegaki's entrustment that *delivers* freedom rather than domination — to arrive, as a *pharmakon*, not at alarm but at a re-embedded world built through symbiosis (*gongsheng* 共生), technological diversity, and attunement (*he* 和). On register, the book is written for the readership of *Reality+*, *Technofeudalism*, and Han's *Non-Things* — with a trade edition revised toward that accessibility (the present draft, shaped to degree requirements, runs more formal) — while the lineage comps carry its depth.

## Audience & market

*Between the Universe and the Metaverse* is written to be read on two levels at once — rigorous enough for the seminar, readable enough for the intelligent reader who has never opened a work of critical realism. It addresses three overlapping readerships.

**(a) Scholarly readers.** The book is built to be cited and assigned across several fields it deliberately holds together rather than choosing among:

- **Philosophy of technology** — readers working in the Stiegler–Hui lineage will find a sustained, critical engagement (exosomatization, the organized vs. the organizing inorganic, *tekhne/nomos* becoming *phusis*) that extends those debates rather than restating them.
- **Science & technology studies / AI & society / AI ethics** — the book's central move (define the metaverse by the *real* — its mechanisms and tendencies — not by today's devices; agentic AI, big data, the Internet of Things, and code-as-law as a tendency's current instance, not the thing itself) reframes the alignment-and-governance conversation by locating the stake *beneath* it.
- **Critical theory / Frankfurt School** — Chapters III–IV engage and *correct* Adorno & Horkheimer, Habermas, and Rosa: domination is only one face of the modern subject; the other is the flight from dependence through entrustment and offloading. Readers of the critical-theory tradition get both a careful reading and a named argument with it.
- **Critical realism** — Bhaskar's stratified ontology is the book's spine; the *adaptational squeeze* is developed as a descendant of Bhaskar's own *primal squeeze*. This is a primary scholarly audience (the International Association for Critical Realism community and the Routledge critical-realism readership).
