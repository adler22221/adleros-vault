# Publishing strategy — working notes

> AI-draft (2026-06-10), distilled from a planning chat. **Not legal/agent advice.** Embargo specifics in §3 are institution- and NCL-dependent and change — confirm with the 系辦 and the library/NCL before signing the 授權書. Build on this freely.

## 1. How book-suited is the thesis?

**Verdict:** the *idea* is book-worthy now; the *manuscript* needs a deliberate re-architecting pass (not a reformat). More book-ready than most theses because the public-facing register work is already done.

**Strengths (why it's a strong candidate):**
- **One big, nameable idea** — "the squeeze between the universe and the metaverse." Books live on a thesis a reader can carry; this has it.
- **Already in a public-facing register** — finished toward the MIT-Press "rigor + style" bar; body de-jargoned; abstruse apparatus in footnotes. That's the hardest thesis→book step, largely done.
- **Timely *and* deep** — real philosophy of technology (Stiegler, Simondon, Hui, Bhaskar, Uegaki) brought to a popular anxiety, with contemporary hooks (the agent-internet material).
- **East–West synthesis is a genuine differentiator** — critical realism + Daoist/Buddhist/Xunzian thought + Needham folded into a philosophy-of-technology argument; few books do this.

**Revision the book will require:**
- **Length & architecture** — monographs run ~80–110k words, crossover ~70–90k; the thesis is shorter, so expand (develop examples, add a chapter or two, give openings room).
- **Shed thesis scaffolding** — "Chapter X will show," methodological hedging, defensive framing, the 80+ footnotes → loosen, move to endnotes, or cut. A book trusts the reader.
- **Hook-first opening** — a scene / stakes before the taxonomy. Books get read on chapter one.
- **Evergreen framing** — the OpenClaw/Moltbook examples date fast; frame them as instances of a structural tendency so the argument outlives them.
- **Pick the book's identity** (see hub decision #1) — crossover vs monograph; they imply different presses and revision strategies.

## 2. Publisher landscape

Grouped by book identity; reasons tied to *this* manuscript (phil-of-tech + posthuman/ontology + East–West + public-facing).

**A. Crossover "big idea," rigorous-but-readable (likeliest target):**
- **MIT Press** — natural home; the register we aimed at; publishes the Hui/Bratton lineage the argument sits in.
- **Princeton UP** — strong crossover phil/tech; wide reach; serious.
- **Polity** — Stiegler's English publisher; would instantly grasp the inheritance; accessible-academic, global distribution.
- **Stanford UP** — published Stiegler's *Technics and Time*; theory list does crossover well.
- **Verso** — if the critique sharpens politically; public-intellectual reach + prestige.

**B. Scholarly monograph with a transdisciplinary edge:**
- **Univ. of Minnesota Press** — its *Posthumanities* series (ed. Cary Wolfe) is almost custom-fit for the nature/technology/biosemiotic/ontology material; Minnesota also published the Uexküll *Foray* the thesis cites.
- **Duke UP** — theory, posthumanism, media; good with hybrid East–West work.
- **Columbia UP** — strong East Asian + Continental philosophy crossover.

**C. If "publicly accessible" means OPEN ACCESS specifically:**
- **punctum books** — explicitly for *antidisciplinary*/experimental scholarship; OA + print; strong ideological fit.
- **meson press** / **Open Humanities Press** — reputable OA in media theory & phil-of-tech.
- **MIT Press "Direct to Open"** — major-press prestige with an OA route.

**Start-here shortlist:** MIT Press · Polity · Minnesota (Posthumanities) — plus **punctum** if OA is a priority.

**Reality of the path:** MA-thesis→book is more ambitious than PhD→book (presses partly read the credential). The idea can carry it, but expect to (a) submit a **proposal + 2 sample chapters**, not the whole manuscript, and (b) possibly **place 1–2 chapters as journal articles first** to build a citable track record. It's a campaign, not a submission.

## 3. Thesis privacy / embargo (the time-sensitive one)

**Short answer:** publishing as a book does **not** automatically make the thesis private — but you should **proactively request an embargo (延後公開 / delayed open access)** on the deposit authorization form (授權書). That's a choice made *at deposit*, not a consequence of the book.

Key distinctions (Taiwan / Soochow 東吳 → university repository + National Central Library 臺灣博碩士論文知識加值系統 / NDLTD-TW):
- **Depositing ≠ open-readable.** You're generally required to *deposit*; the form lets you set access: immediate open full-text / **embargo (延後公開)** / restricted. "Intend to publish as a book" is a standard, accepted embargo reason.
- **An embargo delays the *electronic full-text* open access** (often a few years, sometimes renewable — varies by institution & NCL). The **record + abstract usually stay visible** (discoverability without giving away the full text); **print copies** may still be consultable on-site.
- **Why bother:** it's mostly about the *publisher*. A freely downloadable full thesis that overlaps the book can make some acquisitions editors nervous (sales cannibalization; vague "already published" worry). Standard safe practice = **embargo + substantial revision**; a properly revised book is a materially different work and is generally *not* treated as disqualifying prior publication.
- **Recommendation:** at submission, choose **embargo / delayed open access** (not immediate open), keep the record+abstract public, revise substantially, and **ask the prospective publisher their thesis policy** (most have a stated position; many explicitly accept substantially-revised theses).

**Caveats:** not legal advice; embargo lengths/renewal/form wording are institution- & NCL-specific and change — **confirm with 系辦 + library/NCL before signing**. The signature locks the decision, so get it right at submission.
