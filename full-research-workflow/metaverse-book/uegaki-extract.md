## ¶172 — C. The stance of the inquiry: what does an embedded being depend on?

My inquiry is necessarily transdisciplinary, for the squeeze falls within the borders of no established discipline. It draws, first, on a pair of Continental lineages. The one is the philosophy of technology running from Stiegler to Hui — which furnishes the vocabulary for treating the technological not as a stock of devices but as a mode of exosomatization. The other is the critical theory of modernity — from Weber through Horkheimer and Adorno to Habermas and Rosa — which supplies the diagnostic grammar of disenchantment, instrumental reason, and the modern compulsion toward controllability. These Continental diagnoses are carried into the second body of thought, an eco-anthropological history of the human–environment relation, by two distinct bridges: a postcolonial critique (Mignolo; de Sousa Santos; Chakrabarty) that opens the diagnosis onto the pluriverse and the standing of other worlds, and the post-anthropocentric “ontological turn” in anthropology (Descola; Viveiros de Castro) that dissolves the nature/culture partition and unseats the sovereign human — the two kept distinct, since the pluriversal and the post-anthropocentric are not the same move. The eco-anthropological tradition reconstructs across deep time how humans came to interpose a self-made layer between themselves and nature — and here, in particular, the inquiry leans on the Japanese tradition of synthetic anthropology (総合人間学, sōgō ningengaku, in which Hideo Obara [小原秀雄], Takahide Uegaki [上柿崇英], and Nagamitsu Miura [三浦永光] are integral to this thesis), which diagnoses modern crisis as a “self-alienation” in which humanity is dominated by the artificial it has itself made, and frames the human as the one animal which interposes a self-produced “artificial ecosystem” (人工生態系, jinkō seitaikei) between itself and nature. The inquiry holds these Western and East-Asian diagnoses together by design: where they converge, it reads them as mutual corroboration, and where they diverge as mutual correction — a triangulation whose method I make explicit below. Neither body of thought alone reaches the squeeze; together they make it visible.

## ¶185 — F. The course of the argument

The argument proceeds in six chapters. Chapter I develops the conception of a world as an adaptational operating system nested in the universe, grounding it in the niche-construction record across the tree of life and in the emergence of Homo — the animal that, meeting a mismatch between its biology and its habitat, built a world between the two and lived in that. That a world is, at root, something built to keep its makers from being squeezed will return at the very end as a question: whether a new world might be built now. Chapter II analyzes what worlds are made of — artificiality in its three aspects: the exosomatized organ — the exteriorization of human organs (Lotka, 1945) and memory (Stiegler, 1998); virtuality as causally efficacious nonmaterial artificiality; and the irreducibility of the laws of artificial environments to those of the universe. Chapter III asks why, of the many modes of artificiality, the Enlightenment mode came to predominate, tracing its disposition to universalize to both a material basis (Needham et al., 2004; Pomeranz, 2021) and an epistemic one (Bhaskar, 2008). Chapter IV turns to the human-side engine: through the work of the environmental philosopher and synthetic anthropologist Uegaki Takahide (上柿崇英, 2022a; 2022b), it examines the modern pursuit of a life-as-intended and the self-finishing of human life that follows from reading whatever does not answer to one’s intention as a wrong to be overcome. Chapter V turns to the apparatus-side engine: the self-completion of artificiality, made possible by tertiary protention – the ability for artificiality to anticipate and organize itself accordingly (Hui, 2019), minimally actualizable in the cybernetic coupling of artificial intelligence, big data, the Internet of Things, and code-as-law (Lessig, 2006), and the emergence from it of the metaverse as a second background environment. Chapter VI draws the two together as the evolution of the squeeze, distinguishing the metaverse-stage squeeze from its precursors and reading its present signs. The Conclusion returns to the threat, sets the thesis’s account apart from the governance, ownership, and liberty traditions and from the science-fiction scenario, and asks where, given the diagnosis, the openings lie — whether, in the end, a new world can be built between the universe and the metaverse.

## ¶380 — (B) A world’s standing tension against the universe

Uegaki hardens this slippage into something sharper and names its architecture. Where Obara sees a dis-synchrony that strains, Uegaki describes a severance (切断, setsudan) — a cutting-apart of what was once continuous — and stages it as a sequence of historical “singularities,” each cutting a tie the previous age had taken for granted.

## ¶382 — (B) A world’s standing tension against the universe

The second singularity is the severance of society from nature (〈社会〉と〈自然〉の切断, shakai to shizen no setsudan): once fossil energy releases the social order from the carrying capacity of its territory, society “continues to expand itself by the logic of society alone, ignoring any consistency with nature.” (上柿崇英, 2022a: 16) Here interposition becomes severance proper — the made order no longer answers to the given but follows its own momentum, which is precisely the diagnosis of the Western rise that Pomeranz and Miura supply below.

## ¶383 — (B) A world’s standing tension against the universe

The third, and the one that defines our own moment, is the severance of society from the human (〈社会〉と〈人間〉の切断, shakai to ningen no setsudan): the social order so fully provisions existence that, “for the first time in human history, living no longer requires the other that does not bend to our intention — and, with society severed from the human, the spatiotemporal connections of embedded life give way to a dis-embedded life that no longer requires flesh-and-blood others” (上柿崇英, 2022a; my translation). This last severance is the condition that the type-3 and type-4 squeeze will name in Chapter VI — the human held apart not only from the natural given but from the fellow humans through whom a world was always made.

## ¶384 — (B) A world’s standing tension against the universe

The three are thus not a flat list but a deepening: an interposition that becomes a severance from nature and then a severance from one another, each stage pulling the made world further from the substrate it was built upon. That progressive severance is the squeeze in its severe form, and it is the form toward which, this thesis will argue, the Enlightenment mode tends; Uegaki’s diagnosis is revisited, and pressed harder, in Chapter VI.

## ¶406 — (A) The sovereign subject, a self raised above the given

But mastery is not where the mode begins. It begins one step earlier, with what the contemporary Japanese philosopher Takahide Uegaki (上柿崇英, 2022a) calls the human’s spatio-temporal self-subsistence (時空間的自立性, jikūkanteki jiritsusei): the assumption that the human can stand as “a single individual substance, a subject, prior to the others and the systems of meaning that surround it” — a self that has been, in his exact word, dis-embedded (脱埋め込み, datsu-umekomi) from the connectedness of existence that had always constituted it.

## ¶407 — (A) The sovereign subject, a self raised above the given

The clearest philosophical portrait of this self is the one Uegaki sets against his own position: Max Scheler’s (2009) world-open (weltoffen) human, the being that, alone among the living, is “not bound to drives and umwelt (umweltgebunden), but ‘umwelt-free (umweltfrei)’ and … ‘world-open’: such a being has ‘world’”. For Scheler the human, possessed of Spirit (Geist, 精神 seishin), suspends its reflexive drives and so is “freed from the fetters of the environing world = Umwelt” (環世界, kansekai, Uexküll’s term), becoming a free being raised above it (上柿崇英, 2022a: 90).

## ¶408 — (A) The sovereign subject, a self raised above the given

Scheler is a pure expression of the very stance this thesis is written to correct — the picture of a human who has overcome the Umwelt, escaping the given into transcendence, a face of the dis-embedded sovereign subject. Uegaki rebuts it on the page (2022a: 90–91): world-openness is no transcendence from the Umwelt, but a peculiar structure within the human’s own environment, and that own environment is an artificial one. The thesis follows Uegaki, and joins to him Obara’s account of the human’s artificial ecosystem: the human does not escape the Umwelt; it builds a peculiar, made one. Scheler’s “world-open” subject is thus the sovereign subject in its most flattering self-description — the dis-embedded self-mistaking the environment it has rebuilt for an environment it has left behind. This is also the figure Charles Taylor, tracing its modern emergence, calls the buffered self, sealed against a world to which an earlier self had been porous (2007); and the one Michael Sandel (1984) names the unencumbered self, the self imagined as prior to its ends and attachments — a self Uegaki himself invokes in just this connection (上柿崇英, 2022b: 112).

## ¶410 — (A) The sovereign subject, a self raised above the given

The embedded human of the older worlds stood answerable to its surroundings: it attuned itself to a given it did not author, reading what the world asked of it. The sovereign subject inverts the relation. It takes up what Uegaki describes as a kind of “God’s-eye view” (神の視点, kami no shiten) over an environment, a body, and other persons now reduced to objects to be known (上柿崇英, 2022a) — the very standpoint Scheler’s weltoffen human claimed in overcoming the Umwelt, here redescribed not as a liberation from the given but as a posture toward it. And from that height the given no longer makes demands but owes them — it is the world that must answer to the subject, furnishing what the subject takes to be its due.

## ¶411 — (A) The sovereign subject, a self raised above the given

Artificiality is the instrument of this reversal. It is the means by which the recalcitrant given is offloaded and entrusted — Uegaki’s to entrust (委ねる, yudaneru) — to a made order that will be made to deliver what the subject intends, so that the subject need no longer bend itself to the world. The phrase “reversal of answerability” is my own redescription; the structure it names — the dis-embedded subject, the God’s-eye standpoint, the entrusting of the work of living to the made — is Uegaki’s.

## ¶416 — (B) Sovereignty is not domination: mastery, stewardship, and withdrawal as three faces of one stance

When mastery fails, or wearies of itself, the same dis-embedded self does not return to embeddedness; it withdraws. It turns to the disengagement Uegaki anatomizes as listlessness (無気力, mukiryoku), resignation (諦め, akirame), and finally the void (虚無, kyomu) — the condition of a self “cut off from the connectedness of being” (上柿崇英, 2022a). In the contemporary Sinosphere, this face has found its own name in the refusal called lying flat (躺平, tǎngpíng): the individual, taking their life to be answerable to no one, declining to strive at all.

## ¶491 — H. The mastery of worlds and the dis-embedding of life

One movement remains, and it carries the chapter into the next. A mode founded on the sovereignty of the subject does not, in the end, confine the subject’s entitlement to the world outside the human. The same self that takes the given world to owe it answer turns, eventually, upon the one stretch of the given that is nearest and least tractable — its own life. For the human life is not lived as intended: the body sickens and ages against one’s intention, others resist being made over to one’s design, the given in oneself declines to be merely disposable. Uegaki gives this recalcitrance its name in the negative — the reality that no life is lived entirely as intended (意のままにならない生, i no mama ni naranai sei), the anti-intended in the self as in the world — and the sovereign subject, having taken its life to be its own to dispose of, cannot meet that recalcitrance as a condition to be lived with. It meets it as a hindrance to be overcome, or, failing that, as a void to be borne. The pursuit of a life lived wholly as intended (意のままになる生, i no mama ni naru sei) is the form the sovereign subject’s entitlement takes when it turns upon the human itself.

## ¶498 — IV. The Pursuit of Life-as-Intended and the Self-Finishing Tendency of Human Life

To show this, I read the same phenomenon through three thinkers who saw it from three distances, and I arrange them not as rival theories to be adjudicated but as three faithful renderings of one thing, each true to the moment from which it was written. Theodor Adorno and Max Horkheimer saw the tendency from its darkness, as domination; Jürgen Habermas stood at its hinge, naming both the darkness and what the dark view could not see; and the contemporary Japanese philosopher Uegaki Takahide (上柿崇英) sees it from its brightness, as relief and liberation. The chapter’s final movement shows that the darkness and the brightness are two sides of one coin — two appearances of a single real mechanism — and that the coin, seen whole, is the squeeze this thesis is finally about.

## ¶508 — (C) The caveat: how about genuine liberation?

The limitation is not a refutation. Horkheimer and Adorno saw something real — the dark face of the tendency is not invented, and the chapters ahead will insist on it as firmly as they qualify it. What is needed is not to discard the dark view but to find a vantage from which its truth can be kept while its monism is loosened: a vantage from which one can say that the tendency really does darken into domination, and also that it really does deliver liberations that are not illusions. That vantage is what the next two thinkers supply — Habermas by naming precisely the contradiction into which the dark view’s totality drives it, and Uegaki by describing, from inside the present, the brightness the dark view was structurally unable to see.

## ¶517 — (D) Uegaki’s re-reading of colonization-from-above as emancipation-from-within

This dark thread is the one Uegaki picks up, and the connection is explicit rather than my imposition: Uegaki derives his own central concept — the autonomization (自立化, jiritsuka) of the social apparatus — directly from Habermas’s colonization of the lifeworld, naming the Theory of Communicative Action and the Kolonialisierung at the point where he does so (上柿崇英, 2022a). The hinge, in other words, runs through Uegaki’s own text. What Habermas describes as system colonizing lifeworld, Uegaki will take to its terminus — a society in which the apparatus has so fully absorbed the work of living that the lifeworld of mutual understanding has thinned almost to nothing. But — and this is what makes Uegaki the figure of the brightness rather than a second prophet of the dark — he re-reads the very uncoupling that Habermas diagnoses as pathology. The detachment of the apparatus from the lifeworld is, for the people who live by it, also and at the same time a liberation: it is what frees them from the burdens that the lifeworld of mutual dependence had always imposed. The same process is colonization seen from above and emancipation seen from within. Holding both of those readings together, without letting either cancel the other, is the work of the next movement.

## ¶519 — (E) Rosa’s limitations: what controllability cannot explain

Before turning to Uegaki, one word is owed on why the thesis leads with him and not with the more familiar object-pole lineage that runs alongside his — Weber, Horkheimer and Adorno, and, in our own moment, Hartmut Rosa. Chapter III settled the substance of that choice: the root of the mode is the elevation of the sovereign subject, of which controllability is only the mastering face, the operative principle of one disposition the subject may claim or decline.

## ¶520 — (E) Rosa’s limitations: what controllability cannot explain

Here, it needs only to be restated in the vocabulary this chapter is building. The object-pole lineage diagnoses the modern predicament as a stance toward objects — disenchant them, master them, render them controllable (verfügbar); Uegaki diagnoses what the elevated subject takes itself to be, and therefore to demand. He calls his approach an anthropology of freedom (自由の人間学, jiyū no ningengaku), a diagnosis of the subject’s self-constitution rather than of its handling of the world.

## ¶521 — (E) Rosa’s limitations: what controllability cannot explain

The two stand, once their axes are crossed, as species to genus. Rosa’s axis runs from the controllable to the uncontrollable; Uegaki’s from the as-intended to the anti-intended; and the as-intended is the wider category, since it annexes much that Rosa files as uncontrollable. Rosa’s controllability is one engine of the as-intended life — the instrumental engine that renders the object available, the Aristotelian pole whose signature is Bhaskar’s epistemic fallacy. Uegaki’s as-intended life is the whole of which that engine is a part, carrying also the idealist, soteriological engine of the elevated subject — the Platonic pole, the endowed nature the object-pole tradition does not theorize because its gaze is fixed on the object. To lead with Rosa would be to mistake a part for the whole and to sound like one more critique of controllability; to lead with Uegaki is to keep the subject in view, and so to say what the controllability lineage cannot — that the squeeze is not first a fact about a world made too available, but about a subject that has taken its life-as-intended to be its birthright, and reads whatever in existence will not answer to its intention as an oppression to be overcome.

## ¶522 — (E) Rosa’s limitations: what controllability cannot explain

Rosa names a manifestation; Uegaki names its precondition; and there is, I suspect, a reason the cause is the harder thing for the object-pole lineage to see — its thinkers are themselves heirs of the Enlightenment world, and the self-subsistent subject is the very air they breathe, the standpoint from which they see everything else and so the one thing they cannot easily set before the gaze as one settlement among others. The vantage that makes it visible tends to belong to one who stands at the seam: inside the mode enough to know it from within, heir to another world enough to feel its edge. That Uegaki writes from Japan, and that this thesis is written from Taiwan, is in this respect not incidental but a condition of the seeing — though no claim of pristine exteriority is intended, since the everyday locality of any contemporary Japanese or Taiwanese thinker is already, in Chapter III’s sense, heavily overwritten by the Enlightenment world.

## ¶524 — C. A view toward its brightness: Uegaki

The third thinker writes from inside the present that Horkheimer and Adorno could only foresee and Habermas could only begin to diagnose. Uegaki Takahide, in his study The Formation of the Self-Finishing Society (〈自己完結社会〉の成立, 2022), describes the same tendency the dark view described, but from its bright face — not as a cage closing but as a burden lifting. The difference is not that Uegaki is naïve about the cost; it is that he writes from a vantage from which the brightness is undeniable, and insists that a theory which cannot see it has missed something real.

## ¶525 — C. A view toward its brightness: Uegaki

Before the analysis, the plain version of his claim should be stated, because it is simpler than its apparatus makes it sound: modern people increasingly live lives in which the things that used to be hard — securing a livelihood, being recognized, getting through the day’s needs — are done for them by a vast apparatus, so that they experience as freedom what an earlier generation experienced as dependence; and this experience, Uegaki insists, is not an illusion. The freedoms are real. They are simply purchased at a price the bright face cannot see, and which it is the work of this chapter to name.

## ¶527 — (A) The pursuit of “as-intended life” by entrusting the “social apparatus,” realized in “self-finishing”

Three of Uegaki’s terms carry the argument, and each must be set down with care, because each names something for which English supplies no ready word. The diagnosis is, throughout, a diagnosis of the West: Uegaki writes from Japan, but the worldview he anatomizes is the one the Enlightenment mode universalized, and it is that mode — not Japan — that is the referent of every term below. The first is the as-intended life (意のままになる生, i no mama ni naru sei): a life that answers to one’s intention. “Intention” must here be heard in the full reach of the Sinic intention (意, i) — not the narrow voluntarist “will” but the whole mind-heart, the seat at once of cognition, of feeling, and of striving. This is the inclusive mind Kant gathered under Gemüt and divided into the three faculties of cognition, the feeling of pleasure and displeasure, and desire — and it is the directedness Brentano marked as the intentional character of every mental act, the reference toward an object common alike to thinking, feeling, and willing.

## ¶528 — (A) The pursuit of “as-intended life” by entrusting the “social apparatus,” realized in “self-finishing”

The as-intended life, then, is life as it answers to the whole intending mind — to what one knows, feels, and wants, taken together. Its opposite is the anti-intended (意のままにならない, i no mama ni naranai): whatever does not answer to the intending mind, whatever resists. And the sharpest name Uegaki gives the ideal is the human-worldview (sekaikan = ningenkan, 世界観＝人間観) of the dis-embedded life (mugen no sei, 〈無限の生〉) — where “dis-embedded” carries not the literal “limitless” but its sense: the project of bending the given without remainder to the shape the mind intends, the limitless actualization of an imagined ideal upon a reality from which the self has cut itself loose. Its true opposite is the embedded life (有限の生, yūgen no sei): a life that affirms its embeddedness — its constitution by a body, by others, and by a given it did not author — rather than treating these as hindrances to be overcome.

## ¶529 — (A) The pursuit of “as-intended life” by entrusting the “social apparatus,” realized in “self-finishing”

Note that Uegaki’s coinage, human=-worldview, repairs a silence in the ordinary word. To speak of a “worldview” in English is to speak of a way of viewing the world; what the phrase leaves unspoken — entailed but rarely surfaced — is the question of what kind of human is presupposed in viewing it that way. Every worldview carries within it a view of the human: an account, however tacit, of how the human is to be seen, just as it carries an account of how the world is to be seen. Uegaki’s hyphenated term makes the buried second half explicit, and it is essential to his argument, since the dis-embedded life is at once a picture of a world owed to the self and a picture of the self owed that world.

## ¶530 — (A) The pursuit of “as-intended life” by entrusting the “social apparatus,” realized in “self-finishing”

The second term is the social apparatus (社会的装置, shakaiteki sōchi). By this Uegaki means the whole artificial system to which one entrusts (委ねる) the work of living — the markets, bureaucracies, infrastructures, and now digital platforms that take over the tasks a person once had to perform within a web of mutual dependence. The decisive point, and the one that separates Uegaki’s analysis from Horkheimer and Adorno’s, is the phenomenology of the apparatus: it is felt not as domination but as relief.

## ¶531 — (A) The pursuit of “as-intended life” by entrusting the “social apparatus,” realized in “self-finishing”

To entrust one’s livelihood to an economy, one’s safety to a state, one’s needs to a service, is experienced as a liberation from the burdens that the old face-to-face dependencies imposed. Whatever the apparatus is doing structurally, what it feels like from inside is the lifting of a weight. Any reading that imports the vocabulary of domination at this point — that insists the relief is really subjection — has already refused the datum Uegaki is asking us to hold.

## ¶532 — (A) The pursuit of “as-intended life” by entrusting the “social apparatus,” realized in “self-finishing”

The third term is self-finishing (自己完結化, jiko kanketsuka), and it names the destination. A life becomes self-finishing when it is closed into itself, made complete-in-itself, able to run its loop — to meet its needs, secure its recognition, fill its days — without essential reference to other people. The apparatus is what makes this closure possible: by absorbing the functions that once required others, it lets a life be lived, in principle, alone. The term carries a deliberate double meaning that the English self-finishing preserves: to finish is both to complete and to bring to an end, and the self that finishes itself in the first sense risks finishing itself off in the second. The completion is also a kind of self-undoing. But — and this is Uegaki’s brightness, held to the last — the people living the self-finishing life are not dupes. Through the apparatus’s absorption of the anti-intended, they really do hold the modern values: freedom, equality, autonomy, conviviality, held as users (〈ユーザー〉) of the apparatus that delivers them. Against Horkheimer and Adorno, these are not illusions. The life of a person today is, by these measures, really freer than the life of a person a century ago. That is the datum. The chapter’s task is to understand what it costs.

## ¶534 — (B) The soteriological structure: reclaiming one’s “true endowed nature”

The modern values, Uegaki argues, are not held flatly, as bare political facts; they are grounded ontologically, given a metaphysical weight that turns them from arrangements into destinies. And the structure of that grounding, on his reading, is a secularized salvation-history — the Christian drama of fall and redemption transposed into a worldly key. Three terms carry the structure. There is ontological freedom (存在論的自由, sonzairon-teki jiyū): the conviction that the human has a true nature owed to it, a way it is meant to be — what Uegaki calls the human as it ought to be (本来の人間, honrai no ningen) or one’s true endowed nature (約束された本来性, yakusoku sareta honraisei), and what the German tradition named the vocation of the human (Bestimmung des Menschen). This, Uegaki observes, derives from Renaissance Christianity — it is the imago Dei and the covenant, the promise of a redeemed humanity, secularized into a this-worldly entitlement. There is ontological oppression (存在論的抑圧, sonzairon-teki yokuatsu): the condition of being held back from that endowed true nature — the secular form of the Fall, appearing as Rousseau’s chains, as Marx’s estrangement, as Kant’s self-incurred immaturity (1996). And there is liberation (解放, kaihō): the movement from oppression to freedom, the secular form of redemption. Horkheimer and Adorno themselves belong to this lineage; their critique is itself a redemption-story, which is part of why it could not see its own ground.

## ¶536 — (C) The slippage from a political into an ontological program

The danger lies in a slippage from the political register to the ontological one, and the case of human rights shows it most clearly. At the political stage, ontological freedom overlaps with something this thesis wholly affirms: the right not to be subjected to unjust power, the claim of the enslaved, the colonized, the disenfranchised against those who hold them down. So far the ideal does real and legitimate work. But when the same ideal is read ontologically — as the promise of a endowed true nature owed to the self — the right against unjust subjection slides into a right against the given as such: a claim not merely against the tyrant or the master but against any limit whatever, including the limits the universe imposes on every life. Uegaki marks the fork with two phrases: The first is the reality-accompanying ideal (現実に寄り添う理想), an approach that struggles for a better life by genuinely acknowledging the anti-intended and one’s inherent embeddedness in environments that, ontologically, could never be fully as-intended. The second is the reality-negating ideal (現実を否定する理想), an approach that posits a hypostatized, “self-evident” ideal as the self’s endowed truth – something that must-be-realized – and then condemns lived reality as wrong for failing to match it. These two, in the critical-realist vocabulary this thesis has used, an instance of what Bhaskar called the ontic fallacy — the compulsive determination of knowledge by being, the taking of a hypostatized ideal for a fact of the world (1993: 4). Here the hypostatized idea is the human as it ought to be (本来の人間), the anthropic ideal, taken as though it were a reality the given is failing to deliver — so that the given itself stands convicted of withholding what was owed.

## ¶539 — (D) The problem of the “anti-intended” others

The first limit is the problem of others. Other people are, finally, the anti-intended as such — they do not exist, become, or act as one intends, and no project of self-realization can simply will them into compliance. The soteriologist’s solution is an axiological universalization: the freedom claimed for oneself is extended, in principle, to all, so that each is to be emancipated into their own endowed true nature. But this is, Uegaki argues, naïve, because it leaves the anti-intended unaddressed rather than resolved. It would be unjust, though, to call the solution a mere wish.

## ¶543 — (E) The problem of the “anti-intended” body

The second limit is the problem of the body. Western thought has, since the Greeks, ranked soul above body, and the body is, after the other, the most intimate site of the anti-intended (意のままにならない身体) — it sickens, ages, tires, desires against one’s intention, and finally dies. The soteriological logic, pressed to its limit, arrives at a figure Uegaki names the mind-as-being (思念体, shinentai): a self that has shed even the body, a pure intending mind freed at last from the recalcitrance of flesh. This is, in effect, the res cogitans of Descartes (1985) returned as an aspiration — the thinking thing without extension — and it wears, in its modern form, the look of a secular Gnosticism, salvation as escape from the body rather than redemption of it. These two limits — the other reduced to an encumbrance, the body to a hindrance to be escaped — are not incidental difficulties. They are where the soteriological engine, left to itself, drives; and they are why, as the next section shows, it cannot run without the second engine, the instrumental one, to carry the burden it cannot otherwise discharge.

## ¶545 — (F) The confluence of the value and instrumental rational engines

With Uegaki’s analysis, aided by Bhaskar’s ontology, we see the two engines in the Enlightenment mode – value and instrumental rationality – both drive toward the as-intended life, and they are not rivals but partners — two expressions of a single root, which converge not by historical accident but by internal logic.

## ¶547 — (F) The confluence of the value and instrumental rational engines

The second is the engine Chapter III anatomized: the instrumental engine, the drive that supplies the “can” – means. It reduces the given to what can be measured, predicted, and worked with, so that it may be brought under control. This is the Aristotelian pole, and it runs on the epistemic fallacy — being reduced to the knowable, the knowable to the calculable, the calculable to the controllable. That these two are one at the root can be shown directly. Both grow from the single posture Chapter III named as the root of the whole mode — the sovereignty of the subject: the human posited as standing prior to, and over against, a disenchanted given, what Uegaki called the spatio-temporal self-subsistence (時空間的自立性) of the subject.

## ¶554 — (G) Migrating from the conventional to the structural

As these are offloaded, the values once held in a fabric of mutual dependence come to be held instead, in Uegaki’s vivid image, as users hanging from (ぶら下がっている) the apparatus — freedom, equality, autonomy, and conviviality enjoyed as features and services the system renders rather than as relations sustained. And the directionality of this offloading is exactly the migration Chapter II’s grid charts: from the conventional register, where artificiality holds because people keep enacting it, to the structural register, where it holds because it is built into the operating environment and runs whether or not anyone attends to it. The life of mutual dependence was conventional; the life hung from the apparatus is structural.

## ¶555 — (G) Migrating from the conventional to the structural

This migration is not a thought-experiment; it has a legible history, and Japan furnishes an unusually clear case of it — not because Japan is the cause of the tendency, nor because the case is parochial, but because there the compression of the change into roughly four generations, from the Meiji era to the present, makes visible what elsewhere is spread too thin to see. (The plurality of worlds established in Chapter III is untouched by this: Japan is one legible instance of a migration the mode drives wherever it reaches, not the template of a single world.) Uegaki narrates the change as a sequence of five periods, and as a shift in the figure of the self — from the Traveller (旅人), who leaves a home port for a destination, to the Drifter (漂流人), who has lost port, destination, and compass alike. ## ¶565 — (G) Migrating from the conventional to the structural

The same offloading can be seen, finally, in what becomes of the three deep supports of a life that the apparatus absorbs vertically. The realization of survival — one’s livelihood — is increasingly entrusted to the platforms, which mediates work without the standing bonds of a workplace. One’s existence-in-society — one’s memberhood in communities — is entrusted to the platform infrastructure, where one’s memberhood is increasingly sustained by algorithms rather than enactments by humans. And succession — the handing-on of life across generations — is entrusted to the technologies of reproduction and of digital inheritance, by which even continuity is offloaded onto the apparatus (上柿崇英, 2022a: 138). All three, once carried in a fabric of embeddedness and mutual dependence, now run through the structural-virtual cell, toward the enduring organizing principles of a self-finishing society — which is the threshold the next chapter takes up under the name of self-completion.

## ¶568 — (H) The completion of the Enlightenment ideal

Uegaki’s argument is precise. A society that promises total individual freedom — where each sovereign subject’s true endowed nature is fully realized, freed from the anti-intended other and the anti-intended body — and yet still needs to keep the world operating, has only one feasible form to reconcile both demands at once: the form in which all as-intended are achieved through, and all anti-intended offloaded to, artificiality, thus “the freedom of one” genuinely becomes coextensive with “the freedom of all.” Self-finishing is that form. It is, in Weberian terms, instrumental rationality placed in the service of value rationality — the Aristotelian engine harnessed to the Platonic end — the apparatus built precisely so that the promise of the value-engine can be kept. Its utopian self-description is self-realization (自己実現): everyone lives a life in which everything anti-intended is done for one, so that one is free to become, without hindrance, whatever one intends. This is not the betrayal of the Enlightenment promise; it is its fulfillment.

## ¶569 — (H) The completion of the Enlightenment ideal

Uegaki himself reads the fulfillment as a catastrophe — as a relational pathology (関係性の病理) and a disarray of life (生の混乱), in which the thinning of relationality and the disembodiment of life leave the self-finishing person isolated, unrecognized, and unwell. This normative reading is his, and I record it as his; the thesis does not rest its case on it. For one might dispute, as some do, whether the thinning of mutual dependence is a sickness or a maturation, whether the solitary, apparatus-supported life is impoverished or simply different — and that dispute, conducted in the register of well-being and relationship, is not the one this thesis means to win.

## ¶570 — (H) The completion of the Enlightenment ideal

The thesis’s own verdict is not a moral one about flourishing but an ontological one about the squeeze, and it is reserved for the chapter’s final movement. What the present section establishes is only the structural claim, which holds whether or not Uegaki’s pathology-reading is accepted: that self-finishing is the immanent completion of the Enlightenment mode, the destination toward which the two engines, working together, drive.

## ¶574 — (A) Domination and liberation are not rivals, but two faces of one

The chapter has now set two readings of the self-finishing tendency side by side: Horkheimer and Adorno’s, for which it is domination, and Uegaki’s, for which it is liberation. The natural impulse is to ask which is correct, and to award the palm to one. That impulse should be resisted, because the two are not competing theories of one object but faithful renderings of two different moments — and the apparatus for seeing this was supplied by the critical realism this thesis has used throughout.

## ¶575 — (A) Domination and liberation are not rivals, but two faces of one

Horkheimer and Adorno wrote from the industrial mid-century, from within the catastrophe of the world wars and the culture industry, and what they saw — domination — was the actual face the tendency wore in that moment. Uegaki writes from the digital, globalized present, and what he sees — liberation, the user freed by the apparatus — is the actual face the tendency wears in this one. Each is true to its moment. The error would be to take either actual face for the whole, and the critical-realist name for that error is the conflation of the actual with the real: mistaking one manifestation, however genuine, for the underlying mechanism that produces it. Horkheimer and Adorno risk taking the actual domination of their moment for the real as such; a careless reader of Uegaki risks taking the actual liberation of ours for the real as such. Both would be reading a surface for a depth.

## ¶580 — (B) The paradox: emancipation joins the as-intended engine

The project of liberation — the freeing of the enslaved, the colonized, the subordinated from unjust power — is one this thesis wholly endorses, and nothing in what follows retracts that endorsement. Uegaki is equally explicit on this point, affirming the historical role of the emancipatory tradition even as he questions it (上柿崇英, 2022b: 80). The question is not whether to emancipate, but where emancipation sets its terminus. The structure of the paradox is precise. The ideal of ontological freedom demands liberation from ontological oppression — and because the oppression is defined as whatever holds the self back from its endowed true nature, the demand cannot, by its own logic, ever be satisfied. Each time one chain is struck off, a further chain comes into view, because the ideal, as Uegaki puts it, can never accept the human reality that no human exists who is bound by no chain at all. The pursuit therefore has no end; it is, in his arresting phrase, the no-respite hell (無間地獄, mugen jigoku) of the dis-embedded life (上柿崇英, 2022b: 118). And the endlessness is not a flaw in the fight against oppression — that fight, where the oppression is real and unjust, is right and finite.

## ¶581 — (B) The paradox: emancipation joins the as-intended engine

The endlessness is a property of an ideal that has quietly reset the terminus from the removal of unjust subjection to the removal of all hindrance whatever, the given itself included. It is at that reset, and only there, that the emancipatory will joins the as-intended-life engine: the will to be free of all hindrance is the will of the as-intended life, whatever the hindrance happens to be — a tyrant, or a body, or the fact of other people, or mortality. Here, the precise hedge must be drawn because everything turns on it, and it is Uegaki’s own. The thesis does not question political freedom: the liberation from despotism, from unjust hierarchy, from subordination by power one did not choose. That liberation is real, finite, and right, and Uegaki affirms its institutional establishment without reservation (上柿崇英, 2022b: 189, 193).

## ¶582 — (B) The paradox: emancipation joins the as-intended engine

What the thesis questions is the extension of the emancipatory demand from the political register into the ontological one — the slide from no person shall be subordinated by unjust power to the self shall be answerable to any reality at all. The critique offered here is therefore immanent in the strict sense: it shares the emancipatory project’s commitment to freedom entirely, and presses only on freedom’s terminus, asking it not to slide from the wrong done by a master to the wrong of there being a given at all. And the chapter’s own point, beyond Uegaki’s, is the structural one this whole analysis has built toward: the will to be liberated from all hindrance simply is the as-intended-life engine, whatever its object — so that a critique can be impeccable in its opposition to domination and still, at its outer edge, carry the very premise of the mode it opposes.

## ¶586 — (D) The metaverse: where the coin is seen whole

The metaverse is the point at which the coin is seen whole — at which the two faces, domination and liberation, are shown to be one. For the universalizing controllability that this chapter has traced inward, when it is given the apparatus of Chapter V, forms a real new background environment: a made order so complete that it stands as a second nature in Horkheimer and Adorno’s sense and an autonomized social apparatus in Uegaki’s, at one and the same time. It comes to lie parallel to the universe rather than nested within a world — a made environment that no longer reads as one settlement within the given but presents itself as the encompassing given itself. That is the threshold of the next chapter, which takes up the self-completion (自足完成化) by which the apparatus closes its own loop; the present chapter only brings the argument to its edge. And at that edge the dialectic recoils, exactly as both readings predicted from their opposite sides. The controllability that realizes the values is the same second nature that hollows them — the value-rationality swallowed by the instrumental rationality built to serve it, as Adorno warned. The entrustment that delivers the freedom is the same uncoupling that pathologizes the relation — the colonization Habermas named, the no-respite hell Uegaki named. Liberation and domination arrive together because they were always the same event.

## ¶588 — (E) Neither domination nor pathology, but the squeeze

The thesis’s own verdict, however, is neither Horkheimer and Adorno’s domination nor Uegaki’s pathology. It is ontological, and it is the squeeze. The argument is short, because everything has been laid out for it. As long as there is a human at all, Uegaki concedes, there always remains an anti-intended (意のままにならない) — something in the self and the world that will not answer to the intending mind (上柿崇英, 2022b: 120). That residue is not a deficiency to be engineered away; it is the intransitive, stratified real of Chapter I and Chapter III — the given universe, with its own determinate character, which no controllability subsumes because it was never the subject’s to command. It is what Rosa, from the object-pole, calls the uncontrollable (Unverfügbarkeit). And the squeeze is its monstrous return. The very program of rendering the world controllable, Rosa (2020) observes, produces behind our backs a world that is utterly uncontrollable — so that modernity’s drive to make the world available threatens to produce a new and radical form of uncontrollability, categorically different from the old and worse.

## ¶591 — (E) Neither domination nor pathology, but the squeeze

That is the squeeze: not the domination Horkheimer and Adorno feared, nor the disarray Uegaki diagnosed, but the structural collision of a made world perfected against a given universe that reasserts itself, with the human pressed in the closing gap. The chapters that follow take up that collision directly — first the self-completing apparatus that brings the made order to its perfection, and then the squeeze itself, read off the five working criteria this thesis has carried from the beginning.

## ¶638 — I. The emergence of the “second given”

And the irony is total, for it was the flight that did it: the metaverse is the second given that the sovereign subject’s flight from the first given produced — the offloading meant to free the subject from the anti-intended builds an apparatus that becomes a second given in its own right — one whose liberations and burdens fall unevenly, lived by some as more as-intended and by others as more anti-intended, yet pressing, in the end, on all. Apparatus-side self-completion (自足完成化) and human-side delegation — Uegaki’s self-finishing (自己完結化) — are the two faces of one coin, each driving the other. To specify that pressing — its features, its irreversibility, its “monstrous return of the uncontrollable” — is the work of Chapter VI.

## ¶672 — C. Type 2: the civilizational squeeze, in the embedded mode

The defining fact of Type 2 is not how it fails but the mode in which its artificiality is run, and here the chapter’s debt to the fourth is repaid. The non-Enlightenment world runs its making in the embedded mode — the mode Uegaki calls embedded life. In this mode, the anti-intended — whatever will not answer to the intending mind: the body’s decline, the other’s resistance, the unbidden turn of events — is received as a constitutive feature of being a creature embedded among others, not as a wrong to be abolished. The view is not the exotic option but the inheritance of most cultures since antiquity, so that it is the other mode, the demand that reality answer to the self, that is the historical peculiarity to be explained (上柿崇英, 2022b: 110–111). An embedded world does not, for that, refuse to ease hardship; it builds dikes, domesticates grain, codifies medicine. But it builds them as a creature works within the embeddedness it acknowledges, not as a subject discharging a debt it is owed, and so its making stays tethered to the universe it is made within. The strategy and the world stay highly aligned; the human stays within its biological range; the adaptational arrow still runs from subject toward universe. That tethering is exactly why Type 2 keeps a regeneration base: when an embedded world collapses, what survives — vernacular competence, embodied skill, subsistence knowledge — is still fitted to the universe, so a reduced world can re-form on the ruins (Schwartz and Nichols, 2006). The floor is lower, but it is a floor.

## ¶678 — D. Type 3: the global squeeze, in the dis-embedding mode

It is essential not to read this offloading as merely a loss, for that is precisely what Uegaki saw and the critical tradition is least able to say. The same offloading that the analyst reads as a dispossession is, from within, lived as a liberation, and not a trivial one. To entrust the anti-intended to the apparatus is to be relieved of it, and to be relieved of the body’s recalcitrance, the other’s resistance, and the unbidden turn of events is to draw nearer to the as-intended life that the sovereign mode holds to be the very form a human existence ought to take (上柿崇英, 2022b: 110–114). The goods are real — comfort, capability, the easing of what was once simply borne — and they are chosen daily by people who are not deceived. Ontological freedom is not a cold axiom but an intoxicating one: it tells the subject that it deserves to be free of the recalcitrant, that the body’s decline and the other’s resistance are not merely its lot but a wrong it is entitled to see redressed. The offloading thus has two faces at once, and the squeeze is the name not for the dark face alone but for the single structure of which liberation and dispossession are the two sides. Section (E) follows that liberation to its limit; the point here is only that the Type-3 mode is embraced, not imposed.

## ¶694 — E. Type 4: the metaverse squeeze, in the organizing-inorganic mode

And yet to describe the metaverse only as a pressure is to miss what Uegaki saw most clearly and what this thesis must keep in view: that the offloading consummated here is, from within, a liberation, and the metaverse the very place that liberation has always been reaching toward. Follow the offloading to its limit. Suppose it were total — suppose the body, the universe’s one representative within the human and the single party that cannot be re-coded, were not merely managed but shed: the meat brain replaced, the organism left behind, the human persisting as Uegaki’s mind-as-being, pure mind without flesh (上柿崇英, 2022b: 112). Such a being would no longer be squeezed, for the squeeze requires two poles, and the mind-as-being has let one go. It would not live between the universe and the metaverse but only in the metaverse, the universe relegated beneath it to a mere substrate it no longer experientially inhabits — the metaverse stacked upon the universe rather than set beside it, a first nature in the place of the lost world. Freed of the body’s given, the mind-as-being could in principle become “anything, anywhere, all at once;” and death, the last and most obstinate of the anti-intended, would become at last a thing one elects rather than suffers. This is no dystopia smuggled in as a warning; by the lights of the sovereign mode, it is self-realization, the consummation of ontological freedom, and its appeal is exactly the appeal of the offloading that began the whole sequence.

## ¶714 — H. The fork the diagnosis leaves: full migration, or a re-embedded world

At the level of the sovereign subject, the deepest material is the mode itself, since the Enlightenment escalation began with a stance — the claim to sovereignty and the reading of the anti-intended as a debt to be discharged. The first opening is therefore to claim less and to offload less: to loosen the sovereignty of the subject and to acknowledge more of the anti-intended as constitutive rather than rejecting it. Revisiting the embedded mode itself hints at some possible resources — Uegaki’s embedded life and its reality-acknowledgement, the Buddhist non-self and the Zhuangzian equalizing of things against the absolutizing of the self, and wu wei against the reflex to offload. This is not a counsel of resignation, for affirmation is not acquiescence; it is the re-opening of the fork the sovereign mode forecloses.

## ¶742 — E. Where the openings lie

At the level of the subject’s own stance — its reading of the anti-intended as a wrong — the opening is to claim less and to offload less: to bear what does not answer to intention as a constitutive part of a finite life rather than a debt to be discharged, drawing on the embedded mode’s own resources — the Buddhist non-self, the Zhuangzian equalizing of things, Uegaki’s affirmation of an embedded life (有限の生, yūgen no sei), and the non-action (無為, wúwéi) that meets the reflex to intervene with restraint; this is not resignation, for affirmation is not acquiescence, but the re-opening of a fork the modern mode foreclosed. At the level of artificiality, world, and universe together — our dependence on the given itself — it is to re-embed: to seek modes of making that flourish the world and the universe through their embeddedness in them, as Ivan Illich’s tools of conviviality (1975), Hideo Kawamoto’s design of restoration (河本英夫, 2012), the practices of permaculture, the East Asian figure of co-becoming (共生, gongsheng/kyōsei), Yuk Hui’s technodiversity, and the pluriverse that decolonial thought holds open against the modern world’s claim to be the only one (Escobar, 2015) each in their way attempt. At the level of artificiality as such — our dependence on the made — it is to keep the made from totalizing, after the older ideal of appropriate technology; yet honesty obliges me to mark an open problem, and perhaps a limit of my own sight: I can name no working mechanism of deceleration, foreclosed as such brakes seem by the very radical monopoly and velocity of self-completion the thesis has described — and that absence is itself part of what it means to have called the squeeze disposed toward irreversibility. And at the level of the anticipatory ground itself — our dependence on the apparatus’s foresight — it is not to abolish that foresight but to guide it: to set against the metaverse’s preemptive reduction of the contingent to the most probable some practice of re-opening contingency and plurality, of the kind Audrey Tang’s work on a plurality and pacts of care have begun to model (Tang and Weyl, 2024; Tang, 2025).

## FN48

The two lives turn on opposite readings of the anti-intended, and the difference is worth drawing out, together with the critical-realist gloss the thesis places on each. For the dis-embedded life, the anti-intended is read as ontological oppression (sonzaironteki yokuatsu, 存在論的抑圧) — in effect, an unnatural state, a violation, and therefore something the self is entitled to overturn. In the modernized idiom Uegaki’s logic licenses, the anti-intended figures as a debt or liability owed to the sovereign subject, which the subject may choose whether to claim. Read in critical-realist terms: the onto-teleology of the human — what the human is “owed” by way of an endowed nature — is hypostatized as transitive, projected onto reality as a fact about the world, so that experienced reality is held liable to be bent until it fits that hypostatized nature. A concrete instance: where the dis-embedded life prevails, the ailing, ageing, and finally mortal body ceases to be a condition of being a living creature and becomes a wrong with an address — a deficit the medical and technological apparatus is, in principle, obligated to redress, so that infertility, decline, and death come to be experienced not as the shape of a finite life but as injustices awaiting their remedy. For the embedded life, by contrast, the anti-intended is an integral part of living as a being constituted by its environment — named differently in different cultures, but in each case accepted as belonging to existence rather than indicting it; whether it can or should be overcome, and on what grounds, is left to each culture to decide. Uegaki states the premise directly: such a life “stands on the assumption that there necessarily exists, within human life, something that cannot be controlled by one’s own will — ‘something inescapable’ — and, without denying it but rather affirming it, seeks a better life as a being that is itself finite” (上柿崇英, n.d.). The critical-realist reading: any ideal, any attempt to change, must first acknowledge the self’s embeddedness in a stratified reality that both affords and constrains, and must attend to — accommodate, be answerable to, rather than reject — the emergence of the anti-intended as a feature of lived reality; the pursuit of a better life proceeds only under that precondition. So understood, variants of this view of the human have been the default of most cultures, and Chapter III has already shown two: the Confucian and Daoist accord with the given (the man of Sung who killed his corn by pulling it up to hasten it; wu wei) and the embedded settlements of the no-self (無我) and the equalizing of things (齊物), in which the limit is not a wrong to be abolished but a condition to be lived within. (The non-anthropomorphic “attend to / accommodate / be answerable to” deliberately replaces the warmer “empathize with” of an earlier rendering of 寄り添う; what one attends to is a structural feature of reality, not a person.)
