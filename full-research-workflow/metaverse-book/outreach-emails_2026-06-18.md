Before the book itself, I'd like to share with you the fuller, personal story. The chronic overwork I once described to you did catch up with me — last summer I had a cardiac episode. But taichi, together with rest, targeted nutrition, and traditional East Asian medicine, has helped more than I dared hope: my ischemia has improved from pathological back to borderline-normal. (Our pushing hands in Manila has stayed with me — tai chi really is one of the truer languages between us :) ) Setting most of my work aside to recover also, unexpectedly, gave me the space to write, and two works came of it.

The first is a framework I've been calling the **modes of dependency**. It tries to map the relations among the several dependencies I laid out to you in Manila, and I suspect it will be the spine of my research for the coming decades. Its first formal piece is a forthcoming chapter (in Japanese) for the 20th-anniversary volume of the Japan Association of Synthetic Anthropology, on whose board I serve. You may not remember, but in Manila I said allocation dependence isn't really an education or meritocracy problem, but genuinely transfactual in the critical realist's sense. Among the existing literature, it dialogues closer to Karl Polanyi's; in this chapter I finally make that explicit, in dialogue with Polanyi and Kojin Karatani (柄谷行人). (If it's of interest, here's an AI-assisted English summary: https://awakening-coop-lab.gitbook.io/adleryang/ideas-and-as-change-models/modes-of-dependencies)

The second is the book above. In Manila we mostly talked about dependence — which I'd meant to write up first, and still will; it remains the long-term anchor. The metaverse simply got there first: it was readier (it began as my thesis) and timelier, and since both turn on dependence, it's groundwork more than a detour — the more I researched, the more I came to see the metaverse through that same dependence lens. The argument, briefly, is that human life is now being squeezed between two incommensurable "givens" — the universe (the realist given, indifferent to our intentions) and the metaverse, which I take not as headsets or virtual worlds but as what artificiality becomes once it self-completes into a second given, one that hollows out, from within, the world that used to buffer us. What makes it a *different kind* of given is that its organizing principle won't hold still. Where the universe's is constant — the intransitive referents behind gravity or thermodynamics do not change, and that constancy is what lets organisms learn and adapt to them — the metaverse's is opaque and perpetually self-rewriting, so the very ground one would study in order to survive keeps mutating as artificiality self-completes. I call it an *anthropogenic* three-body problem — after Liu Cixin, except that here we are the ones unsettling the constant.

In CR terms, the heart of it is an **ontology of artificiality**. I use retroduction to characterize the metaverse not by its current devices but by the mechanisms and eco-evolutionary tendencies out of which any such background environment self-organizes — and I treat self-completed artificiality as a *real stratum in its own right*, a "second given" with its own causal powers, parallel to the universe and incommensurable with it. What falls out is the structural condition I call the **adaptational squeeze** — the human pressed between two givens, the world that once buffered us hollowed from within — which I read as a descendant of Bhaskar's own *primal squeeze*. And it reorients the emancipatory question CR cares about: from how to free ourselves from the dependence we're "born into" to how to *attune* ourselves to what we depend on.

The distinctive machinery is cross-traditional — Xunzi's 偽 (the made, not the fake), a stratified *Dao* 道 / *Li* 理 / *Xiang* 相 read through complex adaptive systems, the Needham/great divergence problem, Japanese synthetic anthropology, postcolonialism, and the ontological-turn in anthropology — and along the way it gives an explicit ontological footing to a couple of critical-theory diagnoses (Adorno's "second nature," Habermas's "colonized lifeworld"), reading today's AI-plus-big-data apparatus as a literally self-running "second nature," the autonomized *Geist* Adorno and Horkheimer feared. But that's the manuscript's work, not this email's.

I've attached the full thesis for context (pre-publication — the deposit is embargoed to preserve first-publication rights, and I trust your professional judgment for who to share it with); and if it would ever help for passing along, I also attached a one-page summary. I know your time is precious, so even a brief reaction to the framing of this work would be a real gift.

With gratitude,
Adler
