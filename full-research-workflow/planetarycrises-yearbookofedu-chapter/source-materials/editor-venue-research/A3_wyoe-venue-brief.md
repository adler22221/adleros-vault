# Routledge World Yearbook of Education (WYoE) — Venue Brief

## Series origin and tradition
- The current WYoE series, published by Routledge, traces its lineage to the **Yearbook of Education** of the late 1920s–1930s (Nicholas Hans worked on the *Yearbook of Education* 1920–1939; sources do not cleanly confirm a discrete "1932" launch — treat the 1932 framing with caution).
- The continuous **modern series began in 1965** under Joseph A. Lauwerys (Institute of Education, London) and George Z. F. Bereday (Teachers College, Columbia), originally published by **Evans Brothers** in association with the University of London Institute of Education and Teachers College — then Kogan Page, now Routledge/Taylor & Francis.
- Each annual volume tackles a single thematic problem in comparative/international education, with edited contributions from internationally distributed scholars. The 2026 volume on planetary crises is **Volume 29 of the Routledge era**.
- Series page: https://www.routledge.com/World-Yearbook-of-Education/book-series/WYBE

## Series editors (standing)
- The contemporary series is overseen by a standing editorial team; recurring names in the public materials include **Julie Allan** (University of Birmingham) and **Antoni Verger** (Universitat Autònoma de Barcelona) as series-level editors.
- Each annual volume has its **own guest co-editors** (typically 2–4 senior scholars from different geographies). They are the gatekeepers for proposal acceptance, not the standing series editors.

## Chapter conventions (norms inferred from volumes 2019–2025)
- **Chapter length (final):** typically **6,000–8,000 words** including references, with ~15–18 chapters per volume plus an editor-authored introduction. Volumes hover around 250–350 pages.
- **Proposal stage:** standard WYoE practice (and the practice signalled for the 2026 planetary-crises volume) is a **300–500 word abstract** plus a short author bio. Some calls request a working title, 3–5 keywords, and indication of empirical/theoretical orientation.
- **Structure norms:** chapters are usually organised in **3–5 thematic sections** (see 2023 racialization volume and 2024 digitalisation volume). Volumes accept a mix of: (a) theoretical/conceptual chapters, (b) single-country deep cases, (c) explicitly cross-national comparative chapters, and (d) policy/discourse analyses. Editors typically *frame* the comparative move themselves in the introduction, which gives single-country chapters a viable path so long as the case speaks to a globally legible problem.
- **Citation density:** high — academic monograph register. Expect ~40–80 references per chapter, blending canonical comparative-education theorists with field-specific literature.
- **Figures/tables:** sparing. Most chapters are prose-led; tables appear in policy/empirical chapters, conceptual diagrams occasionally in theoretical chapters. Not a journal-style data-heavy genre.

## Comparative-education framing — what's actually expected
- WYoE is the flagship annual of the *comparative and international education* field, but "comparative" is interpreted **capaciously**: it can mean cross-national comparison, transnational/global-policy lens, North–South contrast, or even a single case explicitly situated against the global-policy field.
- **Single-context chapters are accepted** — see Russia (2022), Cuba (2022), India (2022), South Africa (2023), Canada (2023). What is non-negotiable is that the chapter speaks to a **global problematic** and engages comparative-education theory, not that it surveys multiple countries.
- For the planetary-crises volume specifically (per the public call on the substack of `edforplanetaryfutures`), McKenzie & Silova explicitly invite **empirical and/or theoretical** chapters and signal interest in policy-to-classroom translations across geographies, non-state actors, and the pitfalls/possibilities of the emerging environmental-education global agenda. https://edforplanetaryfutures.substack.com/p/education-and-planetary-crises-comparative

## What gets accepted (themes/methods recurring)
- **Recurring:** global-policy critique (PISA, OECD, World Bank), critical theory/post-structuralism, datafication and governance, postcolonial and decolonial framings, political-economy of education, historical sociology, ed-tech political economy.
- **Methodological tilt:** discourse analysis, policy ethnography, critical historiography, network/assemblage analysis. Less common: large-n quantitative, RCTs, psychometrics, design-based learning research (these belong to other yearbooks).
- **Under-represented (opportunity space):** philosophy of education / ontological framings; East Asian theoretical traditions cited *as theory* rather than as case material; speculative/futures-oriented methods; chapters that explicitly bridge planetary/ecological ontology with comparative-education's political-economy mainstream.

## House style
- **Formal academic English, monograph register** — denser and more theory-forward than journal prose, but not jargon-heavy. Tröhler-Piattoeva-Pinar 2022 and Williamson-Komljenovic-Gulson 2024 are good register models.
- **First person is acceptable** when warranted (positionality, case selection); essayistic flourishes are not — sustained argumentation is expected.
- **Theoretical density:** medium-high. Chapters are expected to *name and use* a theoretical frame, not just describe.

## Submission process
- Proposals go **directly to the volume editors by email** (for V29: marcia.mckenzie@unimelb.edu.au and iveta.silova@asu.edu). No Routledge online portal at proposal stage.
- After editor acceptance, full chapters are drafted to the volume's house template (provided by editors), peer-reviewed internally by co-editors and external readers, then submitted to Routledge as a complete manuscript by the editorial team.
- The **2026 planetary-crises volume deadline** for proposals is reported variously as **May 15** and **May 30, 2026** across public sources — confirm directly with editors before final submission.

## Reviewer-valued qualities (strong vs. weak proposals)
- **Strong WYoE chapters** (per reviews of 2019, 2020, 2023): clear theoretical apparatus deployed throughout (not just in the intro); a defined empirical or textual object; explicit comparative or transnational stakes; engagement with the *volume's* framing question, not just the chapter author's own pet topic; willingness to complicate dominant narratives in comparative ed.
- **Weak chapters** are flagged for: descriptive country-case writing with no theoretical lift; loose use of "global" or "comparative" as buzzwords; failure to connect to other chapters or the volume's spine; methodological vagueness; or — a recurring critique of the series itself — reproducing the very governing logics (measurement, benchmarking) the volume claims to interrogate.
- **Proposal-level winners** state in 300–500 words: (1) the problem and why it matters for the volume's theme, (2) the theoretical frame, (3) the empirical or textual material, (4) the comparative/global stakes, (5) the contribution to the volume's architecture.
