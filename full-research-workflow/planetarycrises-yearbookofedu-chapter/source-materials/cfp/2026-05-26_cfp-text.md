> What is education for in a time of planetary unraveling?

*Education and Planetary Crises*, forthcoming as Volume 29 of the World Yearbook of Education (Routledge), invites proposals for original chapters that confront these tensions directly. Following initial review, the volume is now entering its final stage of development, with contributors and chapter abstracts being assembled for submission to the publisher. Submissions are sought for each of the three sections outlined below, from a range of theoretical and methodological orientations. Proposed chapters can be empirical and/or theoretical in focus.

## Why This Volume — Why Now

Planetary crises — climate change, biodiversity loss, and related global health emergencies — are reshaping societies in profound ways, yet their implications for education remain underexamined at a global, comparative level. Despite the urgency of planetary crises and expanding engagement across schools, communities, and educational scholarship, there has not been a World Yearbook of Education addressing climate or other sustainability challenges. This volume brings together leading contributions to critically examine how education systems worldwide are responding to this urgent and timely topic.

Education is both implicated in and potentially transformative of planetary futures. Political polarisation, rising authoritarianism, technological change, and neoliberal reforms shape how climate and sustainability are taken up in schools, curricula, and universities. At the same time, movements led by young people, Indigenous groups, and community groups are challenging dominant approaches and offering alternative pathways. This tension makes the book more timely than ever: it intervenes at a moment when education is being called on to address global crises but is also constrained by structural inequalities and competing political agendas. By situating education within these broader dynamics, the volume provides both a critical analysis of current challenges and insight into the possibilities for more just and sustainable futures.

Drawing on a wide range of international and interdisciplinary perspectives, this volume will critically and creatively examine how education policy is being reshaped in response to planetary crises. It will trace how climate and sustainability agendas circulate across global frameworks, national systems, schools, and community-based initiatives, highlighting both tensions within dominant approaches and the emergence of alternative pedagogies, ethics, and governance practices. With contributions from education researchers, Indigenous scholars, and in collaboration with youth and community contributors, the volume will offer a timely and necessary reflection on how education can engage with the complexities of climate change, biodiversity loss, and ecological injustice—not only to sustain existing systems, but to cultivate more ecologically just and sustainable planetary futures.

## Section 1: Global policy architectures

The opening section of the Yearbook examines a growing global agenda on addressing environmental issues in education, such as via the UNESCO Greening Education Partnership and the Sustainable Development Goals, and in the work of broader UN agencies and the OECD. Chapters discuss both some of the potential pitfalls (e.g., greenwashing, technocratic approaches, what is excluded), as well as what they can enable and how (e.g., shifts in global monitoring framework, data to leverage policy change, North-South collaboration). The section further traces how policy moves into curriculum, textbooks, and pedagogies through comparative research across a range of geographical and geopolitical settings. It also attends to the role of non-state actors—including universities, youth and Indigenous-led movements, corporations, and NGOs—in relation to global policy programs at multiple scales.

## Section 2: Contexts of crisis: Polarisation, colonialism, and algorithmic futures of education

This section turns to the geopolitical, sociopolitical, and technological contexts that shape and constrain addressing planetary crises in and through education. It considers the effects of political polarisation, democratic erosion, and ideological contestation on how climate and environmental issues are addressed—or avoided—within education systems. Chapters also examine how colonial legacies continue to influence whose knowledge, experiences, and worldviews are legitimized in policy and practice. In parallel, the expanding role of AI and digital technologies raise questions about the environmental costs, ethical implications, and governance structures of technologically mediated education. Together, the chapters in this section interrogate the broader forces that enable or constrain engaging climate and environmental issues education while also tracing how young people, educators, and communities are navigating—and resisting—these shifting terrains.

## Section 3: Education policy pathways for planetary futures

This section focuses on forward-looking approaches to addressing planetary crises in education policy, highlighting possibilities for more just, participatory, and regenerative education systems. It foregrounds the role of futures thinking in shaping education systems capable of responding to long-term ecological and social uncertainty. This includes work on resilient school systems, including infrastructural changes to address safety issues, the displacement of learners, and the disruption of learning from environment-related disasters. Chapters may also explore policy imaginaries emerging from Indigenous leadership, youth participation, and community-based movements. Collectively, the section considers how education policy and practice can move beyond incremental reform in response to planetary crises, to enable deeper structural and systemic change.
