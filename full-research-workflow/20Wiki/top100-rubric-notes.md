## 1. Cross-disciplinary consensus criteria (appear across all fields)

These criteria are field-independent and apply to all admission committees regardless of discipline:

| Criterion | What programs explicitly say |
|-----------|------------------------------|
| **Original argument with clear thesis** | Writing sample must take a position; reader must know whether they agree or disagree. Mere comparison or survey is unacceptable (Millgram/Segal, philosophyadmissions.wordpress.com) |
| **Extended argument structure** | Not a survey of positions — must develop a sustained argument with clear logical progression (MIT, NYU, UChicago requirements) |
| **Engagement with contemporary scholarship** | "Assessment is usually easier if the writing sample explicitly engages with some of the contemporary philosophical literature" (UChicago); "demonstrate awareness of current scholarship" (Segal) |
| **Clarity and signposting** | Make clear at the outset WHY the question matters; clarify at each stage what the paper does next and why (Millgram); "prevent reader disengagement" |
| **Self-containedness** | Must be evaluable as a standalone piece; not dependent on external context |
| **Quality of prose** | "Clear and literate prose"; no sloppiness in spelling, grammar, mechanics |
| **Charitable presentation of existing views** | Present positions "with precision, clarity, and charity" — uncharitable strawmanning is negatively noted |
| **Why this question?** | Must motivate why the question matters philosophically/theoretically *to readers unfamiliar with the topic* — cannot assume shared context |

## 2. Discipline-specific criteria

### 2a. Philosophy (analytic / continental)
Source: MIT, Stanford, NYU, UChicago, Brown admissions pages + Millgram guidelines + Segal/philosophyadmissions

Key emphases (beyond cross-disciplinary above):
- **Philosophical creativity/ingenuity** — Segal's third criterion (after clarity and ambition)
- **Ambition and importance of the project** — not just technical competence; the question must matter
- **Not a response to a prompt** — should read as genuine intellectual investment, not fulfillment of an assignment
- **Contemporary debates** — must engage with the field as it currently stands, not just historical positions
- **Program fit** — topic should overlap with faculty research; the piece demonstrates the applicant can thrive in that specific intellectual community

### 2b. Interdisciplinary Humanities (Stanford MTL, UChicago Division of Humanities)
Source: Stanford MTL admissions; UChicago Division of the Humanities application instructions

Key emphases:
- **Preparedness for interdisciplinary work** — writing sample and recommendation letters should "indicate your preparedness for interdisciplinary work, and why your interests would be best served in a program such as theirs"
- **Does NOT have to be explicitly interdisciplinary** — "although the program encourages applicants to choose writing samples that display their interdisciplinary interests, this is not a requirement"
- **Innovative analysis** — MTL students produce "innovative analyses of diverse texts, forms, and practices" across disciplines
- **~7000 words** recommended; two shorter samples to total can work but are "usually less well suited to demonstrate research and argumentation skills"

Relevant fields at Stanford MTL: literature, history, philosophy, anthropology, law, science, film, visual arts, popular culture, performance, material culture and technology, gender/sexuality, race/ethnicity, science/technology/medicine, media and performance, critical and social theory

### 2c. Media and Communications (LSE)
Source: LSE MPhil/PhD Media and Communications admissions

Key emphases:
- **Theoretical and empirical "gap"** — must identify where the research contributes to the field; the proposal/sample must answer: "Is there a theoretical and empirical gap that your research will seek to fill?"
- **Positioning vis-à-vis theories** — "How do you position yourself vis-à-vis the theories and concepts you propose to use?" — the applicant must declare their theoretical standpoint, not just use theory
- **Why interesting and important** — the research question must be legible as significant to the field
- **Core theories and concepts** — must name what theoretical apparatus the work draws on and why those specifically
- **Methodology** — how the empirical/analytical aspects will be addressed

### 2d. Science and Technology Studies (STS)
Source: Cornell STS, Harvard HASTS, Berkeley PhD-DE in STS

Key emphases (no specific rubric published; derived from field conventions):
- **Sociotechnical framing** — how social and technical dimensions co-constitute; work must avoid technological determinism
- **Reflexivity about method** — STS is methodologically self-aware; work must account for its own position of observation
- **Empirical grounding** — even theoretical STS work is expected to be grounded in cases/examples, not purely abstract
- **Faculty alignment** — "Faculty on admissions committees have very different criteria for what they think is important" (MIT HASTS guide) — program fit is decisive
- **Boundary critique** — STS is a discipline built around questioning disciplinary boundaries; work that does this from within philosophy is legible

### 2e. Anthropology / Philosophical Anthropology
Source: Princeton, Stanford, NYU, UPenn anthropology PhD admissions

Key emphases:
- **Original contribution** — "a substantial and original contribution to anthropological knowledge" is the dissertation standard; writing sample is evaluated for promise toward this
- **Where new research questions enter** — essays should "document students' ability to identify points where new research questions might constructively enter into academic conversations"
- **Think creatively, critically, and independently** in researching and communicating theoretical ideas
- **Theoretical and fieldwork integration** (for empirical anthropology) — but research competency papers "need not be fieldwork based"

### 2f. Critical Theory / Sociology of Culture
Source: Inferred from UChicago Social Sciences, Princeton, LSE Sociology admissions + field conventions

Key emphases:
- **Engagement with the tradition** — cannot use Frankfurt school, Bourdieu, Foucault, etc. without demonstrating knowledge of the primary texts and debates within that tradition
- **Immanent critique** — the scholarly norm is critique from within a tradition, not external rejection; work must show the author knows what they're critiquing from the inside
- **Relevance to contemporary social/cultural conditions** — abstract theory must connect to the contemporary diagnosis
- **Dialectical structure** — how does the argument move? What is the negation and the positive claim?

## 3. Key insight for trans/antidisciplinary positioning (D6)

**The common trap**: an interdisciplinary thesis tries to be everything to everyone and ends up legible to no one. Each field sees a piece that doesn't quite speak its language.

**The correct strategy** (derived from Stanford MTL guidance + Segal + LSE positioning requirement):

> **Frame once, position multiply.**
>
> The thesis does NOT need separate introductions or register-shifts for each field. Instead:
> 1. The *core claim* (squeeze hypothesis, universe/world/metaverse ontology) should be stated at a level of abstraction that is legible across multiple disciplines
> 2. Each *chapter* naturally speaks to a different field's concerns — mark this with explicit signposting (one sentence at chapter opening: "this chapter addresses the question of X, which bears on the debate about Y in [field]")
> 3. The *bibliography* signals field membership by inclusion — a committee in media studies sees Stiegler, Virilio, etc.; a philosophy committee sees the ontological apparatus
> 4. The *abstract* is the key cross-disciplinary interface — it must state the original contribution in terms legible to any of the relevant fields

**Disciplines relevant to this thesis (prioritized by fit):**

| Priority | Discipline | Key connection | Primary committee concern |
|----------|-----------|----------------|--------------------------|
| P1 | **Philosophy of technology** | Universe/world/metaverse ontology; squeeze hypothesis; neo-artificiality | Is the ontological argument sound? Is it novel beyond Stiegler, Ellul, etc.? |
| P1 | **Critical theory** | Frankfurt school engagement; diagnosis of modernity; technosphere | Does it advance beyond existing critique? Immanent or external? |
| P2 | **Media/communication studies** | Metaverse as medium; SNS, platform logics, virtuality | Does it identify a theoretical gap in existing media theory? |

## 4. Finalized D5 and D6 operationalization

### D5 — Methodological reflexivity (revised)

**Core question**: Does the thesis explicitly justify its analytical lens, declare its methodological standpoint, and connect its method to the diagnoses it raises?

**Operationalized diagnostic test** (derived from LSE positioning requirement + STS reflexivity norm + philosophy of technology conventions):

For each chapter:
1. **Declaration**: Where does the author declare the analytical lens being used? (If never: add a declaration.)
2. **Defense**: Is there one sentence or footnote explaining *why* this lens, not another? (If no: add.)
3. **Diagnoses link**: Does the methodology respond to the diagnoses raised in the introduction? (If not: add explicit connection — "this approach responds to diagnosis [i/ii/iii/etc.] by…")
4. **Anti-positivism**: Does the method acknowledge its own standpoint rather than presenting itself as view from nowhere? (If not: add reflexive caveat.)

### D6 — Trans/antidisciplinary positioning (revised)

**Core question**: Is the thesis legible — on its own terms, without pretending to be that field — to admissions readers in philosophy of technology, critical theory, media studies, philosophical anthropology, STS, and comparative philosophy?

**Operationalized strategy**:

1. **Abstract** (cross-disciplinary entry point): Rewrite abstract to state the original contribution in terms legible across P1 and P2 disciplines. Do NOT use discipline-specific jargon of any single field. Use the squeeze hypothesis as the organizing concept.
2. **Chapter openings** (field-specific signposting): Each chapter adds one sentence at the opening that frames its stakes for a specific field (marked as such). Examples:
   - §II opening: "The universe/world distinction proposed here intervenes in debates in philosophy of technology about the nature of technologically-mediated worlds…"
   - §III opening: "The technosphere analysis developed here engages the STS question of how sociotechnical systems shape epistemic possibilities…"
3. **Bibliography** (field membership signal): Ensure that the bibliography visibly includes the canonical works from each P1/P2 field — not for tokenism, but because the thesis must genuinely engage them.
4. **Transdisciplinary character**: When the thesis moves between fields, it does so explicitly as a methodological move, not as an accident. One passage in the introduction (or methodology section) must name the trans/antidisciplinary character of the inquiry and defend it.
