
# supervisor & committee 
I want to thank my supervisor Chienkuo Mi for encouraging and inviting me to join the master's program without a bachelor's degree, and for engaging me in department and school-wide innovations as an intrapreneur. He almost always included me whenever an international workshop or symposium was hosted, allowing me to join dinners with leading international scholars in philosophy. This apprenticeship approach has substantially widened my horizons (让我耳濡目染), exposing me to a broad spectrum of possibilities regarding philosophical approaches, themes, and the practice of philosophy itself.

Although my philosophical influences and personal style are substantially different from my supervisor's—he is an analytical virtue epistemologist philosopher, while I am strongly influenced by social theories, continental philosophy, and systems theory—I genuinely appreciate the space and flexibility he has given me to create my own path. 

In fact, if it wasn't for him probing and challenging me with the theme of the metaverse, as I described in the introduction, I wouldn't have imagined a master's thesis could be so radical. Originally, I proposed a relatively small topic that would typically fit the scope of a master's thesis: It was adjacent to my professional background in education and social change, and explored resources from East Asian philosophy for those purposes.

However, he challenged me to take on the metaverse as my thesis topic, even with my complete absence of a computer science background. If it wasn't for his trust in my industrial experience, transdisciplinary interests, and perspectives, as well as his faith in my autodidact or self-directed learning capacity, I cannot believe how work at this scope would be possible. 

If it wasn't for his open-mindedness and encouragement for me to continue the work, the thesis wouldn't be as authentic as it is today. when I eventually—after my immersion in the latest tech developments in Taiwan's tech communities and a survey of the field—produced my proposal, it already significantly diverged from the direction he had imagined. His focus was mainly anchored in responding to analytical philosophy topics, such as mind-body, mind-world, and mind representation issues. His open-mindedness was further reflected in my review board: by engaging a German philosopher & Sinologist, Kai Marchal, and critical sociologist on technology, Yucheng Liu, he created a genuinely transdisciplinary and interdisciplinary review board to evaluate my work as faithfully and supportively as possible, rather than using the perspectives or standards of analytical philosophy alone to judge my work.
