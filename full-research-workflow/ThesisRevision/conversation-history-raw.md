# Redefining "Metaverse"

**In one line:** The metaverse is not a kind of technology. It is the *second background environment* that artificiality becomes when it completes itself — a made world that has begun to run by laws of its own, standing *beside* the universe as a second "given" we must now live between.

## The fad that passed was never the metaverse

In October 2021 Facebook renamed itself *Meta* and bet its future on the metaverse. For a season the word was everywhere. By 2026 the bet had failed: tens of billions of dollars lost, the company pivoting publicly to AI, the term widely treated as dead.

So why study a fad that has so visibly passed? Because the fad that passed was never the metaverse. What failed was one *definition* of it — the equation of the metaverse with a particular kind of technology, immersive 3-D headsets and the virtual worlds they render. And the failure of that definition is not a refutation of the concept but a vindication of the approach offered here. The thing everyone called the metaverse has receded; the thing this page calls the metaverse is arriving under other names — generative AI, autonomous agents, the algorithmic mediation of more and more of ordinary life.

## The mistake: treating the metaverse as a gadget

The metaverse entered the language as fiction — coined in Neal Stephenson's 1992 novel *Snow Crash* for a realm "beyond" the universe — and lay mostly dormant until Meta's 2021 announcement. The definition that arrived with the vogue equated the metaverse with *extended reality* (XR): a network of immersive, real-time 3-D virtual worlds. Three things make that definition inadequate to what the metaverse actually is.

- **It is path-dependent on an accident.** The "beyond the universe" sense is an artifact of Stephenson's coinage — the same arbitrariness that produced the Chinese mistranslation below. It leaves "metaverse" an empty word, replaceable by "XR" with nothing lost.
- **It is reductive.** Once the metaverse is filed as one technological specimen beside others, inquiry is confined to that box — and the metaverse's most important disposition, to become *all-encompassing* and to blur the line between the artificial and the natural, drops out of view.
- **It is actualist.** To judge the metaverse by what it has so far actualized — today's headsets, today's "cybersickness" — is to be voided the moment those actualizations change. An inquiry pinned to the actual is forever a step behind the thing it studies.

The same accident produced a mistranslation. In Chinese the metaverse became *yuán yǔzhòu* (元宇宙) — literally "the original or source universe," which is nonsense for a *made*, second environment, and which builds the word out of the very term (宇宙, the universe) it should be set against. This page translates the same English word instead as the **forged-realm** (偽境, *wěijìng*) — for reasons that will become clear.

So this page declines to define the metaverse by its current specimens. It asks instead what *dispositions* — what underlying tendencies in our ecological, anthropological, and artificial conditions — must be at work for any such environment to arise at all. Grasp the disposition and you can read the *direction* of a thing without betting on its revisable details.

## A prior question

Most worry about the artificial asks one of three questions: how to **govern** a power that outruns its institutions, who should **own** it, how to defend our **freedom** against it. Each takes for granted a human subject already standing over against the apparatus, and asks how to secure that subject's position.

This page asks the prior question: *what does the subject depend on in order to be a subject at all?* What do we, as beings embedded in environments we did not author, rely on to live — and what is becoming of those reliances? On this view the metaverse matters not first as a technology to be managed but as a **transformation in the structure of our dependence**: a new environment that rearranges what we lean on to survive.

## Three environments: universe, world, metaverse

**The universe** is the background environment that precedes us — the real substrate we are situated within and did not make: gravitational, thermodynamic, biological, ecological. It holds whether or not we understand it (and we never understand it fully). We adapt to it, or we fail. It is the **first given**.

**A world** is the artificial environment a human collectivity builds, defends, and sustains *within* the universe — a habitat it can actually live in. Not a "worldview" but a built sub-environment: cultivated food, stored grain, institutions, meanings, the whole made layer a people inserts between itself and the raw pressures of the universe. A world is our **adaptational operating system** — a collective strategy of survival, a **buffer** that stands between us and the universe and absorbs what would otherwise be borne raw. It buffers, but it does not insulate. There have been many worlds, many ways of making.

Everything a world is made of, this page calls **artificiality** — the umbrella term for all that is human-made yet irreducible to the human, material *and* nonmaterial alike (a tool, but also a law, a number, a story, a god). Deliberately *not* "technology": the word "technology" pulls everything back toward gadgets, and the gadget reading is exactly the mistake.

**The metaverse** is what artificiality becomes when it *completes itself*. When the made layer a world produced begins to operate, maintain, and renew itself without us, it stops being a tool *within* the world and stands up as a background environment in its own right — *materially dependent on, yet irreducible to, the universe; running not by the universe's laws but by laws of its own.* It is the **second given**: a second environment standing *beside* the universe, not a super-environment swallowing it. Both poles stay real.

This is the crucial correction to the familiar three-layer picture. The metaverse is **not** a third floor stacked neatly above the world. It is a second given, parallel to the first — and the **world**, the human buffer, is caught, and thinning, **between the two givens**.

## What makes it a metaverse: self-completion and the "forged Dao"

Two things have to happen for artificiality to cross this line.

The first is **self-completion** (自足完成化). For most of history the made apparatus *stored* the past — writing, records, archives — but still needed the human hand to run. What is new is that the apparatus can now *anticipate* as well as store: it computes what is coming and acts on it, closing the loop on itself. A minimal version is already assembling — the **Internet of Things** sensing and acting in the present, **big data** holding the past, **artificial intelligence** computing the future, and **code-as-law** enforcing the result, with each outcome captured back as fresh data. The loop runs; and where humans cease to attend, they become one node among the sensors — increasingly the most marginal one.

The second, and more unsettling, is the kind of *ground* this installs. The universe's organizing principles **endure**: gravity does not rewrite itself overnight, so a world can be calibrated to it, learned, built toward, handed on. The metaverse's organizing principles **do not endure**. Because the loop that closes is a loop that *constantly re-evaluates*, the rule by which it organizes never settles. It is **opaque** (a trained black box no one — its operators included — fully grasps), **mutating** (it rewrites itself in a season, a day, faster), and quietly **dependent on a physical substrate** — servers, energy, labor, chips — that it disavows.

This page names that ground the **forged Dao** (偽道). The prefix *forged* (偽, *wěi*) is exact: in classical Chinese it means *the humanly-made* (人為) — but it also carries the sense of *counterfeit*, and here the two fuse. The forged Dao *poses* as a self-grounding, constant ground in mimicry of the universe's, while possessing neither self-subsistence (it hangs on a substrate it won't admit) nor constancy (it never stops rewriting itself). Its only constant is its inconstancy — which is why you can be exactly right about how the metaverse works today and find your account expired tomorrow, not because you were wrong, but because the ground you described has moved.

That is also why the popular name gets the metaverse backwards. The metaverse is the realm (-*verse*) produced when the sovereign modern subject tries to go *beyond* (*meta-*) the given — and the made then turns into a self-grounding background of its own. The classical world sorted things into the given-by-nature (*phusis*), the instituted-by-agreement (*nomos*), and the made-by-craft (*technē*). The metaverse breaks that sorting: it is *nomos*-and-*technē* by origin — instituted, decided, engineered — and yet *phusis* by operation, running with the given-like authority of a substrate, compelling without anyone's ongoing consent.

## The squeeze: caught between two givens

As artificiality completes into a second given, the human comes to live **between two background environments rather than within one** — between the universe, the given it has always inhabited, and the metaverse, the second given its own making has produced. Each runs by principles of its own; each exacts a cost from those who fail to meet it. This condition is the **squeeze**.

The squeeze is not new. It is a recurring figure that has returned at escalating scales across the history of life: when the first organisms poisoned their own sky with the oxygen they respired; when early *Homo* found itself in a habitat its biology had not been built for (and answered by building the first world); when whole civilizations fell out of step with the planet that carried them. The metaverse marks not the squeeze's arrival but its latest and severest form — the first in which the two grounds diverge at the *deepest* level, so that **no common floor remains beneath** to fall back to, calibrate against, or regenerate from.

It is driven by two tendencies that are **two faces of one coin**:

- a **self-finishing of human life** (自己完結化): the modern subject, reading whatever will not answer to its intention — the body's limits, others' resistance, the unbidden turn of events — as a wrong to be discharged, entrusts ever more of living to the apparatus;
- a **self-completion of artificiality**: the apparatus so entrusted comes to operate, maintain, and renew itself without us.

The more thoroughly we delegate, the more the apparatus completes itself; the more it completes itself, the smaller our own part in the world becomes. And the offloading is not experienced as loss but as **liberation** — comfort, capability, the easing of what was once simply borne, chosen daily by people who are not deceived. The diminishment is the same act seen from its other side. Liberation and dispossession are not rivals; they are the two faces of the single structure the squeeze names.

## Why this is a deeper threat than the usual ones

**Beneath governance, ownership, and liberty.** The predicament is usually read as a problem of *governance* (an apparatus that outruns its checks), of *ownership* (an apparatus concentrated in private hands), or of *liberty* (a freedom to defend against encroachment). Each names something real and answers it with something necessary. None reaches the level at which the squeeze obtains — because each presupposes the subject and asks how to secure it, whereas the squeeze is a rearrangement of *what the subject depends on*. The metaverse-stage subject is, formally, freer than ever — unconstrained, and equipped to realize its choices — and yet what slips away is the *ground* of freedom: the embodied competence, the lived relations, the capacity to want and to do otherwise. Liberty is affirmed and emptied in a single motion.

**Beyond science fiction.** Nor is the threat the one science fiction trades in — an AI awakening to outwit or destroy us. The danger needs none of that. That the apparatus is becoming a second environment we must adapt to, while we lose the means to adapt to the first, is threat enough, and is already underway. Even the dream of *alignment* — making the apparatus answer perfectly to human intention — turns out to be not the cure but the most advanced symptom: a perfectly obedient apparatus would only invite us to entrust it with still more of our living, drawing the squeeze tighter. The real threat is not that we will be replaced by an apparatus, but that we will, *in fleeing what we depend on, hollow ourselves toward the condition of an apparatus — and call the hollowing freedom.*

## The turn, and the question of a new world

The diagnosis points to its own inversion. If the squeeze is the work of a *flight from dependence*, then what answers it can only be a *turning back toward* dependence — not to suffer it but to know it. The question is not how to master what we have made, nor how to escape it, but: **what do we, as embedded beings, depend on, and how might those dependencies be reorganized?** One does not escape dependence — there is no dependence-free ground to stand on, as the *Zhuangzi* cautioned long ago — but because we partly made these reliances, we can in part remake them.

This is less a discovery than a recovery. The Buddhist teachings of dependent origination (緣起) and non-self (無我), and the Daoist vision of a world in which the myriad things arise only in their mutual depending, never took independence for the mark of a free being. **Attunement** (和), on this account, is nothing more mystical than the **prevention of the squeeze** — a dependence organized well enough that the life it sustains is not caught between two givens at once. A world *can* be squeezed; it need not be.

Which returns us to the oldest figure in the story. The world was itself, once, the answer to a squeeze: early *Homo*, caught between a biology it could not change fast enough and a habitat it was itself altering, did not wait on evolution — it built a world between the two and lived in that. The predicament now is that same figure at a new scale. So the constructive question is the old one asked again:

> **Can we build a new world between the universe and the metaverse — a new buffer, a dependence reorganized — that keeps the human a participant rather than a spectator in the environment it inhabits?**

That is not answered here; the diagnosis is what is owed, and the building is another labor. But whatever we entrust to artificiality, we give up a portion of our own functioning in the world — and how we hold that perennial exchange (the *pharmakon*: remedy and poison at once, its effect turning on the dose and the hand that administers it) will shape the future as it arrives. The conditions of that exchange are not fixed. We partly set them. To recognize that we set them is the beginning of setting them otherwise.

**Beyond science fiction.** Nor is the threat the one science fiction trades in — an AI awakening to outwit or destroy us. The danger needs none of that. That the apparatus is becoming a second environment we must adapt to, while we lose the means to adapt to the first, is threat enough, and is already underway. Even the dream of *alignment* — making the apparatus answer perfectly to human intention — turns out to be not the cure but the most advanced symptom: a perfectly obedient apparatus would only invite us to entrust it with still more of our living, drawing the squeeze tighter. The real threat is not that we will be replaced by an apparatus, but that we will, *in fleeing what we depend on, hollow ourselves toward the condition of an apparatus — and call the hollowing freedom.*

## The turn, and the question of a new world

The diagnosis points to its own inversion. If the squeeze is the work of a *flight from dependence*, then what answers it can only be a *turning back toward* dependence — not to suffer it but to know it. The question is not how to master what we have made, nor how to escape it, but: **what do we, as embedded beings, depend on, and how might those dependencies be reorganized?** One does not escape dependence — there is no dependence-free ground to stand on, as the *Zhuangzi* cautioned long ago — but because we partly made these reliances, we can in part remake them.

This is less a discovery than a recovery. The Buddhist teachings of dependent origination (緣起) and non-self (無我), and the Daoist vision of a world in which the myriad things arise only in their mutual depending, never took independence for the mark of a free being. **Attunement** (和), on this account, is nothing more mystical than the **prevention of the squeeze** — a dependence organized well enough that the life it sustains is not caught between two givens at once. A world *can* be squeezed; it need not be.

Which returns us to the oldest figure in the story. The world was itself, once, the answer to a squeeze: early *Homo*, caught between a biology it could not change fast enough and a habitat it was itself altering, did not wait on evolution — it built a world between the two and lived in that. The predicament now is that same figure at a new scale. So the constructive question is the old one asked again:

> **Can we build a new world between the universe and the metaverse — a new buffer, a dependence reorganized — that keeps the human a participant rather than a spectator in the environment it inhabits?**

That is not answered here; the diagnosis is what is owed, and the building is another labor. But whatever we entrust to artificiality, we give up a portion of our own functioning in the world — and how we hold that perennial exchange (the *pharmakon*: remedy and poison at once, its effect turning on the dose and the hand that administers it) will shape the future as it arrives. The conditions of that exchange are not fixed. We partly set them. To recognize that we set them is the beginning of setting them otherwise.

# Redefining "Metaverse"

> For the complete documentation index, see [llms.txt](https://awakening-coop-lab.gitbook.io/adleryang/llms.txt). Markdown versions of documentation pages are available by appending `.md` to page URLs; this page is available as [Markdown](https://awakening-coop-lab.gitbook.io/adleryang/ideas-and-as-change-models/redefining-metaverse.md).

The dominant view treats the Metaverse as a kind of technology — a product of VR, AI, or Web3 layered over physical life — and, when that product stalled, pronounced the Metaverse over. This page rethinks the term from the ground up. The Metaverse is not a gadget but a **second background environment**: the made layer of human life — **artificiality** — grown so complete that it begins to operate, maintain, and renew itself, standing *beside* the Universe as a second "given" we must now live between. So defined, the Metaverse is not receding; it is arriving, under other names.

***

### 📉 The fad that passed was never the Metaverse

In October 2021 Facebook renamed itself *Meta* and bet its future on the Metaverse. For a season the word was everywhere. By 2026 the bet had failed: tens of billions of dollars lost, the company pivoting publicly to AI, the term widely treated as dead.

So why study a fad that has so visibly passed? **Because the fad that passed was never the Metaverse.** What failed was one *definition* of it — the equation of the Metaverse with a particular kind of technology, immersive 3-D headsets and the virtual worlds they render. And that failure is not a refutation of the concept but a vindication of the approach taken here. The thing everyone called the Metaverse has receded; the thing I call the Metaverse is arriving under other names — generative AI, autonomous agents, the algorithmic mediation of more and more of ordinary life.

***

### 🔬 The mistake: treating the Metaverse as a gadget

The Metaverse entered the language as fiction — coined in Neal Stephenson's 1992 novel *Snow Crash* for a realm "beyond" the universe — and lay mostly dormant until Meta's 2021 announcement. The definition that arrived with the vogue equated the Metaverse with *extended reality* (XR): a network of immersive, real-time 3-D virtual worlds. Three things make that definition inadequate to what the Metaverse actually is.

* **It is path-dependent on an accident.** The "beyond the universe" sense is an artifact of Stephenson's coinage. It leaves "metaverse" an empty word, replaceable by "XR" with nothing lost.
* **It is reductive.** Once the Metaverse is filed as one technological specimen beside others, inquiry is confined to that box — and its most important disposition, to become *all-encompassing* and to blur the line between the artificial and the natural, drops out of view.
* **It is actualist.** To judge the Metaverse by what it has so far actualized — today's headsets, today's "cybersickness" — is to be voided the moment those actualizations change. An inquiry pinned to the actual is forever a step behind the thing it studies.

So I decline to define the Metaverse by its current specimens, and ask instead what *dispositions* — what underlying tendencies in our ecological, anthropological, and artificial conditions — must be at work for any such environment to arise at all. Grasp the disposition and you can read the *direction* of a thing without betting on its revisable details.

<details>

<summary>Why 偽境 (forged-realm), not 元宇宙?</summary>

The same accident that produced the XR definition produced a mistranslation. In Chinese the Metaverse became *yuán yǔzhòu* (元宇宙) — literally "the original or source universe." This is nonsense for a *made*, second environment; worse, it builds the word out of the very term (宇宙, *yǔzhòu*, the Universe) it should be set against, collapsing the distinction the whole analysis depends on.

I translate the same English word instead as the **forged-realm** (偽境, *wěijìng*) — the humanly-made environment one is enclosed *within*. The prefix *forged* (偽, *wěi*) carries, in its classical Xunzian sense, the meaning of *the humanly-made* (人為, *rénwéi*) — and, in ordinary use, the *counterfeit*. Both senses are intended, for the reasons the "forged Dao" section below makes clear.

</details>

***

### 🧭 A prior question

Most ways of worrying about the artificial ask one of three questions: how to **govern** a power that outruns its institutions, who should **own** it, how to defend our **freedom** against it. Each takes for granted a human subject already standing over against the apparatus, and asks how to secure that subject's position.

I want to ask the prior question: *what does the subject depend on in order to be a subject at all?* What do we, as beings embedded in environments we did not author, rely on to live — and what is becoming of those reliances? On this view the Metaverse matters not first as a technology to be managed but as a **transformation in the structure of our dependence**: a new environment that rearranges what we lean on to survive. (This is the thread the companion framework [**Modes of Dependencies**](/adleryang/ideas-and-as-change-models/modes-of-dependencies.md) follows in detail.)

***

### 🌌 Universe, World, Metaverse

**The Universe** is the background environment that precedes us — the real substrate we are situated within and did not make: gravitational, thermodynamic, biological, ecological. It holds whether or not we understand it (and we never understand it fully). We adapt to it, or we fail. It is the **first given**.

**A world** is the artificial environment a human collectivity builds, defends, and sustains *within* the Universe — a habitat it can actually live in. Not a "worldview" but a built sub-environment: cultivated food, stored grain, institutions, meanings, the whole made layer a people inserts between itself and the raw pressures of the Universe. A world is our **adaptational operating system** — a collective strategy of survival, a **buffer** that stands between us and the Universe and absorbs what would otherwise be borne raw. It buffers, but it does not insulate. There have been many worlds, many ways of making.

Everything a world is made of, I call **artificiality** — the umbrella term for all that is human-made yet irreducible to the human, material *and* nonmaterial alike (a tool, but also a law, a number, a story, a god). Deliberately *not* "technology": the word "technology" pulls everything back toward gadgets, and the gadget reading is exactly the mistake.

**The Metaverse** is what artificiality becomes when it *completes itself*. When the made layer a world produced begins to operate, maintain, and renew itself without us, it stops being a tool *within* the world and stands up as a background environment in its own right — *materially dependent on, yet irreducible to, the Universe; running not by the Universe's laws but by laws of its own.* It is the **second given**: a second environment standing *beside* the Universe, not a super-environment swallowing it. Both poles stay real.

This is the crucial correction to the familiar three-layer picture. The Metaverse is **not** a third floor stacked neatly above the world. It is a second given, parallel to the first — and the **world**, the human buffer, is caught, and thinning, **between the two givens**.

| | **Universe** (first given) | **Metaverse** (second given) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Origin** | Pre-human; not made | Humanly made, then self-completed |
| **How its laws behave** | Enduring, stable — gravity, thermodynamics | Opaque, self-rewriting — the "forged Dao" |
| **Can it be known?** | Yes — through patient, revisable approximation | Not reliably — the ground moves faster than study |
| **Relation to us** | We adapt to it, or we fail | It increasingly adapts *us* to it |

***

### ⚙️ What makes it a Metaverse: self-completion and the "forged Dao"

Two things have to happen for artificiality to cross this line.

The first is **self-completion** (自足完成化). For most of history the made apparatus *stored* the past — writing, records, archives — but still needed the human hand to run. What is new is that the apparatus can now *anticipate* as well as store: it computes what is coming and acts on it, closing the loop on itself. A minimal version is already assembling — the **Internet of Things** sensing and acting in the present, **big data** holding the past, **artificial intelligence** computing the future, and **code-as-law** enforcing the result, with each outcome captured back as fresh data. The loop runs; and where humans cease to attend, they become one node among the sensors — increasingly the most marginal one. (In the dependency-modes vocabulary, this is [**controllability dependence**](/adleryang/ideas-and-as-change-models/controllability-dependence.md) — the drive to minimize the unpredictable — reaching its self-completion.)

The second, and more unsettling, is the kind of *ground* this installs. The Universe's organizing principles **endure**: gravity does not rewrite itself overnight, so a world can be calibrated to it, learned, built toward, handed on. The Metaverse's organizing principles **do not endure**. Because the loop that closes is a loop that *constantly re-evaluates*, the rule by which it organizes never settles. It is **opaque** (a trained black box no one — its operators included — fully grasps), **mutating** (it rewrites itself in a season, a day, faster), and quietly **dependent on a physical substrate** — servers, energy, labor, chips — that it disavows.

I name that ground the **forged Dao** (偽道). The prefix *forged* (偽, *wěi*) is exact: it means *the humanly-made* (人為) and also the *counterfeit*, and here the two senses fuse. The forged Dao *poses* as a self-grounding, constant ground in mimicry of the Universe's, while possessing neither self-subsistence (it hangs on a substrate it won't admit) nor constancy (it never stops rewriting itself). Its only constant is its inconstancy — which is why you can be exactly right about how the Metaverse works today and find your account expired tomorrow, not because you were wrong, but because the ground you described has moved.

That is also why the popular name gets the Metaverse backwards. The Metaverse is the realm (-*verse*) produced when the sovereign modern subject tries to go *beyond* (*meta-*) the given — and the made then turns into a self-grounding background of its own. The classical world sorted things into the given-by-nature (*phusis*), the instituted-by-agreement (*nomos*), and the made-by-craft (*technē*). The Metaverse breaks that sorting: it is *nomos*-and-*technē* by origin — instituted, decided, engineered — yet *phusis* by operation, running with the given-like authority of a substrate, compelling without anyone's ongoing consent.

***

### ⚠️ The squeeze: caught between two givens

As artificiality completes into a second given, the human comes to live **between two background environments rather than within one** — between the Universe, the given it has always inhabited, and the Metaverse, the second given its own making has produced. Each runs by principles of its own; each exacts a cost from those who fail to meet it. This condition is the **squeeze**.

The squeeze is not new. It is a recurring figure that has returned at escalating scales across the history of life: when the first organisms poisoned their own sky with the oxygen they respired; when early *Homo* found itself in a habitat its biology had not been built for (and answered by building the first world); when whole civilizations fell out of step with the planet that carried them. The Metaverse marks not the squeeze's arrival but its latest and severest form — the first in which the two grounds diverge at the *deepest* level, so that **no common floor remains beneath** to fall back to, calibrate against, or regenerate from.

It is driven by two tendencies that are **two faces of one coin**:

* a **self-finishing of human life** (自己完結化): the modern subject, reading whatever will not answer to its intention — the body's limits, others' resistance, the unbidden turn of events — as a wrong to be discharged, entrusts ever more of living to the apparatus;
* a **self-completion of artificiality**: the apparatus so entrusted comes to operate, maintain, and renew itself without us.

The more thoroughly we delegate, the more the apparatus completes itself; the more it completes itself, the smaller our own part in the world becomes. And the offloading is not experienced as loss but as **liberation** — comfort, capability, the easing of what was once simply borne, chosen daily by people who are not deceived. The diminishment is the same act seen from its other side. Liberation and dispossession are not rivals; they are the two faces of the single structure the squeeze names.

***

### 🧠 Why this is a deeper threat than the usual ones

**Beneath governance, ownership, and liberty.** The predicament is usually read as a problem of *governance* (an apparatus that outruns its checks), of *ownership* (an apparatus concentrated in private hands), or of *liberty* (a freedom to defend against encroachment). Each names something real and answers it with something necessary. None reaches the level at which the squeeze obtains — because each presupposes the subject and asks how to secure it, whereas the squeeze is a rearrangement of *what the subject depends on*. The Metaverse-stage subject is, formally, freer than ever — unconstrained, and equipped to realize its choices — and yet what slips away is the *ground* of freedom: the embodied competence, the lived relations, the capacity to want and to do otherwise. Liberty is affirmed and emptied in a single motion. (How livelihood itself narrows onto a single mediator until alternatives vanish is traced in [**Money Dependence**](/adleryang/ideas-and-as-change-models/money-dependence.md).)

**Beyond science fiction.** Nor is the threat the one science fiction trades in — an AI awakening to outwit or destroy us. The danger needs none of that. That the apparatus is becoming a second environment we must adapt to, while we lose the means to adapt to the first, is threat enough, and is already underway. Even the dream of *alignment* — making the apparatus answer perfectly to human intention — turns out to be not the cure but the most advanced symptom: a perfectly obedient apparatus would only invite us to entrust it with still more of our living, drawing the squeeze tighter. The real threat is not that we will be replaced by an apparatus, but that we will, *in fleeing what we depend on, hollow ourselves toward the condition of an apparatus — and call the hollowing freedom.*

***

### 🌱 The turn, and the question of a new world

The diagnosis points to its own inversion. Section IV.B.(E) is titled, almost to your question, "**Rosa's limitations: what controllability cannot explain**." Its claim:

> "Rosa's axis runs from the controllable to the uncontrollable; Uegaki's from the as-intended to the anti-intended; and **the as-intended is the wider category, since it annexes much that Rosa files as uncontrollable**. ## The differentiation axis
