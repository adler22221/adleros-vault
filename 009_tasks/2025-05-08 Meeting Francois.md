Good morning, Francois!

Reflecting on yesterday’s conversations—both our meeting with Deputy Minister Benson and our dinner with Ozzie and colleagues—I realized you might have visited us primarily seeking inspiration on how to scale impact, particularly leveraging technology to address global challenges.

It occurred to me we might have unintentionally emphasized constraints and limitations more than intended. I’m sorry if this created the impression that we were making excuses for our limitations or inability.

To clarify, we have achieved some widespread systemic change, even within these constraints. Examples include the remarkable impact of g0v initiatives you’ve already heard of, the numbers and stories Benson and Ozzie shared, the transformations of my mentees (now colleagues), with more detailed insights in the KAS report I provided. (I just added a couple of links to this chat for your reference.)

Importantly, everyone you met yesterday genuinely shares your vision and values. After dinner, we deeply resonated with your trust- and character-centered approach—interestingly very aligned with traditional Confucian values, emphasizing virtue before merit, since merit doesn't always manifest virtue, while a virtuous person with virtuous learning can bear fruit of virtuous merit. We truly admire how authentically you embody this philosophy, which has become increasingly rare as Taiwan adopts more neoliberal meritocratic values.

Because we already align closely with your core values, we didn’t need convincing of their importance. Rather, Benson and Ozzie, despite being among Taiwan’s most influential educational leaders (easily in the top 10), displayed extraordinary humility—something that genuinely surprised even me. Through this openness, I realized we were collectively, without prior arrangement, sharing our vulnerabilities openly and hoping to exchange in-depth/detailed experiences and insights into navigating these wicked problems for which no one has definitive solutions.

While we’ve had some success at initiating change, sustaining and deepening these transformations remains a shared challenge, influenced by various factors, including the geopolitical and cultural complexities we discussed.

Many of us perceive Audrey Tang’s strategy as using Taiwan’s success stories to attract international attention, which is very important. In East Asian culture, “face” matters significantly; global expectations often pressure stakeholders to sustain successes—similar to Asian students feeling compelled to maintain perfect academic records without ever allowing themselves to “fall behind.”

(On another note, while the Confucian, Daoist, and Buddhist philosophies you’ve encountered are considered high culture, since the May 4th Movement a century ago, these traditions have often been seen as obstacles hindering China’s modernization under Western influence. This perception persists and is amplified today, especially as Taiwan seeks to distance itself from China.)

Given our aspiration to build authentic partnerships, we want to present a realistic picture beyond the polished narratives you might typically hear from Audrey.

I wanted to clearly share this context before we explore potential collaborations further. I greatly appreciate your understanding and would be very interested in hearing more about your expectations for Taiwan and East Asia, so we can realistically discuss synergies for our mutual and planetary flourishing🍀

Looking forward to today’s conversation!
