Rising Inequality and Academia’s Broken Economic Promise

Despite claims of meritocracy, university attendance continues to reflect and reinforce socioeconomic stratification.  Even worse, the economic promise of academia—that degrees lead to upward mobility and financial security—is becoming a blank check across the globe. Predicted half a century ago by Richard Freeman,  "overeducation induced underemployment" became reality for Taiwan as early as 2007,  and similar dynamics have since surfaced across other major economies.  In the United States, surging student debt has compounded this crisis, trapping graduates in ongoing financial insecurity.  This employment crisis perpetuates social inequality, and the deeper a disadvantaged youth buys into the upward mobility promise, the more precarious their situation becomes – After surviving decades of filtering, what awaits them is a job market where their skills are obsolete or a shrinking academia with increasingly scarce posts.

Rising Polarization and Academia’s Role in the Widening Divide

While social cohesion is desperately needed, academia often fails to bridge communities, if not polarizing them further. As academic knowledge production is becoming a 'luxury,’ the reproduction of social stratification through academia also shapes research: scholars’ perspectives are skewed by their social positions, which, in turn, reinforce particular presumptions/theory-ladenness and marginalize others.  Simultaneously, the system has created what Michael Sandel terms a "tyranny of merit"—where academic success and failure are internalized as personal moral worth, creating dangerous social divisions.  “Highly educated elites” claim authority over "legitimate facts," frequently dismissing the voices of the “less-educated” as mere "opinion." This dynamic fosters distrust, fuels populism and the spread of misinformation, and, as lived experience is discounted, pushes the marginalized toward alternative knowledge systems. It is this mutual incommunicability, rather than ignorance, that drives the erosion of epistemic authority in the post-truth era. 

A Global Extractive Machinery
Academia systematically reinforces exploitative center-periphery relationships through its institutional design and knowledge production priorities: The hierarchy of institutions and disciplines entrenches global divisions of labor and operates as a "brain drain" mechanism, where the “brightest” individuals from rural/peripheral regions migrate to metropolitan/core centers for education and employment, leaving their communities of origin systematically disempowered.  

Look at Taiwan's obsession with electrical engineering, solidifying its role as a semiconductor hub. Look at Hong Kong's fixation on finance. Look at the percentage of the world’s supposedly “brightest” graduates out of the Ivy League who go to work on Wall Street and in management consulting (to  Gemini: find this data with a reliable source). We educate our young to service the machine, not to question it. This system is the "pathological rural-urban/South-North divide".1 It is inherently unsustainable.

Educational Success, Human Dysfunction?
Academia operates on the assumption that the acquisition of academic and vocational knowledge is sufficient for individuals and societies to thrive. University curricula prioritize economics, physics, chemistry, business, and computer sciences while systematically treating mindfulness, interpersonal relationships, or existential meaning as “private affairs”—domains increasingly recognized as fundamental to human wellbeing and social functioning.
The result? A generation of high-achievers suffering from record-high rates of depression and anxiety. (to Gemini: find reliable evidence to cite to back this claim)

Façade of Reform with 'The Same Level of Thinking

Now, we are not the first to notice these problems. And the world is full of "fixes."

These reforms, however, operate at the same level of thinking that created the problem.

First, we get the conventional responses. We get curricular "add-ons". We get the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). We get university-branded "inter- and transdisciplinary studies" centers. This is "education for sustainable development/intersdisciplinary." But it's a profound contradiction. We teach "SDG 13: Climate Action" or “interdisciplinarity” in a classroom, while the university still works in silos, with its endowment, operations, and social function continue to reproduce unsustainability.

Then, we have the Silicon Valley disruptions.

Peter Thiel's Fellowship correctly critiques the university for loading students with debt, leaving the most imaginative and audacious generation too risk-averse to address the world’s most pressing challenges. His solution? Give $100,000 to 20 elite youths to "quit university, start a business, change the world". This is market-omnipotentialism disguised as liberation. These so-called “brightest” youth learns to ignore democratic processes and checks and balances as long as they are bright and rich enough to bring the “silver bullet.” 

And then... we get the most sophisticated form of ideological reproduction. A prominent "intentional university" co-founded by a renowned cognitive scientist.

This university claims to use the "science of learning" through a pedagogy based on the "forced application" of scientifically and market-validated "Habits of Mind," and students’ motivation isn’t necessary or relevant to the learning outcomes. Indeed, after a year of freshman training, their CLA+ (to Gemini: find a generic term that a non-US audience would understand) outperforms 99 percent of all university seniors. This is not transformation. This is high-tech assimilation. It's a systematic reproduction of "West-centric, corporate-based positivist" elites.

To address the polycrisis, we need a different level of thinking.

Proof of Concepts of Different Levels of Thinking

Fortunately, these "Proofs of Concept" already exist. I have spent years doing fieldwork at these alternatives.
First, as an antidote to human dysfunction and alienation, we have the Tekisen Democratic University (TDU) in Japan.
TDU recognized that Japan’s high rates of hikikomori and social withdrawal stem from an education system that "reduce[s] people to prescribed functional components".
TDU's solution is not a new curriculum. It is a new way of being. It facilitates "authentic relational selves".
How? Through democratic self-governance.1 Students don't just study democracy; they live it, co-creating and sustaining their own community.
How? Through "self-ology" research 1, where students study their own "life difficulties" to emancipate themselves.
How? Through "life design" , discovering a lifestyle that is both financially viable and meaningful.
Students graduate when they decide they are ready.1 TDU heals relational alienation by replacing a predetermined curriculum with an organic, reflexive alignment between the self and society.1

Second, as an antidote to the global extractive machinery, we have Busan On Peum To (BOPT) in Korea.
BOPT addresses the "pathological rural-urban divide" and the "hell-like" living conditions of hyper-competition in cities like Busan.
BOPT breaks down the credit and departmental system entirely. Its program is centered on building and sustaining an ecological, cooperative community in rural Korea.
It attracts urban youth "fed up with their suffocating urban lives"  to an ecovillage. There, they learn by doing. Themes like ecological humanities, wilderness survival, natural food making, ecological architecture, and energy self-reliance are seamlessly embedded in their daily lives.1
This "re-importation of youth"  is regenerating the rural region, creating cooperative jobs and a meaningful alternative to extractive urbanism.

Education/Research AS Sustainable Development
TDU and BOPT are not just another "university." They are Proofs of Concepts that serve as “hubs for systemic transformation”
They prove that academia can function as "education/research as sustainable development," not just "education/research for sustainable development". They are structured as the very systems we seek to create.
