My name is Adler Yang. 

In the past 14 years, I've mainly been involved in education reform and social innovation, and I still do related work for a living. 

However, through practice, I soon realized that the predominent positivistic, reductionistic, mechanistic, and anthropocentric ways of grappling with problems even in the realm of social change greatly limits our work, in the sense that we mostly seek to fix symptoms at the surface level, so the same problems continue to perpetuate with the disguise of different appearances. 

With that realization, I gradually put more and more effort into studying the underlying common mechanisms of such wicked problems, and testing my findings through practice. I'm not sure if its fortunate or not, I had to do most of such research independently because it has been extremely difficult to fit it in any academic programs I have access to so far. 

## Context

So today I'm here to seek for advice. 

My independent research has been mainly guided by the philosophy underlying complex systems theory, and I eventually found my work highly compatible and probably more communicable with critical realism.

I understand that the 10 minute discussion will not be enough, so I'd especially appreciate if you can give "strategic" advice on what might be the best way to improve the rigor of my study, and what might be the best ways to disect my large piece of work into academically rigorous publications, and hopefully also get a degree so I can navigate in the academia more smoothly. 

## My pursuit

Since I was a child, I've been committed to understanding or unconvering the underlying common mechanisms to what is called wicked problems. 

The word common is important here, because even with my childish common sense, I realized that, if the workings of the world isn't segregated by disciplinary boundaries, how in the world would the causes behind problems bounded by disciplines?

With that early intuition, I embrace a position that is shared by both complex systems and critical realism, which is an open systems, stratified, emergentist view of causality. 

## Education as reproduction/transformation

Among the vast options, I chose education as a field to start with, one is because it was most accesible to me as a teenager, but the other is because that education, in a broad sense, is the process of forming people's modes of relations. Because so, I believe that the formation of generative mechanisms can be easier observed and identified through the process of education. 

Now looking back, I find my rationale quite compatible with Archer's morphogenic approach. 

## Diplomaism as wicked problem

The wicked problem I chose, is sometimes translated as "Diplomaism." But it also has other definitions. 

The way it is "wicked" can be seen in these pictures. 

Until roughly 30 years ago, people still do whatever they can do to get great marks in entrance exams. 

But years later, the admission processes have been reformed and reformed with the intents, such as to allow more holistic development of the children, and prevent cramming or teaching to the tests. 

However, while the focus isn't on test content that much anymore, people still do all sorts of extreme things, for example, like in this picture, people line up all night just for the limited spots of volunteering at a hostpital, so they can make their student portfolio look better in admissions.

## From... to...

Because of the time I'll only use one student's story to illustrate.

This boy is actually my childhood friend. To me, he was like child Da Vinci -- unicycle, jazz improptu, self-taught 3d animation before wikipedia was a thing, and always share his theoretical musings about things, such as using these blocks to represent different theories of the structures of the universe.

But later he became convinced that in order to get "good education" that can really help him fulfill his potentials, he began to give up doing all of the things he was passionate about for only the subjects relevant to tests. 

Even in the process he occassionally question why does he have to give up so many things that matter, he still bit the bullet to move on and eventually find satisfaction in test-taking. 

Unfortunately, while his learning at the alternative school made him to a "selective traditional high school," the test-focused traditional high school pedagogy didn't help him to get into the major he wanted. He ended up in a program which he doesn't have any interest in. 

And when he finally decided to take a transfer exam to apply for a program that better suits him, his family went into a financial crisis that required him to contribute to his family's finances. 

Had he continued working on 3d animation, he would've already have around 8 years of experience when he turned 18, and would've been able to make decent money with this potential and passion of his. However, since he stopped doing all that for many years, he didn't have employable skill to make money immediately, so he decided to join the military and became a professional soldier instead. 

## Discovery of Allocation Dependence

Retroducing from my first major study with stories like what I just shared, I discovered a generative mechanism that I call "Allocation Dependence."

## Structure of AD

This mechanism emerges, when an agent perceives, whether true or not, that he or she is dependent upon some allocated resource, and makes the effort to fit into the allocation measures to redeem the allocated resources. 

## Monetization

In this process, people become agnostic of the inherent values of the assimilated & trimmed off qualities. 

Also, whether they like it or not, how well they fit in the allocation measures also function like "how much money you have," in the sense that it determines how many resources are redeemable/exchangeable.

## exhaustion

... In other words, resource circulation/regeneration is externalized from such a structure. 

## Deterioration & alienation

In health, we know that any kind of excess or deficiency, say ingredients or activities, can lead to deterioration of health.

I see alienation as the social version of health deterioration.

And assimilation, trimming, the misallocation, and the monetization or negligence of intrinsic values can all lead to this.
