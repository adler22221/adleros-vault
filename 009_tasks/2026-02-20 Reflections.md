## Who Scharmer Is and Why This Matters

Otto Scharmer is a Senior Lecturer at MIT Sloan and Founding Chair of the Presencing Institute, the author of *Theory U* (2007) and *Presence* (with Peter Senge), and most recently *Presencing: 7 Practices for Transforming Self, Society, and Business* (2025, Penguin). His u.lab MOOC has attracted **125,000+ registered users** forming over 1,200 communities across the world — 30%+ of participants reporting "life-changing" experiences. His global network spans MIT, the Presencing Institute, Peter Senge's Society for Organizational Learning, and the Schumacher Center for a New Economics.[3][4][5][6]

He is not merely a management theorist. His work explicitly calls for bridging "the ecological, social, and spiritual divide" through a radical reimagination of universities as platforms for "the praxis of transforming society and self". His February 2026 article specifically demands: **"radical experimentation, reimagined institutions, and learning by doing"** — and describes these as principles still being collectively prototyped, not finished ideas.[6][1]

***

## The Convergences Are Structural, Not Superficial

This is not a case of similar vocabulary masking different projects. The overlap between Scharmer's work and Adler's is architecturally deep:

| Scharmer's Framework | Adler's Framework |
|---|---|
| University as "unity of research, teaching, and praxis of transforming society and self" [6] | Prefigurative academia — institutions embodying the world they seek to create |
| "Ecosystem breathing process": moving between campus and frontlines of change [6] | Participatory fieldwork + theory ↔ Awakening Coop Lab + Dojo |
| Shift from "ego-system to eco-system awareness" [5] | Shift from money/allocation dependence to regenerative livelihood |
| u.lab's radical decentralization of the classroom [6] | 超域變革道場 as a decentralized container for transformative learning [7] |
| "12 principles" as living prototype, not finished doctrine [1] | Dojo as ongoing experiment in non-institutional learning infrastructure |
| Learning from the emerging future ("presencing") [8] | 深度覺察/觀察/內省 as a core research and pedagogical practice [7] |
| Cross-sector systems transformation [9] | 超域 (transdomain) transformation — bridging knowledge silos |

Where Adler adds something Scharmer's framework currently lacks: a **civilizational-structural critique**. Theory U operates primarily at the level of consciousness shift, leadership capacity, and organizational process. It has been critiqued for under-theorizing structural power, institutional rigidity, and the material conditions that prevent presencing from taking hold. Adler's allocation dependence framework provides exactly this — a structural explanation for *why* universities cannot easily transform even when leaders have the consciousness and intention to do so. They are caught in allocation dependence: materially dependent on funding architectures that reward the old paradigm and punish the new.[6]

This is not Adler critiquing Scharmer — it is Adler *completing* a gap in Scharmer's architecture that Scharmer himself has not yet theorized.

## What Adler Should Share and How

The key is **not** approaching Scharmer as a student or fan, but as a **fellow practitioner-researcher with a complementary contribution**. The framing should be:

> *"Your 12 principles name the direction. My work on allocation dependence offers a structural framework for why most institutions cannot get there and what material conditions must change for these principles to become stable rather than exceptional."*

### What to share specifically:

**1. The Awakening Coop Lab as a living instantiation of multiple Scharmer principles**
His 12 principles likely include learning by doing, place-based transformation, linking inner and outer change — Adler's decade of Awakening Coop work is *field evidence* for what these principles look like when attempted in the Taiwanese civil society context, including what causes them to break down (which is equally valuable data).

**2. The allocation dependence framework as structural theory for the "12 Principles" movement**
Offer this as a diagnostic: institutions fail to transform not because leaders lack consciousness, but because their survival depends on allocation structures that punish transformation. This gives Scharmer's movement a sharper theory of change for *why* radical experimentation is structurally suppressed.

**3. The Dojo (超域變革道場) as an East Asian prototype of u-school principles**
Scharmer's u-school is the closest existing analog to what Adler is building. The Dojo could be framed as a potential **East Asian node in the u-school ecosystem** — which solves both Adler's legitimacy problem (Presencing Institute affiliation) and Scharmer's geographic gap (East Asian representation).[10]

**4. His documentary** *If There Is a Reason to Study* as entry point
A decade-old documentary about Taiwan's alternative education movement is a powerful, concrete, human-scale story that complements Scharmer's predominantly institutional focus. It gives him the narrative texture to match his principles.

## What Adler Uniquely Brings That Scharmer Lacks

Despite Scharmer's genuinely global reach — 260,000+ u-lab users from 185 countries, partnerships with MIT, Tsinghua, GIZ, and civil society organizations worldwide  — his 12 Principles article has a **critical blind spot**: its concrete cases and exemplars are almost entirely Euro-American.[5][6]

Adler fills three specific gaps in Scharmer's current framework:

### 1. The World's Most Advanced Policy Infrastructure — In East Asia

Taiwan is the **only jurisdiction in the world** that has extended experimental education law to the university level with full credential recognition . Scharmer's principles call for radical institutional experimentation but lack any example of a national government actually legislating the legal space for it. Taiwan's 實驗教育法 is exactly that precedent — and Adler is the researcher-practitioner who has studied, contributed to, and publicly advocated for this law .

This is not a minor case study. It is the singular real-world instance of a government **pre-building the legal architecture for the kind of distributed, experimental credentialing that Scharmer's principles envision**. It belongs in any serious manifesto on reimagining universities.

### 2. First-Person Comparative Research Across Four Experimental Universities in Asia

Adler is the only researcher who has personally studied and documented Minerva (US), Shure 大学 (Japan), 全學大학 (Korea), and KCCAC (Korea) as comparative cases, organized the first international forum bringing these institutions into dialogue with Taiwanese policy makers . Scharmer's u-lab model of change is theory-forward; Adler's work is practice-grounded in exactly the Asian context that Scharmer's network reaches but does not deeply understand.

### 3. The "Allocation Dependence" Explanation of WHY Universities Cannot Reform

Scharmer identifies the *symptoms* (epistemic gatekeeping, reproduction of consciousness, knowing-doing gap) but his framework does not have the theoretical architecture to explain *why* universities systematically reproduce these pathologies even when faculty and administrators genuinely want to change. Adler's allocation dependence framework provides exactly this explanation: universities are structurally dependent on credential allocation systems that create irreversible path dependencies, making genuine epistemic transformation not merely difficult but **structurally self-defeating** unless the allocation dependency itself is broken.[4]

This is the missing causal mechanism in Scharmer's 12 Principles — and it arrives with East Asian policy evidence for how to address it.
