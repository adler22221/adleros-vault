Loved this piece, and just a gentle note that the “self‑domesticated apes” insight has a rich prehistory in Japanese ecological and philosophical scholarship—so, truly, great minds think alike.

Ecologist and mammalogist Hideo Obara articulated an ecological “self‑domestication of humans” framework as early as 1963, and later gathered those arguments in volumes whose tables of contents explicitly treat “self‑domestication and self‑artificial selection.”

Obara’s 自己家畜化論 frames humans as reshaping selection pressures via artificial environments to produce domestication‑like traits (e.g., reduced aggression, neotenous features), tied to “self‑artificial selection” and a critical motif of “self‑pet‑ification” of modern life.

This lineage is fairly well known and cited in Japanese literature and venues around the Japan Association of Synthetic Anthropology, which Obara helped launch and where his self‑domestication program became a sustained research thread.

Philosopher Masahiro Morioka (Waseda) extends this arc in his Painless Civilization project, treating painless modernity as an evolutionary outgrowth of human self‑domestication and linking it to deeper questions of desire and the “joy of life.”

Against that backdrop, the 2010 formulation in What Technology Wants remains a vivid and influential modern statement—just not the first time anyone suggested humans domesticated themselves.

For reference, related work tying these threads to contemporary technoculture and the metaverse/universe squeeze continues to build on Obara’s ecological thesis in ongoing research.

Dear Kevin,

I thoroughly enjoyed your latest post on "The Self-Domesticated Ape"—it's a fascinating exploration of how we've shaped our own evolution through imagination and intention. Your argument that humans domesticated ourselves first, as outlined in your 2010 book _What Technology Wants_, resonates deeply and adds a compelling layer to the conversation.

That said, I'd like to gently point out that this idea has roots in earlier ecological scholarship. Ecologist Hideo Obara, co-founder of the Japan Association of Synthetic Anthropology (where I serve on the board), proposed the theory of "human self-domestication" (自己家畜化論) on an ecological basis as far back as 1963, in his book _21st Century Humanity: Where Is Homo Sapiens Headed?_ (republished here: [https://www.akashi.co.jp/book/b65645.html](https://www.akashi.co.jp/book/b65645.html)). Obara's framework posits that humans, through cultural and environmental adaptations, have undergone a process akin to domestication seen in animals—reducing aggression, extending neoteny, and fostering social cooperation to thrive in increasingly complex societies, all while altering our biological traits in response to self-created ecological niches.

His work is relatively well-cited in Japanese literature (as seen in Google Scholar searches for "小原秀雄 自己家畜化"), and it echoes in contemporary philosophy, such as Masahiro Morioka's discussions at Waseda University on "painless civilization" and self-domestication (e.g., in his book _Painless Civilization 1_ and related papers referencing Jonas, Freud, and Bataille). I've also touched on these themes in my own work-in-progress, _Metaverse and Universe: Surviving the Squeeze in Between_.

Great minds think alike! I'd love to hear your thoughts on these earlier perspectives.
