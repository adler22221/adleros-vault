Ontology has tended to be directly shifted into the realm of science; epistemology has tended to be pursued in terms of the realm of science, whilst ethics has either been split away or indirectly transformed by movements in science. Science was to show us how the world is, how it should be investigated and, perhaps, what wo/man’s role in it has been and will be. Since scientific practice and discoveries radically transformed our empirical grasp of the world around us, this shift has been both comprehensible and, in the main, progressive. But at the same time, the capacity of science to misapprehend its own practice and limits, and of society to receive that misapprehension as fact, has fractured philosophical inquiry into its divisions as not only distinguishable issues but distinct realms. This in itself muted the philosophical voice in a way that has more than simply (and legitimately) reduced its role to that of under-labourer, since fracture was accompanied by exclusion: the pursuit of a philosophy of ontology was the first casualty of this social transformation. 

Thereafter, philosophy contributed to the fracture of philosophy itself, as well as to its own conceptual dualisms and contradictions.3 Thus Enlightenment empiricists (Hume), critical idealists (Kant), radical dissenters such as the romantics (Schelling), early existentialists (Kierkegaard) and natural scientists themselves (Darwin) struggled with the repercussions of a redrawing of the boundaries of enquiry (the within and without of science). Is there room for an autonomous, ethically aware human in a ‘clockwork’ universe? Do I impose the clock, or is the clock imposed on me? Can one reconcile the sacred and profane? Thereafter, as science fed industrialization that in turn constructed an increasingly instrumental society, philosophy became a series of specializations within a bounded departmental university structure. As a result, the very constitution of society, its division of labour, has tended to freeze the fracture of epistemology, ontology and ethics that the emergence of science set in motion.

### 1. THE STRUCTURE OF META-REALITY 

#### 1.1 Defining meta-Reality 

The etymology of ‘meta’ is Greek in origin, meaning ‘with’ (genitive) or ‘after’ or ‘beyond’ (accusative). Bhaskar uses the term to refer to beyond reality as currently understood: 

> The ‘meta’ here connotes both the idea of transcendence, that is going to a level beyond or behind, or behind and between reality, while at the same time the ‘reality’ in the title makes it clear that this level is still real and so part of the very same totality that critical realism has been describing all along (RMR, p. 175 ft. 8).10 

According to Bhaskar, reality as currently understood is a compromised formation, a limitation on the possibilities of being. For this reason he refers to current reality as ‘demi-reality’. From the position of the philosophy of meta-Reality, prior critical realist philosophy has been concerned with developing an adequate account of this demi-reality (RMR, pp. xv, 181; MR1, p. vii) with its characteristic elements. In the language of DCR these elements include varieties of power2, master-slave conditions typically expressed in the meta-Reality texts: 

• Relationally/socially: as exploitation, oppression, conflict, environmental degradation, instrumentality, commodification (MR1, p. xxi) 
• Emotionally/individually: as egoism, unhappiness, alienation, jealousy, aggression, apathy, cynicism (MR1, p. 178) 

According to Bhaskar, these conditions of demi-reality entail the translation of our physical individuation (our unique or singularized existence) into an isolating sense of basic separation or lack of connection (MR1, p. 37). This sense is also a philosophical or theoretical error of perception of the embodied practice of physical beings in demi-reality. With this in mind, he refers to the above negatively ascribed relational and individual conditions as aspects of dualism, and their philosophical error as errors of dualism (such as

p.123

Cartesian dualism, MR1, p. 147). ==False knowledge, which from the point of view of meta-Reality includes limiting oneself to demi-reality as the sum total of the possibility of being, is a check on the potential of being that helps to sustain this compromised formation. Demi-reality (the world that is and that we think we know), therefore, also contains ‘illusory reality’—ideas that are real since they are held as concepts but do not correspond in an adequate way to an object of reference (MR1 pp. 45-46).11== [^1]

Crucially, for Bhaskar meta-Reality is more than simply unfulfilled potential held in check by existence in demi-reality; the characteristics he ascribes to meta-Reality are understood as ==basic necessary conditions (stunted though they may be) in the very forms of being experienced in demi-reality== (MR1, pp. 35, 47, 175). It is for this reason, he argues, that one can identify them and their implications and develop them in a way that transcends the compromised formation that is demi-reality. For Bhaskar, ==meta-Reality is thus dialectical: simultaneously a ‘fine structure’ or ‘interior’ (MR1, p. ix) upon which demi-reality is parasitic, and also a distinct potential that can be liberated to produce a new emergent mode of existence== (RMR, p. 197). This liberation, first and foremost does not move one beyond demi-reality since it is an act of transformation by embodied beings in given types of society accessing more basic states that enable the agent to qualitatively change societies—improving relative reality through praxis, especially illusion reduction (MR1, pp. 39, 44–5). However, there is also the implication (or speculation, MR1, pp. 25–9) that there is something beyond this embodied existence, a yet higher form of being potentially attainable in concordance with this ‘fine structure’. Systemizing these aspects (though mainly the former) of transformation is the sense in which Bhaskar sees meta-Reality ‘retotalizing’ critical realism at a new ‘higher’ level (RMR, p. 189; MR1, p. xv).

#### 1.3 Summary: the ontological schema of meta-Reality 

##### Fundamental question: 
What must be the case for human emancipation to be possible? 

##### Devolving, in terms of aporiai, into two: 
i. What powers and potentials do humans possess that are more than social and make social transformation possible? 
ii. How are oppressive structures (and humans themselves) changed by these powers and potentials?

p.131

##### Archimedean point: 
Non-duality inferred from a ‘without conscious thought’ yet focussed element in everyday activity (forms of transcendental identification) 

##### Situated to two errors: 
i. The beautiful soul of traditional spirituality—non-dual or transcendental experience as exceptional and to be sought outside society. 
ii. The dualism of perception that conceives the individual as outside/ separate or discrete from their environment—entailing the ‘I’ that is, in the sense of a distinct ego, an illusion. 

##### Inference from the Archimedean point to inter-implicated categories: 
i. Non-duality implies connectivity or a unity of difference: a cosmic envelope or basic level to totality 
ii. The experience of non-duality implies that connectivity has a ‘fine structure’: the condition of possibility of identity across difference is a common something or ingredient whose prime characteristic must be a co-presence that can be accessed (all within all as both the very possibility of emergence and as a real state at a basic level within each element in the whole which makes possible identity) 

##### Further categories towards the original question: 
i. Fine structure as it manifests within the individual is accessible (beginning from its occurrence as the non-dual element within everyday activity) 
ii. Accessing this non-duality is being in one’s ground state 
iii. Pre-requisites of effective ground state access are present-moment awareness and acceptance of self-referentiality—focussing the individual on their intentionality 
iv. Being in one’s ground state is to become maximally aware of the here, the now, of one’s responsibility for oneself and society and of copresence (entailing a reciprocality) and orienting right-action 
v. Right-action is given operative meaning by the inference that a basic characteristic of fine structure is love, thus co-presence accesses the implicit value of being

p.132

vi. Right action has three dynamics: a reorientation of one’s attitude towards self (enlightenment and happiness through ground state access); a reorientation of one’s everyday activity (what one desires, values, and does) with implications for the reproduction of structures based in instrumentality, conflict, aggression etc; and a momentum towards expanding activity towards the emancipation of others (since that they are subject to social ill means that you are) 

##### Result: 
i. The construction of a philosophical basis for a positive movement towards eudaimonia—against the ills of demi-reality, particularly illusory reality—a becoming within relative reality based on the basic driving forces of meta-Reality (the connection of human powers and potentials beyond the social) 
ii. A system that coheres around the original question or frame of reference in a way that the author considers overcomes the prior errors of others who have worked on the question or frame of reference and makes better sense of the experience or situation from which they proceed—hence the Archimedean point
