Thanks XXX. It's really a privilege to be here, given that I might be the youngest and most unconventional speaker in this seminar. Most of my education comes from alternative schools and home schooling. Despite I teach teachers in Taiwan and Hong Kong, work with the government often, and have published peer-reviewed articles, I do not hold a formal degree. 

Taking this rare opportunity I'd also like to invite you to think out of the box with me: 
People often assume that some people are disadvantaged because they lack resources. But is it possible that the disadvantaged are actually the most resource abundant?

In fact, this is not a thought experiment. Taiwan is already a living counterfactual to the popular narrative. According to research, rural schools and students in Taiwan, overall, enjoy more resources compared to their counterparts in other areas. Whether in terms of funding, hardware, technology facilities, or activities provided to each student, rural schools and students are far from being disadvantaged. However, the achievement gap between rural and urban students are still significant. 

So, if a more equal distribution of educational resources does not improve the achievement gap, what can we still do?

While other researchers try to find the real cause of the achievement gap in Taiwan, as the "lack of resources" is basically ruled out, I take a different direction to examine the presumption of academic achievement itself and its consequences. 

Twelve years ago, when I was 14, I started a longitudinal ethnographic research. I followed my best childhood friend, a Da Vinci-like boy from elementary school to his 20s. I said he's Da Vinci-like because he self-taught himself many cool things, including robotics, 3D animation, and jazz impromptu, before online resources such as Wikipedia were even popular. But to perform well on the college admission test, he sacrificed his time improving his skills on his passions for many years. Instead, he focused on excelling in his academic achievements. However, when he finally entered college and wanted to revive his passions, his family encountered a financial crisis that he didn't have any other choice but to drop out to find a job. Even though he was way ahead of his peers in robotics and 3D animation in elementary school, those skills weren't developed enough for him to find a job that could financially support his family, so he didn't have any other choice but to serve the military. 

This ethnography eventually led to the production of a documentary film If There is a Reason to Study, which received awards internationally and was theatrically released in the greater Chinese-region. It also led towards the development of a theory I call Allocation Dependence, which I've been publishing and presenting in recent years. 

While I can't go into the details of both works, I'd like to share 3 key insights I find relevant to today's topic. 

1. When you judge students against specific standards, some are always more advantageous than others, because human beings are not identical replicas of each others. Therefore, as long as we do so, inequality is inevitable.
2. Academic achievement doesn't necessarily translate to how well off they will be in life. My friend in the ethnography is a living but heartbreaking snapshot of many students who work hard to fit in standards at the cost of their real strengths. 
3. Only resource allocation and consumption are entailed in the design of our education system. People are schooled to not include resource regeneration in their concerns.
