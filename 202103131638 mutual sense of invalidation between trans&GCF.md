[[Pilgrim, David]]

The first existential claim is that real women (i.e. anatomically female from birth) suffer patriarchal oppression arising from their objectively demonstrable collective experiences, for example in relation to reproductive rights, sexual violence, the normative aspects of socialization in childhood and the sexual division of labour in adulthood. From this political standpoint, transwomen have no mandate at all to join the collective existential state of real women. More than that, when men ‘transitioning’ to women demand the right to enter private female spaces, such as prisons, changing rooms and toilets, this creates reactive anger from those feminists.

For their part, trans-activists experience this attack from feminists as undermining their right to exist in the way that they wish to in society. This is particularly the case with the performative aspect of female existence (when entering the segregated world of female spaces or women only roles). In other words, for trans-affirmative ideology, for a man to merely sense himself as a woman (with or without medical interventions) is a private or subjective matter, which then should become a human right as a form of self-determined practice.

Thus an inner sense of self is transformed into a form of visible conduct expressed in a pre-existing social context with its norms and mores. At that point, a personal choice and private belief becomes a public matter. If there is a rejection or censure of the preferred trans conduct, then a grievance emerges on both sides. One is offended by the conduct and the other that the conduct is not respectfully being accepted as a human right. Also, transwomen feel that they really are women and so this places them in a position of both oppression and its implied basis for solidarity with other women, which is then not being acknowledged and respected by gender-critical feminists. Accordingly, trans-activists and their supporters protest about transphobia and they can spell out the rights that they believe should be enjoyed by transwomen in civil society (e.g. Strycher and Aizura 2013; Feinberg 1999).

At this point, we see a stand-off and maybe irreconcilable gap between trans-activists and their political opponents. Both sides of the dispute experience and may
express hurt and anger at their *mutual sense of invalidation*. This shared emotional
reaction created by opposite existential claims about womanhood arise from a
common implication of identity politics and its attendant forms of competing epistemological privilege.

Feminists trying to proffer their views that challenge the existential state of transwomen report abuse on social media, being harassed and being fearful of arranging open public meetings about the topic. Some have been ‘no platformed’. For example, a lesbian feminist Linda Bellos was banned from speaking at a meeting at Cambridge University in early October 2017. Germaine Greer has faced similar calls for banning and her academic career has been affected. She has repeatedly made public her view that transwomen are not women.8 In her sequel to the Female Eunuch (Greer 1999) she discusses rape and transwomen in the same breath: ==‘When he forces his way into the few private spaces woman may enjoy and shouts down their objections, and bombards the women who will not accept him with hate mail, he does as rapists have always done.’==^[This is another potential point of dispute: To which extent is this analogy valid may depend on to which extent could someone claim their act to be respected at the cost of others' discomfort or harm?
