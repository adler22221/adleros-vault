Michael and I attended a “Wizards of OS” event in Berlin.5 We explored there the relationship between
open money and the FLOSS movement. He pursued potential applications ranging from small
international electronic payments with the European Commission and schools in London to the peso
crisis and barter clubs (trueque) in Argentina (Hart 2002). I consulted on the informal economy for the
French Ministry of Culture. Le Monde saw social democracy's future in local exchange systems. The
movement to open money was gathering pace. The gap separating LETS initiatives from humanity’s
problems was narrowing!

In 2003 I gave the keynote at a Bangkok conference on “Trade in organic products” held by the
International Federation of Organic Agriculture Movements (IFOAM). Having read The Memory Bank,
they thought I could address their most pressing problem. Certifying food as “organic” was expensive,
especially for international trade. Only Europe, the US and Japan had consumers who would pay for the
higher costs. But getting in there was difficult. Five corporations led by Nestlé supplied 95% of
international trade in organic products. Conference participants came mostly from Japan, Germany,
New York State and Thailand. Each had flourishing community currencies and DIY markets. IFOAM
might float a global green currency for fair trade in organic products. I came up with the eco – people’s
money for an ecological economy.
My speech was published in an ecological magazine as “Organic trade: towards a global green currency”
(Hart 2004). This butterfly’s flapping wings did not start a hurricane. A currency based on food and
organic values could become a staple of the global alternative economy. We eat a lot of food. It is a
symbol of other values. Controlling market abuse of food has economic and health virtue. Trust
between believers could reduce the costs of certification. By supporting the integration of production
and consumption, city and countryside, green currency could offer protection from unknowable market
forces.

I partly drew on existing Japanese practice. Nishibe’s (2006) online LETS community (“Q”) had launched
fair trade in organic bananas from an island between Japan and the Philippines. An action group called
“Power to the people” imported Palestinian olive oil and soaps. Organic rice and coffee were already
exchanged between countryside and city in Japan. Community currencies are furry mammals living in
the cracks left by the dinosaurs -- the states and corporations who wax fat on conventional money. It is
unlikely that they could work at distance in virtual networks without the support of major players.

Closed circuits promoted greater equality and freedom. We would show how to organize them. ==Most of
the world’s inhabitants have little money to spend. Better to make your own money. A new approach to
money was the most direct way to restore democracy to participation in society. These efforts could
only succeed if they harnessed the internet’s potential.== Michael and his partners focused on developing
the software and technology needed. Community currencies should join wider systems. The banks
could do this, but they saw ccs as a threat.

In Michael’s vision, a proper currency service provider would do two things. Users could create their
own systems in the space available. They would also have access to other similar systems. A stand-alone
community currency is like a radio or TV with only one station or a computer with one programme. A
cross-clearing platform could support communication between people trading in different currencies.
This would use its own money domain naming system (MDNS). There would be a hierarchy of national,
regional and local domains. This would be linked to ‘multi-cc’ smart-card systems which could already
carry 15 different currencies. They were off-line, anonymous and designed for use by shops. A kernel of
cross-platform protocols would become open source software. But there was no organization to
establish standards.

The terms “open” and “closed” are paradoxical (Hart 2009). For most ccs the exchange circuit is closed.
But markets using conventional money are open-ended. They can’t stop money draining away to
remote centres of power. ==LETS promotes local production against the import/export pattern of normal
markets.== But community trading circuits might scale up to a much larger online membership. Closed
circuit networks would then adopt an open source approach to community money. Local community
and global network are twin features of Michael's approach. This is the internet’s infancy and his vision
for network society is Keynesian in scope.
