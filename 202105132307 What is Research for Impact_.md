So, what do we mean by doing research differently? 

We talk about Research for Impact, 

but what exactly do we mean by impact? 

Recently, I was in a meeting with researchers, 

and I claimed, that a lot of researchers don't think about impact. 

They mostly rejected my claim and they said, 

"Yes, we always think about impact when we do our research." 

Then I asked, "How so? 

What is impact for you?" 

After some discussion, we came to the conclusion that for a lot of researchers, 

their role ends at the moment of publishing their findings. 

Then they believe that it is the responsibility of others to bring 

that research to decision makers and to try to convince them to use it. 

Many times I've heard this argument, 

and I think that is actually understandable 

because that is how academia has worked for a long time. 

But this approach to research has to change. 

We will not be able to address wicked problems like climate change if we continue to 

work in a way that separates the research from the impact we hope to achieve. 

Researchers have to think about the change they want to see. 

The impact from the start of 

a research project and engage with those people who need the change, 

the people who want to see the change and those who can make it happen. 

If we want to see change as a result of our research, 

we need to do more than just produce journal articles. 

The idea that research should be used to contribute towards positive change is not new. 

It's human nature to try to better understand our surroundings and use what we have 

learned to make changes that will among 

other things change policies, practices, and behaviors. 

In 1944, Kurt Lewin of MIT, 

the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, 

coined the term "action research." 

This was a clear steer that we had to start thinking about 

moving away from the research for research sake, 

towards the realm of using research to address society's problems. 

Since 1944, a number of 

different research approaches have found their way into the literature, 

including action research, applied research, multidisciplinary research, 

interdisciplinary research, and transdisciplinary research. 

Let's look at the latter. Transdisciplinary research approaches 

involve co-production processes between researchers, 

practitioners, and other societal actors, 

with the explicit intention of generating 

research impact or positive change in the world. 

In 2009, Fred Carden seminal work acknowledged to policy, 

outline the importance of research, evidence, and knowledge, 

and how they can in many ways contribute to informing policies. 

So, where does Research for Impact fit into this landscape? 

Well, Research for Impact has evolved from the transdisciplinary literature and 

years of experience carrying out research in different contexts all over the world. 

However, despite the imperative of Research for Impact having been around for some time, 

current research approaches have not progressed enough to truly 

focus academic research activity on impact. 

Many people today believe we're still doing 

research on people and not enough research with people. 

So, in developing the Research for Impact approach, 

we have aimed to develop a holistic approach with a suite of activities or 

strategies that directly link research processes to impact. 

In theory, this is simple. 

But in adaptation and development, 

we're dealing with complex systems. 

So, there's no one-size-fits-all approach or recipe leading to research impact. 

The approach aims to "dance with these systems" by building in 

flexibility and responsiveness to support the research processes. 

We will get into further details on how this all 

happens when we look at the various elements of Research for Impact later in this module, 

and then dive deeper into each element throughout the course. 

But for now, let's spend some time thinking about how we would 

traditionally think of the relationship between research and impact. 

Traditionally, we would think about academics doing research and 

practitioners disseminating findings or achieving uptake with evidence. 

But research and Research for Impact are two sides of the same coin. 

It's about doing research differently and 

changing the way we think about the researcher's role. 

A central premise of this approach is that researchers and 

academics recognize their role as agents of change. 

So, we're promoting a new way of thinking about 

academic research in relation to influencing for change. 

While this way of thinking is about our role as researchers, 

agents of change and influencers in our own right, 

it is also about creating new opportunities for the co-creation of knowledge. 

It's about how we involve communities in generating evidence, 

to produce meaningful research that facilitates collective action. 

In short, it is about process over outputs. 

It's also important to recognize that in this age of 

global environmental change with climate change as a key driver, 

the time for impartiality has passed. 

Evidence must be credible, relevant, and contextualized, 

so we can build our arguments and put forth a strong case. 

There's no doubt communication and framing our research is important, 

but we should be aiming higher, 

aiming for individual research projects or institutional programs that actively 

pursue positive change and create opportunities for change while the work is being done. 

With this in mind, we can frame Research for Impact 

as being about challenging assumptions, 

about being innovative, transdisciplinary, and inclusive. 

So, how do we define Research for Impact? 

Well, it's about maximizing the opportunities for 

research processes to contribute to changes in policy, practice, and behavior. 

This approach to research goes beyond seeing 

conventional research uptake as a measure of success. 

It is about engaging, communicating, 

and influencing, and integrating these activities throughout the research processes. 

The approach is comprised of five elements: planning, monitoring, and learning, 

stakeholder engagement and strategic partnership, 

strategic communications, capacity development, and influencing. 

Research for Impact is underpinned by stakeholder engagement. 

We must engage early and often and throughout the research process. 

Given the complexity of climate change and the need to 

understand and identify appropriate responses, 

Research for Impact requires us to work at multiple levels and scales. 

From the individual to communities, 

and all the way up through the local and state governments, 

to the international arena. 

As you'll see, the principles or 

Research for Impact elements of working at these levels and scales are 

just as applicable to an individual researcher as they 

would be to a group of senior researchers collaborating in a large consortium.
