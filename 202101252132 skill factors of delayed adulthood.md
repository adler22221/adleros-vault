Given both long-term and recessionary changes in the economy, contemporary young adults face a transition to adulthood that is distinct from that of previous eras. It now takes longer for youth to learn useful skills, and there is a [[delayed adulthood|delayed transition to adulthood]] for many young people [^1] (Côté and Bynner 2008; Furstenberg et al. 2004; Shanahan 2000). In this context, many young people do not subjectively feel like an adult even though they have achieved some of the markers associated with an adult status (Arnett 2000; [[Aronson, Pamela|Aronson]] 2008a). In fact, the criterion for becoming an adult is more individually defined and there is often ambiguity, confusion and ambivalence about moving into adult roles (Hartmann and Swartz 2007). Arnett (2000, 469) calls young adulthood, especially the period between age 18 and 25, ‘emerging adulthood,' because it represents a period of ‘independent role exploration,' including career exploration. However, others view this life course period in a less sanguine way because today, ‘growing up is harder to do' than it was in the past (Furstenberg et al. 2004).

One area of significant change in the transition to adulthood has been the transition to work. Increased global competition and structural changes in the labor market has made work less stable for all workers (Kalleberg 2011), but this is especially the case for those newly entering the labor force, who face greater insecurity than their predecessors (Côté and Bynner 2008; Heinz 2004; Koen, Klehe, and Van Vianen 2012). Compared to other countries, the USA has ==few institutionalized school-to-work linkages==, resulting in an emphasis on ==self-constructed labor market pathways that take a long time to be established== (Heinz 2004; Kerckhoff 2004; Vuolo, Mortimer, and Staff 2013). The lack of ‘[[talent mismatch|occupational relevance]]' of most US college programs [^2] means that young adults are ‘completely on their own' in the transition to work, as they make ==frequent job changes== and ==both employers and workers are often dissatisfied with first job matches== [^3] (Kerckhoff 2004, 257, 264). Previous studies have found, in fact, that some young adults experience a crisis when they graduate from college ([[Aronson, Pamela|Aronson]] 2008a) and many young people ‘flounder' during the transition to work, moving from one job to another (Mortimer 2003).
