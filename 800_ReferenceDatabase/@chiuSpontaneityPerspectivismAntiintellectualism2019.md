# 4 Factual Knowledge and Zhuangzi’s Perspectivism

## p.407

I am saying that an epistemic theory that shows less commitment to either side (rationalism or empiricism) is better than an epistemic theory strongly committed to one side, if only for theoretical virtues. Why does anti-intellectualism show less commitment to either side? This is because either (i) the idea of ability itself does not decide whether abilities are acquired through experience, nor whether they are ever-changing; or (ii) mild anti-intellectualism can accept that propositional knowledge is privileged in some epistemic contexts, but in other contexts other forms of knowledge can prevail with empiricist (or other epistemological doctrine) flavor. That is, it can allow knowledge to take different forms. As Zhuangzi says, “A way becomes completed through our walking upon it, a thing is so because people say it is so” (4/2/33), and the sage can “walk two roads” (liang xing 兩行) (5/2/40). This can symbolize that there are various ways of attaining knowledge.
