title: An Empirical Argument for Mencius’ Theory of Human Nature
authors: Ilari Mäkelä
year: 2022
abstract: Mencius 孟子 is famous for arguing that human nature is good (xingshan 性善). In this article, I offer a reading of Mencius’ argument which can be evaluated in terms of empirical psychology. In this reading, Mencius’ argument begins with three claims: (1) humans naturally have prosocial inclinations, (2) prosocial inclinations can be cultivated into mature forms of virtue, and (3) the growth of prosocial inclinations is more natural than the growth of their alternatives. I also argue that each of these claims is well supported by empirical psychology. The relevant studies demonstrate, for example, that humans’ prosocial inclinations are not merely products of social conditioning or egoistic concerns; that prosocial inclinations can be cultivated by environmental factors and personal effort; that humans—even preverbal infants—have a natural inclination to prefer prosociality over its alternatives; and that growth in prosociality is positively associated with human health. Finally, I suggest we interpret Mencius’ expression “human nature is good” as a rhetorical tool to capture the totality of such empirically minded claims.

...First, ==helping behavior is not unique to some specifically “civilized” human societies. It is not only observed across human societies but occurs in nonhuman primates, too.== As an example, Frans de Waal reports on a group of young female chimpanzees that consistently offer assistance to an old group member, suffering from arthritis, by acts such as carrying water into her mouth (de Waal 2019:160–161). Importantly, the frail beneficiary has no way of ever paying back these favors. This suggests that even chimpanzees have an inclination to help others in need without expecting returns. As Mencius suggested, there is evidence that this behavior is linked to neurochemistry of sympathy (Wittig et al. 2014). In other words, it seems that when we rush to help the child falling into a well, we follow an 

p.240

inclination in our primate nature, not merely a habit drilled into us by social conditioning.

Another strand of evidence in favor of a Mencian view comes from the ==onset of helping behavior in human childhood. Even toddlers at the age of 12 months, hardly able to speak or walk steadily, typically try to help an adult in need.== For example, they eagerly hand over items that the adult drops to the floor (for a review, see Tomasello, Dweck, Silk, Skyrms, and Spelke 2009). Importantly, further studies have confirmed that ==such behavior occurs across the world.== The neo-Xunzian argument rests on an intuition that cultures are dramatically different from each other. In this instance, such an intuition is demonstrably false: there is little variation between the helping tendency of toddlers in a German city, an Indian farming village, a Kenyan gerontocracy, or a Namibian egalitarian community (Kärtner, Keller, and Chaudhary 2010;Callaghan et al. 2011). In line with Mencius, these trajectories do start to diverge after the first years, suggesting the possibility for some environments fostering prosocial growth more than others (see Claim II in this article), but the similarity of our starting points is evidence for a Mencian view.

A final piece of evidence in support of Mencius comes from studies on how external rewards affect children’s helpfulness. The neo-Xunzian counterargument relies on the assumption that helping behavior is drilled and welded onto humans by society, and later, by themselves. Yet young children’s helpfulness is not increased by parental enforcement, such as the mother asking them to help. In a now-classic study, Felix Warneken and Michael Tomasello showed that offering praise or toys as rewards for helping decreased toddlers’ helping behavior in the long run (Warneken and Tomasello 2008). This surprising effect can be explained by the so-called “overjustification effect,” according to which intrinsically-motivated tasks are hindered by external rewards (Lepper, Greene, and Nisbett 1973). These findings give perhaps the most direct support for a Mencian view, where prosocial inclinations grow spontaneously and are “not infused into us from without” (Mencius 6A6). Indeed, ==using toys to increase toddlers’ natural motivation to help brings to mind Mencius’ famous allegory of the farmer who helps his sprouts by pulling them upwards (2A2).==

p.241

...Mencius never discusses the controversial concepts of “egoism” and “altruism” as such. He simply distinguishes between two kinds of motivational stances: one where the goal is to seek profit for oneself, and one where the goal is to act in ways tuned to the needs of others. Relatedly, Mencius does not suggest that virtue does not bring profit to the self. As ZHU Xi 朱熹 (1130–1200) explains in his commentary to the opening passage of the Mencius:

> If one submits to the Heavenly pattern [i.e., to the goodness in our nature] one will not seek profit, but one will naturally never fail to profit.[^1] [^2] If one submits to human desires, then one will never obtain profit though one seeks it, and harm will follow upon it. (Van Norden 2009:93)

p.243

Moreover, Mencius repeatedly uses analogies between growth of virtue and growth of our limbs. However, as Bryan Van Norden has pointed out, “Surely, Mencius knows that some people—either through genetic defect or hostile environment—do not have four limbs” (Van Norden 2007: 222).17 So even in his most naturalistic analogy, Mencius implicitly accepts that human nature accepts exception, even inborn ones. Indeed, ==Mencius does not seem to argue for a strongly uniform human nature, identical across individuals by an ontological necessity.[^3] Instead, he promotes a pragmatist vision== by quoting a certain Master Long (Longzi 龍子) who says: “If someone makes shoes without knowing the size of a person’s feet, I know that he will not make baskets” (Mencius 6A7). Mencius’ argument seems to be that although the size and shape of human feet vary, there is some loose generality, and it is helpful to be aware of this generality. When we read this in conjunction with Van Norden’s point,we arrive at the following suggestion: for Mencius, human nature is polemically universal but factually generic.18 Being generic, Mencius aims at giving a rough sketch of what those interested in ethics should know about the typical human composition. If this sketch applies to the overwhelming majority of cases, then this sketch should be regarded as successful. Admittedly, some thinkers might feel uncomfortable about a “generic” discourse on human nature, which admits exceptions. I suggest that anyone with this

p.244

concern could rephrase the argument as “in an overwhelming majority of cases, humans are naturally good.” A similar clause can be added to each premise.

p.245

A perhaps more promising strategy is to read “human nature” as a dynamic term. In this reading, “human nature is good” does not mean that humans are born with a fixed ontological nature[^4] and this nature is good. Instead, it suggests that goodness is associated with the most natural trajectory for a growing and developing human organism. Indeed, recent scholarship both in China and abroad has argued that for Mencius, the “nature” (xing) of something is not what it starts off as (Graham 1967, Liang 2009). Instead, the “nature” is that which one develops into if one grows in a fertile trajectory. In this regard, barley serves as the key metaphor. 

> Now, let barley be sown and covered with earth; the ground being the same, and the time of planting also the same, it grows rapidly, and in due course of time, it all ripens. Though there may be differences in the yield, this is because the fertility of the soil, the nourishment of the rain and the dew, and the human effort invested are not the same. (Mencius 6A7)


p.254

...Mencius’ view is hardly identical to Rousseau’s. For Mencius, humans are not good when society leaves us untarnished. Rather, humans are good when our family and society offers a healthy and affectionate environment while inspiring us to exert effort into our prosocial growth.[^5]33 On the other side of the matter, Mencius is somewhat immune to many contemporary complaints against discourses on the goodness of human nature. For example, the psychologist Scott Barry Kaufman and his colleagues study “light” and “dark” tendencies in humans, worrying that “too much focus on one aspect of human nature at the expense of the other misrepresents the full capacities of humanity” (Kaufman et al. 2019). Paul Zimbardo, famous for his Stanford Prison Experiment, gives a crisper expression: “Some people say that humans are born good or born bad. I think this is nonsense” (“Philip Zimbardo” 2020). 

Mencius might appear as a classical example of such nonsense. However, Mencius does not say that humans are born good. Like Zimbardo, he repeatedly discusses the role of environment in shaping human behavior. Nor does Mencius deny that humans have both dark and light potentials, as Kaufman and colleagues emphasize. However, Mencius perceptibly observes a certain ==asymmetry in human development: although humans can cultivate virtue or vice, there is a certain sense in which both our bodies and our reflective tendencies naturally favor virtue. Such an asymmetry is in no way necessary apriori, but the evidence reviewed here speaks for its empirical plausibility.[^6]== According to the current argument, it is such a peculiarity in humans that Mencius grasps at with his expression xingshan 性善,or“human nature is good.”

Admittedly, there is a risk that focusing on the theoretical goodness of human nature might lead us to neglect how often humans leave this natural path. With this in mind, we might indeed question if the translated statement “human nature is good” best captures what Mencius meant. According to this discussion, a more fruitful translation could be ==“human’s natural development leans toward virtue.”== Slogans aside, however, Mencius’ observations are of significant merit. Indeed, it seems difficult to deny the impression that Mencius’ theory is great in relevance, high in plausibility, and elevating in its justified faith in our shared humanity.
