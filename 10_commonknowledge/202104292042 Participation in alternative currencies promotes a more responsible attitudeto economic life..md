About what: 

Alternative currencies

All my life money has been an obsession. I would rather understand it than have a lot of it. For a 1980s
public lecture (Hart 1986), I hit on money as the topic. I brought much personal experience to this. None
showed in my presentation. ==One side of a coin (“heads”) reveals money as part of political society, the
other (“tails”) its value in exchange. Both states and markets are indispensable to money.== National
economies suffer from ruinous swings between monetary theories stressing one to the exclusion of the
other.

I mentioned there the recent invention of Local Exchange Trading Systems (LETS) in British Columbia.
Community currencies trade their own money in closed circuits. Firms issue complementary currencies
to tie customers. These include air miles, loyalty cards and supermarket coupons. Community currencies
may be accepted in part payment by listed firms. Both add to purchasing power at little or no cost
(Blanc 2011). Common trust is objectified in everyday transactions. LETS members issue money
whenever they receive a service without payment. ==Participation promotes a more responsible attitude
to economic life. People learn to manage credit and debt in new ways (Hart 2006).==

I met Michael Linton, LETS’ founder, in a Manchester pub. I thought of community currencies as standalone alternative trading circuits in full flight from the capitalist economy. They were harassed by
government agencies. But they embodied the spirit of utopian socialism in small communities. Michael
sketched a different vision of LETS’ potential. He saw it linking urban individuals to exchange circuits
reflecting their plural interests. These would be integrated with normal commerce and tax regimes. IT
would bring the plastic revolution to LETS. We could make our own money and markets inside
capitalism, but on a very different ethical basis.

I left Cambridge for Paris. The first outcome was my book on money (Hart 2000, 2001). ==I argued there
that electronic money shifts power from its producers to its users== (Dodd 2005: 401-406; 2014:306-310).
Michael, his partner Ernie Yacub and I agreed to write a book on LETS together. I wanted to explore
what they called “open money”.3 We worked together 2000-2002 and shared material for a book,
Common Wealth: Building community and economic democracy with Open Money. Nothing came of it;
but I learned a lot.

We launched the Japan Open Money Project sponsored by a magazine linked to Japan’s second largest
advertising agency.4 I saw echoes of the role played by capitalist firms in the early liberal revolutions.
Japan could sustain such a revolution today. I met the New Association Movement (NAM) and
befriended Makoto Nishibe, an inspiring economist working in LETS (2005). I later helped with
translating his book, The Enigma of Money: Gold, banknotes, bitcoin (2016). ==[[柄谷行人|Kojin Karatani]] (2005),
drawing on Kant and Marx in his book, Transcritique: on Kant and Marx, advocates lowkey resistance to
capitalism through LETS (Linton) and consumer boycotts (Gandhi). Unlike strikes and street
demonstrations, these are less vulnerable to violent repression.==
