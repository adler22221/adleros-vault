Research ethics are a set of principles and guidelines for shaping the way any research is designed, conducted and managed. This involves the researchers, practitioners and the people we are working with at every stage of the research process.

## Core principles of research ethics

1.  Aim to **maximise benefit** for individuals and society and **minimise risk and harm**
2.  The **rights** and **dignity** of individuals and groups should be **respected**
3.  Wherever possible, **participation** should be **voluntary** and appropriately **informed**
4.  Research should be conducted with **integrity** and **transparency**
5.  Lines of **responsibility** and **accountability** should be clearly defined
6.  Independence of research should be maintained and where conflicts of interest cannot be avoided they should be made explicit

## When should research ethics be considered?

Ethics need to be considered at every stage of the research process, but it is useful to think about when and why specific considerations should be made.

-   **Designing research** – we need to ensure we reduce risks, increase benefits, protect vulnerable participants, be respectful and culturally-appropriate.
-   **Selecting participants** – the process should aim to benefit participants, and should ensure the process delivers higher benefit than risk to participants.
-   **Gaining consent** – consent must be gained through an Informed and voluntary process, and parents/ guardians’ consent must be secured for children involved. We must be sensitive and careful when gaining consent from vulnerable groups, and no one must be forced to take part.
-   **Conducting research** – research must be carried out by trained researchers and practitioners, we must be contactable and encourage contact, comfortable spaces for participants need to be created, and any serious adverse effects must be reported.
-   **Using findings** - explain how findings will be used, participants can choose how and if they are included, these choices must be recorded, and anonymity must be protected.

## Oxfam Australia ethics checklist example

-   Is the research necessary?
-   Is the research well-planned as a project, and integrated into a programme of practical work?
-   Have you considered the specific ethical issues raised by this project, and how to address them?
-   How will informed consent be obtained from respondents?
-   Are you providing accessible information about your project?
-   What level of confidentiality and anonymity can you offer to participants, and how can they be effectively informed of this?
-   Is there appropriate stakeholder participation in the project?
-   Are you offering appropriate return for assistance?
-   Are respondents able to check your version of the information they have given you?
-   How will you ensure that information is appropriately fed back to those who were researched?
-   What systems are in place to ensure that you learn from your experience?
-   How are respondents to be informed of, or consulted on, the results of the research?
-   Have risks to field researchers been assessed and have ways to minimise them been devised?
