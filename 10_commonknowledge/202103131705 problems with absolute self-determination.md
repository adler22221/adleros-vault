However, because the moral and political mandate and legitimacy of NSM was often
built upon experiential knowledge, the role of subjectivity crept into policy arguments.
In particular, this created the possibility of forms of epistemological privilege dominating discussions. There was, in other words, a new sacralization of subjectivity. Until then, mindindependent criteria of equality and social justice could be applied. With the sacralization of subjectivity, ‘identity politics’ came into being and this altered the mode of argumentation about social progress, democracy and emancipation. ^[See also: [[202103131659 Ontological vandalism]]]

==Now politics was driven by mind-dependent emphases about the experience of oppression, which could not be challenged, persuasively, by those without the epistemological privilege to do so, or mandates from outside were deemed to be weak or dubious (white people on race, sane people on insanity, men on gender oppression etc.).==^[An exclusion of the right to discourse.] Arguably that subjective emphasis was a useful reaction to crude structural definitions and aims about social progress; for example, narrow or reductive demands about property ownership, employment status or income. However, it came at a cost because it set up new criteria of political progress and it created a wider contention beyond the trans debate about Marxism and identity politics.23 One of those criteria was strong subjective individualism and its emergent sense of entitlement: a person should now simply be free to be who they want to be. This political position is articulated well here by Janet Mock in Redefining Realness: 

> Self-definition and self-determination is about the many varied decisions that we make to compose and journey toward ourselves, about the audacity and strength to proclaim, create, and evolve into who we know ourselves to be. It’s okay if your personal definition is in a constant state of flux as you navigate the world. (Mock 2014: 15)

This individualistic and hyper-subjectivist form of politics foregrounds self-assertion at the personal level and, from others, it demands *unquestioning validation*. It is not the universalism, for example, of Gay Liberation (asking to be free citizens on equal terms to everyone else) but instead it has spawned forms of exceptionalism and differentiation.

The two versions of politics (inclusivity and diversity) at times might be reconcilable but at some point they might not.24 From the description of the trans-activist v ‘TERF’ arguments, explained above, it would seem the trans debate reveals an example of incommensurable forms of progressive politics based upon identity. What each side shares is that a special existential state is being claimed, via epistemological privilege. The trans-activists want their subjectively defined
gender status to be the trump card in debates, whereas radical feminists see their
winning card to be that of being real women, within the proven oppressive regime of
patriarchy.

The heat in the trans debate is overwhelmingly about trans women; trans men are
rarely discussed.25 The reason for this could be that it is only the former that cause
offence to feminists. After all, trans men, within the priority of oppression set by radical feminism, are actually still women. Accordingly, they may be habitually shielded from criticism in feminist discourse, even if noted for their folly at times. What predominantly causes offence for feminism is men, in its view, pretending to be women and then claiming a new and unwarranted set of demands arising from that transition, including the right to invade women’s spaces or adopt women only roles.
To demonstrate this point about the relative invisibility of the question of trans men,
look at the controversial pinch points about ‘no platforming’ and the opprobrium emerging for public figures like Greer, Bellos and Murray noted above. These points arise because of the discussion of trans women and the political hares set running by their right to act in the way they want to, in relation to real women. However, there has been some feminist discussion of the maternal dilemmas faced by supportive parents with female children who want to transition to maleness,26 which leads us to the wider topic of the public policy implications of erasing the difference between the intransitive and transitive aspects of reality, when discussing sex and gender.
