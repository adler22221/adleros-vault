According to Mortimer and Zimmer-Gembeck (2007), theories of vocational
development suggest that the optimal approach for career achievement is to develop
career interests, skills, and seek out opportunities that serve as ‘steppingstones' to career goals. Mortimer and Zimmer-Gembeck (2007) found that young adults who ==frequently changed career aspirations==, or ==were unable to articulate them at all==[^1], exhibited lower educational attainment and greater distress in vocational development. Uncertain career aspirations are ultimately associated with lower wage attainment in young adulthood (Staff et al. 2010).
