Critical pedagogy and youth and community work have always been philosophies of
action, and as such have not always paid due attention to their philosophical tensions (Seal and Frost 2014). However, these are not just philosophical concerns —
they impact on our praxis. ==As we have seen, we need to be clear on the nature of
the knowledge we are trying to co-create. We are seeking to have resonance with
others, but we do not want to create dogma. We also do not want purely to
reflect a moment of meaning creation in actors. With critical realism we can try
and create resonance with others and seek unifying knowledge, but recognize the
contingent nature of this.==^[Very inspiring for the considerations about [[台灣共生文化學院]]!] Critical realism means we can invoke human nature as
a motivator for pedagogues as it is a ghost in the machine that means hegemony,
while powerful, is not monolithic. Foucault taught us that power plays out at
many levels, so by definition it can be contested and will eventually start to falter
on some of those levels. This gives us hope for human agency.

Critical realism also points to a method for critical pedagogues. We, and the people
we work with, perform our structural influences intersubjectively, and can re-inscribe
or challenge them together. Archer reminds us that change takes time and our actions and the change that might come from them are not synchronistic. However, there is power in symbolic resistance. It becomes part of a mythology that others take up and carry as expressions of hope. Similarly, as Garvey (2005) recognized, identity politics, be it around race, sexuality, gender and so on, is a necessary stage of creating unity to counter a cultural hegemony to which a people have been subject. ‘Only then, with confidence and pride, can a group of people find unity with other social movements, to see that these categories and identities are ultimately constraining and will change as new understandings arise’ (Seal 2014, 154).
