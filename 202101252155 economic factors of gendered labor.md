Despite persisting gender inequalities in the labor market, it is important to note that
the vast majority of women have similar work trajectories as men (Moen and Roehling
2005), with 70.8% of US mothers with children under 18 and 63.9% of mothers with
preschool children participating in the labor force in 2009 (Bureau of Labor Statistics
2010). In fact, [[globalization]] has eroded the economic standing of the male worker,
resulting in greater reliance on women's employment to meet family needs (Acker 2004). In the current [[recession]], more pronounced layoffs have occurred in traditionally male fields, resulting in even greater reliance on women's income (Rampell 2009). Women now have higher educational attainment than men (while 33% of US women aged 25–29 have obtained at least four years of college, only 26% of men have done so; US Census Bureau 2007) and are more likely than men to take on debt in order to fund their education (Dwyer, Hodson, and McCloud 2013).
