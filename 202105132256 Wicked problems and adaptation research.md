Before we dig into research for impact in more detail, 

we must understand the context of climate change and development research. 

In this type of research, 

we deal with intricate, natural, 

social and political systems that 

interact with each other to generate even more complexity. 

Because climate change is such a complex issue, 

we often refer to it as a wicked problem. 

Many say that it's the world's largest threat 

today and will be even more so in the future. 

Wicked problems are societal problems compounded by complexity. 

They don't have single solutions and different actors with 

different objectives and interests find it hard to agree on what the problem is. 

Never mind on what to do about it. 

Climate change has often been said to be a wicked problem. 

For example, some argue that making use of fossil fuel resources for 

economic development is much more 

important than avoiding long-term climate change impacts, 

while others argue that it's the fossil fuel extraction and use that will set 

development processes back through severe climate change impacts in the future. 

Then even if we're just talking about adaptation to the impacts of climate change, 

there can be disagreements and tensions. 

For example, large-scale irrigation for commercial farming to improve 

national food security maybe at the expense of 

small farmers and other competing interests in a particular location. 

While these wicked problems aren't easy to solve, 

there are things we can do in the research world to 

increase the chance of responses being 

developed that overcome tensions between different needs and perspectives. 

We need to make sure our research works across different dimensions of the problem space, 

and that we work to help those involved, 

understand the multiple issues that are 

present so they can navigate better towards solutions. 

![[Screen Shot 2021-05-13 at 22.56.00.png]]

For example, in our research project ASSAR, 

Adaptation at Scale in Semi-Arid Regions. 

We used scenario planning processes to bring together people from across government, 

civil society and supporting 

non-governmental organizations to understand each other's perspectives and 

needs and then to move towards agreed ways forward that improve 

the livelihoods of local communities in these highly stressed semi-arid areas. 

If we think about how change happens, 

an important point to make here is that we're not talking about 

evidence only influencing policy or practice once it's been generated. 

It's about the overall research processes themselves 

generating the conditions that we need for the uptake of evidence. 

More importantly, the changes in behavior practicing 

policy of those who are critical for moving things forward. 

If the starting point for how to correct change to research is with evidence, 

then we're really missing something important, 

and there's much less chance that our research will actually have an impact. 

We'll have missed opportunities during the process of degeneration of 

the evidence that are useful for informing adaptation policy and practice. 

It's the research process, 

the process of evidence production when informed by or co-generated 

with stakeholders that is just as 

valuable as the eventual evidence that's produced itself. 

![[Screen Shot 2021-05-13 at 22.56.51.png]]

Perhaps it is even more important. 

It's clear that evidence can influence policy processes and lead to 

changes in attitudes and behaviors and even change practice at some levels. 

But it's also clear that the role evidence plays is often minor when 

compared to other factors such as the external political environment, 

pressure from competing priorities and even personal beliefs, values and preferences. 

In fact, one study by the World Bank concluded that public servants make errors or 

misinterpret the data that they have to hand depending on their ideological preferences. 

So, the issue actually goes deeper than not using available evidence. 

The issue is also about whether we actually 

understand and buy into the evidence that we have before us. 

So, there's a real skill involved in doing research that engages with 

key people who have the potential to drive change from different levels in society. 

It requires careful processes and 

continual personal engagement that brings these actors onto 

the research journey without either scaring them off 

or losing their interest because it challenges their ideology. 

I want to move on now to talk about ethics when we're doing Research for Impact. 

Research for Impact generally involves much more participation by 

different actors in the problem area, local communities, 

local governments and non-governmental organizations, 

as well as often people and 

institutions higher up in the system right up to national government. 

This kind of participatory research with people brings with it a range of 

ethical considerations that go beyond standard research ethics. 

First, we need to consider who participates and how. 

Issues like how to give voice to different gender, 

age and other social groups needs to be considered. 

Language and literacy are also important, 

ensuring that participants can express 

themselves in ways that work for them and in languages that they're 

comfortable with will enable 

much fuller participation and then obviously much richer research insights. 

In many rural communities, 

literacy can be low. 

So, using written forms of consent and written surveys can often be alienating and 

also raise suspicion because 

participants don't understand what they're being asked to agree to. 

Then finally in many settings when one's doing this kind of engaged research, 

there are political and social tensions. 

People's safety, lives and jobs may be affected by 

how they participate and what they say in research processes. 

So, for example in some instances, 

it may simply not be appropriate to have multi-actor processes for your research.
