3. Comparison & Contrast
Key arguments of Wolf's [[@wolfMeaningLifeWhy2010]]
- Wolf conflates motivation and practical reason, flattens the concept of meaning, consequentially created a flattened frame for the subsequent discourse on "meaning" in life (in contrast to Kamiya's approach to articulate the nuances and multiple dimensions of ikigai)
- while hybrid, still falls into dualism (in contrast to duality/intersubjectivity)
	- while attempted to create a third category out of an existing dichotomy, she created another dichotomy of "subjective" and "objective" [[@wolfMeaningLifeWhy2010#The Fitting Fulfillment View Defended]] (even the core of "objective" criteria is actually about "nonsubjectiveness," which actually includes the sphere the of "neither subjective nor objective")
- by creating such dichotomy of objectiveness and subjectiveness, she limits herself (as manifests by her criteria) in a "substance-attribute rationale" -- focuses on the "what" (in contrast to process-rationale manifests by Kamiya's criteria -- focuses on the "how")
	- while ikigai and ikigai-taisho can differ individually and can only be subjectively experienced, the process, in principle, can be evaluated objectively -- thus intersubjective
- meaning cannot be experienced by immature beings v.s. ikigai is often lost in the process of growing up^[[[孟子]]]


4. Conclusion

While Wolf attempts to break away from the "morality" "self-interest" dichotomy, she still falls into the subjective-objective dualism in her attempt to "include" both without recognizing the "intersubjective" 

Kamiya's argument sheds light to a non-dichotomous and intersubjective approach to meaning

when we only focus on responding to existing research or argument
