The fact that systems are dynamic and in constant change means that our responses and 

interventions into a system must 

recognize the fact that these systems will continue to change. 

This presents a challenge for adaptation and development interventions, 

since there's no one technological solution that will help fix a system. 

When one part of the system is fixed, 

that inevitably changes the rest of the system, 

whether the system will adapt to that change. 

Climate change requires working with people. 

It is not just a physical climactic problem. It's a social problem. 

Well, it has long been understood as a mostly technical problem. 

Increasingly, there's a recognition that understanding 

the social element of the climate change problematic. 

Indeed, finding solutions or Pathways Forward requires 

working with people and for people to change how they do things. 

It requires understanding people's needs, their aspirations. 

It also requires working with those who make decisions at the end of the day. 

We cannot find solutions that exclude these issues, 

solutions that are simply technocratic and are based on climate models alone. 

Think about building a dam in a river. 

The river itself is going to continue to change upstream and downstream of the dam. 

Some parts of the river may dry up, 

while other parts might flood. 

We can have a good idea of what parts of the river may flood based on topographical maps, 

but we couldn't fully understand the longer-term impacts in advance. 

This is particularly true for 

other related systems such as aquatic ecosystems in the river or, 

for example, the fisher communities who 

depend on fish for their sustenance and livelihoods. 

When we change one part of the system, 

there will be knock-on effects and both the 

short and the long term within the wider system. 

If we try to understand and predict exactly how 

these interrelated systems will respond to any individual change, 

we could spend our life investigating and analyzing the interrelated systems. 

So, while we need to understand how the system operates as a whole, 

we don't, and I would say we can't understand exactly how to control the system. 

Perhaps what's more important is, 

as Donella Meadows has said, 

is to learn to dance with the system. 

In recognition of this complexity, 

over the last several years, 

the idea of adaptation being a process or a pathway has become much more prevalent. 

The idea is to identify, negotiate, 

and agree medium to 

longer-term societal objectives with intermediate milestones along the way, 

and build in flexibility to enable course correction 

as new information from research and experience comes to light.

Then, with the information available, 

we can start on making progress to the first milestone, 

while keeping a weather eye on the longer-term objective and new information about 

climate risks and other factors that might need us to think again about the next steps. 

But for this to happen, 

the information coming from these different sources needs to be accessible and usable. 

There's a push from research funders for research to move in this direction. 

Recent years has seen large-scale transdisciplinary initiatives 

pursuing high-quality research and positive changes in practice and policy. 

This brings us to the heart of this course. 

With ASSAR's goal being to ultimately help vulnerable populations in semi-arid 

regions to benefit from effective and sustained adaptation responses, 

we had to rethink what kind of knowledge we produced, 

but also with whom we produced it and how to do it. 

The question was, what is the purpose of our research? 

Ultimately, how can we ensure our research goes beyond 

academia and contributes to changes in the lives of the most vulnerable? 

To do this, Oxfam, 

together with our partners, 

developed an approach to accompany the research processes in ASSAR. 

This approach is called Research for Impact.
