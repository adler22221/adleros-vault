About what: [[research for impact]][[theory of change]]

So, research and Research for Impact 

are two sides of the same coin. 

If we want to research to have an impact, 

we must do research in a way that places change at the core of what 

we want to accomplish and plan for the impact we want to see. 

It's important to keep in mind that each of 

these elements has its own individual strategy and purpose. 

The elements are not prescriptive but rather a menu or a suite of potential activities 

that we can choose from to maximize the opportunities for research to bring about change. 

So, let's look at these five interdependent elements. 

The first element is Planning, Monitoring, and Learning. 

Change doesn't just happen. 

We need to think about planning for change, 

and one of the tools we use for this planning process is the Theory of Change. 

A Theory of Change helps us to identify the change that we want to see and then 

plan how to get there by sequencing and connecting our strategies and activities. 

But planning is just the start. 

Once we have planned, 

we need to ensure that we stay on the right track. 

Monitoring our work and learning as we go will help us 

stay on course toward the change or changes we want to see. 

We will learn more about this next module. 

Element two, Stakeholder Engagement and Strategic Partnerships. 

Stakeholder engagement, engaging early and often, 

is at the heart of research for impact and underpins all other elements. 

It increases legitimacy, relevance, 

and uptake of research. 

It helps us to identify, understand, 

and respond to issues critical to the most vulnerable women, 

men, girls, and boys. 

Alongside stakeholder engagement, 

we'll also look at the benefits of strategic partnerships. 

This goes beyond the traditional kinds of partnerships 

and asks us to consider novel partnerships between researchers, 

practitioners, journalists, decision makers, and more. 

This can be in a large research consortium like 

the new relationships between Oxfam and the University of Cape Town, 

or it can apply to the individual researcher who is 

working on a master's dissertation on integrated water management, 

partnering with a local citizens' group, 

the water authority, or the town council. 

Element three, Strategic Communications. 

Let's dispel the myth once and for all that dissemination is communications. 

There are a range of options and 

channels which are very closely linked to Stakeholder Engagement. 

Producing a journal article or policy paper 

at the end of your research will not bring about change. 

Communications, as with Stakeholder Engagement, 

must be done early and often. 

In research for impact, 

communications is a two-way street. 

Element four, Capacity Development. 

Researchers and research users need the know-how and skills to plan, engage, 

communicate, and use evidence to ensure our work goes 

beyond academia to improve the lives of the most vulnerable. 

Element five, Influencing. 

We need high quality, credible, 

relevant, and contextualized evidence to change understanding and 

behavior around the key adaptation policy and practice. 

But evidence won't deliver change. 

We must use diverse strategies to change policy, 

practice, and behavior at multiple levels and diverse scales. 

We must also remember that these five elements are interdependent and interrelated, 

from planning a pathway to impact with stakeholders and strategic partners, 

working with stakeholders to research and communicate what matters to the right people, 

strengthening science communication skills to 

collectively deliver the right message to the right people at the right time. 

We can focus energies on 

any one particular element of our suite of activities at any time, 

but we can't ignore the others. 

As we dance with the system, 

we should see the elements as our dancing partners. 

Knowledge is power. 

So, how can we leverage our knowledge to use the research processes to create 

new knowledge that informs adaptation practice and policy to change our world? 

There are no simple answers, 

but if you want research that matters, 

you must continue to ask yourself 

this question from the start of the research until the very end.
