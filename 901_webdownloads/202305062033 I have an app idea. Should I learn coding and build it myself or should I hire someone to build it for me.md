# I have an app idea. Should I learn coding and build it myself or should I hire someone to build it for me?
I have been an entrepreneur for 4 years. And, I completely understand the conundrum that you're facing as I've been in a similar position myself. Unfortunately, I didn't get a lot of help during the initial period and I ended up making too many mistakes which ultimately proved costly for my startup.

The reason why I'm against hiring someone right from the start, unless she's your equally passionate co-founder, is because talented developers don't risk their careers unless they can trust the CEO. And, the only way to garner their trust is by building your credibility or your product's credibility.

So, in order to do that, I'd like to recommend two very basic things to start off with:

-   Take up an app development course on any of the e-learning platforms, like Udemy, Udacity, EdX, Coursera, CodeCademy, etc. Depending on the budget that you have, decide which course to purchase. As you've got no prior experience with app development, I'd recommend purchasing a course that will take you from end to end.
-   Purchase and read a book called The Lean Startup by Eric Ries.

The first suggestion is to build up your skills in actual programming. You won't turn into a genius or a prodigy, but you'll have decent knowledge to get things moving. The second suggestion is to develop your product and management skills which are equally important. Again, you won't turn into a wonder, but, you'll have successfully developed some basic product and management skills so that you'd be able to get things done in a smarter manner.

Now, based on these two acquired skills, develop your minimum viable product (MVP), test your assumptions and 'leap of faith' hypotheses, get at least 1,000 users and start generating some 'Ramen' revenues. What these little and seemingly innocuous milestones will do is that they'll validate your idea and give you inital traction. Because of these little things, you'd no longer have just a 'kickass' idea (in your opinion, as is the case with every aspiring entrepreneur), but, you'd rather have a market validation, both, in terms of user acceptance and revenue.

So, if you were to approach prospective employees after accomplishing these milestones, it'll have a far more meaningful impact on their views about you and your company. They'll be more open towards the proposals you may have. Because, when words and data is compared, data always wins.

Sure, you can avoid all this and hire someone instead, but the people who you'd be able to get won't really be as talented as you'd need. And, you'd regret later on.

Hope that helps.
