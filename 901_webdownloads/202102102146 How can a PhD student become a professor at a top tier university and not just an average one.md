I’m a professor at a Tier-II university, but I attended two Tier-I schools - Berkeley and U.W. Madison. There is absolutely no way to guarantee a professorship at a “top tier” university. The factors are too complex to game the system. A lot of stars and planets have to align to get you that kind of gig in higher education. And, frankly, let me just say there are a lot of brilliant professors teaching in Tier-II schools, and a lot of useless deadwood piled up at Tier-I schools. Being witty and cynical at a cocktail party doesn’t make you a good researcher or a good professor. So, what do you really want, prestige or real success? The very question suggests that the status matters more than the substance.

The academic job market is peculiar, and there are so many unpredictable variables involved in a search that it’s very, very difficult to set yourself up for success in a particular interview. In fact, in my experience, hiring at Tier-I schools is likely to be far less predictable, and far less experience-oriented than hiring at Tier-II schools. It often boils down to intricacies of departmental politics, or even who happens to be in the room during your job talk. I once saw a very creditable candidate destroyed by a single, flippant question from the peanut gallery. I’ve also seen completely unsuitable candidates hired just because they were - for various non-academic reasons - the “darlings” of the hiring committees. Or simply because they were doing the sexy research that was in vogue that week. Such considerations play a major role in hiring in the Social Sciences and Humanities, especially at a Tier I institution. Timing can be everything.

A more useful question to ask yourself is: Do you really want a job at a Tier-I institution? If you’re in the Social Sciences and Humanities (or most other disciplines), it’s going to mean researching and publishing a lot, frankly, and not everyone wants that. It’s also going to mean working in large departments with a lot of high-powered people and seriously divisive departmental politics. This sort of thing seems intriguing as a graduate student, but if that’s your permanent world, it can be toxic. Can you deal with that? How politically savvy are you?

Nothing I am saying invalidates the other responses here: to get a Tier-I job, you’re probably going to have to be top-shelf material. You’re being hired by professors, and most professors make decisions based on evidence. What is the evidence that you deserve a job at a Tier-I school? Maybe you’ll get lucky, and someone will hire you for purely ideological reasons, or worse… but sooner or later, someone is going to come along and expose you. Then what? If you take a job at a Tier-I, you owe it to your colleagues to be the real deal. To paraphrase Dirty Harry, a job candidate needs to know their limitations. If you’re really a mediocre scholar, don’t take a job at a Tier-I school that could be occupied by someone who would actually advance your field. There are already too many of these human log-jams stuck in the system. They can’t retire soon enough.

Assuming that you are a Ph.D. student, I would advise you to sit down and ask yourself some serious questions, such as: 1) What do I really want in life? 2) Am I serious about having a husband/wife, kids, family life? 3) Do I enjoy research or teaching more? 4) Am I most attracted to academia for the life of the mind, or simply for the implied prestige? 5) Do I expect being a professor to be like being a Ph.D. student? (Hint: it’s not.)

In grad school, we’re all taught to measure success by the yardstick of the Tier-I institution where we were trained. Fact is, that’s nonsense, since hardly anyone gets a Tier-I job. What you really need to do is figure out what you really want in life, and pursue the job most likely to deliver that. A lot of professors at Tier-I institutions have lived utterly miserable personal lives.

Writing without having read the other answers. So I apologize if I am merely repeating what others have already said.

In my view, the most important factor in becoming a professor at a top tier university is to get your PhD at a top tier university. All universities want to hire people with PhDs from institutions at least as good as themselves, and preferably better than themselves. I am sorry but I believe that if you got your PhD at what Tier I schools consider to be a school “not as good as” them, your chances have already diminished. It will be an uphill climb. In this case, you will have to work harder, and have something else that shows your potential. You may have to win a dissertation competition, or publish a (preferably single authored) article in a well-respected (preferably top) journal in your field, or possess some other “redeeming” property, redeeming in the sense that something that will carry you to an equal footing with other candidates from Tier I schools that your target institution considers as peers.

You could, of course, have an advisor who is very well-regarded (notice that I am not saying well-known) in the field and/or has worked at the Tier I institution where you are applying. This could put you in the radar and perhaps (depending on how well your advisor is regarded) even help you land a job talk (everything else being equal). But it is a big if. Notice that your advisor having worked at a Tier I institution could also work against you. He or she is not working there anymore (if she was, then you’d already be getting your PhD at a tier I institution, hence we would go back to the above paragraph). That could mean that he or she was not good enough to get tenured there. Still, if they are well-regarded, and well-connected, and if they left a Tier I institution due to factors unrelated to their work performance there, you have a chance.

Having a dissertation on a “hot” topic could put you on the map, but the quality of your dissertation and your ability to convert it into publications will matter more. Your dissertation could also be on a niche topic where a Tier I institution has a very specific need. If there are few candidates in that particular topic, you could get lucky.

May I also gently remind you that your categorization of universities as top vs. average is quite crude. There are many, many universities that are neither top, nor average, and there are many among those that have an excellent reputation and working conditions.

Finally, all of what I said may be specific to my field (Business Administration). Different fields may work differently in terms of the job market.

Best of luck in your endeavors!

As someone directing a program that has placed many students at high level universities, here are my thoughts and strategies based on what we do with our own students. Let’s work backwards – when you are in your dissertation stage (or in your postdoc, depending on your field), you should already be functioning like an Assistant Professor at your goal type of university. The question then is, what does it take to get there? Here are some thoughts, not necessarily in order.

\* You should have a clearly defined area of expertise that is in a domain that will bear obvious research fruit for a long time to come, and you should be able to articulate that domain and your trajectory/plan within that domain clearly to anyone from people in your field to your grandmother.

\* You should have a research identity that is becoming distinct from that of your academic mentors.

\* You should have several papers published in reputable journals, as well as presentations at leading conferences. Your presentations should have a high conversion rate to publications, so you look efficient in your research pipeline.

\* You should have (well mentored) teaching experience, with good ratings. You should be able/willing to teach a variety of courses upon graduation.

\* You should have service, preferably at a national level – conferences often have roles for graduate students in organizing activities or on committees. You can also co-review manuscripts with your advisor for leading journals.

\* You should try to work with younger graduate students (e.g., master’s students) in a mentoring capacity, with your advisor overseeing, so you have experience as a mentor.

\* You should connect with people of prominence in your field at conferences; your advisor should facilitate these connections, getting you on their radar early on.

\* You should look at the CVs of individuals recently hired at institutions of the caliber you are interested in, to get a feel for expectations and what it takes to be competitive.

\* Don’t be too quick to graduate. It takes time to build a CV that is competitive at the highest levels.

\* Try to take course projects for your classes and turn them into presentations and papers.

\* Identify scholars whom you wish to emulate and read their work over and over. Get a feel for what good writing looks like, and how it differs across journals.

You cannot control availability of academic positions, and you might have to take a position at a lower tier institution to start. You might like it, or you might not. But don’t underestimate those places. They have much to offer in the way of training, learning what it’s like to be a faculty member, and outstanding colleagues who just have chosen different (but still valuable) career goals for reasons that may be professional and/or personal. If those institutions have lower research expectations, you will have to work extra hard to keep up your productivity amidst what might be extra teaching and institutional service, and limited mentoring. But if you can, and you decide that you wish to move to a top tier position when one opens up, you will have tremendously valuable experience that should contribute to making you an attractive candidate.

Good luck!

I would say anyone going into a PhD with the expectation of getting an academic job at any tier school is being Naive. But, particularly at a top-tier university.

There are three factors that make this true: 1) Supply, 2) demand, and 3) timing. First, there are many more PhD’s each than there are academic positions. If you want to think about it another way, the number of academics is close to constant, but each professor will graduate multiple PhD’s, particularly at a T-1 university. If everyone wants an academic job, there were always be an oversupply.

On demand, the number of college students is not increasing. It did for a while, mostly when we (USA) went to a society where most went to college. Now, with the majority in college, there can not be the growth in academic positions.

Finally timing: the T-1 jobs are small enough numbers that they basically happen at random. A few per year. If you graduate in a bad year / years, you will not get an academic position.

I graduated with my doctorate in 1994–1996 (finishing was a slow process because I was working full time; I could have finished quicker if I had a job that required me to). In that period, from memory, there were about 4 tenure track positions advertised in my field, and there were about 50 people who finished the PhDs. So, 4 would be happy, 46 have to find other work or go down the postdoc run (what most people did).

Since it is a quantitative field, I could understand the numbers, and decided not to try the academic route. Instead, I went to work for a company that does Gov’t funded R&D. After 26 years, the work is fun and I am paid well. No complaints about my career choice.
