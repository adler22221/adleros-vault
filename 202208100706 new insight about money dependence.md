Money helps making things happen. However, whether if there indeed is the need for such artificial happenings is an (almost always taken for granted) question. 

As humans become separated from nature and deprived from their nature/natural right to make a living through their symbiotic interplay with nature, instead born into the inevitable dependence on money, they lose the option to "do nothing other than sustaining their survival through their symbiotic interplay with nature" because nature becomes "owned properties (Whether its owned by private or the public)." They are only left with the option to sustain their lives through monetary transactions, thus have no choice but to produce/make things happen in order to get money. Thereby "production" becomes the imperative and real needs subordinate. In fact, since there may not be so many needs at all, people hence have no choice but to artificially induce others' dependence on their production.

So the problem might not lie in money itself, but the elimination of the right to a self-sustaining lifestyle without relying on money and the inevitability of production that follows. 

Despite that production and transactions driven by the survival dependence on money generates a world of "abundance" of artificial choices, most people do mundane work just to "make a living." We might suppose that living an ecological lifestyle primitive and mundane. But is the typical job people do really more delighting? For sure it doesn't increase our physical and mental wellbeing, as the lack thereof is crucial for maintaining the dependence on others' services.
