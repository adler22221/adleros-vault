Archer sees agency and structure as distinct entities, not to deny that there is an
interaction, but it is in separating them that we allow for their interaction to be
understood. Archer adds a temporal distinction to this, saying that ==current social
and cultural structures are a result of past social interaction between agents,
which condition the current context within which social agents operate.==^[This is also the same as the premise framework I raised when conducting also of my researches, such as [[Allocation Dependence 配分依存]], [[德中學 Learning by Caring]]!] In this respect she certainly says that social conditioning comes first. However, how these agents then react to their current conditioning will change, in time, the social and cultural structures within which future social actors function. Being distinct, and operating in different temporal spheres, makes it possible to unpick structure and agency analytically. First, we need to isolate and analyse how structural and/or cultural factors provide a context of action for agents. It is then possible to investigate how those factors shape the subsequent interactions of agents and how those interactions in turn reproduce or transform the initial context. Through doing so, argues Archer, it is possible to give empirical accounts of how structural and agential phenomena interlink over time rather than merely stating their theoretical interdependence.

This idea of a time delay has resonance in terms of the aforementioned structural
pessimism of many workers. In a forthcoming book that builds on research a colleague and I conducted into youth violence (Seal and Harris 2016), we note that
many workers were engendering in young people a ‘knowing hopelessness’, an
‘informed sense of powerlessness, not one borne of ignorance of their potential or
chances, but one that stemmed from knowledge of the limited nature of them’
(Seal and Harris 2013, 52). Young people were lacking political agency, hopeless
about what they could actually do in the face of the structural forces to which
they were subject. This seemed to stem from, and be reinforced by, workers’ own
structural pessimism, often based on bitter experience, made bitter through a
naïve wish to impact on the now.

With nowhere to go, young people were turning this hopelessness in on themselves and their communities, manifesting in depression, destruction of their own
environment or communities, lapsing into conspiracy theories, or finding an alternative economic route, often linked to criminality. In the book we make a call for
workers to shift their thinking about social and structural change^[This is also in alignment with [[德中學 Learning by Caring]], although not as explicit. While the modes of coexistence in the past lead to the emergence of the structural factors shaping the modes of coexistence today, education, as the process of formulating human's modes of relationships with everything, has the great potential of shaping the future modes of coexistence.], to not lapse into pessimism or believe we have failed unless we ‘achieve’ structural change. This is not a call to give up trying to ‘effect’ real change; it is a call to value the small achievements as well, recognizing that there will be a time lapse between our actions and social change, as Archer describes. The acts of resistance, even the symbolic ones (Hall and Jefferson 1974; Haenfler 2006), should be celebrated, even if they do not lead to an immediate change in our social relations or our immediate material conditions. Otherwise we encourage the aforementioned sense  of hopelessness in young people. Perhaps worse, unless we reconstruct their ideas about achievement, they will just go for what is achievable.

This pedagogical process, to the degree that a critical pedagogue has influence, is
visceral and in the moment. I have written about this previously, examining how this
pedagogy emphasizes encounter and recognition (Benjamin 1998), combined with
elements of hooks’s (1994) engaged pedagogue. It is in its nature intersubjective,
drawing on individual experience, but epistemologically ‘performed’ within a
more collective context with a focus on exploring the intersubjective, dialogical
and dialectic processes at work. Archer calls this process of change a morphogenetic
sequence, and her description of it has resonance with this. First, we have structural
conditioning. Critical realists conceive of social structure as ‘systems of human
relations among social positions’,^[This is also very similar to my view of modes of coexistence.] as opposed to Giddens’s view that there are
internal rules and resources, that the youth and community worker draws on and
instigates through their practice (Porpora 1998, 339).

Wheelahan (2007) describes how social roles, within a pedagogic context, have
their own causal ‘powers’ and actors invest in the respective roles (Archer 1995,
2000). For example, capacities and powers inhere in the role of the youth and community worker and a young person, or in the relationship between the lecturer in
youth and community work and the student. These relationships derive from the
role each agent plays, which in turn have been pre-structured by previous agential
action, but are active in the moment during the second phase of socio-cultural interaction. In our case this is how the relationships develop, and are performed, enactedand embodied by the lecturer and the student, or the youth and community worker and the young people and communities they work with.

These roles have associated, assumed powers and boundaries that we can adhere
to, deconstruct or play with in the interaction stage. Archer talks about the ‘degrees
of freedom’ for which social structures set the parameters in terms of agency. The
outcome of the exercise of free will within the ‘degrees of freedom’ constituted by
social structure has unplanned and unintended consequences (Archer 1995). Crucially, as Wheelahan (2007, 185) notes, these roles and relationships can be
‘enacted by the occupants of roles in creative and innovative ways (leading to
social change) or enacted in ways similar to those who have gone before (leading
to social reproduction)’. This then leads onto the third stage where there is either
structural change (morphogenesis) or structural re-enforcement (morphostasis).
