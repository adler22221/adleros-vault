My preliminary thinking is that something like "Mindful Commitment (we should definitely still play around with it and not take this as definite)" might be more suitable than "Judgemental Rationality" as the third pillar of CR. 

- Being "Mindful" can be inclusive of both the rational and the beyond-rational, also the ontological, epistemological, and axiological aspects of our activities/states/dynamics of being/becoming. (The shortcoming of "mindful," however, is the connotation of its "mind-focusedness," excluding the body and beyond -- But I guess it's hard to find a better word in English?) 
- On the other hand, I believe "Commitment" better pictures the embodied, inevitably subjective nature of knowledge formation, how it is non-dual with our actions, the responsibility entailed in it, and the fluidity/non-duality of knowledge (i.e., "X and Y may both be true given circumstances ABC, but I'm committed to Y given DEF..."), in contrast to the perspective(spectator theory of knowledge)-based, mind-focused, and clear-cut/dualistic (i.e., "When we compare X and Y, Y is a better account of reality because ABC...") connotation of judgment.

I have been pondering what resources do I use for formulating my argument. 

- Of course, there are already plentiful resources on the problems of rationality in the Frankfurt School and postmodernist works, but I'm afraid that including those will go beyond the scope of a paper, and those aren't as central if our focus this time is to incorporate non-Western thought into the dialogue. 
- In terms of non-Western thought, I'm more familiar with Confucianism, Taoism, (Mahayana-Chinese) Buddhism, and the theoretical underpinnings of Traditional Chinese Medicine. 

- To include all of these four may be more comprehensive, but I'm afraid a proper, systematic treatment of all four will also go beyond the scope of one paper.
- While Confucianism also has wonderful works concerning knowledge, especially the rich debates between the [Cheng-Zhu and Lu-Wang schools in Neo-Confucianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neo-Confucianism#Two-school_model_vs._three-school_model), it might be too complicated for readers unfamiliar with East Asian philosophy, therefore might not be suitable for an invitational paper to incorporate non-Western thought into CR. 
- Similar to the complexity of Confucianism, Buddhism is also extremely complex for the many schools it has and how different cultures and languages represent its philosophy differently. For example, [Buddhism in the Ancient Chinese text that I study is quite different from the modern English versions of Buddhism studies based on Sanskrit](https://www.oxfordbibliographies.com/display/document/obo-9780195393521/obo-9780195393521-0049.xml). 
- The theoretical works of Traditional Chinese Medicine have less discrepancies, but I think that bringing this into the academic discourse of CR constructively will require both proficiency in TCM and modern medicine, which I lack. 
- Compared to the three streams above, I'm now inclined to focus on the philosophies of Classical Taoist [Laozi](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/laozi/) (or Lao-tzu/Lao-tze) and [Zhuangzi (or Chuang-tsu/Chuang-tze)](https://iep.utm.edu/zhuangzi-chuang-tzu-chinese-philosopher/). As mentioned at the conference, their popular and accessible texts (even in the West) have clear CR orientation but certainly have more to add to CR, and a good part of that is on the problems of judgment and rationality.
