Dear Ito-san (cc'ing Daum),

It was such a pleasure to finally meet you at the da0 online meetup last Monday! 

My name is Adler Yang, I'm the guy who spoke Japanese and into transdiciplinary research and experimental higher education. 

I'm writing this email to follow up on our previous conversation, explore potential ways to find synergy among our endeavors and the possibility to meetup in Japan in November or next June. 

My life since 2009 has been motivated by two central concerns, which I find highly relevant to your problematics as you stated in your dissertation:  
1. What are the fundamental common mechanisms, structures, or relationships that continue to perpetuate humanity’s systemic self-harm/planetary harms? 
2. How might the alleviation or transformation of the fundamental common mechanisms above be possible, especially in ways that any person can set off systemic changes without waiting for others or the structure to change (=“be the change”)?

After 14 years of trials and errors, exploring roles in filmmaking, journalism, entrepreneurship, broadcasting, teaching, I eventually find myself emerging as a systems researcher and innovator, investigating across disciplinary boundaries and developing theories (about the common mechanisms underlying wicked problems), change models (to intervene the mechanisms), and "education as social change/social change as education" praxes (i.e., the actual transformative experience). You can take a look at the main issues/concepts I focus on, their interrelationships, and related work at this "Research-Praxes Map" (The rationale linking my transdiciplinary research and my work on experimental higher education is also presented here): https://www.plectica.com/maps/0TY58SS75

Among the issues/concepts, one of the more developed theory is what I call Allocation Dependence. It is a mechanism that shows how agent's actual or perceived dependence on resource allocation (as a rule underpinnning a complex adaptive system) shifts the trajectory of agents from holistic growth to mold-fitting and the dynamics of social systems from resource regeneration to resource exhaustion, eventually leading to the emergence of wicked problems across domains, such as alienation, waste, pollution, inequality, and fragility.

You can get an overview by taking a look at this working paper, which was highly praised by one reviewer 1, but rejected by reviewer 2 due to insufficient ground for such a broad claim:
[(PDF) 分割された文明難局を複雑適応系のアプローチで繋げる：〈自己家畜化論〉と〈自己完結社会論〉を踏まえた〈配分依存論〉の試み (Connecting the Compartmentalized Civilizational Predicaments: A Complex Adaptive Systems-Based Approach Through the Allocation Dependence Theory) (researchgate.net)](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/373444852_fengesaretawenmingnanjuwofuzashiyingxinoapurochidejigeruzijijiachuhualuntozijiwanjieshehuilunwotamaetapeifenyicunlunnoshimi_Connecting_the_Compartmentalized_Civilizational_Predicaments_A_Complex_Adapt)

Or this 15-min presentation in English at the International Association for Critical Realism (IACR) Conference: [Adler Yang: Seeing "Allocation Dependence" Underlying Diplomaism -- A Critical Realist Approach - YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AhD_W8fSFpo)

My short-mid term (approx. 5-year) goal is to publish a book on Allocation Dependence and use it to pursue a Ph.D. by Publication, or "論文博士/博士・乙." I was advised by two veteran scholars at the IACR conference upon my presentation to publish a book instead of series of papers, as a book may be more suitable than papers for conveying systematic, comprehensive ideas like Allocation Dependence. The attempt to pursue a Ph.D. by publication is to experiment (and eventually iterate) such an existing alternative route towards "formal scholarship recognition," which has the potential to be transdisciplinary and self-directed (contra siloed and university/faculty-centered programs), grounded (contra isolated in an ivory tower), and economically accessible (contra the priviledge to not do full-time work).

While I'm aware of and have studied different models of alternative higher educational practices, most of them are not eligible for gaining formal academic recognitions. Ph.D. by publication, I believe, may be a stepping stone for re-engaging them in the recognized academia, for eventually bringing greater transformation of it. 

I understand that you also come from a very alternative, trans/anti-disciplinary, and systemic background, and you share the vision to tackle complex systems' wicked problems and transform the academia so it can better fulfill such a mission. As a younger (currently 28) "同志," I'm eager to learn from your experience and advice, and potentially collaborate towards our shared vision in some way. 

I'll be visiting Japan in November for a philosophy meeting in 金沢 on the 11th - 12th, and presenting at the 全国唯物論研究大会 at 立命館大学びわこ・くさつキャンパス on the 18th - 19th. My schedule is still flexible before, between, and after both meetings, and would love to visit you if you're available.
