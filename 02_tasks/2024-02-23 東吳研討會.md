self-awakening 自覚
place 場所

. A "place" is a predicate of a subject- individual, which is called a "general," or "nothingness." The individual is in the "place" of “nothingness”—- this is the "three-dimensional" structure of the individual, but this structure is by no means static; it is nothing but a movement/process of self-limitation and a kind of condensation or of systole of "nothingness."

While Spinoza explained everything in terms of a "self-caused," infinite entity as the "subject" and ignored the sense of individual life, Goethe emphasized the vitality of the actual individual and its ineffable nature. And such an ineffability means that "each thing has in itself the unity by which this thing is real.” In other words, the individual, the individual thing, is self-existent in its own way. 

 The "uniqueness of our self" is precisely "awakeing" as an infinite process, and as I have already suggested, the "nothingness," which is no other than the "place," is inextricably linked to that “self-awakening."

Contrary to Descartes, Spinoza started from the entity or the substance God, but because he ignored "I think" as its "basis of self-awakening,” he could not grasp God as the "basis of self-awakening," and thus God fell into the category of a mere object. God, who should be the most concrete, became the most abstract "caput mortuum." (11/161)

While "nothingness" in Nishida's sense is the contradiction itself that produces what exists, as Hermann Cohen's differential produces existence out of nothingness, Spinoza's God = Substance cannot develop in such a dynamic way because it is only an necessary Existence. Nishida may have thought so, but then, what about the constant circulation between "natura naturans" (naturing nature) and "natura naturata" (natured nature) as described by Spinoza? There, "natura naturans" creates "natura naturata," and "natura naturata" becomes "natura naturans" and so on. This may be a dynamic process similar to what Nishida himself calls the revers from "what is created" to "what creates."

Although Nishida speaks of "aspect" only negatively here ; but as he himself said of Dilthey, "aspect" was indeed an "event" that "takes shape, develops, and disappears," wasn’t?
