Comments from the Editors and Reviewers:  
  
Reviewer #1: Potential significance for the field:  
1. The author(s) has made very interesting points showing the compatibility between CR and Daoism in terms of their ontological realism and epistemic pluralism.  
2. The author(s) has pungently pointed out that epistemic relativism creates many of the social ills of our time, and highlighted the Daoism approaches in addressing it.  
  
Key points that need clarification or revision:  
  
* Does the author see Daoism as a philosophy of (quasi-natural or quasi-social) science?  
* In what ways are the non-anthropocentric, sensical knowledge and knowledge situations in Daoist writings comparable to the anthropocentric and scientific knowledge/theories and situations discussed in CR?  
* A more critical point is whether and how, from the author's point of view, the Daoism scripts deal with the aspective knowledge and social/natural reality distinctively, and in corollary, the issue of fallibility?  
Although Daoism does not employ such social-natural dichotomy, but in your comparisons, the author might have ignored that Baskar and later critical realists are aware of the "double hermeneutics" nature of the social theories, as well as the pre-existing communal belief or convention as a condition of true knowledge.  
E.g. quoting Roy Bhaskar in The Possibility of Naturalism, "the predicates designating properties special to persons all presuppose a social context for their employment. A tribesman implies a tribe, the cashing of a cheque a banking system. Explanation, whether by subsumption under general laws, advertion to motives and rules, or redescription (identification), always involves irreducibly social predicates." (1989, p. 28).  
Moreover, when social agents and their mental worlds are also considered as part of the real, the interpreted experience of the experiencing agent (not the observers) is then often equated by the critical realists to the efficacy of a fact, which then has nothing to do with the rational judgement theses drawn in your argumentation.  
  
Minor point:  
* The real-life scenario introduced at the beginning of the article is not discussed later in the text, why is this illustration absolutely necessary to make your point? Why not using the TEAM and MMW example, which is later discussed in text, to start addressing the problematics?  
Quality of writing:  
* Very likely, the author has missed out one word "win" in line one, the first quote under the sub-section "Pre-judgments as a necessary condition for judgment."  
* The author(s) has used both "I" and "we" to address themselves in several different places.  
  
  
  
Reviewer #2: This is a welcome contribution to critical realist ontology and epistemology, which has been in large part Eurocentric, though there has been a shift in the field. The author introduces the non-East Asian reader into East Asian thought and conceptual history in a helpful manner, both in the main text and in relevant footnotes. Interesting ideas are presented, such as Western written language being representations of spoken language in a way which East Asian written language is not, which can contribute to Western epistemology as interested in representation in a different manner than East Asian philosophy. This being said, my area of expertise is not East Asian philosophy and I am therefore not in a position to assess the presentation of Daoism. I do note that the author has somewhat of a simplistic approach to Western philosophy, and of sometimes equating critical realism to Western philosophy. It is much more nuanced than some of the 'grand narratives' presented in the paper.  
  
The main conceptual issue with the paper is that it is not explained how Daoist wisdom helps us move beyond post-truth or judgemental relativism - or if we must accept post-truth - nor how an 'aspectual' approach as suggested can be used to critique theories from other aspects: If all beliefs and theories are explained by different aspects of reality/dao, how then can the critical realist rationality be critiqued from its aspect? In the paper the author makes a judgement that judgement is not possible. The author refers to this problem in their own work sometimes, and also how East Asian philosophers commented on it, but the author does not move beyond mentioning that these problems exist. Is the argument in the manuscript that current post-truth times are the same as following the Hundred Schools war, and that judgemental relativism is therefore the correct approach - ie. accept post-truth? This seems, to me, to be the authors conclusion. It is certainly not clear how the possibility for judgemental rationality is supported in what the author presents. If post-truth is the position of the author, then it is not made clear why Bhaskar's work on overcoming radical theoretical differences (which is more than fallibility) is insufficient. Daoists (as the author presents it) discuss how different creatures have differing aspects, but there it is not clear reason why this point necessarily means it is rational to support a leap to say that all people have radically different aspects. The possibility of a person to experience some of that of another person is a central part of Bhaskar's argument for the possibility of judgemental rationality. This point is not discussed in the paper.  
  
It further seems that it is not always critical realist philosophical positions that the author is critiquing, as much as it is critiques of possible interpretations of the nomenclature. I found myself several times thinking, 'well this is what Bhaskar is saying', only to find that the author presents a position from Daoism as in opposition to critical realism - which did not make sense to me. For example, the aspectual argument is found in critical realist work on 'positionality'[^1], which goes back to original critical realism - for example in the transformational model of social activity which the author for example refers to. The important difference is that positionality in critical realism does not presuppose that gaining some of the positionality of another person is impossible. Is this what Daoism says? If so, how is this argued?  
A further issue is the reductionism applied to Bhaskar's work on judgemental rationality as a form of 'epistemic Darwinism'. This seems to me a straw man. I agree that there are parts of his work, such as DREIC and RRRE, which can be interpreted as simply 'elimination' of theories, but his work was more nuanced than this. The author has read SRHE, I would suggest they go back to it to better understand what Bhaskar said on his own nuancing of identifying causal theories and 'elimination'. For example, the author mentions Bhaskar's criteria for theory choice, which includes the potential for synthesis, not just elimination. Further, elimination need not always occur, as there will be many cases where there is not sufficient evidence to make such an assertion. Furthermore, even 'identified' causal explanations can later be reverted, as judgemental rationality is not meant as providing final and absolute answers, but the best to date. There is a tension here between an understanding of 'elimination' in the simplistic sense vs the more nuanced approach, and I agree with the author that the simplistic sense is problematic (and it could certainly be argued that it is the simplistic notion which has sometimes been applied by critical realists). However, reducing Bhaskar's work to the simplistic picture is inaccurate.  
  
On page 20 it is argued that 'there is little or no ontic overlap' between TEAM and MWM. If there were no ontic overlap there would not be a theoretical conflict (see SRHE where Bhaskar discusses this in relation to Lymeswold cheese and chess - they are different but do not compete, because they do not have ontic overlap). It is precisely because there is some ontic overlap that the theoretical problem arises.  
  
It is argued that judgemental rationality exacerbates conflict, but I do not think that this necessarily follows. Immanent critique is an important part of judgemental rationality and can be a means by which to seek to genuinely understand the other. The Daoist position (as presented by the author) seems to assume this is impossible to understand the aspect of the author, and it could be argued that it is such a position that more likely exacerbates conflict by assuming understanding the other is never possible.  
  
In short, this is a very important to critical realism as a whole and the special issue in particular, by discussing judgemental rationality in relation to non-Western perspectives! However, it is suggested that the author does not sufficiently understand Bhaskar's arguments for judgemental rationality, and for the article to become the important contribution it can be the author should gain a greater grasp of the Bhaskar's arguments.

[^1]: aspectiveness isn't just about the position and the view available to the knower, but also the knower's existence, including their faculties and content, as a "real" part of the reality
