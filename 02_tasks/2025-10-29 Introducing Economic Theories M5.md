### Neoclassical economics

![[Pasted image 20251029042519.png]]

The Value of the Marginal Product of Labor (VMPL) is an important concept in labor economics that helps businesses determine how much they should pay their workers. It represents the additional revenue generated from hiring one more unit of labor (like an additional worker) while keeping all other inputs constant. Here's a simple breakdown: 
 1. **Marginal Product of Labor (MPL)**: This is the extra output produced by adding one more worker. For example, if hiring one more worker increases pizza production from 100 to 120 pizzas, the MPL is 20 pizzas. 
 2. **Sales Price (P)**: This is the price at which the product (like a pizza) is sold. If each pizza sells for $6, then the revenue generated from the additional output can be calculated. 
 3. **VMPL Calculation**: The VMPL is calculated using the formula: VMPL = MPL times P. 1. So, if the MPL is 20 pizzas and the price per pizza is $6, the VMPL would be: [ VMPL = 20 \times 6 = 120 ] This means that hiring one more worker would generate an additional $120 in revenue for the business.
 In essence, VMPL helps firms decide how many workers to hire. They will continue to hire additional workers as long as the VMPL is greater than or equal to the wage they have to pay. If the wage exceeds the VMPL, it would not be profitable to hire more workers.

# Financial markets

all this money comes into the economy not because the Central Bank hands it out to households, but because households and firms take [[loan]]s from banks. So with every new [[credit]] taken from a bank, the size of the money supply increases and with every loan repayment it decreases. As a consequence, it's the commercial banking sector supplying money to the economy, not a state agency.

The risk premium is added to the interest rate charged for loans. So whereas a car loan to somebody with a high and stable income may have an interest rate of 8%. A car loan for the same car may have an interest rate of 9% for person with a lower or less stable income. This extra 1% is the risk premium that the bank charges to the borrower, in order to cover the higher risk that the client has for the bank of not paying back the loan. Such a loan is called [[subprime]], so the risk is entirely paid for by the borrower.

![[Pasted image 20251029044755.png]]
If a bank is 10% equity capital, it's going to lend it out ten times to make up 100% of the balance sheet. It does so in fact, by issuing IOUs. The bank gives loans and notes them on the asset site. Firms, need to pay them back. On the liabilities side, the bank takes in savings deposits or it borrows on the capital market. If a bank has 100% equity, so no savings deposits and securities, then it can lend out that money only once. Just like you can lend out your savings to a friend only once. 

But if a bank has only 3% equity, and has a leverage ratio of 3%, it can lend out 33 times. So if the bank wants to maximize its profit, it should lower its equity capital and lend out more, so it can earn more money from the interest rate charge on loans. But this is a downside, namely, the bank takes more risk. What if more borrowers than normally is the case, don't pay back their loan?

The buffer of the bank, which is the equity capital is very small with a high leverage ratio. This is precisely the reason why so many banks went bankrupt in 2008, and the next few years. They either closed down or had to be saved by governments with billions of dollars of taxpayer's money.

### deregulation

in the US, the federal government adopted the law that required banks to be, either an investment bank or a retail bank, but not both. That law remains in place from 1933 until 1999. By then, the banks had developed an effective lobby and managed to get rid of a lot of regulation so that they could grow bigger and make more profit. For 66 years, the world has not seen a serious financial crisis. Until further [[deregulation]] made banks so unstable that the steep drop in value of MBSs and other derivatives turned various banks insolvent. This means, the debts were higher than the assets, and they collapsed.

### failed self-regulation

The rating agencies gave top ratings called triple A to MBSs and other derivatives, even though nobody eventually knew anymore what was precisely in each of these. The agencies were paid by the banks making the derivatives. So, the agencies had no incentives to be critical.

### failed informal institutions

One is the widely accepted belief that as long as you spread risk, for example, in an MBS, the effort rate level is very low. And when you trade in such securities, the effort risks on a financial market is also very low. Why was this idea so widely accepted? Because it was lent credibility by professors of finance. Some of them even received Nobel Prizes. The idea is called the [[efficient market hypothesis]], or EMH. And it states that, prices of assets always reflect the real underlying value, because all information about the asset is already incorporated in the price. This information includes risk now and risk in the future. So, the belief was that financial asset values can never be overvalued. Bubbles do not exist. If prices go up, it must be because the assets really become more valuable.
