My current thoughts about submitting my entry to this call for entries is to lead with "[[德中學 Learning by Caring|learning by caring]]." Some of the key concepts I am thinking of arguing include:

1. The Limitation of Existing Frameworks
   While we already have many ideal educational and pedagogical theories and frameworks (in particular, John Dewey’s "Learn by Doing"), they often fail to address resource dependency. Without addressing this dependency through the learning process itself, education remains directed or even hijacked by those who hold the resources.

2. Marketization and Macro-level Constraints
   This phenomenon can be seen as marketization or neoliberalism. At a macro level, it dictates the direction of student development based on resource availability.

3. The Teacher as a "Habitat"
   At a micro level, the teachers themselves can become a ceiling or the "habitat" that students must adapt to in order to thrive in the classroom. Because the student is dependent on the teacher for learning, education, and resources, the scope of this habitat becomes a limiting factor. 

In this environment, students are directed, afforded, and constrained in terms of their development by the boundaries of that habitat.

Since the expectations, motivations, or the aspirations of the act of care is the organizing principle of this learning, it becomes inherently transdisciplinary or anti-disciplinary. There is little point in following existing disciplinary frameworks and structures; rather, everything that is required to achieve aspirations of care needs to be learned on the go and become interconnected.

Of course, this is not new; this is already in the problem-solving or problem-based learning literature. The difference is that often problem-solving or problem-based learning are still "cookbook" like, where the problem can be predetermined or assigned by the teacher instead of arising from the authentic, genuine care of the learner.

Learning by caring is agent-based, where caring as a self-organizing principle is enabled by conditions such as a student's desire to be cared for or their desire to care for others. At a deeper level, this is enabled by the students' psychological well-being.

In this framework, educators and parents, instead of setting the boundaries of the habitat, become the supporting grounds for the students. As the learning is directed by caring, rather than scaffolded toward predetermined parameters, each student is equal in their dignity. They grow and learn equally at their own paces, instead of being judged by a common measure that unequalizes their uniqueness and differences.

By being agent-based, it also means such learning is adaptive. In principle, there are no predetermined requirements of what qualifies as sufficient educational resources. 

The act of care can happen in any circumstances by utilizing whatever is at hand. Such adaptability enables students and teachers to facilitate learning without being dragged behind by whether they have sufficient educational materials or resources, because anything available can be utilized toward the act of care.

And so the equality of their dignity is made possible because the measure of care depends on their own sense of self-actualization or transcendence, instead of a standardized, common measure. 

So, a very rich student can be in the very beginning of his or her learning-by-caring journey in the sense that there is still a gap in his or her result of care, whereas a materially marginalized or so-called materially disadvantaged student can achieve greatly to the extent that he or she feels satisfied with his or her act of care.

Perhaps what is significant here is that AI no longer becomes a way of cheating. This is because learning does not happen by meeting existing standards or responding to assigned assignments, exams, and homework, but by actualizing the caring act.

When learning is not being appraised in terms of its truth or falsity, but in its contribution to value creation, then the replacement of learning by AI is less of a concern. The act of care necessitates human action that, at least until now, still cannot be replaced by AI completely. Whatever the AI generates or responds, it still requires the human actor to implement or execute it:
1. Ensuring it is delivered or responding to another human or another agent of concern (which might be another living being and so on).
2. The caring act itself still requires much adaptation, empathy, understanding, and reflection.
3. The feedback process requires a learner to recalibrate the AI with the real response he or she receives.

Of course, safety measures should be considered. Instead of focusing on an honor code, the question then probably becomes: how do we implement safety measures so that AI responses don't lead to harms toward the other?

Of course, caring is not a single-directional act; it is an intersubjective act. The act of care must not be the fulfillment of predetermined measures, but a constantly evolving, always adapting means of the other. It requires the other to be recognized, to be understood, to be empathized with, and to be treated with dignity. 

This necessitates social cohesion rather than competition towards predetermined measures, and this might be among one of the keys to addressing social polarization.

I think the article, in general, can take an educational, philosophical, and social theoretical approach, plus autoethnography of my personal journey and perhaps some kind of action research based on my existing action. 

Of course, this would include honest reflections on its limitations, with the emphasis not on how brilliant my intervention was, but on the causal power of learning by caring itself. Once the gist of learning by caring is comprehended by the learner, it nearly becomes automatic and doesn't require my further intervention.

It also should be noted that while "learning by caring," in principle, builds the learner's ability to identify societal needs and develop strategies to address them, it is inherently a value creation process. This process is not inherent in previous paradigms, such as "learning by reasoning" and "learning by doing."
However, just like the literature of essential workers or care workers has shown, the act of addressing genuine social needs does not necessarily, or actually often does not, translate directly into monetary gains or feedback.

While "learning by caring" is nearly guaranteed to grow some forms of capital (social, cultural, and perhaps even symbolic capital in their respective fields or communities of concern), it doesn't necessarily bring financial capital. It facilitates reciprocal mutual aid, and since learners are addressing some kind of social needs, they will typically have some kind of job.

But given the unequal reality of jobs in capitalist society, these jobs may or may not (depending on the type of care that they pursue) lead to an economically and financially secure career.

This might, if facilitated, lead to the care of a collective human condition, which might facilitate some kind of emancipatory impulse or collective action impulse. Whether that can be materialized, however, will depend on the exact conditions in their localities and contexts.

It might also facilitate care towards their livelihoods in terms of food, energy, housing, and so on. These needs cannot be met by a mere act of care towards others or mere value creation, but through money and the consideration of whether an alternative to money is possible.
