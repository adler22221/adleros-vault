## Main principle

Your thesis is now strong enough to support a serious scholarly trade book, with a distinct conceptual architecture—universe, world, metaverse, adaptational squeeze, self-finishing, forged Dao—and a broader argument that cuts across philosophy of technology, modernity critique, AI discourse, and East–West comparative thought. So the strategic test for every opportunity is simple: does it help you finish, place, and leverage the book while protecting health and building an accumulating base for 2030? If not, it is secondary even if it sounds glamorous.[1]

That made more sense when the youth dream proposal was centered on academia reform / prefigurative academia, TIAL collaboration, and a broader institutional design residency; now your immediate trunk is a book on the metaverse and adaptational squeeze, which is adjacent to Geoff’s interests but not identical to the original proposal’s institutional architecture focus.[2][1]

So I would shift the Geoff question from “Should I still aim for a year in London?” to “What is the lightest collaboration with Geoff that materially helps the book and future platform?” A better shape now may be: periodic visits, advisory exchange, co-hosted salon/seminar, endorsement for publishers/funders, or a short jointly framed essay on institutional consequences of the metaverse / self-completing artificiality for education and public institutions. ## Funding the failed proposal differently

Because the original proposal and the book have diverged, you probably should not try to resurrect the whole plan under the same framing. Instead, split it into smaller fundable units: book completion support, convening support, short residency support, and prototype/product support.[1][2]

A realistic reframing would be something like this: the book is the main intellectual output, and the institutional-design / prefigurative-academia thread becomes a **secondary applied stream** that can be funded through client projects, small foundations, or fellowship-style support rather than one giant life-encompassing grant. The notes in your file already point to possible models: Japan Foundation or Sasakawa for Asia convenings, procurement/client-funded TIAL-related work, joint bids around post-university architectures, or paid “AI for regenerative learning” / systems-learning products with partner networks.[1]

So if you do pursue outside funding in 2026–2027, I would not pitch “please fund my whole ideal life design.” I would pitch one of three narrower asks:  
1. short writing residency + seminar series,  
2. institutional design scoping project tied to education futures, or

## Dojo and ethics

Your hesitation about the Dojo is healthy. Since the network has been on hold after your health crisis, forcing it to serve the book too directly could easily create exactly the instrumentalizing feeling you want to avoid. The right move is not to make the Dojo “content supply” for your book, but to let the book clarify the philosophical ground on which a future Dojo can re-emerge.[2][1]

That means, in 2026–2027, the Dojo should probably stay in a **low-burn, non-extractive form**: occasional circle, correspondence, informal salon, or mutual witnessing space, rather than a formal branded initiative asking others to contribute labor under your momentum. If you later want to weave it in, the respectful way is explicit co-authorship, explicit consent, and clear distinction between “this network influenced me” and “this network is a vehicle for my book.”[2]

Practically, the Dojo can still matter in two ways without being subordinated to you: as a trusted first circle for feedback, and as a seedbed for the future applied branch after the book lands. In this sense, the Dojo is not a 2026 deliverable but a 2027–2030 platform embryo.

## Japan visa

Your Japan working holiday visa is the most time-sensitive fork because it disappears if not activated by December 15. But that does not mean you should activate it just because it exists. The question is whether Japan can function as a *book-supporting base* with manageable side income and useful intellectual/network value, rather than as an impulsive escape hatch.

You already have several pieces that make Japan more than fantasy: a July 2026 conference trip with free accommodation and high-value academic contact, existing ties through synthetic anthropology, potential exploratory visits for collaboration, and a possible lower-energy side hustle around your friend’s Airbnb/Tabifuku network. That makes Japan uniquely promising as a **low-cost writing-and-research year with embedded field relations**, if and only if the income side is minimally credible before activation.[3][2]

So I would frame the Japan decision as follows: activate the visa only if by roughly October–November you can secure at least two of the following—one low-burn local income lane, one remote income lane, and one clear intellectual program for the year. The local lane could be Tabifuku or other hospitality-related support work; the remote lane could be consulting, editing, teaching, or funded writing; the intellectual program could be book completion plus Japan-based dialogues on synthetic anthropology / Uegaki / metaverse-related thought.[3][2]

If those pieces do not materialize, Japan should remain a short research trip rather than a year-long gamble. The visa is valuable, but not more valuable than health and financial stability.

## Documentary anniversary

The 10th anniversary of the documentary looks less like a separate campaign and more like a **re-entry point into audience, trust, and intergenerational continuity**. Since Angela Pi and Joanne Shen are co-authoring a book on democratic education, using the anniversary to help promote their work could be elegant if done as a genuine ecosystem gesture rather than a nostalgic self-branding exercise.[3]

This could serve several functions at once: reconnect you with your original audience base, honor the next generation of work that grew partly from your earlier interventions, and re-situate you not just as “the documentary guy from 10 years ago” but as someone whose thinking has evolved into deeper civilizational and institutional questions. If designed well, it can also quietly prepare readers for the metaverse book by showing the arc from exam hell and educational suffering toward dependence, artificiality, and the conditions of life itself.[3][2]

The key is not to make the anniversary campaign do too much. A realistic version might be a curated mini-series: one screening/dialogue, one conversation around Angela and Joanne’s book, one essay/newsletter reflecting on what the documentary saw but could not yet name, and one bridge piece connecting educational suffering to the later concept of the squeeze. That way it becomes a **bridge asset**, not a second startup.[3][2]

## Three scenarios

The cleanest way to plan 2026–2027 is to compare three integrated scenarios.

### Scenario 1: Book-first Taiwan/Japan hybrid

You remain based mainly in Taiwan, use the July Japan trip for research and network deepening, and only activate the working holiday visa if a concrete low-burn model becomes visible by autumn. Geoff/TIAL stays light-touch and advisory; the Dojo remains dormant-but-warm; the documentary anniversary is a modest bridge campaign; and your main output is the book proposal, outreach to presses, and audience-building.[2]

This is the safest scenario for coherence. Its weakness is slower international prestige and possibly thinner income unless you secure enough remote consultancy or writing work.

### Scenario 2: Japan writing year with side hustle

You activate the working holiday visa and spend 2026–2027 in Japan as a writing-and-network year, combining book completion, synthetic anthropology relationships, perhaps occasional convenings, and a light side hustle through Tabifuku or adjacent hospitality work. Geoff becomes periodic collaborator rather than residency host, and the book’s East Asian philosophical grounding becomes more embodied and locally anchored.[3][2]

This has strong symbolic and intellectual fit, and may help your health if the life rhythm is gentler. But it is only wise if income is not purely speculative. Without the side-hustle and remote work pieces in place, this scenario becomes romantic but dangerous.

### Scenario 3: UCL/Europe-facing institutional year

You aggressively try to rebuild a UK/Europe-facing year around Geoff, TIAL, short residencies, and funding proposals, with the book and academia-reform/prefigurative-academia stream both in play. This could create prestige, denser networks, and future platform value, but it risks misaligning your actual trunk project, because the metaverse book is not the same thing as the original institutional-reform proposal.[1][2]

This scenario is the most glamorous and the least clean. I would only choose it if Geoff or another funder signals unusually concrete support very soon.[1]

## My view

Right now, the strongest path seems like a **Scenario 1 core with a possible Scenario 2 pivot**. In plain terms: organize the next 6–9 months around the book, outreach, and foundation-building; keep Geoff alive as an amplifier and occasional collaborator rather than a residency objective; use the documentary anniversary as a reconnection bridge; and treat Japan as a serious but conditional writing-year option, not an automatic yes.[2][3]

That gives you a trunk that is coherent, financially less reckless, and respectful of your health and relationships. It also leaves the future open: if the book gains traction, if a short residency or fellowship appears, if Tabifuku becomes real, or if a TIAL-linked project becomes concrete, you can branch from strength instead of from desperation.[1]

The next useful move would be to turn this into a 2026–2027 decision matrix with columns for income, health load, book progress, relationship fit, Dojo ethics, and 2030 accumulation potential.
