## Part 1: The Concept of One World in Wittgenstein and Carnap as Preconditions of Goodman's Thinking

The second day of the seminar opened with a lecture by Professor Michael Hampe, which focused on the philosophical underpinnings of Nelson Goodman’s **Ways of Worldmaking** (which was the theme of the seminar) by examining the conceptions of the world in the early work of Ludwig Wittgenstein (Ludwig Wittgenstein) and Rudolf Carnap (Rudolf Carnap).

### Introduction: The Problem of the World Concept
*   **The World as a Vague Idea:** Hampe began by referencing Immanuel Kant's **Critique of Pure Reason** (Kritik der reinen Vernunft), which posits that words for totalities like "world" (Welt) are not concepts that can be empirically controlled (like "carrot") or mathematically constructed (like "prime number"). They are merely **ideas** (**Ideen**) whose scientific use is uncontrollable and undecidable.
*   **Five Concepts of World:** The lecturer presented five examples from various disciplines to show that "World" is a concept with no fixed meaning: (1) **Physical Cosmology** (temporal and spatial totality), (2) **History** (semantic and value totality, e.g., the world of ancient China), (3) **Psychiatry** (totality of private experience, e.g., the world of a manic-depressive), (4) **Vocational Studies** (shared semantic and value totality, e.g., the business world), and (5) **History of Philosophy** (etymological differences, e.g., Plato’s *Kosmos* and Descartes’ *Le Monde*).
*   **The Foundationalist Project:** Despite this vagueness, both Wittgenstein and Carnap attempted to **fix** the meaning of "world" to make it the most fundamental concept, a project rooted in the **foundationalist** ambition of philosophy. They sought to get rid of the classic modern philosophical alternative: is the world in us (idealism/subjectivism) or are we in the world (realism/objectivism)?

### Wittgenstein and the Logical World of Facts
*   **Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus:** Wittgenstein's early work defined the world tautologically: "The world is everything that is the case." The world is a **totality of facts (Tatsachen), not of things**.
*   **Atomic Facts (Elementarsätze):** The world is constructed from atomic facts, which are the last, indivisible **truth-makers** in any analysis. Facts are complex and made up of "things" (objects), but things only exist as constituents of facts. The facts are governed by **logical space**, which determines which combinations are possible.
*   **Exclusion of Value:** Following a **scientistic** view, only facts (what is true or false) are "in the world." Everything else—**ethics, aesthetics, religious meaning, and happiness**—is **non-factual** and therefore outside the world. Value statements are **meaningless (sinnlos)**, but not *false* (falsch), because they are mere expressions of a subject's *attitude* to the world, not statements about facts *in* the world. This leads to the tragic conclusion that science is **irrelevant** for life's deepest problems, as knowing the facts cannot make one happy or ethical.

### Carnap and the Logical Construction of the World
*   **Logical Structure of the World (Der logische Aufbau der Welt):** Carnap’s work, influenced by Wittgenstein and neo-Kantian Bruno Bauch, was the other major attempt to regulate the concept of world using a **logical-atomist** and **foundationalist** approach.
*   **Autopsychological Elements:** Carnap attempted to construct the world out of elementary relations between **autopsychological elements** (his version of the logical atoms), which are individual, raw, and unanalyzable units of experience.
*   **Scientistic World:** Like Wittgenstein, Carnap’s construction resulted in a purely **scientistic** conception of the world, where everything not scientifically explicable—especially value and ethics—is excluded. This position, however, was later criticized by Nelson Goodman for arbitrarily privileging the scientific symbol system over others, such as art.

## Part 3: Discussion: Unity and Disunity in Worlds

A brief discussion ensued after the lecture, touching upon the concepts of space, language, and cultural decline.

*   **Spatial vs. Semantic Totality (Fitzcarraldo Example):** Using the film *Fitzcarraldo* as a metaphor, the question was raised whether a single physical space (**Umwelt**) can host two entirely different cultural worlds. This led to a brief linguistic comparison of the concept of **"in"** (spatial containment) and **"content"** (semantic containment) in English, German, and Chinese, with Hampe arguing that the ambiguity of these terms is philosophically critical for defining world totalities in Western thought.
*   **The Logic of Falsehood:** Questions were raised regarding the difference between a logically false sentence (e.g., "a triangle is round") and a meaningless value judgment (e.g., "Putin is a criminal") in Wittgenstein’s *Tractatus*. The distinction hinges on whether the statement refers to the **logical form of the world** (in which case it is meaningless if impossible) or a **fact** (in which case it is false if not the case), or a **value** (in which case it is meaningless because it expresses an attitude).
*   **Sloterdijk and Right-Wing Philosophy:** In response to a question from Adler Yang, Hampe commented on German philosopher Peter Sloterdijk's *Spheres* (Sphären) trilogy. Hampe viewed the work as a **biomythological** narrative of existential loss (from the womb/sphere), which is **not scientifically controlled** and has not been widely accepted as a new mythology. The discussion connected Sloterdijk to a lineage of German philosophers of **cultural decline** (e.g., Oswald Spengler's *Decline of the West* and Martin Heidegger), noting that a desire to return to an **unquestioned unity** (the "sphere" or "authentic existence") can deny the reflective nature of human existence and conceptually align with right-wing thought, as demonstrated by the political views of a former Sloterdijk disciple.
