The seminar session was divided into a philosophical lecture and a guided discussion, focusing on the work of Ernst Cassirer in relation to cultural studies and the concept of "world."

## Part 1: The Reception of Ernst Cassirer in the Chinese-Speaking World (Lecture in Chinese)

The introductory lecture, given by Professor 林遠澤, covered three main areas: the history of Cassirer’s reception in the Chinese-speaking world, the development of his philosophical system, and an argument for establishing a "Sinology of Man" (漢學人類學) based on his thought.

### Cassirer's Reception
*   **Early Interest:** Interest in Cassirer's philosophy, particularly his **Essay on Man** (《人論》), began early, with translations and discussions occurring in the Chinese-speaking world as early as the 1950s and 60s.
*   **Influence on Intellectuals:** The philosopher Mou Zongsan (牟宗三) was noted as an early figure influenced by Cassirer, citing *The Myth of the State* (《國家神話》) to analyze totalitarianism in the modern state.
*   **Contemporary Focus:** Interest in Cassirer intensified in the 1990s and after the 2000s, moving beyond the popular *Essay on Man* to explore his deeper systematic work, the **Philosophy of Symbolic Forms** (《符號形式哲學》).

### Connecting Cassirer and Chinese Culture
*   **The Research Gap:** The core argument was that Cassirer's work, which views humans as "symbolic animals," primarily analyzed cultural expressions (like language, myth, and science) within the framework of Western inflected languages (German/European).
*   **The Proposed Solution:** To establish an "autonomous system of thought for the humanities and social sciences" rooted in the Chinese context, a Cassirer-style study is needed for the Chinese language—specifically, an isolating language with ideographic characters.
*   **Sinology of Man:** This research should apply Cassirer's stages of cultural philosophy (Cultural Science, Philosophy of Symbolic Forms, Philosophical Anthropology) to Chinese cultural domains (literature, philosophy, religion, art, ethics) to provide a unified explanation of human subjective capacities (**sensibility, understanding, imagination, will, and belief**) and answer the universal question: "What is Man?"

## Part 2: Discussion: One World or Many Worlds?

The discussion explored the philosophical ramifications of cultural and individual difference on the concept of "world," contrasting the idea of a single, universal reality with a plurality of co-existing worlds.

### Arguments for "Many Worlds"
*   **Linguistic Worlds:** Language was posited as a primary differentiator. The structure of a language (e.g., German forcing grammatical gender and an agent; Japanese/Chinese marking social hierarchy) forces its speakers to make certain decisions, thereby creating distinct *world versions* or even separate worlds.
*   **Individual *Umwelten* (Environments):** The concept of *Umwelt* (Umwelt, or "surrounding world") from biologist Jakob von Uexküll (Uexküll) was introduced. An *Umwelt* is the subjectively limited, sensory, and functional world of an individual organism (e.g., the tick, with its limited sensory input). Each person is seen as having their own *Umwelt*, leading to an individuating factor of experience that is "never fully accessible to others."
*   **Symbolic Worlds:** Differences also arise from distinct symbolic forms (Cassirer's view) and spheres of experience, such as the worlds of **dreams, fictional literature, religion, and historical narratives.**

### Arguments for "One World" (or Unity via Plurality)
*   **Pragmatic Unity:** The possibility of communication, translation (e.g., multilingual individuals switching between different linguistic and cultural systems), and shared experience (e.g., convening in the same seminar room) suggests an underlying, common reality.
*   **The Inaccessible World:** A universal "one world" may exist, but it is **never directly accessible** or fully theorizable; it is only approached through a plurality of different cultural and individual versions.
*   **The Logical Problem of Pluralism:** Philosopher Michael Hampe raised a critical logical challenge to strict pluralism: if worlds are truly separate and isolated, from which "world" does one articulate the hypothesis that a plurality of worlds exists? He suggested that the difficulty stems from treating the term "world" as an all-encompassing totality, similar to Russell's Paradox.

The discussion concluded with the general agreement that while one world may exist, it can only be accessed and understood through an inherent **plurality of symbolic forms and world versions.**
