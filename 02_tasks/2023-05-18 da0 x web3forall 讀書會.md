While the article didn't use the language of complexity or systems thinking/theories, it's an excellent example that articulates the self-organising/adaptive nature of structures, and how structures reproduce themselves when the underlying mechanisms and mental models have not changed. What it didn't articulate enough is that, "imposing" structure doesn't solve such problem per se, as the mentioned problems do not automatically dissolve in structured organizations -- non-written rules still exist and dominate; and often time, if not most of the time, the formalism of the "formal structures" becomes decorational/symbolic ritual-game played (玩弄於鼓掌之上) by the persons in power, which justify and help reproduce the status quo. In other words, the "formal structure" approach doesn't eliminate the tyranny as raised by Jo, but only offer temporal stablizations (which may be for the better or worse) between the iterations of power struggles. What can  complexity/systems perspectives offer in face of this predicament? While more empirical research is needed, the theory of complex adaptive systems (CAS) tells us that complex phenomenon emerge from agents interacting based on simple rules. Contra "floor-plan" approaches based on static snapshots of ideal scenarios, which has reductionistic and mechanistic implications and often overlook the dynamic (self-organizing/adaptive) nature of organizations, "rule-based" approaches based on the knowledge of the "rule-emergent property" correspondence might better address the (formal) "structure/structureless" dichotomy. Of course, the problems of the source, legitimacy, and implementation of rules are additional problems to be addressed.
