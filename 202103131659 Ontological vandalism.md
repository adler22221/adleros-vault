[[@pilgrimReclaimingRealityRedefining2018]]
[[Pilgrim, David]]

Ontological vandalism at times attends a new style of political rhetoric linked to identity politics, which is based upon privileging our unending right to self-define ourselves as human beings. ==We can choose to be whoever we want to be and others should simply and immediately accept that fact. If they do not then they are violating our human rights.==^[Can be related to [[上柿崇英]]'s work.] At this point, actual reality and a subjectively desired or imagined reality are conflated by the desiring subject. If others accept that ontological claim then it simply goes unnoticed. The political problem arises when others are offended by the claim and its mandate for action. At this point, incommensurable political positions inevitably emerge: the very scenario of the fractious ‘trans debate’.

A man who feels the need to experience life as a woman, but without any surgical interference, might self-define himself as a ‘woman with a penis’. He might not only dress as a women but enter the female changing rooms of their local swimming pool. The ‘woman with a penis’ might reserve the right to strip naked there. The reaction of women present would probably be one of anger and distress, as the gap between the intransitive aspects of sex and the transitive aspects of gender is literally laid bare. The conduct of ‘the woman with a penis’ is premised on the idea that reality is only a matter of personal choice. An implied corollary of this claim to existential legitimacy is that we can act in any way we wish in accordance with these self-definitions.

To demonstrate the anti-realism of this form of political action, and its implied radical
social constructivism, take the example of ‘trans racialism’. Within the equivalent logic, a white person could have injected pigmentation and seek nose broadening and lip thickening. In the other direction a black person could bleach their skin and straighten their hair. But neither candidate for transracial identities would have altered their genotypically determined natal racial ontology. Ironically trans-racialism actually implicates more ontological ambiguity because race, unlike sex, is not a binary. Our highly mixed human gene pool entails variations in the alignment between genotypes and phenotypes. Whereas a man and a man or a woman and a woman cannot produce a fertilized ovum, men and women from any racial background can do so.

Currently, transracial subjective aspirations are seen as problematic or even comical,
whereas transgender aspirations are being normalized in liberal democracies. And yet
both reflect a desire by some people, for psychological or political reasons, to want to
be some body12 that they are not at present.13 And this is not just a matter of redefining our natal selves. For example, an emergent trans phenomenon is trans-ageism: an adult could claim that they are really still a child and so they demand to be treated by others accordingly.14 

The attempted erasure by radical social constructivism of biological ontology and its replacement by bids for legitimacy for unendingly plural trans identities has been reflected in current health policy. Apart from the recent recommendation that ‘persons’ not women become pregnant, health service budgets are spent on providing positive support for gender re-assignment. In the UK NHS clinics are designated for this purpose, implicating the specialist labour of psychiatrists, psychologists, endocrinologists and surgeons. The Department of Health provides policy documents normalizing their interventions. Self-ascribed gender assignment in conjunction with sympathetic medical decision-making is now enshrined in law.
