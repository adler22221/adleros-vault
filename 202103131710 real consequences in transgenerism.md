This final sub-section spells out the implications for public policy formation, when and if there is no distinction made between sex in the intransitive domain of reality and
gender in the transitive domain. One has already been explored above because of its centrality within trans politics: transgender status warrants access to special healthcare interventions in the absence of physical pathology. Transgenderism thereby brings with it *medicalization* and *commercialization*, with costs at times to the public purse.^[Could probably be viewed in parallel with how the free choice in education lead to the commercialization of education?]

A second implication is the confusion set in train about human rights. For example
should a trans woman prisoner have the right to be transferred to a women’s prison?
What if they were a rapist? On the other hand, what if transwomen are retained in male prisons and then they are sexually assaulted? Confusion ensues immediately about these questions because of the gap between sex and gender. If penal and healthcare policies about adults are controversial, then so too is the matter of trans-children.^[A response to this is to resolve this practically: Dedicate special prisons for trans persons. A potential challenge might also arise, as sexual assault doesn't only happen among opposite sexes, but also happens among same-sexes. If cis male might also sexually assault cis males, so as cis females to cis females, what makes transwomen's assault to cis female special?]

Adult trans people and their ‘TERF’ opponents will and do exchange their competing
views and demands on rights of access. However, the trans debate has brought with it a mixed reaction from parents when their children express a need to transition to
another gender. From a ‘TERF’ perspective, when parents are drawn into this to the
extent of unconditional support and giving permission for hormonal interventions, then this is considered a form of child abuse. Concerns about the latter are supported by empirical evidence that most children expressing gender dysphoria who are supported to desist ‘transitioning’, later want to return to their allocated natal sex (Cantor 2017).

Given that all children who receive endocrine interventions (to block puberty or introduce sex hormones) or surgery (such as the removal of breast tissue or castration) are being exposed to iatrogenic risk, then this becomes a serious matter of medical ethics. After all, children cannot give fully informed consent to medical treatment. It also is a source of angst for parents. Parents may (or may not) believe that they are supporting the right of their child to become who they want to be, while being anxious to make the correct decision about their child’s future. ‘Gender dysphoria’ then has consequences for the distress of significant others, it is not merely about the distress of the identified child-patient.
